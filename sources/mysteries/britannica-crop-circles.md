**crop circle**, large geometric pattern of flattened crops, most often found in fields in southern England. Crop circles are said by some who have studied them to be messages from intelligent [extraterrestrial life](https://www.britannica.com/science/extraterrestrial-life), but many have been proved to be the work of humans.

Beginning in the late 1970s, simple crop circles began to appear regularly in the fields of Wiltshire, Oxfordshire, Somerset, and Gloucestershire in southern England. They were made at night, and over the years they became more complex, growing into large patterns of geometric forms hundreds of feet across. Some people who studied crop circles were convinced that their intricacy and the fact that the plants seemed to be bent but not broken precluded a human creator, which meant that they were being produced either by some unknown natural phenomenon or by extraterrestrials.

[![Crop circle in England.](https://cdn.britannica.com/14/152614-050-DE21103A/Crop-circle-England.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/14/152614-050-DE21103A/Crop-circle-England.jpg)

[Crop circle in England.](https://cdn.britannica.com/14/152614-050-DE21103A/Crop-circle-England.jpg)(more)

In 1991 Doug Bower and Dave Chorley, of Southampton, England, confessed to having made more than 200 crop circles since the late 1970s with nothing more complex than ropes and boards. They had initially been inspired by a 1966 account of a [UFO](https://www.britannica.com/topic/unidentified-flying-object) sighting near Tully, Queensland, Australia, in which a flying saucer supposedly landed in a lagoon and left behind a depressed area of reeds. As crop circles became more prominent in the media, Bower and Chorley’s patterns became more complex, and they delighted in confounding the expectations of those who studied the circles.

**agronomy**, branch of [agriculture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agriculture) that deals with field [crop](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crop-agriculture) production and [soil](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil) management. Agronomists generally work with crops that are grown on a large scale (e.g., small grains) and that require relatively little management. Agronomic experiments focus on a variety of factors relating to crop plants, including yield, [diseases](https://www.britannica.com/science/plant-disease), [cultivation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cultivation), pest and weed management, and sensitivity to factors such as climate and soil. Agronomists may specialize in [plant breeding](https://www.britannica.com/science/plant-breeding) and [biotechnology](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biotechnology) to improve crops. Many agronomists also utilize ecological principles to conserve and protect the [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) from the negative effects of agriculture, a field known as agroecology.

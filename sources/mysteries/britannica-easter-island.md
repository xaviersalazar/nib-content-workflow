- What are the giant statues on Easter Island called?







The giant statues on Easter Island are called [moai](https://www.britannica.com/art/moai-figure/Introduction#ref1). These figures, of which there are over 600, are thought to be representations of ancestors. Most moai range from 10 to 20 feet in height, but some are much larger, with the biggest formerly standing on an ahu (stone platform) being about 32 feet tall. The statues were carved from yellow-gray tuff found in the crater walls of the volcano Rano Raraku.

- How were the moai statues built and moved across the island?







The [moai](https://www.britannica.com/art/Oceanic-art/Polynesia#ref225516) are monolithic statues on Easter Island that represent deified important personalities. Carved from yellow-gray tuff found in the volcano Rano Raraku, the work on the statues began around 900 CE.



The process involved completing the front and sides before detaching the back from the bedrock. Then, the statue was slid to a spot below the quarries to have its back finished before being moved. Eye cavities and topknots were added after the monument was erected.



Experiments in 1955–56, based on island traditions, showed that the basalt picks were suitable for carving the hard tuff. In 1986, an experiment revealed that 15 men could move a medium-sized statue upright by jerking it ahead with ropes.

**Easter Island**, Chilean [dependency](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dependency) in the eastern [Pacific Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean). It is the easternmost outpost of the Polynesian island world. It is famous for its giant stone statues. The island stands in isolation 1,200 miles (1,900 km) east of [Pitcairn Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pitcairn-Island) and 2,200 miles (3,540 km) west of [Chile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chile). Forming a triangle 14 miles (23 km) long by 7 miles (11 km) wide, it has an area of 63 square miles (163 square km); its highest point, Mount Terevaka, is 1,969 feet (600 metres) above [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level).

To its original inhabitants the island is known as Rapa Nui (“Great Rapa”) or Te Pito te Henua (“Navel of the World”). The first European visitors, the Dutch, named it Paaseiland (“Easter Island”) in memory of their own day of arrival. Its mixed population is predominantly of Polynesian descent; almost all live in the village of [Hanga Roa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hanga-Roa) on the sheltered west coast. Pop. (2002) 3,304; (2017) 7,750.

## Relief

![Easter Island](https://cdn.britannica.com/04/3304-050-EB21D983/Easter-Island.jpg)

[Easter Island](https://cdn.britannica.com/04/3304-050-EB21D983/Easter-Island.jpg)(more)

The small and hilly island is not part of a sunken landmass but is a typical oceanic high island formed by volcanoes rising from the seafloor. Geologic and oceanographic evidence shows that no perceptible emergence or submergence of the island’s coastline has taken place since the last fall in sea level, which occurred less than 10,000 years ago. However, in the early 21st century Easter Island experienced increasing coastal erosion because of rising sea levels, which also threatened various archeological sites. Three extinct volcanoes chiefly composed of [tuff](https://www.britannica.com/science/tuff) (a [porous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/porous) rock formed of compacted volcanic fragments) and joined by their own [lava](https://www.britannica.com/science/lava-volcanic-ejecta) flows give the island its characteristic triangular shape. Parasitic tuff craters and cones (i.e., craters and cones formed on the side of, or near, volcanoes after the original vent has become plugged up) are interspersed in the landscape, which is otherwise dominated by eroded lava fields in which [obsidian](https://www.britannica.com/science/obsidian) is commonly found.

Most of these fields are thickly packed with both large and small lumps of cellular and tuffaceous lava that is either black or rusty in colour. Stoneless surface [soil](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil) is sparse; it is suitable for extensive cultivation mainly in the [Hanga Roa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hanga-Roa) and Mataveri area in the southwest, at Vaihu and on the plain southwest of the [volcano](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcano) Rano Raraku, and on the prehistorically cleared Poike peninsula in the eastern corner of the island. Rain collects in the partly bog-covered crater lakes of the volcanoes Rano Kao, Rano Raraku, and Rano Aroi. One [intermittent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intermittent) stream, fed by the Rano Aroi crater lake, flows down Mount Terevaka’s slopes before disappearing into the porous soil. Water from the extremely deep crater of Rano Kao, which is about 3,000 feet (900 metres) wide, is piped to Hanga Roa. The coast is formed by soft, eroded, ashy cliffs, with a vertical drop of about 500 to 1,000 feet (150 to 300 metres); the cliffs are intercepted by long stretches of [low](https://www.britannica.com/science/cyclone-meteorology), hard, and rugged lava formations.

There is no natural harbour, but anchorage is found off Hanga Roa on the west coast; off Vinapu and Hotu-Iti on the south coast; and off Anakena and in the Bahía la Perouse on the north coast. Notable among the few small offshore islets are Motu-Nui, Motu-Iti, and Motu-Kaokao (which figured in a local bird cult) near the southwest cape. The only true sand beach is at Anakena; most other beaches are of gravel. Caves abound, many consisting of subterranean rooms joined by narrow tunnels extending far into the lava beds.

## Climate

The climate is subtropical: i.e., sunny and dry. The warmest months are January through March, when the average temperature is 73 °F (23 °C), and the coolest months are June through August, when the average temperature is 64 °F (18 °C). Average annual [precipitation](https://www.britannica.com/science/precipitation) is about 49 inches (1,250 mm) but with considerable annual variation. September is the driest month, and the heaviest rainfall occurs in June and July in accordance with the passage of austral winter fronts. Winds in June and August are irregular; during the rest of the year trade winds from the east and southeast are dominant. From September through March the [Peru](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru-Current) (or Humboldt) Current, which has an average temperature of about 70 °F (21 °C), flows against the island.

## Plant and animal life

[Indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Indigenous) plants and animals are few. At the time of European arrival the toromiro tree, [endemic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/endemic) to the island, was the only wild tree and the Carolina wolfberry the only wild shrub, the vegetation being predominantly herbaceous. The toromiro tree was overexploited by the island wood carvers, and the last local specimen died in the 1950s. The species was saved from [extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology), however; the Norwegian Archaeological Expedition collected seeds and planted them in the Gothenburg Botanical Garden, and saplings from the garden were reintroduced to Easter Island in 1988. Analysis of [pollen](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollen) deposits has revealed that other trees and shrubs, among them the giant Chile [palm](https://www.britannica.com/plant/palm-tree) ( _Jubaea spectabilis_), were formerly present on the island until exterminated by extensive fires occurring at the time of aboriginal human settlement.

Today only 31 wild flowering plants, 14 ferns, and 14 mosses are reported. Grass and small ferns dominate the barren landscape, whereas the boggy crater lakes are thickly covered by two imported American species, the totora reed (an important building material) and _Polygonum acuminatum_ (a medicinal plant). A number of [cultivated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultivated) species of plants were also introduced partly from [America](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) and partly from Polynesia before the arrival of Europeans; of these the principal species was the [sweet potato](https://www.britannica.com/plant/sweet-potato), which was cultivated in extensive plantations and formed the staple diet. Bottle gourds, sugarcane, bananas, taro, yams, and two useful trees (i.e., the Asiatic [paper mulberry](https://www.britannica.com/plant/paper-mulberry), with bark used for cloth manufacture, and the American _Triumfetta semitriloba_, with bark used for rope making) were of aboriginal importation, as also probably were the husk-tomato, a small variety of pineapple, and the coconut.

Before the arrival of human beings, the only vertebrates were either fish or seabirds capable of long flights. The animal life on land was otherwise restricted to a very few species of [isopods](https://www.britannica.com/animal/isopod) (an order of crustaceans), spiders, insects, worms, a snail, and a centipede. Vast quantities of flies, large cockroaches, and a small scorpion were introduced recently. A small, long-legged chicken reported to have laid blue eggs was introduced in pre-European times but later interbred with European varieties. The aboriginal edible Polynesian rat was subsequently replaced by larger European species. Sheep, horses, cattle, and pigs were introduced by the missionaries who established themselves ashore in 1864. Sheep were especially numerous for almost a century after foreign ranchers began commercial ranching in 1870; sheep ranching came to an end in the mid-1980s, but cattle ranching was [enhanced](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhanced). A large wild cat, living in caves, is of unknown introduction. A Chilean partridge, a quail, and a small hawk have been added to the wildlife since 1880. Sea turtles and seals are now rare curiosities, but crayfish and various coastal and deep-sea fishes abound around the coast.

# People of Easter Island

The island’s population represents the easternmost settlement of a basically Polynesian subgroup that probably derived from the [Marquesas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Marquesas-Islands) group. The original Rapa Nui vocabulary has been lost except for some mixed Polynesian and non-Polynesian words recorded before the Tahitian [dialect](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dialect) was introduced to the decimated population by missionaries in 1864. Today Spanish is generally spoken. In their traditions, the islanders consistently divide themselves into descendants of two distinct ethnic groups, the “Long-Ears” and the “Short-Ears” (see below). Intermarriage is common, and an influx of foreign blood has become increasingly dominant in recent years.

## Economy

Whereas the aboriginal economy was based on the cultivation of [sweet potato](https://www.britannica.com/plant/sweet-potato), chicken raising, and coastal fishing, the island has shifted to a cash economy based on tourism. The opening of an airport at Mataveri near [Hanga Roa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hanga-Roa) has permitted an increasing influx of tourists since the 1960s, and a few small hotels have been built in the village area, where many islanders and settlers from continental [Chile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chile) also have accommodations in their homes. The ties to continental Chile are strengthened through twice-weekly flights from [Santiago](https://www.britannica.com/place/Santiago-region-Chile) and the building of schools, hospitals, and a large [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) hall for sports and performances. A well-organized Chilean [national park](https://www.britannica.com/science/national-park) system provides guided tours and security for the unique archaeological monuments. Reforestation projects have been successfully initiated, including eucalyptus plantations at Vaitea and coconut groves in Anakena Bay.

## History

[![Easter Island: moai](https://cdn.britannica.com/38/94538-050-83B18723/Moai-Easter-Island-Chile.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/38/94538-050-83B18723/Moai-Easter-Island-Chile.jpg)

[Easter Island: _moai_](https://cdn.britannica.com/38/94538-050-83B18723/Moai-Easter-Island-Chile.jpg) _Moai_ (busts) on Easter Island, Chile.(more)

The first European to land on Easter Island was the Dutch admiral [Jacob Roggeveen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jacob-Roggeveen), who paid it a single day’s visit in 1722. He and his crew found a population that they described as being of mixed physical types who worshiped huge standing statues with fires while they prostrated themselves to the [rising sun](https://www.britannica.com/topic/rising-sun-Egyptian-amulet). Some of them, said to be “white men,” had their earlobes slit and hanging to their shoulders, a distinctly non-Polynesian custom.

An [expedition](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/expedition) dispatched by the Spanish viceroy of [Peru](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru) rediscovered the island in 1770. The Spanish spent four days ashore and were the first to report that the aborigines had their own local form of script. They estimated a population of some 3,000 persons.

A civil war seems to have raged on the island before the arrival of the British navigator Captain [James Cook](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Cook) in 1774; a decimated, poverty-stricken Polynesian population of only about 600 or 700 men and fewer than 30 women was found by the Englishmen, who also observed that the large statues were no longer venerated, most of them having been deliberately overthrown. In 1786 the French navigator [Jean-François de Galaup, comte de La Pérouse](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Francois-de-Galaup-comte-de-La-Perouse), arrived and found some 2,000 people on the island; he tried in vain to introduce domestic animals. A number of sailing vessels, including whalers, visited the island from 1792 onward. By 1860 the population was about 3,000, but a major slave raid launched from Peru in 1862, followed by [smallpox](https://www.britannica.com/science/smallpox) [epidemics](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/epidemics), reduced the population to 111 in 1877. At the end of the 19th century it began to increase once more. In 1864 Brother Eugène Eyraud, a French Catholic missionary, became the first foreigner to settle on the island; as a result, the population became converted to Christianity by 1868. Settlers from [Tahiti](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tahiti) began to raise sheep in 1870.

In 1888 the island was annexed by Chile, which leased nearly all its territory for sheep raising; in 1954 the Chilean navy administration took over the sheep range. In 1965 a civilian governor was appointed by the Chilean government, and the islanders became full Chilean citizens. Within a single generation the Easter Islanders successfully responded to a complete [acculturation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/acculturation) to continental standards without losing their pride in their own ancestors and their skills and customs. Annually in February old and young of both sexes meet in contests to revive the arts and practices of the island’s past, including carving, tattooing, reed-boat building, and traditional singing and dancing. In 2018 the Rapa Nui Marine Protected Area, [encompassing](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompassing) 286,000 square miles (740,000 square km) of [Pacific Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean) surrounding Easter Island, was established.

### Has Amelia Earhart been found?

No, neither Amelia Earhart nor her plane have ever been found. On July 2, 1937, Earhart and her navigator, Fred Noonan, were attempting to land on [Howland Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Howland-Island), a small coral atoll in the southwestern [Pacific Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean). The duo were trying to fly around the world. Earhart had radioed the U.S. Coast Guard cutter _Itasca_ that the plane was running out of fuel. About an hour later she announced, “We are running north and south.” That was the last transmission received by the _Itasca_. The plane was believed to have gone down some 100 miles (160 km) from the island. Searches in the ensuing decades have failed to find Earhart and Noonan.

### What did Amelia Earhart accomplish?

Amelia Earhart was one of the world’s most celebrated aviators and was the first woman to [fly](https://www.britannica.com/technology/aviation) solo across the [Atlantic Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean) (1932). In addition to her piloting feats, Earhart was known for encouraging women to reject constrictive social norms and to pursue various opportunities, especially in the field of aviation.

### Why is July 24 Amelia Earhart Day?

July 24 is National Amelia Earhart Day, honoring the famed aviator’s birthday (July 24, 1897). Earhart was one of the world’s most celebrated aviators, setting numerous records. Notably, in 1932 she became the first woman to fly solo across the [Atlantic Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean). In 1937 Earhart and her copilot disappeared over the [Pacific Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean) while attempting to fly around the world. Their fate became an enduring mystery that captured the public’s imagination and generated numerous theories.

### What is Amelia Earhart remembered for?

Amelia Earhart was famous during her life for her numerous [aviation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/aviation) records, most notably being the first woman to [fly](https://www.britannica.com/technology/aviation) solo across the [Atlantic Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean) (1932). The mysterious disappearance of Earhart and her copilot in the [Pacific Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean) during their attempt to fly around the world (1937) captured the public’s imagination and generated numerous theories.

### What were Amelia Earhart’s jobs?

Before she became famous, Amelia Earhart was a nurse’s aid tending to injured [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I) soldiers in [Toronto](https://www.britannica.com/place/Toronto) and was a [social worker](https://www.britannica.com/topic/social-service) at a [settlement house](https://www.britannica.com/topic/social-settlement) in [Boston](https://www.britannica.com/place/Boston). When she became a celebrity for her [aviation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/aviation) feats, she began delivering lectures and writing books about her flights.

**Amelia Earhart** (born July 24, 1897, [Atchison](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atchison-Kansas), [Kansas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kansas), U.S.—disappeared July 2, 1937, near Howland Island, central Pacific Ocean) was an American aviator, one of the world’s most celebrated, who was the first woman to fly solo across the [Atlantic Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean). Her disappearance during a flight around the world in 1937 became an enduring mystery, fueling much speculation.

## Early life

[![Atchison: childhood home of Amelia Earhart](https://cdn.britannica.com/69/152769-004-1FD2E12E/Childhood-home-Amelia-Earhart-Atchison-Kansas.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/69/152769-004-1FD2E12E/Childhood-home-Amelia-Earhart-Atchison-Kansas.jpg)

[Atchison: childhood home of Amelia Earhart](https://cdn.britannica.com/69/152769-004-1FD2E12E/Childhood-home-Amelia-Earhart-Atchison-Kansas.jpg) Childhood home of Amelia Earhart, Atchison, Kansas.(more)

Earhart’s father was a railroad lawyer, and her mother came from an [affluent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/affluent) family. While still a child, Earhart displayed an adventurous and independent nature for which she would later become known. After the death of her grandparents, the family struggled financially amid her father’s [alcoholism](https://www.britannica.com/science/alcoholism). The Earharts moved often, and she completed [high school](https://www.britannica.com/topic/high-school) in [Chicago](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chicago) in 1916. After her mother received her inheritance, Earhart was able to attend the Ogontz School in Rydal, Pennsylvania. However, during a visit to her sister in Canada, Amelia developed an interest in caring for soldiers wounded in [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I). In 1918 she left [junior college](https://www.britannica.com/topic/junior-college) to become a nurse’s aide in [Toronto](https://www.britannica.com/place/Toronto).

## Historic flights

[![Amelia Earhart](https://cdn.britannica.com/02/164802-050-8514C49E/Amelia-Earhart-cockpit-airplane-Lockheed-Electra.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/02/164802-050-8514C49E/Amelia-Earhart-cockpit-airplane-Lockheed-Electra.jpg)

[Amelia Earhart](https://cdn.britannica.com/02/164802-050-8514C49E/Amelia-Earhart-cockpit-airplane-Lockheed-Electra.jpg) Amelia Earhart in the cockpit of a Lockheed Electra airplane.(more)

After the war, Earhart entered the premed program at [Columbia University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Columbia-University) in [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City) but left in 1920 after her parents insisted that she live with them in California. There she went on her first [airplane](https://www.britannica.com/technology/airplane) ride in 1920, an experience that prompted her to take flying lessons. In 1921 she bought her first plane, a Kinner Airster, and two years later she earned her pilot’s license. In the mid-1920s Earhart moved to [Massachusetts](https://www.britannica.com/place/Massachusetts), where she became a social worker at the Denison House, a settlement home for immigrants in [Boston](https://www.britannica.com/place/Boston). She also continued to pursue her interest in aviation.

During this time promoters sought to have a woman fly across the Atlantic Ocean, and in April 1928 Earhart was selected for the flight. Some [speculated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/speculated) that the decision was partly based on her resemblance to [Charles Lindbergh](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Lindbergh), who had become the first man to fly nonstop solo across the Atlantic the previous year. On June 17, 1928, Earhart departed Trepassey, [Newfoundland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Newfoundland-and-Labrador), Canada, as a passenger aboard a seaplane piloted by Wilmer Stultz and Louis Gordon. After landing at Burry Port, [Wales](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wales), on June 18, Earhart became an international celebrity. She wrote about the flight in _20 Hrs. 40 Min._ (1928) and undertook a lecture tour across the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States). Much of the publicity was handled by publisher George Palmer Putnam, who had helped organize the historic flight. The couple married in 1931, but Earhart continued her career under her maiden name. That year she also piloted an [autogiro](https://www.britannica.com/technology/autogiro) to a record-setting [altitude](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/altitude) of 18,415 feet (5,613 metres).

Determined to justify the renown that her 1928 crossing had brought her, Earhart crossed the Atlantic alone on May 20–21, 1932. Her flight in her Lockheed Vega from [Harbour Grace](https://www.britannica.com/place/Harbour-Grace), [Newfoundland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Newfoundland-and-Labrador), to [Londonderry](https://www.britannica.com/place/Londonderry-city-and-district-Northern-Ireland), [Northern Ireland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Northern-Ireland), was completed in a record time of 14 hours 56 minutes despite a number of problems. She notably experienced mechanical difficulties and inclement weather and was unable to land in her scheduled destination of Paris. Afterward she published _The Fun of It_ (1932), in which she wrote about her life and interest in flying. Earhart then undertook a series of flights across the United States.

In addition to her piloting feats, Earhart was known for encouraging women to reject constrictive social [norms](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/norms) and to pursue various opportunities, especially in the field of [aviation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/aviation). In 1929 she helped found an organization of female pilots that later became known as the Ninety-Nines. Earhart served as its first president. In addition, she debuted a functional clothing line in 1933, which was designed “for the woman who lives actively.”

[![Amelia Earhart](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/164576-050-40FF17F0/Amelia-Earhart.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/164576-050-40FF17F0/Amelia-Earhart.jpg)

[Amelia Earhart](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/164576-050-40FF17F0/Amelia-Earhart.jpg)(more)

In 1935 Earhart made history with the first solo flight from [Hawaii](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hawaii-island-Hawaii) to [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state), a hazardous route of 2,408 miles (3,875 km), which was about 500 miles longer than her 1932 crossing of the Atlantic. She departed from [Honolulu](https://www.britannica.com/place/Honolulu) on January 11 and, after 17 hours and 7 minutes, landed in [Oakland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Oakland-California) the following day. Later that year she became the first person to fly solo from [Los Angeles](https://www.britannica.com/place/Los-Angeles-California) to [Mexico City](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico-City).

## Final flight and disappearance

[![Amelia Earhart](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/164577-050-4AEF5C99/Amelia-Earhart-reporters-1937.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/164577-050-4AEF5C99/Amelia-Earhart-reporters-1937.jpg)

[Amelia Earhart](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/164577-050-4AEF5C99/Amelia-Earhart-reporters-1937.jpg) Amelia Earhart (center) talking to reporters, 1937.(more)

In 1937 Earhart set out to fly around the world, with Fred Noonan as her navigator, in a twin-engine Lockheed Electra. On June 1 the duo began their 29,000-mile (47,000-km) journey, departing from [Miami](https://www.britannica.com/place/Miami-Florida) and heading east. Over the following weeks they made various refueling stops before reaching [Lae](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lae), [New Guinea](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Guinea), on June 29. At that point, Earhart and Noonan had traveled some 22,000 miles (35,000 km).

They departed on July 2, headed for [Howland Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Howland-Island), approximately 2,600 miles (4,200 km) away. The flight was expected to be [arduous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/arduous), especially since the tiny coral atoll was difficult to locate. To help with navigation, two brightly lit U.S. ships were stationed to mark the route. Earhart was also in [intermittent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intermittent) radio contact with the _Itasca_, a [U.S. Coast Guard](https://www.britannica.com/topic/United-States-Coast-Guard) cutter near Howland. Late in the journey, Earhart radioed that the plane was running out of fuel. About an hour later she announced, “We are running north and south.” That was the last transmission received by the _Itasca_. The plane was believed to have gone down some 100 miles (160 km) from the island, and an extensive search was undertaken to find Earhart and Noonan. However, on July 19, 1937, the operation was called off, and the pair was declared lost at sea. Throughout the trip, Earhart had sent her husband various materials, including letter and diary entries, and these were published in _Last Flight_ (1937).

Earhart’s mysterious disappearance captured the public’s imagination and generated numerous theories and claims. Notably, some believed that she and Noonan had crashed on a different island after failing to locate Howland, and others posited that they were captured by the Japanese. However, no definitive evidence was found for such claims. Most experts believe that Earhart’s plane crashed in the Pacific near Howland after running out of fuel. A fixture in popular [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture), she was the subject of numerous books and movies.

**Women Airforce Service Pilots (WASP)**, [U.S. Army Air Forces](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-United-States-Air-Force) program that tasked some 1,100 civilian women with noncombat military flight duties during [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II). The Women Airforce Service Pilots (WASP) were the first women to fly U.S. military aircraft.

WASP had its origins with a pair of exceptionally skilled and ambitious female flyers. Prior to the U.S. entry into World War II, Nancy Harkness Love, the youngest American woman to have earned her private pilot’s license until that time, had lobbied for the creation of a program that would allow female pilots to ferry warplanes from factories to air bases. At the same time, [Jackie Cochran](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jacqueline-Cochran), one of the most-accomplished pilots of her era, demonstrated the feasibility of such an idea by flying a [lend-lease](https://www.britannica.com/topic/lend-lease) [bomber](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bomber-aircraft) to [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) and organizing a group of female pilots for war transport service as part of the British Air Transport Auxiliary. By 1942, as the war reduced the number of qualified male pilots available for transport duty, American military leaders had become increasingly receptive to Love’s and Cochran’s ideas.

[![Women Airforce Service Pilots](https://cdn.britannica.com/87/164687-050-924BF154/flight-line-Women-Airforce-Service-Pilots-Laredo-1943.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/87/164687-050-924BF154/flight-line-Women-Airforce-Service-Pilots-Laredo-1943.jpg)

[Women Airforce Service Pilots](https://cdn.britannica.com/87/164687-050-924BF154/flight-line-Women-Airforce-Service-Pilots-Laredo-1943.jpg) Three Women Airforce Service Pilots (WASP) walking on the flight line at Laredo Army Airfield in Laredo, Texas, _c._ 1943.(more)

In September 1942 Love organized the Women’s Auxiliary Ferrying Squadron (WAFS), and more than two dozen of the country’s best female civilian pilots were soon reporting to New Castle Army Air Base in [Delaware](https://www.britannica.com/place/Delaware-state) for transport training. Two months later Cochran persuaded Army Air Forces commander Gen. [Henry (“Hap”) Arnold](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-Harley-Arnold) to activate the Women’s Flying Training Detachment (WFTD), a similar program based at Howard Hughes Airport in [Houston](https://www.britannica.com/place/Houston). The two programs operated separately until August 1943, when they were merged as WASP, with Cochran taking the role as director. More than 25,000 women applied to serve in WASP, although fewer than 10 percent of that number were accepted. Candidates had to be between 21 and 35 years old, possess a commercial pilot’s license, and have the physical endurance to complete the military training regimen that was part of the selection process.

The women of WASP logged more than 60 million miles (100 million km) in the air and flew every type of aircraft in the army air forces. In October 1944 Ann Baumgarter, serving with WASP as a test pilot, became the first American woman to fly a jet aircraft when she took to the skies in a YP-59A Airacomet. In addition to ferrying aircraft, WASP towed targets for aerial and ground-to-air gunnery practice, made test and demonstration flights, and served as flight instructors. Unlike the [Women’s Army Corps](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Womens-Army-Corps) (WAC) or the [Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service](https://www.britannica.com/topic/WAVES-United-States-naval-organization) (WAVES), the WASP were considered part of the civil service and were not militarized as an official auxiliary force. Thus, the 38 women who were killed while serving in WASP were not entitled to burial expenses or survivor benefits; the cost of shipping home the bodies of the dead was often borne by fellow WASP. In December 1944, as victory in Europe seemed imminent and more male pilots were becoming available, the WASP program was quietly disbanded.

Three decades would pass before women would again be allowed to pilot U.S. military aircraft, and it would be nearly half a century before women would return to the cockpits of U.S. fighter planes. Thanks in large part to the efforts of U.S. Sen. [Barry Goldwater](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Barry-Goldwater), who had himself served as a ferry pilot during World War II, WASP were finally militarized in 1977, an act that made official the veteran status of those who had served. In 2009 U.S. Pres. [Barack Obama](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Barack-Obama) signed a bill conferring the Congressional Gold Medal upon the WASP. The following year, more than 200 surviving WASP attended a ceremony at the [U.S. Capitol](https://www.britannica.com/topic/United-States-Capitol) to receive their decorations.

**Nazca Lines**, groups of geoglyphs, large line drawings that appear, from a distance, to be etched into Earth’s surface on the arid Pampa Colorada (“Coloured Plain” or “Red Plain”), northwest of the city of [Nazca](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nazca) in southern [Peru](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru). They extend over an area of nearly 190 square miles (500 square km).

[![Nazca Lines](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/153441-050-10B39A3A/Aerial-view-Nazca-Lines-northwest-Pampa-Colorada.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/153441-050-10B39A3A/Aerial-view-Nazca-Lines-northwest-Pampa-Colorada.jpg)

[Nazca Lines](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/153441-050-10B39A3A/Aerial-view-Nazca-Lines-northwest-Pampa-Colorada.jpg) Aerial view of Nazca Lines, on the Pampa Colorada, northwest of the city of Nazca, Peru.(more)

Most of the Nazca Lines were constructed more than 2,000 years ago by the people of the [Nazca culture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nazca) (c. 200 bce–600 ce), though some clearly predate the Nazca and are considered to be the work of the earlier [Paracas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Paracas) culture. While the images dated to the Paracas are often humanlike and bear some resemblance to still earlier petroglyphs in the region, the subjects of the Nazca-made lines are generally plants and animals—such as a monkey (some 360 feet \[110 metres\] long), a killer whale (210 feet \[65 metres\]), a bird resembling a condor (443 feet \[135 metres\]), a hummingbird (165 feet \[50 metres\]), a pelican (935 feet \[285 metres\]), a spider (150 feet \[46 metres\]), and various flowers, trees, and other plants—as well as geometric shapes, including triangles, trapezoids, and spirals. Although the figures have been said to be virtually indecipherable from ground level, some claim that one cannot understand their meaning without walking what are now believed by some to be the sacred paths.

[![Nazca whale](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/132495-050-82D6625F/view-whale-representation-Nazca-Lines-city-Pampa.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/132495-050-82D6625F/view-whale-representation-Nazca-Lines-city-Pampa.jpg)

[Nazca whale](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/132495-050-82D6625F/view-whale-representation-Nazca-Lines-city-Pampa.jpg) Aerial view of Nazca Lines, possibly a representation of a whale, on the Pampa Colorada, northwest of the city of Nazca, Peru. (more)

Since their discovery in the 1920s, the lines have been variously interpreted, but their significance remains largely shrouded in mystery. The American historian Paul Kosok observed the lines from an airplane in 1941 and hypothesized that they were drawn for astronomical purposes. María Reiche, a German translator who spent years studying the site and lobbying for its preservation, concluded that it was a huge astronomical calendar and that some of its animal sketches were modeled after groupings of stars in the night sky. In 1967, however, the American astrophysicist Gerald Hawkins found no correlation between changes in the celestial bodies and the design of the Nazca Lines.

In 1997 an international team of archaeologists, geographers, archaeometrists, and others formed the Nasca-Palpa Project in order to document and analyze the Nazca Lines and a number of similar figures near the town of Palpa. The conjecture of this group is that the platforms present at one end of many of the images reveal their ceremonial processional nature. This evidence, together with the presence in one excavated platform of thorny oyster ( _Spondylus_) fragments, suggests water-related religious ceremonies—not unlikely in this desert region.

The Nazca Lines are preserved naturally by the region’s dry climate and by winds that sweep sand out of their grooves. UNESCO added the Nazca site to its [World Heritage List](https://www.britannica.com/topic/World-Heritage-site) in 1994.

### What were the main regions where pre-Columbian civilizations developed?

[Pre-Columbian civilizations](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pre-Columbian-civilizations) developed in Mesoamerica (part of Mexico and Central America) and the [Andean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Andes-Mountains) region (western South America). Mesoamerica was home to [urban](https://www.britannica.com/topic/urbanization) societies such as the [Olmec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Olmec), the [Maya](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-people), and the [Aztec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Aztec). Andean urban societies included the [Moche](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Moche), [Chimú](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chimu), and [Inca](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Inca). Other regions of the Americas were also home to settled peoples at various times.

### What were the main crops cultivated in pre-Columbian civilizations?

Different [pre-Columbian civilizations](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pre-Columbian-civilizations) cultivated different crops. In Mesoamerica the main crops were [corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corn-plant), [beans](https://www.britannica.com/plant/bean), and squash, which were grown together in the [milpa](https://www.britannica.com/science/three-sisters) system. This system eventually spread through much of [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America). [Chili peppers](https://www.britannica.com/plant/chili-pepper) and [cotton](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cotton-fibre-and-plant) were also important crops in the region. In the Andes the [potato](https://www.britannica.com/plant/potato) was an important crop, as was the [lima bean](https://www.britannica.com/plant/lima-bean). Like their northern neighbors Andean peoples also grew squasha and corn.

**pre-Columbian civilizations**, the [Indigenous American](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indigenous-peoples-of-the-Americas) urban [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) that developed in Mesoamerica (part of [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico) and [Central America](https://www.britannica.com/place/Central-America)) and the Andean region (western [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America)) prior to Spanish exploration and conquest in the 16th century. The pre-Columbian civilizations were extraordinary developments in human society and [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture), ranking with the early civilizations of [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt), [Mesopotamia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mesopotamia-historical-region-Asia), and [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China/History#ref214398). Like the ancient urban societies of the Old World, those in the New World were characterized by kingdoms and empires, great monuments and cities, and refinements in the arts, metallurgy, and writing; the ancient civilizations of the Americas also display in their histories similar cyclical patterns of growth and decline, unity and disunity.

In the New World the roots of urban life lay in a native [agricultural way of life](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agriculture). These agricultural beginnings go back several millennia, to perhaps about 7000 bce and the first experimentations by the early Americans with plant cultivation. The [domestication](https://www.britannica.com/science/domestication) of successful food plants proved to be a long, slow process, and it was not until much later that a condition of permanent village farming life was achieved in the tropical latitudes of the two continents.

Sedentary village farming in Mesoamerica came into being by about 1500 bce. [Corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corn-plant) (maize), [beans](https://www.britannica.com/plant/bean), [squashes](https://www.britannica.com/plant/squash), [chili peppers](https://www.britannica.com/plant/chili-pepper), and [cotton](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cotton-fibre-and-plant) were the most important crops. These early villagers wove cloth, made [pottery](https://www.britannica.com/art/pottery), and practiced other typical [Neolithic](https://www.britannica.com/event/Neolithic) skills. It appears that such villages were economically self-contained and politically [autonomous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/autonomous), with an egalitarian social order. But rather quickly after this—between about 1200 and 900 bce—the building of large earthen pyramids and platforms and the carving of monumental stone sculptures signaled significant changes in this heretofore simple social and political order. These changes first appeared in the southern Gulf coast region of what is now [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico); and the sculptures, rendered in a style now called [Olmec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Olmec), are presumed to depict chiefs or rulers. From these and other archaeological indications it has been inferred that a class-structured and politically centralized society developed. There appeared subsequently other large capital towns and cities in neighboring regions that also displayed a similar Olmec art style. This [Olmec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Olmec) horizon (i.e., a cultural [diffusion](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diffusion) that is [contemporaneous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contemporaneous) at widely scattered sites) represents the first climax, or era of “unification,” in the [history](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history) of [Mesoamerican civilization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mesoamerican-civilization).

After about 500 bce the Olmec “unification” gave way to an era (consisting of the Late Formative and Classic periods) of separate regional styles and kingdoms. These lasted until c. 700–900 ce. Among these are the well-known Maya, [Zapotec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zapotec), [Totonac](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Totonac), and [Teotihuacán](https://www.britannica.com/place/Teotihuacan) civilizations. While sharing a common Olmec heritage, they also displayed many differences. For example, the [Maya](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-people) excelled in the [intellectual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectual) pursuits of [hieroglyphic writing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-hieroglyphic-writing), [calendar making](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-calendar), and [mathematics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics), while the [Teotihuacán civilization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Teotihuacan-civilization) placed its emphasis on political and commercial power. [Teotihuacán](https://www.britannica.com/place/Teotihuacan), in the Valley of Mexico, was an urban center of some 150,000 people, and the influence of its civilization eventually radiated over much of Mesoamerica. As such, Teotihuacán [constituted](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituted) a second grand civilizational climax or “unification” (400–600 ce). Teotihuacán power waned after about 600, and a “time of troubles” ensued, during which a number of states and [nascent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nascent) empires competed for supremacy. Among these competitors were the [Toltecs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Toltec) of [Tula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tula-ancient-city-Mexico), in central Mexico, who held sway from perhaps 900 to 1200 (the Early Postclassic Period). After their decline (in the Late Postclassic Period), another interregnum of warring states lasted until 1428, when the [Aztec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Aztec) defeated the rival city of Azcapotzalco and emerged as the dominant force in central Mexico. This last [Indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Indigenous) Mesoamerican empire was conquered by [Hernán Cortés](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hernan-Cortes) (or Cortéz) and the Spaniards in 1521.

In the [Andean](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Andean-civilization) area, the [threshold](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/threshold) of a successful village agricultural economy can be placed at c. 2500 bce, or somewhat earlier than was the case in Mesoamerica. The oldest primary food crops there were the [lima bean](https://www.britannica.com/plant/lima-bean) and the [potato](https://www.britannica.com/plant/potato), which had long histories of domestication in the area, although corn appeared soon after the beginnings of settled village life. Indications of a more complex sociopolitical order—huge platform mounds and densely populated centers—occurred very soon after this (c. 1800 bce); however, these early Andean civilizations continued for almost a millennium before they participated in a shared stylistic “unification.” This has become known as the [Chavín](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chavin) horizon, and Chavín sculptural art has been found throughout the northern part of the area.

The Chavín horizon disappeared after about 500 bce, and it was replaced by regional styles and cultures that lasted until about 600 ce. This period of regionalization (called the Early Intermediate Period) saw the florescence of a number of large kingdoms both on the [Pacific](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean) coast and in the Andean highlands; among them were the [Moche](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Moche), Early [Lima](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lima), [Nazca](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nazca), [Recuay](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Recuay), and Early Tiwanaku. The period was brought to an end by the Tiwanaku–Huari horizon (Middle Horizon; 600–1000), which was generated from the highland cities of [Tiwanaku](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tiwanaku) (in modern northern Bolivia) and [Huari](https://www.britannica.com/place/Huari) (in central highland Peru). There is evidence—such as the construction of new centers and cities—that this Tiwanaku–Huari phenomenon, at least in many regions, was a tightly controlled political empire. The horizon and its influences, as registered in ceramics and textiles, died away rather gradually in the ensuing centuries, and it was replaced by the several regional styles and kingdoms of what has become known as the Late Intermediate Period (1000–1438).

The [terminal](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/terminal) date of the Late Intermediate Period marked the beginning of the [Inca](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Inca) horizon and of the Inca conquests, which spread from the Inca capital, [Cuzco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cuzco), in the southern highlands of what is now [Peru](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru). By 1533, when [Francisco Pizarro](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francisco-Pizarro) and his cohorts took over the empire, it extended from what is now the [Ecuador](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ecuador)– [Colombia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Colombia) border to central [Chile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chile).

The synchroneity of horizon unifications and alternating regionalizations in Mesoamerica and the Andean region is striking and prompts the question of communication between these two areas of pre-Columbian high civilization. Although it is known that there were contacts—with the result that knowledge of food plants, ceramics, and metallurgy was shared between the two areas—it is also highly unlikely that political or religious [ideologies](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ideologies) were so spread. Rather, the peoples of each of these major cultural areas appear to have responded to their own internally generated stimuli and to have followed essentially separate courses of development. There are fundamental differences between the two cultural traditions. Thus, in Mesoamerica there was, from early on, a profound interest in hieroglyphic writing and calendar making. Religious [ideology](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ideology), judged from [art](https://www.britannica.com/art/visual-arts) and [iconography](https://www.britannica.com/art/iconography), was more highly developed in Mesoamerica than in the Andean region. In Mesoamerica the market was a basic institution; it does not appear to have been so in the Andes, where the redistributive economy of the Inca empire—with such features as its government warehouses and a system of highways—must have had deep roots in the past. On the other hand, in the early development and deployment of metallurgy and in governmental institutions and empire-building, the ancient Peruvians were much more efficient than their Mesoamerican [contemporaries](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/contemporaries).

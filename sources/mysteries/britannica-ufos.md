### What is an unidentified flying object (UFO)?

An [unidentified flying object](https://www.britannica.com/story/unidentified-flying-objects-what-we-know) (UFO) is any airborne object or optical phenomenon that an observer cannot readily identify. Interest in UFOs increased after [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II) with the development of [rocketry](https://www.britannica.com/technology/rocket-jet-propulsion-device-and-vehicle). Some believed that UFOs were evidence of [intelligent extraterrestrial life](https://www.britannica.com/science/extraterrestrial-intelligence) visiting Earth.

The first widely publicized UFO sighting occurred in 1947, when businessman Kenneth Arnold claimed he saw high-speed, crescent-shaped objects. A newspaper misreported that the objects were saucer-shaped, coining the term _flying saucer_.

From 1952 to 1969 the [U.S. Air Force](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-United-States-Air-Force) investigated UFO sightings through [Project Blue Book](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Project-Blue-Book/Introduction#ref1). Of 12,618 sightings, 701 remained “unidentified” when the project concluded. The project aimed to apply a methodical approach to the program by documenting subjective experiences.

### Why are some flying objects considered “unidentified”?

Objects are considered “ [unidentified flying objects](https://www.britannica.com/story/unidentified-flying-objects-what-we-know)” (UFOs) because they are aerial objects or optical phenomena that observers cannot readily identify. These sightings often spark interest because they suggest the possibility of [extraterrestrial life](https://www.britannica.com/science/extraterrestrial-life) or the use of advanced technology.

Several factors contribute to why objects remain unidentified. Visual impressions can be unreliable because of optical illusions, environmental conditions (such as fog or haze), and the fallibility of the human eye. Additionally, the psychological desire to interpret images can lead to misidentification. In some instances, sightings may be hoaxes or misinterpretations of known objects or phenomena.

### What are some common shapes or features people report seeing in UFOs?

People reporting [unidentified flying object](https://www.britannica.com/story/unidentified-flying-objects-what-we-know) (UFO) sightings describe a variety of shapes and features. The term _flying saucer_ came from a misreporting of [Kenneth Arnold](https://www.britannica.com/story/unidentified-flying-objects-what-we-know/Introduction#ref1)’s 1947 account, in which he described crescent shapes moving like saucers skipping on water; a newspaper mistakenly described the objects as saucer-shaped.

Witnesses often describe objects moving unnaturally or suddenly, without making sound, and displaying unusual changes in brightness or color. Visual impressions regarding the distance and speed of UFOs are unreliable, because they are based on an assumed size, often against a blank sky.

### How have governments and scientists investigated UFO sightings?

Governments and scientists have investigated [UFO](https://www.britannica.com/story/unidentified-flying-objects-what-we-know) sightings through various projects and studies. [Project Blue Book](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Project-Blue-Book), run by the [U.S. Air Force](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-United-States-Air-Force) from 1952 to 1969, recorded more than 12,000 sightings, of which 701 remained “unidentified.” The [CIA](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Central-Intelligence-Agency) created the Robertson Panel in 1953 to review UFO sightings.

A more recent government-funded study, the Unidentified Aerial Phenomena Task Force, was launched in 2020 by the [U.S. Department of Defense](https://www.britannica.com/topic/US-Department-of-Defense). Some countries, such as the United States, the former [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union), and [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China), have used UFO sightings as cover for secret military tests.

### What are some possible explanations for UFO sightings?

[UFO](https://www.britannica.com/story/unidentified-flying-objects-what-we-know) sightings have various possible explanations, ranging from natural phenomena to psychological effects. Many sightings result from misinterpreting bright lights, such as the planet [Venus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venus), or optical illusions caused by reflections or [camera](https://www.britannica.com/technology/camera) [lens](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lens-optics) effects.

Psychological factors may also play a significant role. Some “abduction” stories are attributed to [sleep paralysis](https://www.britannica.com/science/sleep-paralysis), a sleep disorder that can cause hallucinations. Additionally, in the former [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union) and [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) some sightings resulted from secret military tests and from governments’ encouragement of the belief that the objects were of extraterrestrial origin to hide the true nature of the experiments.

### How have UFOs influenced popular culture and people’s beliefs about life beyond Earth?

[Unidentified flying objects](https://www.britannica.com/story/unidentified-flying-objects-what-we-know) (UFOs) have become a captivating subject in popular culture, sparking widespread interest and speculation about [extraterrestrial life](https://www.britannica.com/science/extraterrestrial-life). The surge in public interest can be traced back to the post- [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II) era, coinciding with advancements in [rocket](https://www.britannica.com/technology/rocket-jet-propulsion-device-and-vehicle) technology.

The term _flying saucer_ emerged from a misinterpretation of a 1947 sighting, further fueling public fascination. Although many sightings can be attributed to natural phenomena or artificial objects, some individuals continue to interpret UFOs as evidence of [alien](https://www.britannica.com/science/extraterrestrial-life) visitation. This belief has been reinforced by government inquiries, such as [Project Blue Book](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Project-Blue-Book), and the work of such researchers as J. Allen Hynek, who founded the Center for UFO Studies (CUFOS).

## Flying saucers and [Project Blue Book](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Project-Blue-Book)

[![The Flying Saucer as I Saw It](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/111346-050-942D9105/cover-Kenneth-Arnold-The-Flying-Saucer.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/111346-050-942D9105/cover-Kenneth-Arnold-The-Flying-Saucer.jpg)

[_The Flying Saucer as I Saw It_](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/111346-050-942D9105/cover-Kenneth-Arnold-The-Flying-Saucer.jpg) Autographed front cover of Kenneth Arnold's _The Flying Saucer as I Saw It_ (1950).(more)

The first well-known UFO sighting occurred in 1947, when businessman [Kenneth Arnold](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kenneth-Arnold) claimed to have seen a group of nine high-speed objects near [Mount Rainier](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Rainier) in [Washington](https://www.britannica.com/place/Washington-state) state while flying his small [plane](https://www.britannica.com/technology/airplane). Arnold estimated the speed of the crescent-shaped objects as several thousand miles per hour and said they moved “like saucers skipping on water.” A subsequent newspaper report mistakenly stated that the objects were saucer-shaped—hence the term _flying saucer_.

Sightings of unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP) increased, and in 1948 the [U.S. Air Force](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-United-States-Air-Force) began an investigation of these reports, [Project Sign](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Project-Sign). The initial opinion of those involved with the project was that the UFOs were most likely sophisticated [Soviet](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union) aircraft, although some researchers suggested that they might be [spacecraft](https://www.britannica.com/technology/spacecraft) from other worlds, the so-called extraterrestrial hypothesis (ETH). Within a year, Project Sign was succeeded by Project Grudge, which in 1952 was itself replaced by the longest-lived of the official inquiries into UFOs, [Project Blue Book](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Project-Blue-Book), headquartered at Wright-Patterson Air Force Base in [Dayton](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dayton-Ohio), [Ohio](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ohio-state). From 1952 to 1969 Project Blue Book compiled reports of 12,618 sightings or events, each of which was ultimately classified as (1) “identified” with a known astronomical, atmospheric, or artificial (human-caused) phenomenon or (2) “unidentified.” The latter category, 701 sightings, included cases for which there was insufficient information to make an identification with a known phenomenon.

In 2014 the U.S. [Central Intelligence Agency](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Central-Intelligence-Agency) (CIA) declassified a history of the [U-2](https://www.britannica.com/technology/U-2) spy plane program, which revealed that [Project Blue Book](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Project-Blue-Book) investigators regularly contacted the CIA and found that “U-2 and later OXCART \[the [Lockheed](https://www.britannica.com/money/Lockheed-Martin-Corporation) A-12 spy plane\] flights accounted for more than one-half of all UFO reports during the late 1950s and most of the 1960s.”

## The Robertson Panel and the [Condon Report](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Condon-Report)

An American obsession with the UFO phenomenon was underway. In the hot summer of 1952 a provocative series of [radar](https://www.britannica.com/technology/radar) and visual sightings occurred near National Airport in [Washington, D.C.](https://www.britannica.com/place/Washington-DC) Although these events were attributed to [temperature inversions](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature-inversion) in the air over the city, not everyone was convinced by this explanation. Meanwhile, the number of UFO reports had climbed to a record high. Consequently, the U.S. [Central Intelligence Agency](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Central-Intelligence-Agency) (CIA) prompted the federal government to establish an expert panel of scientists to investigate the phenomena. The panel was headed by [H.P. Robertson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Howard-P-Robertson), a physicist at the [California Institute of Technology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/California-Institute-of-Technology) in [Pasadena](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pasadena-California), [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state), and included other physicists, an astronomer, and a rocket engineer. The Robertson Panel met for three days in 1953 and interviewed military officers and the head of Project Blue Book. In addition, it reviewed films and photographs of UFOs. It concluded that (1) 90 percent of the sightings could be easily attributed to astronomical and meteorologic phenomena (e.g., bright [planets](https://www.britannica.com/science/planet) and [stars](https://www.britannica.com/science/star-astronomy), [meteors](https://www.britannica.com/science/meteor), [auroras](https://www.britannica.com/science/aurora-atmospheric-phenomenon), ion clouds) or to such earthly objects as aircraft, balloons, [birds](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal), and [searchlights](https://www.britannica.com/technology/searchlight-lighting), (2) there was no obvious security threat, and (3) there was no evidence to support the ETH. Parts of the panel’s report were kept classified until 1979, and this long period of secrecy helped fuel suspicions of a government cover-up.

A second committee was set up in 1966, at the request of the Air Force, to review the most interesting material gathered by Project Blue Book. Two years later this committee, which made a detailed study of 59 UFO sightings, released its results as _Scientific Study of Unidentified Flying Objects_—also known as the Condon Report, named for [Edward U. Condon](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edward-U-Condon), the physicist who headed the investigation. The Condon Report was reviewed by a special committee of the [National Academy of Sciences](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Academy-of-Sciences-American-organization). A total of 37 scientists wrote chapters or parts of chapters for the report, which covered investigations of the 59 UFO sightings in detail. Like the Robertson Panel, the committee concluded that there was no evidence of anything other than commonplace phenomena in the reports and that UFOs did not warrant further investigation. This, together with a decline in sighting activity, led to the dismantling of Project Blue Book in 1969.

# Other investigations of UFOs

Despite the failure of the ETH to make headway with the expert committees, a few scientists and engineers, most notably J. Allen Hynek, an astronomer at [Northwestern University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Northwestern-University-Evanston-Illinois) in [Evanston](https://www.britannica.com/place/Evanston-Illinois), [Illinois](https://www.britannica.com/place/Illinois-state), who had been involved with Projects Sign, Grudge, and Blue Book, concluded that a small fraction of the most-reliable UFO reports gave definite indications of the presence of [extraterrestrial visitors](https://www.britannica.com/science/extraterrestrial-intelligence). Hynek founded the Center for UFO Studies (CUFOS), which continues to investigate the phenomenon.

Another major U.S. study of UFO sightings was the Advanced Aviation Threat Identification Program (AATIP), a secret project that ran from 2007 to 2012. When the existence of the AATIP was made public in December 2017, the most newsworthy aspect of it was a report that the U.S. government possessed [alloys](https://www.britannica.com/technology/alloy) and [compounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound), purportedly obtained from UFOs, that were unidentifiable, but many scientists remained skeptical about this claim.

Aside from the American efforts, the only other official and fairly complete records of UFO sightings were kept in [Canada](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canada), where they were transferred in 1968 from the Canadian Department of National Defense to the Canadian National Research Council. The Canadian records concerned about 750 sightings. Less complete records have been maintained in the [United Kingdom](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-Kingdom), [Sweden](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sweden), [Denmark](https://www.britannica.com/place/Denmark), [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia), and [Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece). In the United States, CUFOS and the Mutual UFO Network (MUFON) continue to log sightings reported by the public.

In the [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union), sightings of UFOs were often prompted by tests of secret military [rockets](https://www.britannica.com/technology/rocket-jet-propulsion-device-and-vehicle). In order to obscure the true nature of the tests, the government sometimes encouraged the public’s belief that these rockets might be extraterrestrial craft, but it eventually decided that the descriptions themselves might give away too much information. UFO sightings in [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) and the U.S. have been similarly provoked by military activity that is unknown to the public.

## Possible explanations for UFO sightings and alien abductions

[![Is there proof of alien life?](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/225154-138-D4C3AED0/what-the-fact-aliens-in-history.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/what-the-fact-aliens-in-history/-258436)

UFO reports have varied widely in reliability, as judged by the number of witnesses, whether the witnesses were independent of one another, the observing conditions (e.g., [fog](https://www.britannica.com/science/fog), [haze](https://www.britannica.com/science/haze), type of illumination), and the direction of sighting. Typically, witnesses who take the trouble to report a sighting consider the object to be of extraterrestrial origin or possibly a military craft but certainly under intelligent control. This inference is usually based on what is perceived as [formation flying](https://www.britannica.com/technology/formation-flying) by sets of objects, unnatural—often sudden—motions, the lack of sound, changes in brightness or color, and strange shapes.

That the unaided eye plays tricks is well known. A bright light, such as the [planet](https://www.britannica.com/science/planet) [Venus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venus-planet), often appears to move. An astronomical object can be disconcerting to [car](https://www.britannica.com/technology/automobile) drivers, as it can seem to “follow” cars. Visual impressions of distance and speed of UFOs are also highly unreliable, because they are based on an assumed size and are often made against a blank sky, with no background object (such as [clouds](https://www.britannica.com/science/cloud-meteorology) or [mountains](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform)) by which to set a maximum distance. Reflections from [windows](https://www.britannica.com/technology/window) and [eyeglasses](https://www.britannica.com/science/eyeglasses) can produce superimposed views, and complex optical systems, such as [camera](https://www.britannica.com/technology/camera) [lenses](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lens-optics), can turn point sources of light into apparently saucer-shaped phenomena. Such optical illusions and the psychological desire to interpret images are known to account for many visual UFO reports, and at least some sightings are known to have been [hoaxes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hoax). [Radar](https://www.britannica.com/technology/radar) sightings, although in certain respects more reliable, fail to discriminate between artificial objects and [meteor](https://www.britannica.com/science/meteor-shower) trails, [ionized](https://www.britannica.com/science/ionization) gas, [rain](https://www.britannica.com/science/rain), or thermal discontinuities in the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere).

“Contact events,” such as abductions, are often associated with UFOs, because they are ascribed to [extraterrestrial visitors](https://www.britannica.com/science/extraterrestrial-intelligence). However, the credibility of the ETH as an explanation for abductions is disputed by most psychologists who have investigated this phenomenon. They suggest that a common experience known as [sleep paralysis](https://www.britannica.com/science/sleep-paralysis) may be the culprit, as this causes sleepers to experience temporary immobility and a belief that they are being watched.

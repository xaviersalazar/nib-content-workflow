# The Hindenburg Disaster

**At 6:25 p.m. EST on May 6, 1937,** while approaching Lakehurst Naval Station’s mooring mast between storms, _Hindenburg_ burst into flames. Within thirty-four seconds, fire consumed the entire airship. Passengers and crew members jumped from the burning airship, some falling to their deaths. Thirty-five of the ninety-seven men and women on board, plus one member of the ground crew, died. The disaster ended transatlantic commercial travel in lighter-than-air vessels. Debates continue regarding the cause of the spark that ignited the hydrogen and outer covering.

**_Hindenburg_ Final Flight Cologne Drop Card**

![Vintage German airmail envelope with stamps, postmarks, and handwritten addresses, sent to Zurich in 1937.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-227584_33_29_1a.png)

_Hindenburg final flight Cologne drop card, 1937_

Departing Germany for the last time, _Hindenburg_ dropped mail bags at Cologne. The mail had been held from the cancelled May Day flight to Berlin, as indicated by the red-boxed marking on the mail.

**_Hindenburg_ Postmaster Letter**

![Typed letter certifying undelivered mail from the Hindenburg disaster, dated May 8, 1937, signed by the postmaster.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-2011_2034_1.jpg)

_Hindenburg postmaster letter, 1937_

When _Hindenburg_ met tragedy May 6, 1937, more than 17,000 pieces of mail burned to ashes. Max Zabel signed a letter explaining the loss of mail for philatelic subscribers of F.W. von Meister, Zeppelin Company representative in the United States.

**_Hindenburg_ Salvaged Fork, Knife and Spoon**

![Old, tarnished fork, knife with a rusty blade, and spoon, all with worn metal handles, arranged side by side.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-fork-knife-spoon1.png)

_Hindenburg salvaged fork, knife and spoon, 1937_

_Courtesy Navy Lakehurst Historical Society_

The silverware onboard had been specially created for the zeppelin, and passengers had used it several times the day before the fatal landing attempt.

**_Hindenburg_ Salvaged Serving Bowl**

![A weathered, metallic bowl with tarnished spots and dents on its surface.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-2011_6603_1e.png)

_Hindenburg salvaged serving bowl, 1937_

_Courtesy Henry Applegate_

_Hindenburg_’s logo, luxuriously etched into the silver, contrasts with the burnt edges, unexpectedly illustrating the triumph and tragedy of the zeppelin’s brief time as North America’s first regularly scheduled air service.

**_Hindenburg_ Disaster Card**

![A vintage airmail envelope with German stamps, addressed to Mr. John Schoonbrod in New York, USA.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-1993_2002_70_1c.png)

_Hindenburg disaster card, 1937_

Postal officials salvaged only about 160 burned pieces of mail out of more than 17,000 pieces that had been onboard.

**_Hindenburg_ Disaster Cover in Glassine**

![A burnt, yellowed envelope with handwritten text, a red stamp, and covered in clear protective wrap.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-2012_6601_1c.png)

_Hindenburg disaster cover in glassine, 1937_

_Courtesy John Hotchner_

Salvaged from the wreckage, the U.S. Post Office Department enclosed the fragile, charred remains of this cover in a glassine envelope, officially sealing it before delivery to the addressee.

**_Hindenburg_ Disaster Cover**

![A worn envelope with German stamps, a Hindenburg airmail postmark dated 5.5.1937, and faded handwritten addresses.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-2011_6608_1a.png)

_Hindenburg disaster cover, 1937_

_Courtesy Dr. Edward and Joanne Dauer_

Passenger Hermann Doehner posted this envelope onboard, addressed to himself. In Germany on business, he was returning home to Mexico City with his wife and three children. He and his daughter died in the disaster.

**_Hindenburg_ Disaster Cover**

![A vintage German postcard from 1937 with colorful stamps and handwritten and printed text.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-2011_6605_1a.png)

_Hindenburg disaster cover, 1937_

_Courtesy anonymous_

A New York paquebot mark cancelled 176 salvaged unburned pieces of mail four days after the disaster. Having been stored in a protective, sealed container while awaiting postal service on the return flight, this uncancelled mail survived intact.

### Photo Gallery

**Postal Clerk Bill Schneider**

![Smiling man in suit stands in front of a vintage airplane marked D-1929 at an airfield with people in the background.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-g-2011-13.jpg)

_Bill Schneider was a postal clerk in Rahway, New Jersey. He traveled to Lakehurst to witness the ten Hindenburg arrivals in 1936. He collected mail flown by zeppelins, and crew members often autographed his mail. Schneider developed friendships with the crew and, as an amateur photographer, often photographed them._

_Courtesy Bill Schneider Photograph Collection_

**_Hindenburg_'s Approach, May 6, 1937**

![A large airship floats in the cloudy sky above a field, with a person standing in the foreground.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-g-2011-110.jpg)

_On Schneider’s visit to Lakehurst the evening of May 6, 1937, he took a series of photographs of the approach._

_Courtesy Bill Schneider Photograph Collection_

**_Hindenburg_ Bursts Into Flames**

![A blurred black-and-white photo of a large airship exploding and emitting smoke above the ground.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-g-2011-112.jpg)

_When Hindenburg burst into flames, his hand-held camera captured his sense of horror as his hands shook uncontrollably._

_Courtesy Bill Schneider Photograph Collection_

**_Hindenburg_ Bursts Into Flames**

![The Hindenburg airship bursts into flames at its mooring mast, with smoke and fire billowing into the sky.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-g-2011-72.jpg)

_Moments after Hindenburg’s bow lines dropped, eyewitnesses saw a red glow ahead of the upper tail fin, and then the ship burst into flames._

_Courtesy National Air and Space Museum_

**_Hindenburg_ Crashes to Earth**

![The Hindenburg airship bursts into flames in mid-air, with smoke and fire billowing upwards as people watch below.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-g-2011-73.jpg)

_A blaze of hydrogen raged within Hindenburg, destroying the ship in thirty-four seconds. The rapidly disintegrating airship crashed to earth tail first._

_Courtesy National Air and Space Museum_

**_Hindenburg_ Landing Crew Rushes to Help Save Lives**

![People run toward a giant plume of black smoke rising from a burning airship on an open field.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-g-2011-116.jpg)

_Landing crew ran into the intense heat to help save lives. Amazingly, sixty-two of the ninety-seven persons onboard Hindenburg survived._

_Courtesy National Air and Space Museum_

**_Hindenburg_'s Smoldering Wreckage**

![Aerial view of the burned wreckage of the Hindenburg airship, Lakehurst, New Jersey, May 6, 1937.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-g-2011-80.jpg)

_A small quantity of mail survived Hindenburg’s fire and was salvaged from the smoldering wreckage._

_Courtesy anonymous_

**Customs Inspectors Examine Salvaged Mail**

![Three men examine and sort through stacks of mail and packages on a table, one wearing a uniform and another a hat.](https://postalmuseum.si.edu/sites/default/files/fire-and-ice-g-2011-119.jpg)

_Customs inspectors examined two kinds of Hindenburg salvaged mail: burnt mail that had been tightly packed together and, amazingly, survived the flames and unburnt, uncancelled mail (lower left) that had been in a fireproof container._

_Courtesy anonymous_

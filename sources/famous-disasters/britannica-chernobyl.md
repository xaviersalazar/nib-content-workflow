### When did the Chernobyl disaster occur?

The [Chernobyl disaster](https://www.britannica.com/event/Chernobyl-disaster) occurred on April 25 and 26, 1986, at the Chernobyl nuclear power station in [Ukraine](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ukraine), which was then part of the [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union). On April 27 the 30,000 inhabitants of Pryp’yat began to be evacuated. A cover-up was attempted, but on April 28 Swedish monitoring stations reported abnormally high levels of wind-transported radioactivity and pressed for an explanation. The Soviet government admitted there had been an accident at Chernobyl, thus setting off an international outcry over the dangers posed by the radioactive emissions. By May 4 both the heat and the radioactivity leaking from the reactor core were being contained, albeit at great risk to workers. Chernobyl is one of the worst disasters in the history of [nuclear power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/nuclear-power) generation.

### What happened in the Chernobyl disaster?

The [Chernobyl disaster](https://www.britannica.com/event/Chernobyl-disaster) occurred when technicians at nuclear reactor Unit 4 attempted a poorly designed experiment. They shut down the reactor’s power-regulating system and its emergency safety systems, and they removed control rods from its core while allowing the reactor to run at 7 percent power. These mistakes, compounded by others, led to an uncontrolled chain reaction that resulted in several massive explosions.

### How many people died as a result of the Chernobyl disaster?

Some sources state that two people were killed in the initial explosions of the [Chernobyl disaster](https://www.britannica.com/event/Chernobyl-disaster), whereas others report that the figure was closer to 50. Dozens more contracted serious [radiation sickness](https://www.britannica.com/science/ionizing-radiation-injury); some of these people later died. In addition, thousands of deaths from radiation-induced illnesses and cancer were expected years later.

### What caused the Chernobyl disaster?

The [Chernobyl disaster](https://www.britannica.com/event/Chernobyl-disaster) occurred when technicians at nuclear reactor Unit 4 attempted a poorly designed experiment. They shut down the reactor’s power-regulating system and its emergency safety systems, and they removed control rods from its core while allowing the reactor to run at 7 percent power. These mistakes were compounded by others, and at 1:23 am on April 26, 1986, the chain reaction in the core went out of control. Several explosions triggered a large fireball and blew off the heavy steel and concrete lid of the reactor. This and the ensuing fire in the graphite reactor core released large amounts of radioactive material into the atmosphere, where it was carried great distances by air currents. A partial meltdown of the core also occurred.

### How big was the exclusion zone created after the Chernobyl disaster?

As a result of the Chernobyl disaster, the Soviet Union created an exclusion zone with a radius of about 18.6 miles (30 km) centered on the nuclear power plant, covering 1,017 square miles (2,634 square km) around the plant. The zone was later expanded to 1,600 square miles (4,143 square km) to include heavily radiated areas outside the initial zone.

### What effects did the Chernobyl disaster have?

The Chernobyl disaster caused serious [radiation sickness](https://www.britannica.com/science/ionizing-radiation-injury) and contamination. Between 50 and 185 million curies of [radionuclides](https://www.britannica.com/science/radioactive-isotope) escaped into the atmosphere. Millions of acres of forest and farmland were contaminated, livestock was born deformed, and humans suffered long-term negative health effects.

**Chernobyl disaster**, [accident](https://www.britannica.com/topic/accident) in 1986 at the Chernobyl [nuclear power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/nuclear-power) station in the [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union), the worst [disaster](https://www.britannica.com/science/disaster) in the history of nuclear power generation. The Chernobyl power station was situated at the settlement of [Pryp’yat](https://www.britannica.com/place/Prypyat), 10 miles (16 km) northwest of the city of Chernobyl (Ukrainian: [Chornobyl](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chornobyl)) and 65 miles (104 km) north of [Kyiv](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kyiv), [Ukraine](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ukraine). The station consisted of four reactors, each capable of producing 1,000 megawatts of [electric power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electric-power); it had come online in 1977–83.

The accident at the Chernobyl nuclear power plant resulted from the combination of a reactor design with [inherent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inherent) control problems and the reckless and deliberate disregard of established safety procedures by the plant operators. The disaster occurred on April 25–26, 1986, when technicians at [reactor](https://www.britannica.com/technology/reactor-chemistry) Unit 4 attempted a poorly designed experiment. Workers shut down the reactor’s power-regulating system and its emergency safety systems, and they withdrew most of the control rods from its core while allowing the reactor to continue running at 7 percent power. These mistakes were [compounded](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounded) by others, and at 1:23 am on April 26 the [chain reaction](https://www.britannica.com/science/chain-reaction) in the core went out of control. Several explosions triggered a large fireball and blew off the heavy [steel](https://www.britannica.com/technology/steel) and [concrete](https://www.britannica.com/technology/concrete-building-material) lid of the reactor. This and the ensuing fire in the [graphite](https://www.britannica.com/science/graphite-carbon) reactor core released large amounts of [radioactive material](https://www.britannica.com/science/radioactive-isotope) into the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere), where it was carried great distances by [air](https://www.britannica.com/science/air) currents. A partial [meltdown](https://www.britannica.com/technology/meltdown) of the core also occurred.

## Timeline of the Chernobyl disaster

April 25, 1986

- **1:00 am:** Reactor operation was reduced to 50 percent of full power as the first step in setting up test conditions.

- **2:00 pm:** Thirteen hours later, the emergency core [cooling system](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cooling-system) was disconnected, at which time further preparations were postponed because power from the reactor was needed for the local energy grid. The reactor operated in this condition for the next nine hours, when test preparations were resumed.

- **Just after 11:00 pm:** The power level was further reduced, and computer control of some of the automatic control rods was switched off. At low power levels, however, the operators had trouble maintaining a stable output from the reactor (in part because of the previous prolonged operation at low power), and it took almost two hours to get the power output to the desired level.


**At this point almost all the safety features of the reactor had been disconnected or turned off.**

April 26, 1986

- **Shortly after 1:00 am:** The operators turned on additional water [circulation](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/circulation) pumps to begin the final steps in setting up the test. At the 7 percent power level at which the reactor was operating, this action dropped the steam pressure and the water level in the steam separation drums below emergency levels, but the operators blocked the signals to the emergency reactor protection system.

- **1:19 am:** The operators attempted to build up the water level by introducing additional water into the steam system. This led to further control problems.

- **1:23 am:** The operators decided conditions were satisfactory for the actual test to begin. Blocking off further emergency protection signals so that the test could be repeated, the operators shut off the flow of steam to the turbine, which began to coast down to a stop. As a result, the circulation pumps also slowed, and the flow of coolant to the reactor declined. This led to a sharp temperature rise in the fuel channels of the reactor. Because of the reactor design this meant that the reactor was out of control.


> Realizing an emergency was at hand, the supervisor ordered a “scram,” the term used in the nuclear power industry for the rapid insertion of all control rods to shut the reactor down. The reactor was already so overheated, however, that most of the control rods could not go in all the way.

- **1:24 am:** Within three seconds the power output of the reactor rose from 7 to 50 percent of full power. This caused the fuel elements to burst apart, placing the superheated fuel particles in direct contact with the cooling water, which immediately flashed to steam. At least two explosions occurred in rapid [succession](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/succession). There was an almost instantaneous pressure shock that blew off the top of the reactor vessel, blew the roof off the building, and caused the servicing crane to come crashing down on the reactor.

- **1:28 am:** The first firefighters arrive on the scene. They are unaware of the lethal levels of [radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiation) at the scene.


The rupture of the fuel elements and cooling tubes also brought steam in direct contact with the zirconium coating on the fuel elements and with the graphite blocks surrounding the cooling tubes. Both zirconium and graphite react with steam at high temperatures to produce [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen). With the reactor exposed to the atmosphere, the hydrogen ignited and caused fires that burned out of control for almost four hours.

## Evacuation of Pryp’yat and initial containment efforts

[![Chernobyl disaster](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/196898-050-B82EFB62/school-Prypyat-disaster-Ukraine-Chernobyl.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/196898-050-B82EFB62/school-Prypyat-disaster-Ukraine-Chernobyl.jpg)

[Chernobyl disaster](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/196898-050-B82EFB62/school-Prypyat-disaster-Ukraine-Chernobyl.jpg) A school in Pryp'yat, Ukraine, abandoned following the Chernobyl disaster.(more)

On April 27 the 30,000 inhabitants of Pryp’yat began to be evacuated. A cover-up was attempted, but on April 28 Swedish monitoring stations reported abnormally high levels of [wind](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind)-transported [radioactivity](https://www.britannica.com/science/radioactivity) and pressed for an explanation. The Soviet government admitted there had been an accident at Chernobyl, thus setting off an international outcry over the dangers posed by the [radioactive emissions](https://www.britannica.com/science/fallout-nuclear-physics). By May 4 both the heat and the radioactivity leaking from the reactor core were being contained, [albeit](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/albeit) at great risk to workers. Radioactive debris was buried at some 800 temporary sites, and later in the year the highly radioactive reactor core was enclosed in a concrete-and-steel [sarcophagus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sarcophagus) (which was later deemed structurally unsound).

## Deaths, radioactivity, and the creation of the Chernobyl Exclusion Zone

[![monument to emergency workers who responded to Chernobyl disaster](https://cdn.britannica.com/52/108152-050-F107A1A0/Monument-emergency-workers-Chernobyl-accident-nuclear-power-1986.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/52/108152-050-F107A1A0/Monument-emergency-workers-Chernobyl-accident-nuclear-power-1986.jpg)

[monument to emergency workers who responded to Chernobyl disaster](https://cdn.britannica.com/52/108152-050-F107A1A0/Monument-emergency-workers-Chernobyl-accident-nuclear-power-1986.jpg) Monument to the emergency workers (known as “liquidators”) who responded to the 1986 accident at the Chernobyl nuclear power station; the monument is located in Chernobyl, Ukraine.(more)

Some sources state that two people were killed in the initial explosions, whereas others report that the figure was closer to 50. Dozens more people contracted serious radiation sickness; some of them later died. Between 50 and 185 million curies of radionuclides (radioactive forms of [chemical elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element)) escaped into the atmosphere—several times more radioactivity than that created by the [atomic bombs](https://www.britannica.com/technology/atomic-bomb) dropped on [Hiroshima](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hiroshima-Japan) and [Nagasaki](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nagasaki-Japan), Japan. This radioactivity was spread by the wind over [Belarus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Belarus), [Russia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Russia), and Ukraine and soon reached as far west as [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) and [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy).

Millions of acres of [forest](https://www.britannica.com/science/forest) and farmland were contaminated, and, although many thousands of people were evacuated, hundreds of thousands more remained in contaminated areas. In addition, in subsequent years many [livestock](https://www.britannica.com/animal/livestock) were born deformed, and among humans several thousand radiation-induced illnesses and [cancer](https://www.britannica.com/science/cancer-disease) deaths were expected in the long term. The Chernobyl disaster sparked [criticism](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criticism) of unsafe procedures and design flaws in Soviet reactors, and it heightened resistance to the building of more such plants. Chernobyl Unit 2 was shut down after a 1991 fire, and Unit 1 remained on-line until 1996. Chernobyl Unit 3 continued to operate until 2000, when the nuclear power station was officially decommissioned.

Following the disaster, the Soviet Union created a circle-shaped exclusion zone with a radius of about 18.6 miles (30 km) centred on the nuclear power plant. The exclusion zone covered an area of about 1,017 square miles (2,634 square km) around the plant. However, it was later expanded to 1,600 square miles (4,143 square km) to include heavily radiated areas outside the initial zone. Although no people actually live in the exclusion zone, scientists, scavengers, and others may file for permits that allow them to enter for limited amounts of time. With the [dissolution of the Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/event/the-collapse-of-the-Soviet-Union) in 1991, control of the site passed to Ukraine. In 2011 the Ukrainian government opened parts of the exclusion zone to organized tour groups, and Chernobyl and the abandoned city of Pryp’yat became popular destinations for so-called “dark tourists.” During the [Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022](https://www.britannica.com/event/Russia-Ukraine-War), Russian forces attacking from Belarus captured Chernobyl after a brief but pitched battle. Combat at the site of the world’s worst nuclear disaster led to concerns about damage to the containment structure and the possibility of [widespread](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/widespread) radioactive contamination.

**nuclear power**, electricity generated by [power](https://www.britannica.com/science/power-physics) plants that derive their heat from fission in a [nuclear reactor](https://www.britannica.com/technology/nuclear-reactor). The nuclear power industry went through a period of remarkable growth until about 1990, when the portion of electricity generated by nuclear power reached a high of 17 percent. That percentage remained stable through the 1990s and began to decline slowly around the turn of the 21st century, primarily because of the fact that total electricity generation grew faster than electricity from nuclear power while other sources of energy (particularly [coal](https://www.britannica.com/science/coal-fossil-fuel) and natural gas) were able to grow more quickly to meet the rising demand. This [trend](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/trend) appears likely to continue well into the 21st century. The [Energy Information Administration](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Energy-Information-Administration) (EIA), a statistical arm of the [U.S. Department of Energy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/US-Department-of-Energy), has projected that world electricity generation between 2005 and 2035 will roughly double (from more than 15,000 terawatt-hours to 35,000 terawatt-hours) and that generation from all energy sources except [petroleum](https://www.britannica.com/science/petroleum) will continue to grow.

In 2012 more than 400 nuclear reactors were in operation in 30 countries around the world, and more than 60 were under construction. The [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) has the largest nuclear power industry, with more than 100 reactors; it is followed by France, which has more than 50. Of the top 15 electricity-producing countries in the world, all but two, Italy and Australia, [utilize](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/utilize) nuclear power to generate some of their electricity. The overwhelming majority of nuclear reactor generating capacity is concentrated in [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America), Europe, and Asia. The early period of the nuclear power industry was dominated by North America (the United States and Canada), but in the 1980s that lead was overtaken by Europe. The EIA projects that [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia) will have the largest nuclear capacity by 2035, mainly because of an ambitious building program in [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China).

A typical nuclear power plant has a generating capacity of approximately one gigawatt (GW; one billion watts) of electricity. At this capacity, a power plant that operates about 90 percent of the time (the U.S. industry average) will generate about eight terawatt-hours of electricity per year. The predominant types of power reactors are [pressurized water reactors](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pressurized-water-reactor) (PWRs) and boiling water reactors (BWRs), both of which are categorized as [light water reactors](https://www.britannica.com/technology/light-water-reactor) (LWRs) because they use ordinary (light) water as a moderator and coolant. LWRs make up more than 80 percent of the world’s nuclear reactors, and more than three-quarters of the LWRs are PWRs.

## Issues affecting nuclear power

Countries may have a number of motives for [deploying](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/deploying) nuclear power plants, including a lack of [indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/indigenous) energy resources, a desire for energy independence, and a goal to limit [greenhouse gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/greenhouse-gas) emissions by using a carbon-free source of electricity. The benefits of applying nuclear power to these needs are substantial, but they are tempered by a number of issues that need to be considered, including the safety of nuclear reactors, their cost, the disposal of radioactive waste, and a potential for the nuclear fuel cycle to be diverted to the development of nuclear weapons. All of these concerns are discussed below.

## Safety

The safety of nuclear reactors has become paramount since the [Fukushima accident](https://www.britannica.com/event/Fukushima-accident) of 2011. The lessons learned from that disaster included the need to (1) adopt risk-informed regulation, (2) strengthen management systems so that decisions made in the event of a severe accident are based on safety and not cost or political [repercussions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/repercussions), (3) periodically assess new information on risks posed by natural hazards such as earthquakes and associated tsunamis, and (4) take steps to [mitigate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/mitigate) the possible consequences of a station blackout.

The four reactors involved in the [Fukushima accident](https://www.britannica.com/event/Fukushima-accident) were first-generation BWRs designed in the 1960s. Newer Generation III designs, on the other hand, incorporate improved safety systems and rely more on so-called passive safety designs (i.e., directing cooling water by gravity rather than moving it by pumps) in order to keep the plants safe in the event of a severe accident or station blackout. For instance, in the Westinghouse AP1000 design, residual heat would be removed from the reactor by water circulating under the influence of gravity from reservoirs located inside the reactor’s containment structure. Active and passive safety systems are incorporated into the European Pressurized Water Reactor (EPR) as well.

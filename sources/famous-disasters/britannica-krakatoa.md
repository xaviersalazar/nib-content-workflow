- What is Krakatoa?







[Krakatoa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Krakatoa/Introduction#ref1) is a volcano located on Rakata Island in the Sunda Strait, between Java and Sumatra, Indonesia. Lying along the convergence of the Indian-Australian and Eurasian tectonic plates, it sits in a zone known for high volcanic and seismic activity. The 1883 eruption of Krakatoa is considered the loudest sound in modern history, with an estimated 310 decibels. The blast was heard as far as 3,000 miles (4,800 km) away. The explosions caused tidal waves throughout Southeast Asia and ash clouds that decreased solar radiation and produced spectacular sunsets for more than a year.

- What happened during the 1883 eruption of Krakatoa?







The 1883 eruption of [Krakatoa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Krakatoa/Introduction#ref1), a volcano on Rakata Island, was one of history's most catastrophic. Activity started in May 1883, with ash clouds and explosions. The climax occurred on August 27, with tremendous explosions heard 2,200 miles away in Australia and ash propelled 50 miles high. Nearly 5 cubic miles of rock fragments were thrown into the air, and ash fell over 300,000 square miles, plunging the surrounding region into darkness for two and a half days.



The volcano's collapse triggered tsunamis, with the greatest wave reaching 120 feet, taking some 36,000 lives in Java and Sumatra. All life on the Krakatoa island group was buried under a thick layer of sterile ash. The eruption ejected about 18 cubic km of magma, causing the volcano to collapse and form a caldera, with 23 square km of the island disappearing.

- How did the eruption of Krakatoa affect the surrounding areas?

The eruption discharged nearly 5 cubic miles (21 cubic km) of rock fragments into the air, depositing ash over 300,000 square miles (800,000 square km). **Krakatoa**, [volcano](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcano) on Rakata Island in the [Sunda Strait](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sunda-Strait) between Java and Sumatra, [Indonesia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indonesia). Its explosive eruption in 1883 was one of the most catastrophic in history.

Krakatoa lies along the convergence of the Indian-Australian and Eurasian tectonic plates, a zone of high volcanic and seismic activity. Sometime within the past million years, the volcano built a cone-shaped [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) composed of flows of [volcanic rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/extrusive-rock) [alternating](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/alternating) with layers of cinder and ash. From its base, 1,000 feet (300 metres) below [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level), the cone projected about 6,000 feet (1,800 metres) above the sea. Later (possibly in 416 ce), the mountain’s top was destroyed, forming a [caldera](https://www.britannica.com/science/caldera), or bowl-shaped depression, 4 miles (6 km) across. Portions of the caldera projected above the water as four small islands: Sertung (Verlaten) on the northwest, Lang and Polish Hat on the northeast, and Rakata on the south. Over the years, three new cones were formed, merging into a single island. The highest of the three cones rose to 2,667 feet (813 metres) above [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level).

The only confirmed eruption prior to 1883 was a [moderate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/moderate) one in 1680. On May 20, 1883, one of the cones again became active; ash-laden clouds reached a height of 6 miles (10 km), and explosions were heard in Batavia (Jakarta), 100 miles (160 km) away, but by the end of May the activity had died down. It resumed on June 19 and became paroxysmal by August 26. At 1:00 pm of that day the first of a series of increasingly violent explosions occurred, and at 2:00 pm a black cloud of ash rose 17 miles (27 km) above Krakatoa. The climax was reached at 10:00 am on August 27, with [tremendous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/tremendous) explosions that were heard 2,200 miles (3,500 km) away in Australia and propelled ash to a height of 50 miles (80 km). Pressure waves in the atmosphere were recorded around the [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth). Explosions diminished throughout the day, and by the morning of August 28, the volcano was quiet. Small eruptions continued in the following months and in February 1884.

The discharge of Krakatoa threw into the air nearly 5 cubic miles (21 cubic km) of rock fragments, and large quantities of ash fell over an area of some 300,000 square miles (800,000 square km). Near the volcano, masses of floating pumice were so thick as to halt ships. The surrounding region was plunged into darkness for two and a half days because of ash in the air. The fine dust drifted several times around the Earth, causing spectacular red and orange sunsets throughout the following year.

After the explosion, only a small islet remained in a [basin](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/basin) covered by 900 feet (250 metres) of ocean water; its highest point reached about 2,560 feet (780 metres) above the surface. As much as 200 feet (60 metres) of ash and pumice fragments had accumulated on Verlaten and Lang islands and on the remaining southern part of Rakata. Analysis of this material revealed that little of it consisted of debris from the former central cones: the fragments of old rock in it represented less than 10 percent of the volume of the missing part of the island. Most of the material was new magma brought up from the depths of the Earth, most of it distended into pumice or completely blown apart to form ash as the gas it contained expanded. Thus, the former volcanic cones were not blown into the air, as was first believed, but sank out of sight, the top of the volcano collapsing as a large volume of magma was removed from the underlying reservoir.

Krakatoa was apparently uninhabited, and few people died outright from the eruptions. However, the volcano’s collapse triggered a series of [tsunamis](https://www.britannica.com/science/tsunami), or seismic sea waves, recorded as far away as [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) and Hawaii. The greatest wave, which reached a height of 120 feet (37 metres) and took some 36,000 lives in nearby coastal towns of Java and Sumatra, occurred just after the climactic explosion. All life on the Krakatoa island group was buried under a thick layer of [sterile](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/sterile) ash, and plant and animal life did not begin to reestablish itself for five years.

Krakatoa was quiet until December 1927, when a new eruption began on the seafloor along the same line as the previous cones. In early 1928 a rising cone reached sea level, and by 1930 it had become a small island called Anak Krakatau (“Child of Krakatoa”). ### What is a tsunami?

A tsunami is a catastrophic ocean wave, usually caused by a submarine [earthquake](https://www.britannica.com/science/earthquake-geology), an underwater or coastal [landslide](https://www.britannica.com/science/landslide), or a [volcanic](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcano) eruption. Waves radiate outward from the generating impulse at speeds of up to 500 miles (800 km) per hour, reaching maximum heights of 100 feet (30 metres) near coastal areas. Although often called [tidal waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/tide), the occurrence of tsunamis have no connection with tides. The word _tsunami_ is Japanese for “harbour wave.”

### What have been some of the worst tsunamis in history?

Perhaps the most destructive tsunami in recorded history was the [Indian Ocean Tsunami of 2004](https://www.britannica.com/event/Indian-Ocean-tsunami-of-2004). A 9.1-magnitude earthquake occurred off the coast of [Sumatra](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sumatra) in Indonesia. Waves as high as 30 feet (9 metres) struck the eastern coasts of India and Sri Lanka—some 750 miles (1,200 km) away—and traveled more than 1,800 miles (3,000 km) to East Africa. The final death toll was at least 225,000, mostly in Indonesia, Thailand, India, and Sri Lanka. The affected countries also reported extensive economic and infrastructural damage.

### What are the signs of a tsunami?

Because of frequent tsunamis in the Pacific Basin, many adjacent countries have established tsunami warning systems that look for large [earthquakes](https://www.britannica.com/science/earthquake-geology) (magnitude 7.0 or higher) and unusual changes in sea level. Depending on the distance from the seismic disturbance, this warning system may give people several hours to evacuate coastal areas.

### Where is the safest place to go during a tsunami?

During a tsunami, experts recommend that people attempt to find higher ground that is as far inland as possible in order to avoid the deadly waves.

### Can tsunamis occur on other planets?

Tsunamis are not limited to bodies of water on Earth. A 2016 analysis of the [Martian surface](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mars-planet) revealed evidence of two separate tsunami events that occurred long ago, likely as a result of comet or asteroid impacts.

## Origin and development

![tsunami](https://cdn.britannica.com/87/177687-050-ECA25E29/earthquake-landslide-tsunami-water-coastline-ocean.jpg)

[tsunami](https://cdn.britannica.com/87/177687-050-ECA25E29/earthquake-landslide-tsunami-water-coastline-ocean.jpg) After being generated by an undersea earthquake or landslide, a tsunami may propagate unnoticed over vast reaches of open ocean before cresting in shallow water and inundating a coastline.(more)

After an earthquake or other generating impulse occurs, a train of simple, progressive oscillatory waves is propagated great distances over the ocean surface in ever-widening circles, much like the waves produced by a pebble falling into a shallow pool. In deep [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) a tsunami can travel as fast as 800 km (500 miles) per hour. The [wavelengths](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength) are enormous, sometimes exceeding 500 km (about 310 miles), but the wave [amplitudes](https://www.britannica.com/science/amplitude-physics) (heights) are very small, only about 30 to 60 cm (1 to 2 feet). The waves’ periods (the lengths of time for successive crests or troughs to pass a single point) are very long, varying from five minutes to more than an hour. These long periods, coupled with the extremely low steepness and height of the waves, enables them to be completely obscured in deep water by normal [wind](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind) waves and swell. A ship on the high seas experiences the passage of a tsunami as an insignificant rise and fall of only half a metre (1.6 feet), lasting from five minutes to an hour or more.

As the waves approach the coast of a continent, however, [friction](https://www.britannica.com/science/friction) with the rising sea bottom reduces the [velocity](https://www.britannica.com/science/velocity) of the waves. As the velocity lessens, the wavelengths become shortened and the wave amplitudes (heights) increase. Coastal waters may rise as high as 30 metres (about 100 feet) above normal [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level) in 10 to 15 minutes. The [continental shelf](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-shelf) waters begin to oscillate after the rise in sea level. Between three and five major oscillations generate most of the damage, frequently appearing as powerful “run-ups” of rushing water that uproot [trees](https://www.britannica.com/plant/tree), pull buildings off their foundations, carry boats far inshore, and wash away entire [beaches](https://www.britannica.com/science/beach), peninsulas, and other low-lying coastal formations. Frequently the succeeding outflow of water is just as destructive as the run-up or even more so. In any case, oscillations may continue for several days until the ocean surface reaches equilibrium.

Much like any other [water waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-water), tsunamis are reflected and refracted by the topography of the seafloor near shore and by the configuration of a coastline. As a result, their effects vary widely from place to place. Occasionally, the first arrival of a tsunami at a coast may be the trough of the wave, in which case the water recedes and exposes the shallow seafloor. Such an occurrence took place in the bay of [Lisbon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lisbon), Portugal, on November 1, 1755, after a large [earthquake](https://www.britannica.com/event/Lisbon-earthquake-of-1755); many curious people were attracted to the bay floor, and a large number of them were drowned by the wave crest that followed the trough only minutes later.

## Notable tsunamis

[![Pixelated window with the text "TSUNAMI OR WAVE?" and a large cursor overlaying a background of a large wave hitting the coast.](https://cdn.britannica.com/23/272923-138-1ED781BE/what-is-a-tsunami.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/what-is-a-tsunami/-339714)

One of the most destructive tsunamis in antiquity took place in the eastern [Mediterranean Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea) on July 21, 365 ce. A fault slip in the [subduction zone](https://www.britannica.com/science/subduction-zone) beneath the [island](https://www.britannica.com/science/island) of [Crete](https://www.britannica.com/place/Crete) produced an [earthquake](https://www.britannica.com/science/earthquake-geology) with an estimated magnitude of 8.0–8.5, which was powerful enough to raise parts of the western third of the island up to 10 metres (33 feet). The earthquake spawned a tsunami that claimed tens of thousands of lives and caused widespread damage throughout the Mediterranean, from islands in the [Aegean Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Aegean-Sea) westward to the coast of present-day [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain). Tsunami waves pushed ships over harbour walls and onto the roofs of houses in [Alexandria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alexandria-Egypt), [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt), while also ruining nearby croplands by inundating them with [salt](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt) water.

Perhaps the most destructive [tsunami](https://www.britannica.com/event/Indian-Ocean-tsunami-of-2004) in recorded history took place on December 26, 2004, after an earthquake of magnitude 9.1 displaced the ocean floor off the Indonesian island of [Sumatra](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sumatra). Two hours later, waves as high as 9 metres (30 feet) struck the eastern coasts of [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India) and [Sri Lanka](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sri-Lanka), some 1,200 km (750 miles) away. Within seven hours of the quake, waves washed ashore on the [Horn of Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Horn-of-Africa), more than 3,000 km (1,800 miles) away on the other side of the [Indian Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indian-Ocean). More than 200,000 people were killed, most of them on Sumatra but thousands of others in [Thailand](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thailand), India, and Sri Lanka and smaller numbers in [Malaysia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Malaysia), [Myanmar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Myanmar), [Bangladesh](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bangladesh), [Maldives](https://www.britannica.com/place/Maldives), [Somalia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Somalia), and other locations.

On March 11, 2011, seafloor displacement resulting from a magnitude-9.0 earthquake in the [Japan Trench](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan-Trench) of the Pacific Ocean created a large [tsunami](https://www.britannica.com/event/Japan-earthquake-and-tsunami-of-2011) that devastated much of the eastern coast of [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/event/Japan-earthquake-and-tsunami-of-2011)’s main island of [Honshu](https://www.britannica.com/place/Honshu). Waves measuring as much as 10 metres (33 feet) high struck the city of [Sendai](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sendai-Miyagi-prefecture-Japan) and other low-lying coastal regions of [Miyagi prefecture](https://www.britannica.com/place/Miyagi) as well as coastal areas in the prefectures of [Iwate](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iwate), [Fukushima](https://www.britannica.com/place/Fukushima-prefecture-Japan), [Ibaraki](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ibaraki), and [Chiba](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chiba-prefecture-Japan). The tsunami also instigated a [major nuclear accident](https://www.britannica.com/event/Fukushima-accident) at the Fukushima Daiichi power station along the coast.

One of the most notable prehistoric tsunamis took place during the [K-T extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/K-T-extinction), a global [extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology) event that eliminated approximately 80 percent of all animal [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) about 66 million years ago. Many scientists argue that the event was mostly caused by the impact of a large [meteor](https://www.britannica.com/science/meteor) or [comet](https://www.britannica.com/science/comet-astronomy) on the [Yucatán Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yucatan-Peninsula) near Chicxulub, [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico). The impact caused an enormous 1.6-km- (1-mile) tall tsunami that washed up on the shores of the [Gulf of Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gulf-of-Mexico-Gulf-of-America) and the islands of the [Caribbean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Caribbean-Sea) before propagating across the [Atlantic Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean) and other [ocean basins](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean-basin).

Other tsunamis of note include those that followed the spectacular explosive eruption of the [Krakatoa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Krakatoa) (Krakatau) volcano on August 26 and 27, 1883, and the [Chile earthquake of 1960](https://www.britannica.com/event/Chile-earthquake-of-1960). A series of blasts from Krakatoa submerged the island of Rakata between [Sumatra](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sumatra) and [Java](https://www.britannica.com/place/Java-island-Indonesia), creating waves as high as 35 metres (115 feet) in many East Indies localities, and killed more than 36,000 people. The largest [earthquake](https://www.britannica.com/event/Chile-earthquake-of-1960) ever recorded (magnitude 9.5) took place in 1960 off the coast of [Chile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chile), and it caused a tsunami that killed approximately 2,000 people in Chile, 61 people 15 hours later in [Hawaii](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hawaii-state), and 122 people 22 hours later in Japan.

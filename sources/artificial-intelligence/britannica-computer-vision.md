**computer vision**, field of [artificial intelligence](https://www.britannica.com/technology/artificial-intelligence) in which programs attempt to identify objects represented in digitized images provided by cameras, thus enabling [computers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer) to “see.” Much work has been [done](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/done) on using deep learning and [neural networks](https://www.britannica.com/technology/neural-network) to help computers process visual information. Computers can be given a large data set of visual images and identify features and patterns within those images that the computers can then apply to other images.

**neural network**, a [computer program](https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-program) that operates in a manner inspired by the natural neural network in the [brain](https://www.britannica.com/science/brain). The objective of such artificial neural networks is to perform such [cognitive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cognitive) functions as [problem solving](https://www.britannica.com/science/problem-solving) and [machine learning](https://www.britannica.com/technology/machine-learning). The theoretical basis of neural networks was developed in 1943 by the neurophysiologist Warren McCulloch of the [University of Illinois](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Illinois) and the mathematician Walter Pitts of the [University of Chicago](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Chicago). In 1954 Belmont Farley and Wesley Clark of the [Massachusetts Institute of Technology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Massachusetts-Institute-of-Technology) succeeded in running the first simple neural network. The primary appeal of neural networks is their ability to emulate the brain’s pattern-recognition skills. Among commercial applications of this ability, neural networks have been used to make investment decisions, recognize handwriting, and even detect bombs.

A distinguishing feature of neural networks is that knowledge of its domain is distributed throughout the network itself rather than being explicitly written into the program. This knowledge is modeled as the connections between the processing elements (artificial neurons) and the adaptive weights of each of these connections. The network then learns through exposure to various situations. Neural networks are able to accomplish this by adjusting the weight of the connections between the communicating neurons grouped into layers, as shown in the figure of a simple feedforward network. The input layer of artificial neurons receives information from the [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment), and the output layer communicates the response; between these layers may be one or more “hidden” layers (with no direct contact with the environment), where most of the [information processing](https://www.britannica.com/technology/information-processing) takes place. The output of a neural network depends on the weights of the connections between neurons in different layers. Each weight indicates the relative importance of a particular connection. If the total of all the weighted inputs received by a particular [neuron](https://www.britannica.com/science/neuron) surpasses a certain [threshold](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/threshold) value, the neuron will send a signal to each neuron to which it is connected in the next layer. In the processing of loan applications, for example, the inputs may represent loan applicant profile data and the output whether to grant a loan.

Two modifications of this simple feedforward neural network account for the growth of applications, such as facial recognition. First, a network can be equipped with a feedback mechanism, known as a [back-propagation algorithm](https://www.britannica.com/technology/back-propagation-algorithm), that enables it to adjust the connection weights back through the network, training it in response to representative examples. Second, recurrent neural networks can be developed, involving signals that proceed in both directions as well as within and between layers, and these networks are capable of vastly more complicated patterns of association. (In fact, for large networks it can be extremely difficult to follow exactly how an output was determined.)

Training neural networks typically involves supervised [learning](https://www.britannica.com/science/learning), where each training example contains the [values](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/values) of both the input data and the desired output. As soon as the network is able to perform sufficiently well on additional test cases, it can be applied to the new cases. For example, researchers at the [University of British Columbia](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-British-Columbia) have trained a feedforward neural network with temperature and pressure data from the tropical [Pacific Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean) and from [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) to predict future global [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather) patterns.

In contrast, certain neural networks are trained through unsupervised learning, in which a network is presented with a collection of input data and given the goal of discovering patterns—without being told what specifically to look for. Such a neural network might be used in [data mining](https://www.britannica.com/technology/data-mining), for example, to discover clusters of customers in a marketing data warehouse.

Neural networks are at the forefront of cognitive computing, which is intended to have information [technology](https://www.britannica.com/technology/technology) perform some of the more-advanced human mental functions. Deep learning systems are based on multilayer neural networks and power, for example, the [speech recognition](https://www.britannica.com/technology/speech-recognition) capability of [Apple’s](https://www.britannica.com/money/Apple-Inc) mobile assistant [Siri](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Siri). Combined with exponentially growing computing power and the massive [aggregates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aggregates) of [big data](https://www.britannica.com/technology/big-data), deep-learning neural networks influence the distribution of work between people and machines.

Soap has some pretty nifty chemical properties that allow oils and water to mix.

[Soap](https://www.britannica.com/science/soap) is able to clean hands and dishes because of some pretty nifty chemistry. Soap molecules have on one end what’s known as a polar salt, which is hydrophilic, or attracted to water. The other end of the molecule is a nonpolar chain of [fatty acids](https://www.britannica.com/science/fatty-acid) or hydrocarbons, which is hydrophobic—meaning that it’s repelled by water but attracted to grease and other oily substances. When you wash your hands, the soap forms something like a molecular bridge between the water and the dirty, germ-laden oils on your hands, attaching to both the oils and the water and lifting the grime off and away. Soaps can also link up with the [fatty membranes](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-membrane) on the outside of [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria) and certain [viruses](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus), lifting the infectious agents off and even breaking them apart. Once the oily dirt and germs are off your hands, the soap molecules thoroughly surround them and form tiny clusters, known as micelles, that keep them from attaching to anything else while they wash down the drain.

- What are soap and detergent?







[Soaps](https://www.britannica.com/science/soap/Introduction#ref1) and [detergents](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/detergent#dictionary-entry-2) are cleansing agents that, when dissolved in water, can remove dirt from surfaces. Soap is made through the saponification reaction of fats or oils with an alkali, while detergents are synthetic, water-soluble compounds chemically different from soaps.



Both contain hydrophobic and hydrophilic parts that attach to soil and water, respectively. Detergents don't form scum in hard water like soaps do, and they offer formulation flexibility. They can be anionic, cationic, nonionic, or ampholytic, depending on the charge of the colloidal ions produced in solution.

- How do soap and detergent help clean things?







[Soaps](https://www.britannica.com/science/soap/Introduction#ref1) and [detergents](https://www.britannica.com/technology/detergent/Introduction#ref1) are cleansing agents that help remove dirt from surfaces by reducing water's surface tension, allowing it to wet surfaces more effectively. Soap molecules have a [hydrophilic](https://www.britannica.com/story/how-does-soap-work/Introduction#ref1) (water-attracting) end and a [hydrophobic](https://www.britannica.com/story/how-does-soap-work/Introduction#ref1) (water-repelling) end. The hydrophobic end attaches to [grease and dirt](https://www.britannica.com/story/how-does-soap-work/Introduction#ref1), while the hydrophilic end attaches to water, lifting the dirt away. The soap molecules then surround the dirt particles, forming clusters called micelles, which prevent the dirt from redepositing on the surface.

- What are the main ingredients used to make soap and detergent?







The primary ingredients for making soap are fat and alkali. A hot caustic alkali solution, such as caustic soda (sodium hydroxide), acts on natural fats or oils, such as tallow or vegetable oil, to produce sodium fatty acid salt (soap) and glycerin (or glycerol). This saponification reaction is the basis for all soapmaking.



Detergents contain alkaline materials that increase their action. The largest quantities of synthetic detergents are consumed in the household in the form of spray-dried powders. They are produced from an aqueous slurry, which is prepared continuously or in batches and contains all the builder components. Builders, consisting of certain alkaline materials, are almost universally present in laundry soaps. The most important are sodium silicate (water glass), sodium carbonate (soda ash), and various phosphates.

- How are soap and detergent different from each other?







While both [soap](https://www.britannica.com/science/soap/Introduction#ref1) and [detergent](https://www.britannica.com/story/www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/detergent#dictionary-entry-2) can remove dirt from surfaces, they differ chemically. Soap is made from natural fats or oils reacting with an alkali, while detergents are synthetic.



Detergents are superior to soaps in several ways. Soaps form scum in [hard water](https://www.britannica.com/science/hard-water/Introduction#ref1), but detergents don't react as easily with metal salts or acids. This makes detergents more effective in various water conditions. The molecules of both must contain a hydrophobic part, such as a fatty acid, and a hydrophilic part, such as a sulfo group.

- Why do soaps not work well in hard water, but detergents do?







While it's true that soaps don't work well in hard water and detergents do, soaps and detergents aren't just chemical compounds; they're complex mixtures. Here's why:



### Soap vs. Detergent



**Soaps** are made from natural fats or oils mixed with an alkali, like sodium hydroxide. In hard water, the calcium and magnesium ions react with soap to form an insoluble curd or scum, which reduces soap's cleaning effectiveness.

**Detergents** are synthetic cleaning agents made from petroleum products. They are designed to work in hard water because they don't form insoluble precipitates with calcium and magnesium ions. Some detergents even contain water softeners to counteract hard water effects.



### How They Work



Both soaps and detergents have a [hydrophobic](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrophobic) (water-insoluble) part that attaches to dirt and a [hydrophilic](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrophilic) (water-soluble) part that attaches to water, lifting dirt away. Detergents can maintain this action even in hard water, while soaps are hindered by scum formation.

- How does the molecular structure of soaps and detergents allow them to remove grease and oil from surfaces?







Soaps and detergents clean because their molecular structures combine both hydrophobic and hydrophilic properties. A [hydrophobic](https://www.britannica.com/science/lipid/Substituent-groups#ref257716) end attracts grease and oil, while a [hydrophilic](https://www.britannica.com/science/lipid/Substituent-groups#ref257716) end is attracted to water.



This dual attraction allows soap molecules to act as a bridge between water and oily dirt. The hydrophobic ends attach to the oil, and the hydrophilic ends attach to the water, lifting the grime away. Soap molecules surround the oil and dirt, forming clusters called micelles, which are then washed away with water.

**soap and detergent**, [substances](https://www.britannica.com/topic/substance-philosophy) that, when [dissolved](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/dissolved) in [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water), possess the [ability to remove dirt](https://www.britannica.com/technology/detergent) from surfaces such as the [human](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being) skin, textiles, and other solids. When soap and water are not available for hand washing or when repeated hand washing compromises the natural skin barrier (e.g., causing scaling or fissures to develop in the skin), [hand sanitizers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hand-sanitizer)—coming in [foam](https://www.britannica.com/science/foam), [gel](https://www.britannica.com/science/gel), or [liquid](https://www.britannica.com/science/liquid-state-of-matter) form—have been recommended.

The seemingly simple process of cleaning a soiled surface is, in fact, complex and consists of the following physical-chemical steps:

1. Wetting of the surface and, in the case of textiles, penetration of the fibre structure by wash liquor containing the [detergent](https://www.britannica.com/technology/detergent). Detergents (and other [surface-active agents](https://www.britannica.com/science/surfactant)) increase the spreading and wetting ability of water by reducing its surface tension—that is, the [affinity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/affinity) its molecules have for each other in preference to the molecules of the material to be washed.

2. Absorption of a layer of the soap or detergent at the interfaces between the water and the surface to be washed and between the water and the soil. In the case of ionic surface-active agents (explained below), the layer formed is ionic (electrically polar) in nature.

3. Dispersion of soil from the fibre or other material into the wash water. This step is [facilitated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/facilitated) by mechanical agitation and high temperature; in the case of hand soap, soil is dispersed in the foam formed by mechanical action of the hands.

4. Preventing the soil from being deposited again onto the surface cleaned. The soap or detergent accomplishes this by suspending the dirt in a protective colloid, sometimes with the aid of special additives. In a great many soiled surfaces the dirt is bound to the surface by a thin film of oil or grease. The cleaning of such surfaces involves the displacement of this film by the detergent solution, which is in turn washed away by rinse waters. The oil film breaks up and separates into individual droplets under the influence of the detergent solution. [Proteinic](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) stains, such as egg, milk, and blood, are difficult to remove by detergent action alone. The proteinic stain is nonsoluble in water, adheres strongly to the fibre, and prevents the penetration of the detergent. By using [proteolytic enzymes](https://www.britannica.com/science/proteolytic-enzyme) (enzymes able to break down proteins) together with detergents, the proteinic substance can be made water-soluble or at least water-permeable, permitting the detergent to act and the proteinic stain to be dispersed together with the oily dirt. The enzymes may present a toxic hazard to some persons habitually exposed.


If detached oil droplets and dirt particles did not become suspended in the detergent solution in a stable and highly dispersed condition, they would be inclined to [flocculate](https://www.britannica.com/science/flocculation), or coalesce into [aggregates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aggregates) large enough to be redeposited on the cleansed surface. In the washing of fabrics and similar materials, small oil droplets or fine, deflocculated dirt particles are more easily carried through interstices in the material than are relatively large ones. The action of the detergent in maintaining the dirt in a highly dispersed condition is therefore important in preventing retention of detached dirt by the fabric.

In order to perform as detergents (surface-active agents), soaps and detergents must have certain chemical structures: their molecules must contain a hydrophobic (water-insoluble) part, such as a [fatty acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/fatty-acid) or a rather long chain carbon group, such as [fatty alcohols](https://www.britannica.com/science/fatty-alcohol) or alkylbenzene. The molecule must also contain a hydrophilic (water-soluble) group, such as ―COONa, or a sulfo group, such as ―OSO3Na or ―SO3Na (such as in fatty alcohol sulfate or alkylbenzene sulfonate), or a long ethylene oxide chain in nonionic [synthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/synthetic) detergents. This hydrophilic part makes the molecule soluble in water. In general, the hydrophobic part of the molecule attaches itself to the solid or fibre and onto the soil, and the hydrophilic part attaches itself to the water.

Four groups of surface-active agents are distinguished:

1. [Anionic detergents](https://www.britannica.com/technology/anionic-detergent) (including soap and the largest portion of modern synthetic detergents), which produce electrically negative colloidal ions in solution.

2. [Cationic detergents](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cationic-detergent), which produce electrically positive ions in solution.

3. [Nonionic detergents](https://www.britannica.com/technology/nonionic-detergent), which produce electrically neutral colloidal particles in solution.

4. [Ampholytic](https://www.britannica.com/technology/ampholytic-detergent), or amphoteric, detergents, which are capable of acting either as anionic or cationic detergents in solution depending on the pH (acidity or alkalinity) of the solution.


The first detergent (or surface-active agent) was soap. In a strictly chemical sense, any [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) formed by the reaction of a water-insoluble fatty acid with an organic [base](https://www.britannica.com/science/base-chemical-compound) or an [alkali metal](https://www.britannica.com/science/alkali-metal) may be called a soap. Practically, however, the soap [industry](https://www.britannica.com/money/industry) is concerned mainly with those water-soluble soaps that result from the interaction between fatty acids and alkali metals. In certain cases, however, the salts of fatty acids with ammonia or with triethanolamine are also used, as in shaving preparations.

## History

## Use

Soap has been known for at least 2,300 years. According to [Pliny the Elder](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pliny-the-Elder), the Phoenicians prepared it from goat’s [tallow](https://www.britannica.com/science/tallow) and wood ashes in 600 bce and sometimes used it as an article of barter with the Gauls. Soap was widely known in the [Roman Empire](https://www.britannica.com/place/Roman-Empire); whether the Romans learned its use and manufacture from ancient Mediterranean peoples or from the [Celts](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Celt-people), inhabitants of [Britannia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Roman-Britain), is not known. The Celts, who produced their soap from animal [fats](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fat) and plant [ashes](https://www.britannica.com/science/ash-residue), named the product _saipo,_ from which the word soap is derived. The importance of soap for washing and cleaning was apparently not recognized until the 2nd century ce; the Greek physician [Galen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Galen) mentions it as a [medicament](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pharmaceutical) and as a means of cleansing the body. Previously soap had been used as [medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/medicine). The writings attributed to the 8th-century Arab [savant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/savant) [Jābir ibn Hayyān](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abu-Musa-Jabir-ibn-Hayyan) (Geber) repeatedly mention soap as a cleansing agent.

In Europe, soap production in the Middle Ages centred first at [Marseille](https://www.britannica.com/place/Marseille), later at [Genoa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Genoa-Italy), and then at [Venice](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venice). Although some soap manufacture developed in Germany, the substance was so little used in central Europe that a box of soap presented to the Duchess of Juelich in 1549 caused a sensation. As late as 1672, when a German, A. Leo, sent Lady von Schleinitz a parcel containing soap from Italy, he accompanied it with a detailed description of how to use the mysterious product.

The first [English](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) soapmakers appeared at the end of the 12th century in [Bristol](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bristol-England). In the 13th and 14th centuries, a small [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) of them grew up in the neighbourhood of Cheapside in [London](https://www.britannica.com/place/London). In those days soapmakers had to pay a duty on all the soap they produced. After the [Napoleonic Wars](https://www.britannica.com/event/Napoleonic-Wars) this tax rose as high as three pence per pound; soap-boiling pans were fitted with lids that could be locked every night by the tax collector in order to prevent production under cover of darkness. Not until 1853 was this high tax finally abolished, at a sacrifice to the state of over £1,000,000. Soap came into such common use in the 19th century that [Justus von Liebig](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Justus-Freiherr-von-Liebig), a German chemist, declared that the quantity of soap [consumed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/consumed) by a nation was an accurate measure of its wealth and civilization.

## Early soap production

Early soapmakers probably used ashes and animal fats. Simple wood or plant ashes containing potassium carbonate were dispersed in water, and fat was added to the solution. This mixture was then boiled; ashes were added again and again as the water evaporated. During this process a slow chemical splitting of the neutral fat took place; the fatty acids could then react with the alkali carbonates of the plant ash to form soap (this reaction is called [saponification](https://www.britannica.com/science/saponification)).

Animal fats containing a percentage of free fatty acids were used by the Celts. The presence of free fatty acids certainly helped to get the process started. This method probably [prevailed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/prevailed) until the end of the [Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages), when [slaked lime](https://www.britannica.com/science/calcium-hydroxide) came to be used to causticize the alkali carbonate. Through this process, chemically neutral fats could be saponified easily with the caustic lye. The production of soap from a handicraft to an industry was helped by the introduction of the Leblanc process for the production of soda ash from brine (about 1790) and by the work of a French chemist, [Michel Eugène Chevreul](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Michel-Eugene-Chevreul), who in 1823 showed that the process of saponification is the chemical process of splitting fat into the alkali salt of fatty acids (that is, soap) and [glycerin](https://www.britannica.com/science/glycerol).

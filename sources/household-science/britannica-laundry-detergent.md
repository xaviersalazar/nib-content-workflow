**detergent**, any of various [surfactants](https://www.britannica.com/science/surfactant) (surface-active agents) particularly effective in dislodging foreign matter from soiled surfaces and retaining it in suspension. The term usually denotes a [synthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/synthetic) substance that is not prepared by saponifying fats and oils (as is soap).

A brief treatment of detergents follows. For full treatment, _see_ [soap and detergent](https://www.britannica.com/science/soap).

Key People: [William Hesketh Lever](https://www.britannica.com/money/William-Hesketh-Lever)_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/detergent)

Dishwashing and laundering of [clothing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dress-clothing) are the principal applications of detergents for which the [liquid](https://www.britannica.com/science/liquid-state-of-matter) bath is [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water). Detergents also are used as emulsifiers in many applications. Detergents that function in nonaqueous media include [dispersing agents](https://www.britannica.com/science/dispersing-agent) added to lubricating oils used in automotive engines to prevent the accumulation of varnishlike deposits on the cylinder walls, to [gasoline](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gasoline-fuel) to prevent the buildup of gummy residues in the [carburetor](https://www.britannica.com/technology/carburetor), and to [dry-cleaning](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dry-cleaning) solvents to [facilitate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/facilitate) the removal of soil from garments.

[schematic diagram of the emulsion-polymerization method](https://cdn.britannica.com/66/1666-050-522977DF/diagram-emulsion-polymerization-method-Monomer-molecules-initiators-surfactant.jpg) Schematic diagram of the emulsion-polymerization method. Monomer molecules and free-radical initiators are added to a water-based emulsion bath along with soaplike materials known as surfactants, or surface-acting agents. The surfactant molecules, composed of a hydrophilic (water-attracting) and hydrophobic (water-repelling) end, form a stabilizing emulsion before polymerization by coating the monomer droplets. Other surfactant molecules clump together into smaller aggregates called micelles, which also absorb monomer molecules. Polymerization occurs when initiators migrate into the micelles, inducing the monomer molecules to form large molecules that make up the latex particle. (more)

**surfactant**, substance such as a [detergent](https://www.britannica.com/technology/detergent) that, when added to a [liquid](https://www.britannica.com/science/liquid-state-of-matter), reduces its [surface tension](https://www.britannica.com/science/surface-tension), thereby increasing its spreading and wetting properties. In the dyeing of [textiles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/textile), surfactants help the [dye](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dye) penetrate the fabric evenly. They are used to disperse aqueous suspensions of insoluble dyes and [perfumes](https://www.britannica.com/art/perfume).

The surface-active [molecule](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) must be partly [hydrophilic](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrophilicity) ( [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water)-soluble) and partly [lipophilic](https://www.britannica.com/science/lipophilicity) (soluble in [lipids](https://www.britannica.com/science/lipid), or oils). It concentrates at the interfaces between bodies or droplets of water and those of oil, or lipids, to act as an emulsifying agent, or foaming agent.

Other surfactants that are more lipophilic and less hydrophilic may be used as defoaming agents, or as demulsifiers. Certain surfactants are germicides, [fungicides](https://www.britannica.com/science/fungicide), and [insecticides](https://www.britannica.com/technology/insecticide).

Also called: surface-active agent_(Show more)_

Surfactants are used in [corrosion](https://www.britannica.com/science/corrosion) inhibition, in ore flotation, to promote oil flow in porous rocks, and to produce [aerosols](https://www.britannica.com/science/aerosol).

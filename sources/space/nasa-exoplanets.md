## Overview

Most of the exoplanets discovered so far are in a relatively small region of our galaxy, the Milky Way. (“Small” meaning within thousands of light-years of our solar system; one light-year equals 5.88 trillion miles, or 9.46 trillion kilometers.) Even the closest known exoplanet to Earth, [Proxima Centauri b](https://science.nasa.gov/exoplanet-catalog/proxima-centauri-b/), is still about 4 light-years away. We know there are more planets than stars in the galaxy.

By measuring exoplanets’ sizes (diameters) and masses (weights), we can see compositions ranging from rocky (like Earth and Venus) to gas-rich (like Jupiter and Saturn). Some planets may be dominated by water or ice, while others are dominated by iron or carbon. We’ve identified [lava worlds](https://science.nasa.gov/exoplanet-catalog/55-cancri-b/) covered in molten seas, puffy planets the [density of Styrofoam](https://science.nasa.gov/exoplanet-catalog/kepler-7b/) and dense [cores of planets](https://science.nasa.gov/exoplanet-catalog/psr-b125712-b/) still orbiting their stars.

### Nancy Grace Roman Telescope Now Set to Launch Aug. 30

NASA’s next great orbiting observatory, the Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope, now has an official launch date — eight months ahead of schedule. After it launches Aug. 30, 2026, the Roman Telescope will create enormous cosmic panoramas, and potentially capture more than 50 times as much sky in its first five years as the then-revolutionary Hubble Space Telescope has in 30 years. Scientists expect Roman will help settle essential questions regarding dark energy and astrophysics, discover more than 100,000 exoplanets, and spot hundreds more planetary systems as they’re forming.

## 30 Years of Exoplanets

2025 marked 30 years of exoplanet discoveries. The first planet discovered orbiting a Sun-like star, 51 Pegasi b, was identified in October 1995; in the three decades since then, we’ve confirmed 6,000 more, out of billions that we believe exist. The search for and study of exoplanets (extra-solar planets, those found outside our solar system) has grown rapidly in a short time thanks to new detection methods and increased scrutiny from tools ranging from the most advanced spacecraft, such as the James Webb Space Telescope, to ground-based observatories worldwide, and even citizen scientists using backyard telescopes.

Learn more about that initial discovery 30 years ago, about the accelerating advances in exoplanet science since then, and about the bold plans for the future of the field, with instruments and observatories to expand and refine the search for other worlds – perhaps even habitable ones like our own.

Tau Ceti is real — it's one of the closest Sun-like stars. And the Super-Earth exoplanet in its orbit, Tau Ceti e, nicknamed "Adrian" in the story? Well, it was identified in 2017, but upon further study …we're skeptical. The Rocky exoplanet, "Erid," is strictly fictional, for now. We're always looking, then checking our results again and again — welcome to exoplanet research. Now dive deeper into the astrophysics, astrobiology, heliophysics, human spaceflight, and the other science fields behind the science fiction.

[Learn More: The Science Behind ‘Project Hail Mary’](https://science.nasa.gov/the-science-behind-project-hail-mary/)

## **Do NASA Science and Explore Key Questions about the Universe**

- ### **Exoplanet Watch**




NASA invites people of all ages and backgrounds to participate in authentic NASA research via "citizen science" or "participatory science" projects, where volunteers and amateurs have helped make thousands of important scientific discoveries. As a part of the Exoplanet Watch project, you'll help scientists track and study exoplanets as they transit in front of stars.



[Get Involved](https://science.nasa.gov/citizen-science/summary/exoplanet-watch/)

- ### UNITE




Do you have your own telescope? You can help observe newly discovered exoplanets and collect information about their transits to help characterize their orbits. UNITE participants coordinate with other citizen scientists and researchers around the globe to track and learn about these new worlds.



[Learn More](https://science.nasa.gov/citizen-science/summary/unite-unistellar-network-investigating-tess-exoplanets/)

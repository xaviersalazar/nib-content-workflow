**sushi**, a staple [rice](https://www.britannica.com/plant/rice) dish of Japanese [cuisine](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cuisine), consisting of cooked rice flavoured with [vinegar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vinegar) and a variety of [vegetable](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vegetable), [egg](https://www.britannica.com/topic/egg-food), or raw [seafood](https://www.britannica.com/topic/seafood) garnishes and served cold. Restaurants specializing in sushi abound in Japan, where subtleties of preparation find a discriminating [clientele](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/clientele), and the dish has gained popularity in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) and elsewhere (see also [sashimi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sashimi)).

_Nigiri-zushi_ is a hand-formed oblong of rice topped with sliced raw seafood and a dab of [_wasabi_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/wasabi) (a green [paste made of true wasabi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/wasabi-paste) or [horseradish](https://www.britannica.com/plant/horseradish)); the ingredients of _oshi-zushi_ are pressed to shape in a mold. For _maki-zushi_, a sheet of _nori_ ( [laver](https://www.britannica.com/science/laver), a [seaweed](https://www.britannica.com/science/seaweed)) is spread with rice, then with seafood or vegetables and garnishes. The whole is rolled into a cylinder and sliced. In _chirashi-zushi_, a homestyle version, the ingredients are not formed, rather the vinegared rice is strewn with toppings and garnishes. Vinegar-pickled [ginger](https://www.britannica.com/plant/ginger) root ( _sūshoga_) is a traditional palate-clearing accompaniment to sushi.

### What is _kaiseki_ cuisine?

_Kaiseki_ is a traditional [Japanese](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) multicourse meal that is prepared with [seasonal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/growing-season) ingredients and is usually served on special occasions. It can refer to either a light meal before a [tea ceremony](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tea-ceremony) or an elaborate meal with [sake](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sake).

### What is the historical origin of _kaiseki_?

_Kaiseki_’s origins are linked to the history of [tea](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tea-beverage) in [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/). During the [Heian period](https://www.britannica.com/event/Heian-period), Japanese monks brought tea from [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China), which led to tea becoming popular in the emperor’s court and to the evolution of [tea ceremonies](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tea-ceremony).

### What are some typical courses in a _kaiseki_?

Typical courses in a _kaiseki_ include _sakizuke_ (appetizer), _hassun_ ( [seasonal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/growing-season) flavors), _suimono_ ( [soup](https://www.britannica.com/topic/soup)), _mukozuke_ ( [sashimi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sashimi)), _yakimono_ (grilled dish), _takiawase_ (simmered dish), _shokuji_ ( [rice](https://www.britannica.com/plant/rice) dish), _agemono_ (deep-fried dish), _shiizakana_ (savory dish), and _mizumono_ ( [dessert](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dessert)).

### What are some etiquette rules for _kaiseki_ dining?

Some examples of etiquette that are observed during a _kaiseki_ include the host thanking the chef prior to eating, the host starting the meal with “ _itadakimasu_,” using chopsticks properly, and thanking the chef again at the end with “ _gochisosama deshita_.”

**kaiseki**, traditional [Japanese](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) [cuisine](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cuisine) that consists of a multicourse meal prepared with [seasonal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/growing-season) ingredients and that is generally served on special occasions.

The term _kaiseki_ can refer to one of two types of meals, depending on the [kanji](https://www.britannica.com/topic/kanji) characters used in its spelling. _Kaiseki_ (懐石), also known as _cha-kaiseki_, is a light meal meant to fill the [stomach](https://www.britannica.com/science/stomach) before a [tea ceremony](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tea-ceremony). The kanji characters translate roughly to “stones in the robe” and refer to warm stones that [Buddhist](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism) monks would fold into their robes to stay warm and to keep hunger at bay when [fasting](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fasting). _Kaiseki_ (会席), also known as _kaiseki-ryori_, is an [elaborate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/elaborate) meal accompanied by [sake](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sake) and ending with tea. The kanji characters translate roughly to “social gathering with seating” and refer to a formal occasion. The term _kaiseki_, in its modern use, generally refers to the more contemporary, elaborate cuisine.

The origins of _kaiseki_ are linked to the history of [tea](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tea-beverage) in Japan. During the [Heian period](https://www.britannica.com/event/Heian-period) (794–1185), [monks](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monk-monasticism) traveled to [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) to study [Zen](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zen) Buddhism during the [Tang dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tang-dynasty) and returned with knowledge about tea. The emperor’s court praised the qualities and elegance of tea, and tea drinking became popular among the court’s [aristocracy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/aristocracy) and in its religious ceremonies. Tea ceremonies evolved over time and in the 16th century began to include a simple meal.

## _Kaiseki_ courses and dishes

_Kaiseki_ meals traditionally consist of multiple courses, with the precise number depending on the seasonal availability of ingredients and the chef’s preferences.

Typical _kaiseki_ courses and dishes

- _Sakizuke_: An appetizer course that introduces the chef’s style. It usually features [pickled](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food-preservation/Dehydration#ref50571) foods and is served with sake.

- _Hassun_: A course that represents the current season’s flavors and ingredients, establishing the seasonal theme of the meal.

- _Suimono_: A [soup](https://www.britannica.com/topic/soup) course made with dashi broth and meant to cleanse the palate. This dish showcases the chef’s skill, as creating a clear, simple, [yet](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/yet) flavorful soup is exceptionally challenging.

- _Mukozuke_: A [sashimi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sashimi) course featuring premium seasonal [fish](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fish) served with dipping [sauces](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sauce).

- _Yakimono_: A grilled course of fish, [chicken](https://www.britannica.com/animal/chicken), [pork](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pork), or other [proteins](https://www.britannica.com/topic/meat).

- _Takiawase_: A simmered dish of [vegetables](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vegetable) mixed with [meat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/meat), fish, or [tofu](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tofu).

- _Shokuji_: A [rice](https://www.britannica.com/plant/rice) dish, often cooked in a clay hot pot, served with [miso](https://www.britannica.com/topic/miso) soup and accompanied by [pickles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pickle).

- _Agemono_: A deep-fried course commonly featuring [tempura](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tempura)-battered [seafood](https://www.britannica.com/topic/seafood), fish, meats, and vegetables, accompanied by dipping sauces.

- _Shiizakana_: A small savory or salty dish of the chef’s choice that balances out the milder dishes and is meant to be enjoyed with sake.

- _Mizumono_: A [dessert](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dessert) platter featuring a variety of sweets, often including [fruits](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body), confections, [ice cream](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ice-cream), and [cake](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cake).


## _Kaiseki_ table etiquette

The refined form of _kaiseki_ dining requires showing respect and [consideration](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/consideration) to others. Examples of some basic formalities include the host thanking the chef prior to the meal by saying, “ _arigato gozaimashita_” (“thank you”), and the host signaling to the guests that it is time to begin eating by saying, “ _itadakimasu_” (“I receive this meal”) and nodding. The host begins eating first, followed by the guest of honor, followed by the remainder of guests. The _oshibori_ (hand towel) should be used only to wipe hands, never the face. It should be folded neatly after use, with the soiled side in, and placed back on the towel tray. Chopsticks are used only to pick up [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food), never to poke food or cut it into smaller pieces; they are placed on the _hashi-oki_ (chopstick rest) when not in use. After eating, the chopsticks are placed in a horizontal position, with the eating ends facing left. At the end of the meal, the host thanks the chef by saying, “ _gochisosama deshita_” (“thank you for the meal”).

### Where is the potato originally from?

The potato is native to the Peruvian-Bolivian Andes. It was cultivated in South America by the Incas as early as 1,800 years ago. The Spaniards who colonized South America introduced potatoes into Europe during the second half of the 16th century.

### What does a potato plant look like?

Potatoes have compound leaves that are spirally arranged. Each leaf is 20–30 cm long (about 8–12 inches) and consists of a terminal leaflet and two to four pairs of leaflets. The white, lavender, or purple flowers have five fused petals and yellow stamens. The fruit is a small poisonous berry with numerous seeds.

### How are potatoes prepared as a food?

Potatoes are frequently served whole or mashed as a cooked vegetable. They are also ground into potato flour, used in baking, and as a thickener for sauces.

### What nutrients are present in potatoes?

Potatoes are a source of vitamin C, protein, thiamin, and niacin.

**potato**, ( _Solanum tuberosum_), [annual](https://www.britannica.com/science/annual) [plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant) in the nightshade family ( [Solanaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Solanaceae)), grown for its starchy edible [tubers](https://www.britannica.com/science/tuber). The potato is native to the Peruvian-Bolivian [Andes](https://www.britannica.com/place/Andes-Mountains) and is one of the world’s main [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) crops. Potatoes are frequently served whole or mashed as a cooked [vegetable](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vegetable) and are also ground into [potato flour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/potato-flour), used in baking and as a thickener for sauces. The tubers are highly digestible and supply [vitamin C](https://www.britannica.com/science/vitamin-C), protein, [thiamin](https://www.britannica.com/science/thiamin), and [niacin](https://www.britannica.com/science/niacin).

Potatoes are thought to have been independently domesticated several times and were largely [cultivated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultivated) in [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) by the [Incas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Inca) as early as 1,800 years ago. Encountered by the invading Spaniards, potatoes were introduced into Europe during the second half of the 16th century. By the end of the 17th century the plant was a major [crop](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crop-agriculture) in [Ireland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ireland), and by the end of the 18th century it was a major crop in continental Europe, particularly [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany), and in the west of England. It continued to spread, in both Western and Eastern hemispheres, during the first four decades of the 19th century, and the Irish economy itself became dependent upon the potato. However, the disastrous failures of the [Irish](https://www.britannica.com/event/Great-Famine-Irish-history) crops in the mid-19th century (especially in 1846 and 1848), because of [late blight](https://www.britannica.com/science/late-blight) ( _Phytophthora infestans_), and the resulting [Irish Potato Famine](https://www.britannica.com/event/Great-Famine-Irish-history) generated a more-cautious attitude toward dependence on the plant.

## Physical description

The potato is one of some 150 tuber-bearing species of the genus _Solanum_ (a [tuber](https://www.britannica.com/science/tuber) is the swollen end of an underground stem). The [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) [leaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy) are spirally arranged; each leaf is 20–30 cm (about 8–12 inches) long and consists of a terminal leaflet and two to four pairs of leaflets. The white, lavender, or purple [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) have five fused petals and yellow [stamens](https://www.britannica.com/science/stamen). The [fruit](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body) is a small poisonous [berry](https://www.britannica.com/science/berry-plant-reproductive-body) with numerous seeds.

The stems extend underground into structures called [stolons](https://www.britannica.com/science/stolon-biology). The ends of the stolons may enlarge greatly to form a few to more than 20 tubers, of variable shape and size, usually ranging in weight up to 300 grams (10 ounces) but occasionally to more than 1.5 kg (3.3 pounds). The skin varies in colour from brownish white to deep purple; the starchy flesh normally ranges in colour from white to yellow, but it too may be purple. The tubers bear spirally arranged [buds](https://www.britannica.com/science/bud) (eyes) in the axils of aborted leaves, of which scars remain. The buds sprout to form clones of the parent plant, allowing growers to vegetatively [propagate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagate) desired characteristics. Indeed, [vegetative reproduction](https://www.britannica.com/science/vegetative-reproduction) is always used commercially, though the resulting decrease in genetic [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity) has made the popular varieties more [vulnerable](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vulnerable) to pests and [diseases](https://www.britannica.com/science/plant-disease).

**starch**, a white, granular, [organic](https://www.britannica.com/science/organic-compound) chemical that is produced by all green [plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant). Starch is a soft, white, tasteless powder that is insoluble in cold water, [alcohol](https://www.britannica.com/science/alcohol), or other [solvents](https://www.britannica.com/science/solvent-chemistry). The basic chemical formula of the starch molecule is (C6H10O5)n. Starch is a [polysaccharide](https://www.britannica.com/science/polysaccharide) comprising [glucose](https://www.britannica.com/science/glucose) monomers joined in _α_ 1,4 linkages. The simplest form of starch is the linear polymer [amylose](https://www.britannica.com/science/amylose); [amylopectin](https://www.britannica.com/science/amylopectin) is the branched form.

[![fingerling potatoes](https://cdn.britannica.com/20/191620-050-161F6867/fingerling-potatoes.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/20/191620-050-161F6867/fingerling-potatoes.jpg)

[fingerling potatoes](https://cdn.britannica.com/20/191620-050-161F6867/fingerling-potatoes.jpg) Organic fingerling potatoes ( _Solanum tuberosum_).(more)

Starch is manufactured in the green leaves of plants from excess glucose produced during [photosynthesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/photosynthesis) and serves the plant as a reserve food supply. Starch is stored in [chloroplasts](https://www.britannica.com/science/chloroplast) in the form of granules and in such storage organs as the [roots](https://www.britannica.com/science/root-plant) of the [cassava](https://www.britannica.com/plant/cassava) plant; the [tuber](https://www.britannica.com/science/tuber) of the [potato](https://www.britannica.com/plant/potato); the stem [pith](https://www.britannica.com/science/pith) of [sago](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sago); and the [seeds](https://www.britannica.com/science/seed-plant-reproductive-part) of [corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corn-plant), [wheat](https://www.britannica.com/plant/wheat), and [rice](https://www.britannica.com/plant/rice). When required, starch is broken down, in the presence of certain [enzymes](https://www.britannica.com/science/enzyme) and water, into its constituent monomer glucose units, which diffuse from the cell to nourish the plant tissues. In humans and other animals, starch from plants is broken down into its constituent sugar molecules, which then supply energy to the tissues.

Most commercial starch is made from corn, although wheat, tapioca, and potato starch are also used. Commercial starch is obtained by crushing or grinding starch-containing tubers or seeds and then mixing the pulp with water; the resulting paste is freed of its remaining impurities and then dried. Aside from their basic nutritional uses, starches are used in brewing and as thickening agents in baked goods and confections. Starch is used in [paper](https://www.britannica.com/technology/paper) manufacturing to increase the strength of paper and is also used in the surface sizing of paper. Starch is used in the manufacture of corrugated paperboard, paper bags and boxes, and gummed paper and tape. Large quantities of starch are also used in the textile industry as warp sizing, which imparts strength to the thread during weaving.

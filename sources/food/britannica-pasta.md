**pasta**, any of several starchy [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) preparations ( _pasta alimentaria_) frequently associated with [Italian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy/Daily-life-and-social-customs#ref27025) [cuisine](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cuisine) and made from [semolina](https://www.britannica.com/topic/semolina), the [granular](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/granular) product obtained from the [endosperm](https://www.britannica.com/science/endosperm) of a type of [wheat](https://www.britannica.com/plant/wheat) called [durum](https://www.britannica.com/plant/durum-wheat), and containing a large proportion of [gluten](https://www.britannica.com/science/gluten) (elastic protein). It is formed into ribbons, cords, tubes, and various special shapes, all originally developed for specific characteristics, such as ability to retain heat or hold sauces.

![Italian-style pasta products](https://cdn.britannica.com/23/623-050-193459BB/Italian-pasta-linguine-spaghetti-vermicelli-lasagna-macaroni-ziti-rigatoni-manicotti-farfalle-ruote-rotini-conchiglie.jpg)

[Italian-style pasta products](https://cdn.britannica.com/23/623-050-193459BB/Italian-pasta-linguine-spaghetti-vermicelli-lasagna-macaroni-ziti-rigatoni-manicotti-farfalle-ruote-rotini-conchiglie.jpg)(more)

In commercial processing, the semolina mixed with warm water is kneaded into a smooth stiff [dough](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dough) and extruded. The dough, moved forward while it is being compacted and mixed, is forced through perforated plates, or dies, that form it into the desired shape. Hollow tubular forms, such as [macaroni](https://www.britannica.com/topic/macaroni-pasta), result when the perforations are small and contain steel pins, while smaller holes without pins produce [spaghetti](https://www.britannica.com/topic/spaghetti). Flat ribbonlike types are made by slitted perforations. Shell forms are produced by a special die; small fancy shapes are produced by rotary knives slicing the dough as it emerges from the die. The formed dough is next dried, reducing its moisture content from about 31 percent to approximately 12 percent. The drying is carefully regulated, as very rapid drying may result in cracking, and very slow drying may produce stretching or encourage the growth of mold or of organisms that produce souring.

Doughs may be coloured with [spinach](https://www.britannica.com/plant/spinach) juice, producing green pasta; with [beet](https://www.britannica.com/plant/beet) juice, resulting in red types; and with [eggs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/egg-food), adding bright yellow colour. Eggs are frequently added to homemade pastas.

Among the popular cord forms are spaghetti (“little string”), a finer type called spaghettini, and the very fine [vermicelli](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vermicelli) (“little worms”). Tubular types include macaroni, shaped into tubes of 1/2-inch (12.7-mm) diameter, such variations as the small elbow-shaped pieces called _dita lisci_, and the large, fluted, elbow-shaped pieces called [rigatoni](https://www.britannica.com/topic/rigatoni). Ribbon types include the wide [lasagna](https://www.britannica.com/topic/lasagna) and the narrow [linguini](https://www.britannica.com/topic/linguini). Farfels are ground, granulated, or shredded. The wide variety of special shapes includes _farfalloni_ (“large butterflies”), _lancette_ (“little spears”), _fusilli_ (“spindles”), and _riccioline_ (“little curls”).

Pastas are prepared by [boiling](https://www.britannica.com/topic/boiling-punishment) and may be cooked until firm and [resilient](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/resilient) to the bite ( _[al dente](https://www.britannica.com/topic/al-dente)_) or until very tender. Prepared Italian style, they may be tossed with [butter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/butter), [cheese](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cheese), and seasoning ( [nutmeg](https://www.britannica.com/topic/nutmeg), [pepper](https://www.britannica.com/plant/black-pepper-plant)) or served with a variety of sauces— [tomato](https://www.britannica.com/plant/tomato), [cream](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cream-dairy-product), [seafood](https://www.britannica.com/topic/seafood), or meat-based mixtures such as [Bolognese sauce](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bolognese-sauce). Shaped pastas are often stuffed with [meat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/meat), cheese, spinach, or a combination of these and other ingredients. Pastas are also cooked in [soups](https://www.britannica.com/topic/soup) and are used in casseroles and other dishes that call for the use of noodles, a similar starch preparation ( _see_ [noodle](https://www.britannica.com/topic/noodle)). Uncooked pastas retain their freshness from three to six months.

[Salt](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt) is added to [pasta](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pasta) water primarily to season the pasta directly as it cooks. Aside from flavor considerations, though, adding salt to pasta water also slightly raises its [boiling point](https://www.britannica.com/science/boiling-point) in a process known as boiling point elevation. Although this change is minimal, it can help pasta cook more evenly, which is particularly useful when preparing larger batches.

Related Topics: [pasta](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pasta)_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/Why-Do-You-Salt-Pasta-Water)

Cooking pasta in salted water also has the added benefit of helping the pasta maintain its structure and firmness, or _al dente_ texture, which prevents it from becoming too soft.

**ice cream**, frozen [dairy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dairy-product) [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) and common [dessert](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dessert) made from [cream](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cream-dairy-product) or [butterfat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/butterfat), [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk), [sugar](https://www.britannica.com/science/sugar-chemical-compound), and flavorings. Frozen [custard](https://www.britannica.com/topic/custard) and French-type [ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice) creams also contain [eggs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/egg-food). Containing less air than the ice cream produced in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) (which has more butterfat), and [denser](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/denser) and more intensely flavored, is the related Italian [gelato](https://www.britannica.com/topic/gelato). Hundreds of flavors of ice cream have been devised, the most popular being [vanilla](https://www.britannica.com/plant/vanilla), [chocolate](https://www.britannica.com/topic/chocolate), and [strawberry](https://www.britannica.com/plant/strawberry). (When the three flavors are combined, it is known as Neopolitan.) Ice cream is also the basis of other desserts, such as [baked Alaska](https://www.britannica.com/topic/baked-Alaska), which consists of ice cream layered between a slice of sponge [cake](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cake) and a covering of [meringue](https://www.britannica.com/topic/meringue) that is browned in either an oven at high heat or by a butane culinary torch.

Iced desserts were introduced into Europe from the East. [Marco Polo](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Marco-Polo) brought back descriptions of fruit ices from his travels in [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China). Italian cooks developed recipes and techniques for making both [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) and milk ices; Buontalenti, one of the cooks taken to France by [Catherine de Medici](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Catherine-de-Medici), first prepared such treats for the French court. In 1686 a Sicilian, Francesco Procopio, opened a café in [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris) and began to sell ices and [sherbets](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sherbet), which became so popular that numerous other ice makers soon opened in the capital. Tortoni, owner of a café in late-18th-century Paris, is [credited](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/credited) with developing cream ices. In the United States, ice cream was served by [George Washington](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Washington), [Thomas Jefferson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thomas-Jefferson), and [Dolley Madison](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Dolley-Madison). Philadelphia became the hub of ice-cream manufacture in the United States; the ice-cream soda was invented there in 1874. The ice-cream cone, portable and self-contained, originated at the 1904 World’s Fair in St. Louis, [Missouri](https://www.britannica.com/place/Missouri-state). [San Francisco](https://www.britannica.com/place/San-Francisco-California/Industry-and-tourism#ref29190)’s Ghirardelli Square, home to the onetime chocolate factory, became famous for its hot-fudge sundaes.

Commercial ice cream is made by combining, under constant agitation, the liquid ingredients (milk, cream, syrups, etc.). These liquids are heated and combined with dry ingredients (sugar, stabilizers, dried eggs or milk) to form a mix, which is then pasteurized and homogenized. The mix is ripened for several hours in a refrigerated vat, then combined with finely chopped fruit, nuts, or other solids. The mix is agitated while being frozen in order to incorporate air and control the size of the ice crystals that are formed. The partially frozen ice cream is drawn off into packages and frozen [solid](https://www.britannica.com/science/solid-state-of-matter), or “hardened.” So-called soft-service ice cream was invented in 1939; it is served directly from the [freezing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/freezing-food-preservation) machine without being allowed to harden.

Homemade ice cream is often made with a base of boiled custard. The mix, with flavorings, is poured into a canister surrounded with ice and salt or a refrigerator unit. The contents of the canister are agitated by means of a hand crank or [electric motor](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electric-motor) until the ice cream is softly frozen. The ice cream is usually allowed to harden further in a freezer.

Top Questions


### What are the main types of dairy products made from milk?

The main types of dairy products made from [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk) include [butter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/butter), [cheese](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cheese), [ice cream](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ice-cream), [yogurt](https://www.britannica.com/topic/yogurt), and condensed milk.

### When is National Milk Day in the United States?

National Milk Day is January 11 in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States). This day commemorates the first time that [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk) was delivered in glass bottles in 1878 by Alexander Campbell of the New York Dairy Company.

### Which animal’s milk is most commonly used worldwide?

[Cow](https://www.britannica.com/animal/cow)’s [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk) is by far the principal type used throughout the world.

### What factors affect the composition of milk?

Factors affecting [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk) composition include breed, genetic constitution, age, stage of [lactation](https://www.britannica.com/science/lactation), milking interval, disease conditions, and quality of feed.

### What is lactose intolerance?

[Lactose intolerance](https://www.britannica.com/science/lactose-intolerance) is a condition in which individuals cannot metabolize [lactose](https://www.britannica.com/science/lactose) because of a deficiency in the [enzyme](https://www.britannica.com/science/enzyme) [lactase](https://www.britannica.com/science/lactase). This leads to [intestinal](https://www.britannica.com/science/intestine) distress.

### How is milk homogenized?

[Milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk) is [homogenized](https://www.britannica.com/science/homogenization) by forcing it through small openings under high pressure, reducing fat globules to less than 2 micrometers in diameter, resulting in a uniform distribution of fat throughout the [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk).

**dairy product**, [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk) and any of the [foods](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) made from [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk), including [butter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/butter), [cheese](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cheese), [ice cream](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ice-cream), [yogurt](https://www.britannica.com/topic/yogurt), and condensed and dried milk.

Milk has been used by humans since the beginning of recorded time to provide both fresh and storable nutritious foods. In some countries almost half the milk produced is [consumed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/consumed) as fresh pasteurized whole, low-fat, or skim milk. Milk was delivered in glass bottles for the first time in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) on January 11, 1878. Most milk is manufactured into more stable dairy products of worldwide commerce, such as butter, cheese, dried milk, ice cream, and condensed milk.

[Cow](https://www.britannica.com/animal/cow)’s milk (bovine species) is by far the principal type used throughout the world. Other animals utilized for their milk production include [buffalo](https://www.britannica.com/animal/buffalo-mammal) (in [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China), [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt), and the [Philippines](https://www.britannica.com/place/Philippines)), [goats](https://www.britannica.com/animal/goat) (in the Mediterranean countries), [reindeer](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reindeer) (in northern [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe)), and [sheep](https://www.britannica.com/animal/domesticated-sheep) (in southern Europe). This section focuses on the processing of cow’s milk and milk products unless otherwise noted. In general, the processing technology described for cow’s milk can be successfully applied to milk obtained from other species.

In the early 1800s the average dairy cow produced less than 1,500 liters (396 gallons) of milk annually. With advances in animal nutrition and [selective breeding](https://www.britannica.com/science/selective-breeding), one cow now produces an average of 6,500 liters (1,717 gallons) of milk a year, some cows producing up to 10,000 liters (2,641 gallons). The [Holstein-Friesian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Holstein) cow produces the greatest volume, but other breeds such as [Ayrshire](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Ayrshire-breed-of-cattle), [Brown Swiss](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Brown-Swiss), [Guernsey](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Guernsey-breed-of-cattle), and [Jersey](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Jersey-breed-of-cattle), while producing less milk, are known for supplying milk that contains higher levels of [fat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fat), [protein](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein), and total solids. _See also_ [dairy farming](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dairying).

## Nutrient composition

Although milk is a liquid and most often considered a drink, it contains between 12 and 13 percent total solids and perhaps should be regarded as a [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food). In contrast, many “solid” foods, such as tomatoes, carrots, and lettuce, contain as little as 6 percent solids.

Many factors influence the [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) of milk, including breed, genetic constitution of the individual cow, age of the cow, stage of [lactation](https://www.britannica.com/science/lactation), interval between milkings, and certain disease conditions. Since the last milk drawn at each milking is richest in fat, the completeness of milking also influences a sample. In general, the type of [feed](https://www.britannica.com/topic/feed-agriculture) only slightly affects the composition of milk, but feed of poor quality or insufficient quantity causes both a low yield and a low percentage of total solids. Current feeding programs utilize computer technology to achieve the greatest [efficiency](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/efficiency) from each animal.

The composition of milk varies among [mammals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mammal), primarily to meet growth rates of the individual species. The proteins contained within the mother’s milk are the major components contributing to the growth rate of the young animals. Human milk is relatively low in both proteins and minerals compared with that of cows and [goats](https://www.britannica.com/animal/goat).

Goat milk has about the same [nutrient](https://www.britannica.com/science/nutrient) composition as cow’s milk, but it differs in several characteristics. Goat milk is completely white in color because all the beta-carotene (ingested from feed) is converted to vitamin A. The fat globules are smaller and therefore remain suspended, so the [cream](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cream-dairy-product) does not rise and mechanical [homogenization](https://www.britannica.com/science/homogenization) is unnecessary. Goat milk [curd](https://www.britannica.com/topic/curd) forms into small, light flakes and is more easily [digested](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/digested), much like the curd formed from human milk. It is often prescribed for persons who are allergic to the proteins in cow’s milk and for some patients with [stomach ulcers](https://www.britannica.com/science/peptic-ulcer).

[Sheep](https://www.britannica.com/animal/domesticated-sheep) milk is rich in nutrients, having 18 percent total solids (5.8 percent protein and 6.5 percent fat). [Reindeer](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reindeer) milk has the highest level of nutrients, with 36.7 percent total solids (10.3 percent protein and 22 percent fat). These high-fat, high-protein milks are excellent ingredients for cheese and other manufactured dairy products.

The major components of milk are water, fat, protein, carbohydrate (lactose), and minerals (ash). However, there are numerous other highly important micronutrients such as [vitamins](https://www.britannica.com/science/vitamin), essential [amino acids](https://www.britannica.com/science/amino-acid), and trace minerals. Indeed, more than 250 chemical [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds) have been identified in milk.

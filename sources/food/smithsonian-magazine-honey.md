**honey**, [sweet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/candy-food), [viscous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/viscous) liquid [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food), dark golden in colour, produced in the honey sacs of various [bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bee) from the [nectar](https://www.britannica.com/science/nectar) of [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower). Flavour and colour are determined by the flowers from which the nectar is gathered. Some of the most commercially desirable honeys are produced from [clover](https://www.britannica.com/plant/clover-plant) by the domestic [honeybee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honeybee). The nectar is ripened into honey by inversion of the major portion of its [sucrose](https://www.britannica.com/science/sucrose) [sugar](https://www.britannica.com/science/sugar-chemical-compound) into the sugars levulose ( [fructose](https://www.britannica.com/science/fructose)) and dextrose ( [glucose](https://www.britannica.com/science/glucose)) and by the removal of excess moisture.

[![Honeycomb](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/129358-050-082EEC52/Bee-honeycomb.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/129358-050-082EEC52/Bee-honeycomb.jpg)

[Honeycomb](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/129358-050-082EEC52/Bee-honeycomb.jpg) A western honeybee ( _Apis mellifera_) on a honeycomb.(more)

Honey is stored in the beehive or nest in a [honeycomb](https://www.britannica.com/science/honeycomb-biology), a double layer of uniform hexagonal cells constructed of [beeswax](https://www.britannica.com/topic/beeswax) (secreted by the worker bees) and propolis (a plant [resin](https://www.britannica.com/science/resin) collected by the workers). Honeycomb is used in winter as food for the [larvae](https://www.britannica.com/science/larva) and other members of the colony. It is commonly sold by beekeepers as a delicacy, or the [wax](https://www.britannica.com/technology/wax) may be extracted for various purposes.

Honey contains about 18 percent [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water), is water soluble, and may granulate between 50 and 65 °F (10 and 18 °C). Somewhat [acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/acid), it has mild antiseptic properties and has been used in the treatment of [burns](https://www.britannica.com/science/burn) and [lacerations](https://www.britannica.com/science/laceration). One of the most easily [assimilated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/assimilated) foods, it is widely used in baked goods, candies, prepared fruits, cereals, and medicines.

Honey was almost the only source of sugar available to the ancients and was valued for its medicinal benefits. It was used to make [mead](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mead), a fermented beverage, and was mixed with wine and other alcoholic drinks. In [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt) it was employed as an [embalming](https://www.britannica.com/topic/embalming) material. In [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India) and other Asian countries it was used to preserve fruit and make cakes, sweetmeats, and other foods. Honey is mentioned in the [Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible) and in the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran).

Only a handful of the more than 20,000 species of [bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bee) on Earth make [honey](https://www.britannica.com/topic/honey), with [domesticated honeybees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/western-honeybee) ( _Apis mellifera_) being the major commercial source of the sweet liquid. Honeybees first collect [nectar](https://www.britannica.com/science/nectar) from [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) (and sometimes non-floral nectar sources) and store it in their “honey stomachs.” As the bees fly back to their hive, [enzymes](https://www.britannica.com/science/enzyme) in their stomachs start breaking down the nectar’s complex sugars into simpler ones, a process that is crucial for the transformation into honey. Indeed, nectar is ultimately converted into honey by the breaking down of its [sucrose](https://www.britannica.com/science/sucrose) sugar into [fructose](https://www.britannica.com/science/fructose) and [glucose](https://www.britannica.com/science/glucose), which are simpler molecules.

Related Topics: [beekeeping](https://www.britannica.com/topic/beekeeping)[honeybee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honeybee)[honey](https://www.britannica.com/topic/honey)[food chain](https://www.britannica.com/science/food-chain)[meliponiculture](https://www.britannica.com/technology/meliponiculture)_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/How-Is-Honey-Made)

Once back at the hive, the bees store the nectar in honeycomb cells made of [beeswax](https://www.britannica.com/topic/beeswax), where sucrose continues to break down and the water content is reduced. While nectar contains up to 80 percent water, honey has only about 18 percent water, making it less prone to [fermentation](https://www.britannica.com/science/fermentation) and spoilage. To promote the [evaporation](https://www.britannica.com/science/evaporation) of excess water, bees rapidly beat their wings over the filled cells until the honey reaches the desired consistency. The cells are then sealed with wax to preserve the honey for future use by the colony. Honey is a vital food source for the bees and their [larvae](https://www.britannica.com/science/larva), especially during the winter months when flowers are scarce.

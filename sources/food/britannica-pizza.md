**pizza**, dish of [Italian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy) origin consisting of a flattened disk of [bread](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bread) [dough](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dough) topped with some combination of [olive oil](https://www.britannica.com/topic/olive-oil), [oregano](https://www.britannica.com/plant/oregano), [tomato](https://www.britannica.com/plant/tomato), [olives](https://www.britannica.com/plant/olive-plant), [mozzarella](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mozzarella) or other [cheese](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cheese), and many other ingredients, baked quickly—usually, in a commercial setting, using a wood-fired oven heated to a very high temperature—and served hot.

One of the simplest and most traditional pizzas is the [Margherita](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Margherita-pizza), which is topped with tomatoes or tomato [sauce](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sauce), mozzarella, and basil. Popular [legend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legend) relates that it was named for Queen Margherita, wife of [Umberto I](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Umberto-I), who was said to have liked its mild fresh flavour and to have also noted that its topping colours—green, white, and red—were those of the [Italian flag](https://www.britannica.com/topic/flag-of-Italy).

[![pizza](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/128651-050-60CA6B6A/pizza-margherita.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/128651-050-60CA6B6A/pizza-margherita.jpg)

[pizza](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/128651-050-60CA6B6A/pizza-margherita.jpg) Neapolitan pizza Margherita.(more)

Italy has many variations of pizza. The Neapolitan pizza, or Naples-style pizza, is made specifically with buffalo mozzarella (produced from the milk of Italian Mediterranean buffalo) or _fior di latte_ (mozzarella produced from the milk of prized Agerolese cows) and with San Marzano tomatoes or _pomodorino vesuviano_ (a variety of grape tomato grown in Naples). Roman pizza often omits tomatoes (an early 16th-century import) and uses onions and olives. The [Ligurian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Liguria) pizza resembles the _pissaladière_ of [Provence](https://www.britannica.com/place/Provence-region-France) in [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), adding [anchovies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/anchovy) to olives and [onions](https://www.britannica.com/plant/onion-plant). Pizza has also spread from [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy) throughout much of the rest of the world, and, in regions outside of Italy, the toppings used vary with the ingredients available and the flavour profile preferred.

The popularity of pizza in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) began with the Italian [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) in [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City), where the Neapolitan pizza had an early influence. The first pizzeria appeared in New York City about the turn of the 20th century. After [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II) the pizza industry boomed. Soon there was scarcely a hamlet without a pizzeria. [Sausage](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sausage), [bacon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bacon), ground [beef](https://www.britannica.com/topic/beef), [pepperoni](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pepperoni), [mushrooms](https://www.britannica.com/science/mushroom), and [peppers](https://www.britannica.com/plant/pepper-plant-Capsicum-genus) are traditional toppings familiar to many Americans, but ingredients as varied as [arugula](https://www.britannica.com/plant/arugula), [pancetta](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pancetta), and [truffles](https://www.britannica.com/science/truffle-fungus) have found their way onto pizzas there. Variations are also often tied to different regions in the country, [Chicago’s deep-dish pizza](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chicago-style-pizza) and [California-style pizza](https://www.britannica.com/topic/California-style-pizza) among them.

Pizza, as we know it today, originated centuries ago in [Naples](https://www.britannica.com/place/Naples-Italy), Italy, where it was crafted as a humble dish for the working class. The classic Neapolitan pizza, known as pizza Margherita, was a thin layer of dough adorned with [tomato](https://www.britannica.com/plant/tomato) sauce, [mozzarella](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mozzarella) cheese, and [basil](https://www.britannica.com/plant/basil). This combination not only provided a delicious meal but also represented the colors of the Italian flag: red, white, and green—an observation popularly attributed to Italy’s Queen Margherita, after whom the pizza style is named.

Over time, the ingredients used in [pizza](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pizza) evolved, with variations emerging across different regions of [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy). For instance, Neapolitan pizza is known for its use of buffalo [mozzarella](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mozzarella) and San Marzano tomatoes, while Roman pizza often incorporates onions and olives. Ligurian pizza, on the other hand, adds [anchovies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/anchovy) to the mix. These regional variations highlight the adaptability of pizza to local tastes and available ingredients.

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Italian immigrants introduced pizza to the United States, where it quickly gained popularity. The first licensed pizzeria, G. Lombardi’s, opened in [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City) in 1905, marking the beginning of pizza’s widespread appeal in America. In the post- [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II) era, Italian-Americans migrated west and into suburbia, further promoting the popularity of pizza.

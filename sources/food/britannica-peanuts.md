**peanut**, ( _Arachis hypogaea_), tropical South American [legume](https://www.britannica.com/science/legume) plant widely [cultivated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultivated) for its edible seeds. The seeds, which are also known as peanuts, are a nutritionally dense [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food), rich in [protein](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) and [fat](https://www.britannica.com/science/saturated-fat). Despite its several common names, the peanut is not a true [nut](https://www.britannica.com/science/nut-plant-reproductive-body). As with other legumes, the plant adds [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen) to the soil by means of [nitrogen-fixing bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen-fixing-bacteria) and is thus particularly valuable as a soil-enriching crop.

## Physical description and geocarpy

The peanut is an [annual](https://www.britannica.com/science/annual) and can either be an erect shrubby plant, 45–60 cm (18–24 inches) high with short branches, or have a spreading form, 30–45 cm (12–18 inches) high with long branches that lie close to the [soil](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil). The stems are sturdy and hairy and bear pinnately [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) [leaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy) with two pairs of leaflets. The [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) are borne in the axils of the leaves and feature golden-yellow petals about 10 mm (0.4 inch) across. The oblong [pods](https://www.britannica.com/science/legume) have rounded ends and are most commonly 25–50 mm (1–2 inches) long with two or three seeds; the pods are contracted between the seeds and have a thin, netted, spongy shell. The [seeds](https://www.britannica.com/science/seed-plant-reproductive-part) vary from oblong to nearly round and have a papery [seed](https://www.britannica.com/science/seed-plant-reproductive-part) coat that ranges in color from whitish to dark purple.

Peanut legumes have the peculiar habit of ripening underground, a phenomenon known as [geocarpy](https://www.britannica.com/science/geocarpy). After [pollination](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollination) and the [withering](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/withering) of the flower, an unusual stalklike structure called a peg grows from the base of the flower toward the soil. The fertilized [ovules](https://www.britannica.com/science/ovule) are carried downward in the sturdy tip of the peg until the tip is well below the soil surface, at which point the peg tip starts to develop into the characteristic pod. The pegs sometimes reach down 10 cm (4 inches) or more before their tips can develop fruits. These unusual fruits appear to function as [roots](https://www.britannica.com/science/root-plant) to some degree, absorbing mineral nutrients directly from the soil. The pods may not develop properly unless the soil around them is well supplied with available [calcium](https://www.britannica.com/science/calcium), regardless of the nutrients available to the roots.

## Cultivation

Peanut growing requires at least five months of warm weather with rainfall (or irrigation equivalent) of 60 cm (24 inches) or more during the [growing season](https://www.britannica.com/topic/growing-season). The best soils are well-drained sandy [loams](https://www.britannica.com/science/loam) underlain by deep friable (easily crumbled) [loam](https://www.britannica.com/science/loam) subsoils. At harvest the entire plant, except the deeper roots, is removed from the soil. The pods are often cured by allowing the harvested plants to wilt for a day, then placing them for four to six weeks in stacks built around a sturdy stake driven upright into the soil. The pods are placed toward the inside of each stack to protect them from weather.

## Uses

[![Peanut butter and jelly](https://cdn.britannica.com/03/218403-050-99A8256D/african-american-father-and-daughter-making-peanut-butter-and-jelly-sandwich.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/03/218403-050-99A8256D/african-american-father-and-daughter-making-peanut-butter-and-jelly-sandwich.jpg)

[Peanut butter and jelly](https://cdn.britannica.com/03/218403-050-99A8256D/african-american-father-and-daughter-making-peanut-butter-and-jelly-sandwich.jpg) A father and daughter making a peanut butter and jelly sandwich, a staple of lunches for decades.(more)

Peanuts are sold boiled or roasted and are commonly used to produce an [edible oil](https://www.britannica.com/science/oil-chemical-compound) with a high smoke point. In the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) the seeds are also ground into [peanut butter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/peanut-butter) and widely used in candy and bakery products. The peanut is used extensively as feed for livestock in some places; the tops of the plants, after the pods are removed, usually are fed as [hay](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hay-animal-feed), although the entire plant may be so used. The development of some 300 [derivative](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/derivative) products from peanuts—including flour, soaps, and plastics—stems mainly from research conducted in the early 20th century by [George Washington Carver](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Washington-Carver).

**legume**, [fruit](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body) of plants in the pea family ( [Fabaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Fabaceae)). Most legumes are dehiscent fruits that release their seeds by splitting open along two seams, though some, such as [peanuts](https://www.britannica.com/plant/peanut) ( _Arachis hypogaea_) and [carobs](https://www.britannica.com/plant/carob) ( _Ceratonia siliqua_), do not naturally open. The fruits come in a variety of sizes and shapes; many, however, are long and narrow and bear their seeds in a single line. The largest legumes are borne by the monkey ladder ( _Entada gigas_) and can reach up to 2 metres (6.6 feet) in length. At maturity, legume fruits are usually dry and papery or hard and woody; the legumes of certain food crops, such as snow peas (variety of _Pisum sativum_), [edamame](https://www.britannica.com/plant/soybean) ( _Glycine max_), and [green beans](https://www.britannica.com/plant/green-bean) ( _Phaseolus vulgaris_), are harvested while still green and fleshy.

Legumes [furnish](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/furnish) food for humans and animals and provide edible oils, fibres, and raw material for plastics. Many are grown for their edible seeds, which are high in [protein](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) and contain many of the essential [amino acids](https://www.britannica.com/science/amino-acid).

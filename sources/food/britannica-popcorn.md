**popcorn**, a variety of [corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corn-plant) (maize), the kernels of which, when exposed to heat or microwaves, are exploded into large fluffy masses. The corn used for popping may be any of about 25 different varieties of _Zea mays_; the two major types are rice popcorn, in which the grains are pointed at both base and [apex](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/apex), and pearl popcorn, in which the grains are rounded and compact. A popcorn kernel has an extremely hard hull and hard outer endosperm, and within there is a mass of moist, starchy, white endosperm. The moisture is optimally about 13.5 percent. When such kernels are heated to about 400 °F (about 200 °C), the moisture in the starch turns into steam and builds up [pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/pressure) until the kernel explodes inside out into a white fluffy, irregular mass, about 20 to 40 times the original size.

Popcorn is native to the [Western Hemisphere](https://www.britannica.com/place/Western-Hemisphere). Thousand-year-old kernels of popcorn have been found by archaeologists in Peru and Utah. The first European explorers of the New World described the toasting of popcorn by the Indians for [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food), for scattering in religious ceremonies, and for wearing as decoration in the hair. Today the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) grows almost all the world’s popcorn.

### What is corn?

Corn is a tall annual [cereal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cereal) grass ( _Zea mays_) that is widely grown for its large elongated ears of starchy seeds. The seeds, which are also known as corn, are used as food for humans and livestock and as a source of [biofuel](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biofuel) and can be processed into a wide range of useful chemicals.

### When was corn first domesticated?

Corn was originally [domesticated](https://www.britannica.com/science/domestication) in Mexico by native peoples by about 9,000 years ago. They used many generations of [selective breeding](https://www.britannica.com/science/plant-breeding) to transform a wild [teosinte](https://www.britannica.com/plant/teosinte) grass with small grains into the rich source of food that is modern _Zea mays_.

### Why do corn kernels pop?

A popcorn kernel has an extremely hard hull that surrounds a mass of moist starchy [endosperm](https://www.britannica.com/science/endosperm) (the food of the embryo). When such a kernel is heated to about 400 °F (about 200 °C), the moisture in the [starch](https://www.britannica.com/science/starch) turns into steam and builds up pressure until the kernel explodes inside out into an irregular white fluffy mass about 20 to 40 times the kernel’s original size. About 25 corn varieties are suitable for popcorn.

**corn**, ( _Zea mays_), [cereal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cereal) plant of the grass family ( [Poaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Poaceae)) and its edible [grain](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cereal). The domesticated [crop](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crop-agriculture) originated in the Americas and is one of the most widely distributed of the world’s [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) crops. Corn is used as human food, [livestock](https://www.britannica.com/animal/livestock) feed, as [biofuel](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biofuel), and as raw material in industry.

## Domestication and history

[![Milpa farming](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/259109-050-8233E80A/Milpa-agriculture-corn-beans-squash-mexico-farm-worker-three-sisters.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/259109-050-8233E80A/Milpa-agriculture-corn-beans-squash-mexico-farm-worker-three-sisters.jpg)

[Milpa farming](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/259109-050-8233E80A/Milpa-agriculture-corn-beans-squash-mexico-farm-worker-three-sisters.jpg) A farmer holding beans and corn harvested from a traditional milpa in Michoacán, Mexico.(more)

Corn was first domesticated by native peoples in southern [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico) about 10,000 years ago. Modern corn is believed to have been derived from the Balsas [teosinte](https://www.britannica.com/plant/teosinte) ( _Zea mays parviglumis_), a wild [grass](https://www.britannica.com/plant/grass). Its [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) had spread as far north as southern Maine by the time of European settlement of [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America), and Native Americans taught European colonists to grow the grains. In Mesoamerica and much of North America, [Indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Indigenous) peoples have long interplanted corn, [squash](https://www.britannica.com/plant/squash), and [beans](https://www.britannica.com/plant/common-bean) in a productive and complimentary agricultural system known as [milpa](https://www.britannica.com/science/three-sisters), or the Three Sisters. Given its importance and ancient history, corn remains a culturally significant plant for many Native peoples throughout the Americas.

Since its introduction into [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) by [Christopher Columbus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Christopher-Columbus) and other explorers and colonizers, corn has spread to all areas of the world suitable to its cultivation. It is grown from 58° N latitude in [Canada](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canada) and [Russia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Russia) to 40° S latitude in [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America), with a corn crop maturing somewhere in the world nearly every month of the year. It is the most important crop in the United States and is a [staple](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/staple) food in many places.

The corn plant is a tall [annual](https://www.britannica.com/science/annual) grass with a stout, erect, solid stem. The large narrow [leaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy) have wavy margins and are spaced alternately on opposite sides of the stem. Staminate (male) [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) are borne on the tassel terminating the main axis of the stem. The pistillate (female) inflorescences, which mature to become the edible ears, are spikes with a thickened axis, [bearing](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/bearing) paired spikelets in longitudinal rows; each row of paired spikelets normally produces two rows of grain. Varieties of yellow and white corn are the most popular as food, though there are varieties with red, blue, pink, and black kernels, often banded, spotted, or striped. Each ear is enclosed by modified leaves called shucks or husks.

## Types of corn

[![Dent corn](https://cdn.britannica.com/24/191624-050-88053885/Feed-corn-harvest-field-Kings-Hill-County.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/24/191624-050-88053885/Feed-corn-harvest-field-Kings-Hill-County.jpg)

[Dent corn](https://cdn.britannica.com/24/191624-050-88053885/Feed-corn-harvest-field-Kings-Hill-County.jpg) Used for animal feed, dent corn, is ready for harvest after drying in the field. (more)

Commercial classifications, based mainly on kernel texture, include [dent corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/dent-corn), flint corn, [flour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/flour) corn, sweet corn, and [popcorn](https://www.britannica.com/topic/popcorn). Dent corn, primarily grown as [animal feed](https://www.britannica.com/topic/feed-agriculture) and for food manufacturing, is characterized by a depression in the crown of the kernel caused by unequal drying of the hard and soft [starch](https://www.britannica.com/science/starch) making up the kernel. [Flint corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/flint-corn), containing little soft starch, has no depression; it is used for decoration and is eaten as [hominy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hominy) in the Americas. Flour corn, composed largely of soft starch, has soft, mealy, easily ground kernels and is an important source of [corn flour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cornstarch).

[Sweet corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/sweet-corn), commonly sold fresh, frozen, or canned as a [vegetable](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vegetable), has [succulent](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/succulent), translucent seeds. The plant [sugar](https://www.britannica.com/science/sugar-chemical-compound) is not converted to starch as in other types, giving the grain its sweet flavor.

## Genetically modified corn

[![Genetically engineered corn (maize)](https://cdn.britannica.com/61/131361-050-1A1D205E/corn.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/61/131361-050-1A1D205E/corn.jpg)

[Genetically engineered corn (maize)](https://cdn.britannica.com/61/131361-050-1A1D205E/corn.jpg) Plants can be genetically engineered to acquire traits that are not naturally present or are inefficiently expressed.(more)

Many industrial and feedstock varieties of corn are [genetically modified organisms](https://www.britannica.com/science/genetically-modified-organism) (GMOs) engineered for resistance to the [herbicide](https://www.britannica.com/science/herbicide) [glyphosate](https://www.britannica.com/technology/glyphosate) or to produce proteins from [_Bacillus thuringiensis_](https://www.britannica.com/science/Bacillus-thuringiensis) (Bt) to kill specific insect pests. In addition, some strains have been genetically engineered for greater [drought](https://www.britannica.com/science/drought) [tolerance](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/tolerance) and to increase their nutritional value. Most of the corn grown in the United States is GMO, which may reduce the need for herbicides and insecticides.

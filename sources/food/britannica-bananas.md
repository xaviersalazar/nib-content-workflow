Top Questions


### What are the different types of bananas?

There are hundreds of varieties of banana, differing in size, color, texture, and flavor, but they are broadly classified into dessert bananas and plantains. Dessert bananas, such as Cavendish, Gros Michel, Lady Finger, and red bananas, are sweet and generally eaten raw and fresh. Plantains are starchier and less sweet and are typically cooked at various stages of ripeness. Plantains are a staple food crop for millions of people in tropical regions worldwide.

### Do bananas have seeds?

Although wild bananas are studded with large inedible seeds, modern domesticated bananas do not have functional seeds and are essentially sterile. Instead, they reproduce through suckers or shoots that arise from the rhizome after the main plant has fruited and been cut down. This means that banana plants are clones of one another and are thus especially vulnerable to diseases.

Bananas are thought to have been gradually domesticated in New Guinea at least 6,500 years ago.

**banana**, domesticated [fruit](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body) of the [genus](https://www.britannica.com/science/genus-taxon) _Musa_, one of the most important fruit crops of the world. There are hundreds of varieties of bananas, with plantains accounting for about 85 percent of all banana cultivation [worldwide](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/worldwide). Indeed, nearly all bananas are grown and consumed in the tropics, where they are a staple [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) crop. [Cavendish](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Cavendish-banana) bananas, a type of dessert banana, are by far the most common variety imported by nontropical countries. The banana is valued worldwide for its flavor, nutritional value, and availability throughout the year.

## Types and uses

[![Mofongo](https://cdn.britannica.com/69/272569-050-591AED2F/Spicy-mofongo-with-plantains-garlic-and-chicharron.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/69/272569-050-591AED2F/Spicy-mofongo-with-plantains-garlic-and-chicharron.jpg)

[Mofongo](https://cdn.britannica.com/69/272569-050-591AED2F/Spicy-mofongo-with-plantains-garlic-and-chicharron.jpg) A mound of Puerto Rican mofongo, made of fried plantains, garlic and chicharrón (pork cracklings), served with meat.(more)

Dessert bananas are most commonly eaten fresh and ripe, though they may be fried or mashed and chilled in pies or puddings. They may also be used to flavor muffins, cakes, or breads. Cooking varieties, or [plantains](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plantain), are starchy rather than sweet and are boiled or fried when ripe or immature. The fruits can also be ground into a flour used to make dumplings (e.g., [fufu](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fufu)) or porridges. Banana flowers are also edible and are popular in [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) and [Vietnam](https://www.britannica.com/place/Vietnam). A ripe banana contains as much as 22 percent of [carbohydrate](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbohydrate) and is high in [dietary fiber](https://www.britannica.com/science/dietary-fiber), [potassium](https://www.britannica.com/science/potassium), [manganese](https://www.britannica.com/science/manganese), and vitamins [B6](https://www.britannica.com/science/vitamin-B6) and [C](https://www.britannica.com/science/vitamin-C).

[![Banana leaves](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/274509-050-F3E82775/overhead-shot-honduran-tamales-made-with-banana-leaves-food.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/274509-050-F3E82775/overhead-shot-honduran-tamales-made-with-banana-leaves-food.jpg)

[Banana leaves](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/274509-050-F3E82775/overhead-shot-honduran-tamales-made-with-banana-leaves-food.jpg) A person preparing tamales wrapped in banana leaves in Honduras.(more)

In addition to their nutritional importance, various parts of banana plants are used for a variety of other purposes across [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures). The large, flexible leaves are commonly used as natural wrappers for [cooking](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cooking), steaming, and storing food, as well as makeshift plates or linings for pots and cooking pits. The plant’s false trunk yields strong fibers that have long been woven into textiles, ropes, mats, and paper, notably in [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) and other parts of [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia), while dried fronds can be fashioned into roofing materials or fabrics. Banana peels have been applied in traditional medicine for their anti-inflammatory and antiseptic properties, particularly in treating minor wounds. More recently, banana peels have been processed into [biodegradable](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biodegradability) [plastics](https://www.britannica.com/science/plastic) and films for food packaging.

## History

[![Wild banana](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/131151-004-6CD1FCD7/unripened-fruit-from-wild-banana-tree.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/131151-004-6CD1FCD7/unripened-fruit-from-wild-banana-tree.jpg)

[Wild banana](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/131151-004-6CD1FCD7/unripened-fruit-from-wild-banana-tree.jpg) The unripened fruit from a wild banana tree ( _Musa_).(more)

Bananas are thought to have been gradually [domesticated](https://www.britannica.com/science/domestication) in [New Guinea](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Guinea) at least 6,500 years ago. Their [consumption](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consumption) is mentioned in early Greek, Latin, and Arabic writings; [Alexander the Great](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alexander-the-Great) saw bananas on an expedition to [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India). Bananas had likely reached Africa from Asia around 2,000 years ago, though [recent archaeological evidence](https://www.science.org/content/article/early-africans-went-bananas) suggests the fruits may have been harvested by humans in [Uganda](https://www.britannica.com/place/Uganda) as early as 4,500 years ago. Shortly after Europeans landed in the Americas, bananas were taken from the [Canary Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canary-Islands) to the New World, where they were first established in Hispaniola and soon spread to other islands and the mainland. Enslaved Africans also brought bananas with them to the New World, and many plantain-based recipes in those regions have roots in African foodstuffs. Cultivation increased until bananas became a [staple](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/staple) crop in many regions, and in the 19th century they began to appear in the markets of the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States).

## Physical description

[![Banana plants](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/157964-050-CD0D89CB/Banana-plants-plantation-fruit-bunch-herbaceous-trunk.jpg?w=300)\\
1 of 2](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/157964-050-CD0D89CB/Banana-plants-plantation-fruit-bunch-herbaceous-trunk.jpg)

[Banana plants](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/157964-050-CD0D89CB/Banana-plants-plantation-fruit-bunch-herbaceous-trunk.jpg) Banana plants growing on a plantation. Each herbaceous trunk bears only one bunch of fruit and is cut down after harvest to encourage new growth from the rhizome (underground stem).(more)

[![Banana inflorescence](https://cdn.britannica.com/61/175661-050-6D553500/banana-inflorescence-Thailand-bract-curls-flowers-clusters.jpg?w=300)\\
2 of 2](https://cdn.britannica.com/61/175661-050-6D553500/banana-inflorescence-Thailand-bract-curls-flowers-clusters.jpg)

[Banana inflorescence](https://cdn.britannica.com/61/175661-050-6D553500/banana-inflorescence-Thailand-bract-curls-flowers-clusters.jpg) A banana inflorescence in Thailand. Each purple-red bract curls, exposing the clusters of pistillate (female) flowers underneath.(more)

The banana plant is a gigantic herb that springs from an underground stem, or [rhizome](https://www.britannica.com/science/rhizome), to form a false trunk 3–6 meters (10–20 feet) high. This trunk is composed of the basal portions of leaf sheaths and is crowned with a rosette of 10 to 20 oblong to elliptic leaves that sometimes attain a length of 3–3.5 meters (10–11.5 feet) and a breadth of 65 cm (26 inches). A large flower spike, carrying numerous yellowish [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) protected by large purple-red bracts, emerges at the top of the false trunk and bends downward to become bunches of 50 to 150 individual [fruits](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body), or fingers. The individual fruits, or bananas, are grouped in [clusters](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/clusters), or hands, of 10 to 20.

After a plant has fruited, it is cut down to the ground, because each trunk produces only one bunch of fruit. The dead trunk is replaced by others in the form of suckers, or shoots, which arise from the rhizome at roughly six-month intervals. The life of a single rhizome thus continues for many years, and the weaker suckers that it sends up through the soil are periodically pruned, while the stronger ones are allowed to grow into fruit-producing plants.

## Cultivation and disease susceptibility

[![Banana plantation](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/140708-050-1AC574C6/Banana-plantation-Guadeloupe.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/140708-050-1AC574C6/Banana-plantation-Guadeloupe.jpg)

[Banana plantation](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/140708-050-1AC574C6/Banana-plantation-Guadeloupe.jpg) A commercial banana plantation in Guadeloupe.(more)

Banana plants thrive naturally on deep, loose, well-drained [soils](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil) in humid tropical climates, and they are grown successfully under [irrigation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation) in such semiarid regions as southern Jamaica. Suckers and divisions of the rhizome are used as planting material; the first crop ripens within 10 to 15 months, and thereafter fruit production is more or less [continuous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/continuous). Frequent [pruning](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pruning) is required to remove surplus growth and prevent crowding in a banana plantation.

Desirable commercial bunches of bananas consist of nine hands or more and weigh 22–65 kg (49–143 pounds). Three hundred or more such bunches may be produced annually on one acre of land and are harvested before they fully ripen on the plant. For export, the desired degree of maturity attained before harvest depends upon distance from market and type of transportation, and ripening is frequently induced artificially after shipment by exposure to [ethylene](https://www.britannica.com/science/ethylene) gas. (Bananas and many other fruits naturally produce ethylene gas to promote fruit ripening.) Developing bunches of bananas are often bagged to protect them from insects and birds, from wind abrasion, and to keep them clean for market.

[![Gros Michel banana](https://cdn.britannica.com/56/194356-050-1F919D81/bananas-Gros-Michel.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/56/194356-050-1F919D81/bananas-Gros-Michel.jpg)

[Gros Michel banana](https://cdn.britannica.com/56/194356-050-1F919D81/bananas-Gros-Michel.jpg) Once the dominant desert banana, the delicious Gros Michel was decimated by Panama disease and is no longer cultivated on a large scale.(more)

Given that each banana variety is [propagated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagated) clonally, there is very little genetic [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity) in the domesticated plants. This makes bananas especially [vulnerable](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vulnerable) to pests and diseases, as a novel pathogen or pest could quickly decimate a variety if it were to exploit a genetic weakness among the clones. Indeed, this very phenomenon occurred in the late 1950s with the [Gros Michel](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Gros-Michel) dessert variety, which had dominated the world’s commercial banana business. Richer and sweeter than the modern Cavendish, the Gros Michel fell victim to an invading soil [fungus](https://www.britannica.com/science/fungus) that causes [Panama disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/Panama-disease), a form of [Fusarium wilt](https://www.britannica.com/science/fusarium-wilt). Powerless to breed resistance into the sterile clones and unable to rid the [soil](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil) of the fungus, farmers were soon forced to abandon the Gros Michel in favor of the hardier Cavendish. Although the Cavendish has thus far been resistant to such a pestilent invasion, its lack of genetic diversity leaves it equally vulnerable to evolving pathogens and pests. Indeed, a strain of Panama disease known as Tropical Race (TR) 4 has been a threat to the Cavendish since the 1990s, and many scientists worry that the Cavendish too will eventually go [extinct](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology).

Bananas are vulnerable to several other fungal diseases, including black sigatoka and [anthracnose](https://www.britannica.com/science/anthracnose). They are also susceptible to viral infections, including banana bunchy top virus and banana [mosaic virus](https://www.britannica.com/science/mosaic-plant-disease), and bacterial diseases such as banana bacterial [wilt](https://www.britannica.com/science/wilt). Soil [nematodes](https://www.britannica.com/animal/nematode) can also be problematic. _See also_ [list of plant diseases](https://www.britannica.com/topic/list-of-plant-diseases-2033263).

## Nomenclature

[![Bananas ripening](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/194358-050-91CE9CB6/Gros-Michel-banana-tree.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/194358-050-91CE9CB6/Gros-Michel-banana-tree.jpg)

[Bananas ripening](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/194358-050-91CE9CB6/Gros-Michel-banana-tree.jpg) Large bunch of ripening bananas.(more)

Although there are hundreds of varieties of bananas in cultivation, their [taxonomy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/taxonomy) has been [contentious](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contentious) because of their ancient [domestication](https://www.britannica.com/science/domestication), sterility, hybridization, and the use of [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) common names to refer to the same variety. As most [cultivated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultivated) varieties of bananas are either interspecific hybrids of _Musa acuminata_ and _M. balbisiana_ or hybrids of the subspecies of _M. acuminata_, a genome-based system has led to an overhaul of the [nomenclature](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nomenclature) of domesticated bananas. Unlike most plants, these varieties are identified by their ploidy (number of sets of [chromosomes](https://www.britannica.com/science/chromosome)) and parent plant rather than traditional binomial [designations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/designations). A system of letters (“A,” “B,” or “AB”) represents the parent plant(s), with a given letter repeated to indicate the ploidy. The popular Cavendish, for example, is referred to as AAA ‘Dwarf Cavendish,’ where “AAA” signifies its triploidy (three sets of chromosomes) as well as its derivation from _M. acuminata_.

**Panama disease**, a devastating [disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/disease) of [bananas](https://www.britannica.com/plant/banana-plant) caused by the soil-inhabiting fungus species _[Fusarium oxysporum](https://www.britannica.com/science/Fusarium-oxysporum)_ forma specialis _cubense_. A form of [fusarium wilt](https://www.britannica.com/science/fusarium-wilt), Panama disease is [widespread](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/widespread) throughout the tropics and can be found wherever susceptible [banana](https://www.britannica.com/plant/banana-plant) [cultivars](https://www.britannica.com/science/cultivar) are grown. Notoriously difficult to control, the disease decimated global plantations of the [Gros Michel](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Gros-Michel) banana in the 1950s and ’60s, which had dominated the commercial industry until its downfall. Its replacement, the modern [Cavendish](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Cavendish-banana), has been threatened with a strain of the disease known as Tropical Race (TR) 4 since the 1990s; in 2019 TR 4 was confirmed in [Colombia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Colombia), marking the first appearance of the strain in the Americas.

The _Fusarium_ fungus invades young [roots](https://www.britannica.com/science/root-plant) or root bases, often through wounds. Some infections progress into the [rhizome](https://www.britannica.com/science/rhizome) (rootlike stem), followed by rapid invasion of the rootstock and leaf bases. Spread occurs through vascular [bundles](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/bundles), which become discoloured brown or dark red, and finally purplish or black. The outer edges of older leaves turn yellow. Within a month or two, all but the youngest leaves turn yellow, [wilt](https://www.britannica.com/science/wilt), collapse, and hang downward, covering the trunk (pseudostem) with dead brown leaves. All aboveground parts are eventually killed, although fresh shoots may form at the base. These later wilt and the entire plant dies, usually within several years. The _Fusarium_ fungus then continues to thrive in surrounding [soil](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil), preventing the success of future plantings.

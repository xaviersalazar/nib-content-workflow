**salt (NaCl)**, [mineral](https://www.britannica.com/science/mineral-chemical-compound) substance of great importance to human and animal health, as well as to industry. The mineral form [halite](https://www.britannica.com/science/halite), or rock salt, is sometimes called common salt to distinguish it from a class of [chemical compounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound) called [salts](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt-acid-base-reactions).

Properties of common salt are shown in the [Click Here to see full-size table](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/3880-004-0A462C72/Properties-Salt.jpg) ![properties of salt](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/3880-004-0A462C72/Properties-Salt.jpg)table. Salt is essential to the health of both people and animals. Table salt, used universally as a seasoning, is fine-grained and of high purity. To ensure that this hygroscopic (i.e., water-attracting) substance will remain free-flowing when exposed to the atmosphere, small quantities of [sodium](https://www.britannica.com/science/sodium) aluminosilicate, tricalcium phosphate, or [magnesium](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnesium) silicate are added. [Iodized salt](https://www.britannica.com/topic/iodized-salt)—that is, salt to which small quantities of [potassium](https://www.britannica.com/science/potassium) iodide have been added—is widely used in areas where [iodine](https://www.britannica.com/science/iodine) is lacking from the diet, a [deficiency](https://www.britannica.com/science/iodine-deficiency) that can cause swelling of the [thyroid gland](https://www.britannica.com/science/thyroid-gland), commonly called [goitre](https://www.britannica.com/science/goitre). Livestock also require salt; it is often made available in solid blocks.

The meat-packing, sausage-making, fish-curing, and food-processing industries use salt as a [preservative](https://www.britannica.com/topic/preservative) or [seasoning](https://www.britannica.com/technology/seasoning-treatment-process) or both. It is employed for curing and preserving hides and as a brine for refrigeration.

In the [chemical industry](https://www.britannica.com/technology/chemical-industry), salt is required in the manufacture of sodium bicarbonate (baking soda), sodium hydroxide (caustic soda), [hydrochloric acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrochloric-acid), [chlorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/chlorine), and many other chemicals. When applied to [snow](https://www.britannica.com/science/snow-weather) or [ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice), salt lowers the [melting point](https://www.britannica.com/science/melting-point) of the mixture. Thus, large amounts are used in northern climates to help rid thoroughfares of accumulated snow and ice. Salt is used in water-softening equipment that removes [calcium](https://www.britannica.com/science/calcium) and magnesium compounds from water.

## History of use

In some parts of the Western Hemisphere and in India, the use of salt was introduced by Europeans, but in parts of central Africa it is still a luxury available only to the rich. Where people live mainly on milk and raw or roasted meat (so that its natural salts are not lost), sodium chloride supplements are unnecessary; nomads with their flocks of sheep or herds of cattle, for example, never eat salt with their food. On the other hand, people who live mostly on cereal, vegetable, or boiled meat diets require supplements of salt.

The habitual use of salt is intimately connected with the advance from [nomadic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/nomadism) to agricultural life, a step in civilization that profoundly influenced the [rituals](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ceremonial-object) and cults of almost all ancient nations. The gods were worshipped as the givers of the kindly fruits of the earth, and salt was usually included in sacrificial offerings consisting wholly or partly of cereal elements. Such offerings were prevalent among the [Greeks](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Greece) and [Romans](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Rome) and among a number of the Semitic peoples.

[Covenants](https://www.britannica.com/topic/covenant-religion) were ordinarily made over a [sacrificial](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacrifice-religion) meal, in which salt was a necessary element. The [preservative](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food-preservation) qualities of salt made it a peculiarly fitting symbol of an enduring compact, sealing it with an obligation to fidelity. The word _salt_ thus acquired connotations of high esteem and honour in ancient and modern languages. Examples include the Arab avowal “There is salt between us,” the Hebrew expression “to eat the salt of the palace,” and the modern Persian phrase _namak ḥarām_, “untrue to salt” (i.e., disloyal or ungrateful). In English the term “salt of the earth” describes a person held in high esteem.

Salt contributes greatly to our knowledge of the ancient highways of commerce. One of the oldest [roads](https://www.britannica.com/technology/road) in Italy is the Via Salaria (Salt Route) over which Roman salt from [Ostia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ostia) was carried into other parts of Italy. [Herodotus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Herodotus-Greek-historian) tells of a [caravan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/caravan-desert-transport) route that united the salt oases of the Libyan Desert. The ancient trade between the [Aegean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Aegean-Sea) and the [Black Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Black-Sea) coast of southern Russia was largely dependent on the salt pans (ponds for evaporating seawater to obtain salt) at the mouth of the [Dnieper River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dnieper-River) and on the salt fish brought from this district.

China, the United States, India, Germany, Canada, and Australia are the world’s largest salt producers in the early 21st century.

## [Seawater](https://www.britannica.com/science/seawater)

[![Why is seawater so salty?](https://cdn.britannica.com/81/179581-138-731DA04C/explanation-seawater.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/explanation-seawater/-193380)

Why is seawater so salty?An explanation of why seawater is salty.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt/images-videos)

Though the material that gives [seawater](https://www.britannica.com/science/seawater) its salty flavour is composed of many substances, sodium chloride, or common salt, is by far the predominant compound. On the assumption that 1 gallon (about 4 litres) of seawater contains 0.231 pound (about 105 grams) of salt and that rock salt on the average is 2.17 times as dense as water, it has been estimated that if the oceans of the world were completely dried up, they would yield at least 4.5 million cubic miles of rock salt, or about 14.5 times the bulk of the entire continent of Europe above the high-water mark.

[![map of ocean salinity, 2011](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/152739-050-DB157F64/map-ocean-salinity-Aquarius-SAC-D-spacecraft-September-2011.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/152739-050-DB157F64/map-ocean-salinity-Aquarius-SAC-D-spacecraft-September-2011.jpg)

[map of ocean salinity, 2011](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/152739-050-DB157F64/map-ocean-salinity-Aquarius-SAC-D-spacecraft-September-2011.jpg) The first map of ocean salinity taken by the Aquarius/SAC-D spacecraft, August–September 2011.(more)

Seawater contains on the average about 3 percent salt, although the actual concentration varies from about 1 percent (in the polar seas) to 5 percent. Enclosed waters such as the [Mediterranean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea) and [Red seas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Red-Sea) contain a higher proportion of salt than does the open ocean at the same [latitude](https://www.britannica.com/science/latitude). Irrespective of the source of the seawater, salt obtained by the evaporation of seawater has the following composition: sodium chloride 77.76 percent, [magnesium](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnesium) chloride 10.88 percent, magnesium sulfate 4.74 percent, [calcium](https://www.britannica.com/science/calcium) sulfate 3.60 percent, [potassium](https://www.britannica.com/science/potassium) chloride 2.46 percent, magnesium bromide 0.22 percent, and calcium carbonate 0.34 percent.

## Natural [brines](https://www.britannica.com/science/brine)

[Brine](https://www.britannica.com/science/brine) is water containing a high concentration of salt. Natural brines of commercial importance are found in the [Dead Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dead-Sea) as well as in Austria, France, Germany, India, the United States, and the United Kingdom. Salt in brines is nearly always accompanied by chlorides and [sulfates](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfate) of potassium, calcium, and magnesium; [carbonates](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbonate) and the [element](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) [bromine](https://www.britannica.com/science/bromine) often are present as well.

[![Dead Sea](https://cdn.britannica.com/22/5922-050-171239AA/Archaea-environments-range-salt-deposits-shores-Dead.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/22/5922-050-171239AA/Archaea-environments-range-salt-deposits-shores-Dead.jpg)

[Dead\\
Sea](https://cdn.britannica.com/22/5922-050-171239AA/Archaea-environments-range-salt-deposits-shores-Dead.jpg) Salt deposits on the southwestern shore of the Dead Sea near Masada, Israel.(more)

The [Dead Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dead-Sea), which covers an area of 1,020 square km (394 square miles), contains approximately 12,650,000,000 tons of salt. The [Jordan River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jordan-River), which contains only 35 parts of salt per 100,000 parts of [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water), adds 850,000 tons of salt to this total each year.

The concentration of salts in the Dead Sea varies from 270 to 300 parts per thousand to a depth of 40 metres (130 feet); it increases gradually from 40 to 100 metres (130 to 330) feet and remains a fairly constant 332 parts per thousand below 100 metres. Dead Sea water is relatively free from sulfates and has a high proportion of potassium and bromine. Because atmospheric conditions favour evaporation by sunlight (solar evaporation) for about eight months of the year, the production of salt, potassium, and bromine is feasible in the Dead Sea area. The process used for recovery of salt and potash is similar to that described below under [Salt manufacture](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt/Salt-manufacture#ref53235). The Indian brines at Khārāghoda resemble seawater in the character of their dissolved salts but are much more concentrated and in some cases virtually saturated; that is, they have dissolved all the salt they can.

Certain natural brines occurring in the [United Kingdom](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-Kingdom) and the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) are of special interest because they contain [salts](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt-acid-base-reactions), such as the chlorides of [barium](https://www.britannica.com/science/barium) and [strontium](https://www.britannica.com/science/strontium), that are not usually found in brines. Special processing methods are required to produce salt from such brines. In Britain these unusual brines are found at great depths during test drillings for [petroleum](https://www.britannica.com/science/petroleum), while in the United States such brines occur in deep wells in several places.

## Rock salt

Rock salt is crystalline sodium chloride, called [halite](https://www.britannica.com/science/halite) by mineralogists. It occurs widely in the form of rock masses and beds and is abundant in rocks from all geologic periods. Because of its great solubility in water, it occurs under extremely thick cover in humid regions but lies close to the surface in arid regions.

All major rock salt deposits originated from the evaporation of [seawater](https://www.britannica.com/science/seawater) at some time during the geologic past. Approximately 78 percent of the mineral matter in normal seawater is sodium chloride. Upon evaporation of about nine-tenths of the volume of seawater, rock salt is precipitated. Calcium sulfate ( [gypsum](https://www.britannica.com/science/gypsum) and [anhydrite](https://www.britannica.com/science/anhydrite)) and potassium and magnesium salts also are precipitated. Deposits are found in beds from a few feet to many hundreds of feet thick. The ages of these beds range through much of geologic time. Because evaporation of a large quantity of seawater leaves only a small amount of salt, it is theorized that many extremely thick rock salt beds were deposited in partly enclosed arms of the seas in which evaporation was greater than the inflow of salt water. A barrier on the seafloor at the entrance to the basin prevented the outflow of the concentrated saline water.

Such bedded salt deposits occur in the Punjab [Salt Range](https://www.britannica.com/place/Salt-Range) in Pakistan and in Iran; however, these deposits have been little exploited. Similar deposits in the United States and Canada are worked extensively for both industrial and domestic use. Other important salt deposits, usually classified by the age of the surrounding rock, are found in Germany, Nova Scotia, the sub- [Carpathian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Carpathian-Mountains) region extending from Poland through Hungary and Romania, and the province of Sichuan in China, where salt wells have been in existence for more than 2,000 years.

Another economically important type of rock salt deposit is the [salt domes](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt-dome), which were formed when earth pressure forced up plugs of rock salt measuring approximately a mile across. The domes appear to result from pressure, which pushes the salt up through the rocks from depths as great as 50,000 feet (15,000 metres). Many domes occur at shallow depths and are extensively mined. Domes in the sub-Carpathian region of Europe have been worked since ancient times. The North German Plain has many extensively mined domes, which are thought to have originated below 6,000 feet; domes also are abundant along the U.S. Gulf Coast. Rock salt may be obtained from domes by the usual mining methods or by drilling wells into the salt strata and pumping water down to dissolve the salt; the brine is then returned to the surface, where it is processed like natural brine.

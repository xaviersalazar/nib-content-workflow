- What is ancient Egyptian religion?







Ancient Egyptian religion was polytheistic, with a vast array of deities, including animal-headed figures and mixed forms. The king held a unique position as a mediator between humans and gods. Key deities included the sun god (Re) and Osiris, god of the underworld. Religion permeated all aspects of life, encompassing the worship of gods, contact with the dead, magic, and divination.



The Egyptians believed the cosmos was ordered but surrounded by chaos, and maintaining this order was the king's primary role. Their beliefs are known primarily from elite sources, with less information available about the practices of the general population. Mortuary practices, including mummification, were central, aiming to ensure a successful afterlife. Festivals played a significant role, often celebrating seasonal renewal and reenacting myths.

- Who were some important gods and goddesses in ancient Egyptian religion?







Ancient Egyptian religion featured a vast pantheon of gods and goddesses, with their characteristics evolving over time. Among the most significant deities were:



  - **Osiris:** God of the underworld, death, resurrection, and the Nile's fertility.
  - **Isis:** A powerful goddess embodying wifehood, motherhood, and magic, who resurrected Osiris.
  - **Horus:** The falcon-headed sky god, associated with divine kingship and the son of Isis and Osiris.
  - **Seth:** God of chaos, violence, deserts, and storms, known for murdering Osiris.
  - **Ra:** The primary sun god, often depicted with a hawk's head, who traveled across the sky daily.
  - **Anubis:** God of funerary rites and the dead, represented as a jackal or a man with a jackal's head.
  - **Thoth:** God of writing, wisdom, and the moon, credited with inventing language.
  - **Hathor:** Goddess of motherhood, fertility, and protection, often depicted as a cow or a woman with a cow's head.
  - **Ptah:** A creator god associated with craftsmen and builders, worshipped in Memphis.
  - **Bastet:** A cat goddess, initially depicted with a lion's head, later evolving into a domestic cat form.
  - **Amon:** A god of the air, who rose to national importance and was often merged with the sun god Re.

- What did ancient Egyptians believe happened to people after they died?







Ancient Egyptians believed that after death, individuals embarked on a journey to the afterlife. This passage was facilitated by ensuring the body's preservation through [mummification](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mummy), as they believed the body needed to remain intact for the deceased to live in the next world. The afterlife could be envisioned in various locations, such as near the tomb, in the celestial regions with the sun god, or in the underworld, the domain of [Osiris](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Osiris-Egyptian-god). A crucial part of this journey was the [judgment](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Last-Judgment-religion) after death, where the deceased's life was assessed. Those who passed this judgment would enjoy an ideal existence, while those who failed would "die a second time".

- How did pharaohs connect to religion in ancient Egypt?







In ancient Egypt, pharaohs were considered divine rulers and mediators between the gods and humanity. They were believed to possess sacred powers and were responsible for maintaining _maat_, the god-given order of the cosmos. The pharaoh was the chief priest of the land, presiding over festivals and making offerings to the gods, with priests acting as his representatives. This divine status legitimized their rule and their role in preserving order against chaos. After death, the pharaoh was identified with the god Osiris and became divine, passing on sacred powers to his successor.

- What kinds of religious rituals or ceremonies did Egyptians practice?







Ancient Egyptian religious rituals and ceremonies were multifaceted, encompassing daily temple cults, grand festivals, and elaborate funerary practices. Daily temple rituals involved the purification, dressing, and feeding of a deity's image, symbolizing the maintenance of cosmic order through reciprocity between humans and gods.



Festivals offered opportunities for more direct interaction, with processions where deities' images were carried among the people. These events often included communal celebrations and reenactments of myths. Funerary practices were central, with mummification aimed at preserving the body for the afterlife. The journey to the next world was believed to involve trials, including a judgment of the deceased's life. Offerings of food, drink, and sacrifices were made to deities and the dead. Seasonal renewal festivals, such as those dedicated to Osiris, Amon-Re, Horus, and Hathor, were also significant, often involving feasting, sacrifices, and ritual dances.

- How did temples and priests help people worship in ancient Egyptian religion?







In ancient Egyptian religion, temples served as the earthly homes for deities, where priests performed daily rituals of tending to divine images. These rituals, including purification, dressing, and feeding the gods, were crucial for maintaining reciprocity between the divine and human realms, thereby sustaining the cosmos. While the daily cult was largely managed by a select group of priests, festivals offered opportunities for broader public interaction. During these festivals, the divine images were brought out, allowing people to witness and sometimes even ask questions of the gods, with responses potentially given through the movement of the barque carrying the image.



The priesthood, initially part-time, grew in importance. Priests adhered to strict rules of purity and abstinence while performing their duties. This system, in earlier periods, allowed more people to be involved in the cult and gain access to the temple. Women also participated, particularly in the cult of Hathor, and some held the title of "chantress". Temples themselves were constructed as microcosms, symbolizing the ordered world. Beyond the formal temple cult, people also engaged with the divine through votive offerings, personal names with religious content, and consultations with oracles, demonstrating a reciprocal relationship between individuals and the gods.

## Nature and significance

Egyptian religious beliefs and practices were closely [integrated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrated) into Egyptian society of the historical period (from _c._ 3000 bce). Although there were probably many survivals from prehistory, these may be relatively unimportant for understanding later times, because the transformation that established the Egyptian state created a new [context](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context) for [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion).

Religious phenomena were [pervasive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/pervasive), so much so that it is not meaningful to view religion as a single entity that cohered as a system. Nevertheless, religion must be seen against a background of potentially nonreligious human activities and values. During its more than 3,000 years of development, Egyptian religion underwent significant changes of emphasis and practice, but in all periods religion had a clear consistency in character and style.

It is inappropriate to define religion narrowly, as consisting only in the cult of the gods and in human piety. Religious behaviour [encompassed](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompassed) contact with the dead, practices such as divination and oracles, and magic, which mostly exploited divine instruments and associations.

There were two essential foci of public religion: the [king](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pharaoh) and the gods. Both are among the most characteristic features of Egyptian civilization. The [king](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monarchy) had a unique status between humanity and the gods, partook in the world of the gods, and constructed great, religiously motivated funerary monuments for his afterlife. Egyptian gods are renowned for their wide variety of forms, including animal forms and mixed forms with an animal head on a [human body](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-body). The most important deities were the [sun god](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sun-worship), who had several names and aspects and was associated with many supernatural beings in a [solar cycle](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-cycle) modeled on the alternation of night and day, and [Osiris](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Osiris-Egyptian-god), the god of the dead and ruler of the underworld. With his consort, [Isis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Isis-Egyptian-goddess), Osiris became dominant in many [contexts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contexts) during the 1st millennium bce, when solar [worship](https://www.britannica.com/topic/worship) was in relative decline.

The Egyptians conceived of the cosmos as including the gods and the present world—whose centre was, of course, Egypt—and as being surrounded by the realm of disorder, from which [order](https://www.britannica.com/topic/chaos-and-order) had arisen and to which it would finally revert. Disorder had to be kept at bay. The task of the king as the protagonist of human society was to retain the [benevolence](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/benevolence) of the gods in maintaining order against disorder. This ultimately pessimistic view of the cosmos was associated principally with the sun god and the solar cycle. It formed a powerful legitimation of king and elite in their task of preserving order.

Despite this pessimism, the official presentation of the cosmos on the monuments was positive and optimistic, showing the king and the gods in perpetual [reciprocity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reciprocity) and harmony. This implied contrast reaffirmed the fragile order. The restricted character of the monuments was also fundamental to a system of [decorum](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/decorum) that defined what could be shown, in what way it could be shown, and in what context. Decorum and the affirmation of order reinforced each other.

# Sources and limitations of ancient and modern knowledge

The only extensive [contemporaneous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contemporaneous) descriptions of ancient Egyptian [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) from the outside were made by Classical Greek and Roman writers. Their works include many important observations about Egyptian [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion), which particularly interested the writers and which until late antiquity was not fundamentally different in type from their own religions. [Herodotus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Herodotus-Greek-historian) (5th century bce) remarked that the Egyptians were the most religious of people, and the comment is apt because popular religious practices proliferated in the 1st millennium bce. Other significant Classical sources include [Plutarch’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Plutarch) essay on [Isis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Isis-Egyptian-goddess) and [Osiris](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Osiris-Egyptian-god) (1st century ce), which gives the only known connected narrative of their [myth](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myth), and the writings of [Apuleius](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Lucius-Apuleius) (2nd century ce) and others about the Isis cult as it spread in the Greco-Roman world.

In other respects, [ancient Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt) has been recovered archaeologically. Excavation and the recording of buildings have produced a great range of material, from large monuments to small objects and texts on perishable papyrus. Egyptian monuments are almost unique in the amount of inscription they bear; vast numbers of texts and representations with religious content are preserved, especially from the later 2nd and 1st millennia bce. Much of this material is religious or has religious [implications](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implications). This dominance may be misleading, partly because many monuments were in the desert, where they are well preserved, and partly because the lavishing of great resources on religious monuments for the king and the gods need not mean that people’s lives were dominated by religion.

In addition to favouring large monuments and the elite, the archaeological record has other important biases. The formal cults of major deities and the realm of the dead are far better known than everyday religious activities, particularly those occurring in towns and villages, very few of which have been excavated. The absence of material deriving from the religious practice of most people in itself [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) evidence suggesting both the inequality of society and the possibility, confirmed by other strands of evidence, that many people’s religious life did not focus on official cult places and major temples.

Many official works of art present standard [conceptions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conceptions) of the divine world and of the king’s role in this world and in caring for the gods. Much religious evidence is at the same time artistic, and the production of works of art was a vital [prestige](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/prestige) concern of king and elite. Religious activities and rituals are less well known than this formalized artistic presentation of religious conceptions. The status of personal religion in the [context](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context) of official cults is poorly understood.

Official forms were idealizing, and the [untoward](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/untoward), which is everywhere an important focus of religion, was excluded almost entirely from them. The world of the monuments is that of Egypt alone, even though the Egyptians had normal, sometimes [reciprocal](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reciprocal), relations with other peoples. [Decorum](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Decorum) affected what was shown. Thus, the [king](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pharaoh) was almost always depicted as the person offering to the gods, although temple rituals were performed by priests. Scenes of offering and of the gods conferring benefits on the king may not depict specific rituals, while the equal form in which king and gods are depicted bears no direct relation to real cult actions, which were performed on small cult images kept inside shrines.

An additional limitation is that knowledge of many central concerns was restricted. The king was stated to be alone in knowing aspects of the [solar cycle](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-cycle). Knowledge of some religious texts was reserved to initiates, who would benefit from them both in this life and in the next. Magic evoked the power of the exotic and [esoteric](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/esoteric). Evidence for some restricted material is preserved, but it is not known who had access to it, while in other cases the restricted knowledge is only [alluded](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alluded) to and is now inaccessible.

Death and the next world dominate both the archaeological record and popular modern conceptions of Egyptian religion. This dominance is determined to a great extent by the landscape of the country, since tombs were placed if possible in the desert. Vast resources were expended on creating prestigious [burial](https://www.britannica.com/topic/burial-death-rite) places for absolute rulers or wealthy officials. Tombs contained elaborate grave goods (mostly plundered soon after deposition), representations of “daily life,” or less commonly of religious subjects, and some texts that were intended to help the deceased attain the next world and prosper there. The texts came increasingly to be inscribed on coffins and stone sarcophagi or deposited in burials on papyrus. Some royal tombs included long passages from religious texts, many of them drawn from nonmortuary [contexts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contexts) and hence more broadly valuable as source material.

One crucial area where religion extended beyond narrow bounds was in the [ethical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ethical) instructions, which became the principal [genre](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/genre) of Egyptian literature. These are known from the Middle Kingdom (c. 1900–1600 bce) to the Roman period (1st century ce). As with other sources, the later texts are more overtly religious, but all show inextricable connections between proper conduct, the order of the world, and the gods.

## King, cosmos, and society

The king was the centre of human society, the guarantor of order for the gods, the recipient of god-given benefits including life itself, and the [benevolent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/benevolent) ruler of the world for humanity. He was ultimately responsible for the cults of the dead, both for his predecessors in office and for the dead in general. His dominance in religion corresponded to his central political role: from late predynastic times (c. 3100 bce), state organization was based on kingship and on the service of officials for the king. For humanity, the king had a superhuman role, being a [manifestation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifestation) of a god or of various deities on earth.

The king’s principal original title, the [Horus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Horus) name, proclaimed that he was an aspect of the chief god Horus, a sky god who was depicted as a falcon. Other identifications were added to this one, notably “Son of [Re](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Re)” (the sun god) and “Perfect God,” both introduced in the 4th [dynasty](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynasty) (c. 2575–2465 bce), when the great pyramids were constructed. The epithet “Son of Re” placed the king in a close but dependent relation with the leading figure in the pantheon. “Perfect God” (often rendered “Good God”) indicated that the king had the status of a minor deity, for which he was “perfected” through accession to his office; it restricted the extent of his divinity and separated him from full deities.

In his intermediate position between humanity and the gods, the king could receive the most extravagant divine [adulation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adulation) and was in some ways more prominent than any single god. In death he aspired to full divinity but could not escape the human context. Although royal funerary monuments differed in type from other tombs and were vastly larger, they too were pillaged and vandalized, and few royal mortuary cults were long-lasting. Some kings, notably [Amenhotep III](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Amenhotep-III) (1390–53 bce), [Ramses II](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ramses-II-king-of-Egypt) (1279–13 bce), and several of the Ptolemies, sought deification during their own lifetime, while others, such as [Amenemhet III](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Amenemhet-III) (1818–c. 1770 bce), became minor gods after their death, but these developments show how restricted royal divinity was. The divinized king coexisted with his mortal self, and as many nonroyal individuals as kings became deified after death.

The gods, the king, humanity, and the dead existed together in the cosmos, which the creator god had brought into being from the preexistent [chaos](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/chaos). All living beings, except perhaps the creator, would die at the end of time. The [sun god](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sun-worship) became aged and needed to be rejuvenated and reborn daily. The ordered cosmos was surrounded by and shot through with disorder, which had to be kept at bay. Disorder menaced most strongly at such times of transition as the passage from one year to the next or the death of a king. Thus, the king’s role in maintaining order was cosmic and not merely social. His exaction of service from people was necessary to the cosmos.

The concept of _[maat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maat-Egyptian-goddess)_ (“order”) was fundamental in Egyptian thought. The king’s role was to set _maat_ in place of _[isfet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/isfet)_ (“disorder”). _Maat_ was crucial in human life and embraced notions of [reciprocity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reciprocity), [justice](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/justice), truth, and moderation. _Maat_ was personified as a goddess and the creator’s daughter and received a cult of her own. In the cult of other deities, the king’s offering of _maat_ to a deity [encapsulated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encapsulated) the relationship between humanity, the king, and the gods; as the representative of humanity, he returned to the gods the order that came from them and of which they were themselves part. _Maat_ extended into the world of the dead: in the weighing of the heart after death, shown on papyri deposited in burials, the person’s heart occupies one side of the scales and a representation of _maat_ the other. The meaning of this image is deepened in the accompanying text, which asserts that the deceased behaved correctly on earth and did not overstep the boundaries of order, declaring that he or she did not “know that which is not”—that is, things that were outside the created and ordered world.

This role of _maat_ in human life created a [continuity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/continuity) between religion, political action, and elite [morality](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/morality). Over the centuries, private religion and morality drew apart from state concerns, paralleling a gradual separation of king and temple. It cannot be known whether religion and morality were as closely [integrated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrated) for the people as they were for the elite, or even how fully the elite subscribed to these beliefs. Nonetheless, the [integration](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integration) of cosmos, king, and _maat_ remained fundamental.

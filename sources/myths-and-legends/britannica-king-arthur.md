### Who is King Arthur?

King Arthur is a legendary British king who appears in a series of stories and medieval romances as the leader of a knightly fellowship called the [Round Table](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Round-Table-Arthurian-legend).

### Was King Arthur a real person?

Historians cannot confirm King Arthur’s existence, though some speculate that he was a real warrior who led British armies against Saxon invaders in the 6th century.

### When did stories about King Arthur become popular?

Stories about King Arthur became popular before the 11th century. [Geoffrey of Monmouth](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-of-Monmouth)’s _[Historia regum Britanniae](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Historia-regum-Britanniae)_, written between 1135 and 1139, brought Arthur European fame. Today the character of King Arthur appears in comic books, novels, television shows, and films.

### How did Arthur become king?

[Legends](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Arthurian-legend) disagree on how Arthur became king, though most involve his famous sword, [Excalibur](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Excalibur-Arthurian-legend). Some involve Arthur fulfilling a prophecy by pulling Excalibur from a stone, whereas others say the sword was given to him by a magical woman in a lake.

### Who was King Arthur’s wife?

King Arthur was married to [Guinevere](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Guinevere) in most legends. Early traditions of abduction and infidelity follow Guinevere, who in some stories was carried off by Arthur’s rivals and in others had an adulterous affair with the knight Lancelot.

**King Arthur**, legendary British [king](https://www.britannica.com/topic/king-monarch) who appears in a cycle of [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) [romances](https://www.britannica.com/art/romance-literature-and-performance) (known as the [Matter of Britain](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Arthurian-legend)) as the [sovereign](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/sovereign) of a knightly fellowship of the [Round Table](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Round-Table-Arthurian-legend). It is not certain how these [legends](https://www.britannica.com/art/legend-literature) originated or whether the figure of Arthur was based on a historical person. The [legend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legend) possibly originated either in [Wales](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wales) or in those parts of northern [Britain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Britain-island-Europe) inhabited by [Brythonic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Brythonic-languages)-speaking [Celts](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Celt-people). (For a fuller treatment of the stories about King Arthur, _see also_ [Arthurian legend](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Arthurian-legend).)

[![King Arthur](https://cdn.britannica.com/79/147179-050-14CCB8B7/King-Arthur-illustration-NC-title-page-Wyeth.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/79/147179-050-14CCB8B7/King-Arthur-illustration-NC-title-page-Wyeth.jpg)

[King Arthur](https://cdn.britannica.com/79/147179-050-14CCB8B7/King-Arthur-illustration-NC-title-page-Wyeth.jpg) King Arthur, illustration by N.C. Wyeth for the title page of _The Boy's King Arthur_ (1917).(more)

Assumptions that a historical Arthur led Welsh resistance to the West [Saxon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Saxon-people) advance from the middle [Thames](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thames-River-Ontario) are based on a conflation of two early writers, the religious polemicist [Gildas](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Gildas) and the historian [Nennius](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Nennius), and on the _Annales Cambriae_ of the late 10th century. The 9th-century _[Historia Brittonum](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Historia-Brittonum)_, traditionally attributed to [Nennius](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Nennius), records 12 battles fought by Arthur against the Saxons, culminating in a victory at Mons Badonicus. The Arthurian section of this work, however, is from an undetermined source, possibly a poetic text. The _Annales Cambriae_ also mention Arthur’s victory at Mons Badonicus (516) and record the Battle of Camlann (537), “in which Arthur and Medraut fell.” Gildas’s _De excidio et conquestu Britanniae_ (mid-6th century) implies that Mons Badonicus was fought in about 500 but does not connect it with Arthur.

Early [Welsh literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/Welsh-literature) quickly made Arthur into a king of wonders and marvels. The 12th-century prose romance _[Culhwch and Olwen](https://www.britannica.com/art/Culhwch-and-Olwen)_ associated him with other [heroes](https://www.britannica.com/art/hero-literary-and-cultural-figure), and this [conception](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conception) of a heroic band with Arthur at its head doubtless led to the idea of Arthur’s court.

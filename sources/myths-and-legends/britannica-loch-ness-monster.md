### What is the Loch Ness monster?

The [Loch Ness monster](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Loch-Ness-monster-legendary-creature) is a legendary creature said to live in Scotland’s [Loch Ness](https://www.britannica.com/place/Loch-Ness-lake-Scotland-United-Kingdom). It is widely considered a myth, though reports of a monster in the loch date back to ancient times. The legend gained traction in 1933, with a reported sighting of an enormous animal. The following year the so-called “surgeon’s photograph” was taken, and it appeared to show the monster’s head and neck, sparking speculation that the creature was a plesiosaur, an extinct marine reptile. Expeditions and sonar explorations have failed to locate the creature, and many alleged photographs have been discredited as fakes, including the 1934 image. Despite the lack of evidence, the Loch Ness monster remains popular and is a tourist attraction, contributing to Scotland’s economy.

### Where is the Loch Ness monster said to live?

The [Loch Ness monster](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Loch-Ness-monster-legendary-creature) is said to live in [Loch Ness](https://www.britannica.com/place/Loch-Ness-lake-Scotland-United-Kingdom), a lake in the Highland council area of Scotland. It is the largest body of fresh water by volume in the United Kingdom. Though many claim this large marine creature inhabits the loch, much of the alleged evidence has been discredited, leading many to believe that the monster is a myth.

### Is the Loch Ness monster real?

There is no conclusive evidence to support the existence of the [Loch Ness monster](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Loch-Ness-monster-legendary-creature), a large marine creature said to inhabit Scotland’s [Loch Ness](https://www.britannica.com/place/Loch-Ness-lake-Scotland-United-Kingdom). Reports of a mysterious monster date back to ancient times. In 1933 a couple saw an enormous animal, which they compared to a “dragon or prehistoric monster.” After crossing the road in front of their car, it disappeared into the water. The following year the so-called “surgeon’s photograph” appeared to show the monster’s small head and neck. The image sparked a global sensation, and many speculated that the creature was a [plesiosaur](https://www.britannica.com/animal/plesiosaur), a marine reptile that went extinct some 65.5 million years ago.

The Loch Ness area attracted numerous monster hunters. Over the years, several sonar explorations (notably in 1987 and 2003) were undertaken to locate the creature, but none were successful. In 1994 the “surgeon’s photograph” was revealed to be a hoax: the “monster” was actually a plastic-and-wooden head attached to a toy submarine. In 2018 researchers conducted a DNA survey of Loch Ness to determine what organisms live in the waters. No signs of a plesiosaur or other such large animal were found. However, the results indicated the presence of numerous eels, leaving open the possibility that the creature is an oversized eel.

**Loch Ness monster**, large marine [creature](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monster-mythology) believed by some people to inhabit [Loch Ness](https://www.britannica.com/place/Loch-Ness-lake-Scotland-United-Kingdom), [Scotland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Scotland). However, much of the [alleged](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alleged) evidence supporting its existence has been discredited, and it is widely thought that the monster is a [myth](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myth).

## Early sightings

Reports of a monster inhabiting Loch Ness date back to ancient times. Notably, local stone carvings by the [Pict](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pict) depict a mysterious beast with flippers. The first written account appears in a 7th-century biography of [St. Columba](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Columba). According to that work, in 565 ad the monster bit a swimmer and was prepared to attack another man when Columba intervened, ordering the beast to “go back.” It obeyed, and over the centuries only occasional sightings were reported. Many of these alleged encounters seemed inspired by Scottish [folklore](https://www.britannica.com/topic/folklore-academic-discipline), which abounds with mythical water creatures.

## The myth goes global

[![Loch Ness, Scotland](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/2977-050-6CEA9F21/Loch-Ness-Highlands-of-Scotland-head-monastery.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/2977-050-6CEA9F21/Loch-Ness-Highlands-of-Scotland-head-monastery.jpg)

[Loch Ness, Scotland](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/2977-050-6CEA9F21/Loch-Ness-Highlands-of-Scotland-head-monastery.jpg) Loch Ness, in the Highlands of Scotland. At the head of the loch is the monastery at Fort Augustus.(more)

In 1933 the Loch Ness monster’s [legend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legend) began to grow. At the time, a road [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) to Loch Ness was finished, offering an unobstructed view of the lake. In April a couple saw an enormous animal—which they compared to a “dragon or prehistoric monster”—and after it crossed their car’s path, it disappeared into the water. The incident was reported in a Scottish newspaper, and numerous sightings followed. In December 1933 the _[Daily Mail](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Daily-Mail)_ commissioned Marmaduke Wetherell, a big-game hunter, to locate the [sea serpent](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sea-serpent). Along the lake’s shores, he found large footprints that he believed belonged to “a very powerful soft-footed animal about 20 feet \[6 meters\] long.” However, upon closer inspection, zoologists at the [Natural History Museum](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Natural-History-Museum-London) determined that the tracks were identical and made with an umbrella stand or ashtray that had a [hippopotamus](https://www.britannica.com/animal/hippopotamus-mammal-species) leg as a base; Wetherell’s role in the [hoax](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hoax) was unclear.

The news only seemed to spur efforts to prove the monster’s existence. In 1934 English physician Robert Kenneth Wilson photographed the alleged creature. The [iconic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/iconic) image—known as the “surgeon’s photograph”—appeared to show the monster’s small head and neck. The _Daily Mail_ printed the photograph, sparking an international sensation. Many speculated that the creature was a [plesiosaur](https://www.britannica.com/animal/plesiosaur), a marine reptile that went extinct some 65.5 million years ago.

## Searches and hoaxes

The Loch Ness area attracted numerous monster hunters. Over the years, several [sonar](https://www.britannica.com/technology/sonar) explorations (notably in 1987 and 2003) were undertaken to locate the creature, but none were successful. In addition, numerous photographs allegedly showed the beast, but most were discredited as fakes or as depicting other animals or objects. Notably, in 1994 it was revealed that Wilson’s photograph was a [hoax](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/hoax) spearheaded by a revenge-seeking Wetherell; the “monster” was actually a plastic-and-wooden head attached to a toy submarine. In 2018 researchers conducted a DNA survey of Loch Ness to determine what organisms live in the waters. No signs of a plesiosaur or other such large animal were found, though the results indicated the presence of numerous eels. This finding left open the possibility that the monster is an oversized eel. Despite the lack of conclusive evidence, the Loch Ness monster remained popular—and profitable. In the early 21st century it was thought that it contributed nearly $80 million annually to Scotland’s economy.

**brownie**, in English and Scottish [folklore](https://www.britannica.com/topic/folklore-academic-discipline), a small, [industrious](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/industrious) [fairy](https://www.britannica.com/art/fairy) or hobgoblin believed to inhabit houses and barns. Rarely seen, he was often heard at night, cleaning and doing housework; he also sometimes mischievously disarranged rooms. He would ride for the midwife, and in Cornwall he caused swarming bees to settle quickly. Cream or bread and [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk) might be left for him, but other gifts offended him. If one made him a suit of clothes, he would put it on and then vanish, never to return.

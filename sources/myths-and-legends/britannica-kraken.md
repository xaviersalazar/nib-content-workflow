**kraken**, legendary sea monster of [Scandinavian](https://www.britannica.com/art/Scandinavian-literature) and [Norwegian](https://www.britannica.com/art/Norwegian-literature) [lore](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/lore), perhaps imagined from sightings of [giant squids](https://www.britannica.com/animal/giant-squid) and [octopi](https://www.britannica.com/animal/octopus-mollusk). Sailors have discussed giant sea monsters for thousands of years, and Danish historian Erik Pontoppidan described the kraken (as well as [sea serpents](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sea-serpent) and [mermaids](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mermaid)) in detail in his _Natural History of Norway_ (first published in Danish in 1752), claiming the beast was "round, flat, and full of arms" and "the largest and most surprising of all the animal creation." The sea monster is also the subject of [Alfred, Lord Tennyson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alfred-Lord-Tennyson)’s poem “The Kraken” (1871):

> Below the thunders of the upper deep,
>
>  Far, far beneath in the abysmal sea,
>
>  His ancient, dreamless, uninvaded sleep
>
>  The Kraken sleepeth: faintest sunlights flee
>
>  About his shadowy sides; above him [swell](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/swell)
>
>  Huge sponges of millenial growth and height;
>
>  And far away into the sickly light,
>
>  From many a wondrous grot and secret cell
>
>  Unnumber’d and enormous polypi
>
>  Winnow with giant arms the slumbering green.
>
>  There hath he lain for ages, and will lie
>
>  Battening upon huge sea-worms in his sleep,
>
>  Until the latter fire shall heat the deep;
>
>  Then once by man and angels to be seen,
>
>  In roaring he shall rise and on the surface die.

In 2021, [Seattle](https://www.britannica.com/place/Seattle-Washington), [Washington](https://www.britannica.com/place/Washington-state), gained a professional hockey team, which it named the [Seattle Kraken](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Seattle-Kraken) after the legendary sea monster and the giant Pacific [octopus](https://www.britannica.com/animal/octopus-mollusk) found living in the waters of [Puget Sound](https://www.britannica.com/place/Puget-Sound), where Seattle is situated.

**Asgard**, in [Norse mythology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Germanic-religion-and-mythology), the dwelling place of the gods, comparable to the Greek [Mount Olympus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Olympus-mountain-Greece). [Legend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Legend) divided Asgard into 12 or more realms, including [Valhalla](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Valhalla-Norse-mythology), the home of [Odin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Odin-Norse-deity) and the [abode](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/abode) of heroes slain in earthly battle; Thrudheim, the realm of [Thor;](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Thor-Germanic-deity) and Breidablik, the home of [Balder](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Balder-Norse-mythology).

Old Norse: Ásgardr_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/Asgard)

Each important god had his own palace in Asgard, and many [Germanic peoples](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Germanic-peoples) believed that these mansions were similar in design to those of their own nobility. Asgard could be reached from earth only by the bridge [Bifrost](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bifrost) (the rainbow).

### What is a mermaid?

A mermaid is a fabled marine creature with the head and upper body of a woman and the tail of a fish. In European folklore, mermaids were natural beings who, like fairies, had magical and prophetic powers.

### Can a human marry a mermaid?

Many folktales record marriages between mermaids (who might assume human form) and men. In most, the man steals the mermaid’s cap or belt or her comb or mirror. While the objects are hidden, she lives with him; if she finds them, she returns at once to the sea.

### Are mermaids good or bad for humans?

Though sometimes kindly, mermaids were usually dangerous to humans. Their gifts brought misfortune and could cause disasters. They sometimes lured mortals to death by drowning or enticed young people to live with them underwater.

### What fairy tale is about a mermaid?

"The Little Mermaid" is a fairy tale by Hans Christian Andersen about a young mermaid who longs to be part of the human world.

### What gave rise to the myth of mermaids?

Aquatic mammals, such as the dugong and manatee, that suckle their young in human fashion above water are considered by some to be the origin of myths about mermaids.

**mermaid**, a fabled marine creature with the head and upper body of a [human being](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being) and the tail of a [fish](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fish). Similar divine or semidivine beings appear in ancient mythologies (e.g., the Chaldean sea god Ea, or Oannes). In European [folklore](https://www.britannica.com/topic/folklore-academic-discipline), mermaids (sometimes called sirens) and mermen were natural beings who, like [fairies](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/fairies), had magical and prophetic powers. They loved music and often sang. Though very long-lived, they were mortal and had no souls.

Many folktales record marriages between mermaids (who might assume human form) and men. In most, the man steals the mermaid’s cap or belt, her comb or mirror. While the objects are hidden she lives with him; if she finds them she returns at once to the sea. In some variants the marriage lasts while certain agreed-upon conditions are fulfilled, and it ends when the conditions are broken.

### What is Leviathan?

In Jewish mythology, the name Leviathan can refer to a variety of monstrous creatures, including a [primordial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/primordial) [sea serpent](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sea-serpent), a dragon, a snake, a crocodile, or a whalelike animal. It likely developed from pre-biblical Middle Eastern mythology, especially that of the sea monster in the Ugaritic myth of Baal. Leviathan appears in several books of the [Hebrew Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-Bible) (Old Testament).

### What does the word Leviathan mean?

The name Leviathan comes from the Hebrew Livyatan, which comes from a root that means “to twist, turn, wind, or coil.” Today the name is often used to refer generally to a sea monster or to any gigantic powerful thing.

### What does Leviathan symbolize in the Bible?

In one [psalm](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Psalms), Leviathan is a whalelike creature, perhaps embodying the wonder and strangeness of creation. In the [Book of Isaiah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Book-of-Isaiah), Leviathan is a sea serpent symbolizing Israel’s enemies. In the [Book of Job](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Book-of-Job), Leviathan is a fire-breathing crocodile, perhaps personifying an aspect of creation that is beyond human comprehension or control.

**Leviathan**, in Jewish mythology, a [primordial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/primordial) [sea serpent](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sea-serpent). Its source is in prebiblical [Mesopotamian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mesopotamian-mythology) [myth](https://www.britannica.com/topic/myth), especially that of the sea monster in the Ugaritic [myth](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myth) of Baal ( _see_ [Yamm](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yamm)). In the [Old Testament](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Old-Testament), Leviathan appears in Psalms 74:14 as a multiheaded sea serpent that is killed by God and given as food to the Hebrews in the wilderness. In Isaiah 27:1, Leviathan is a serpent and a symbol of Israel’s enemies, who will be slain by God. In Job 41, it is a sea monster and a symbol of God’s power of creation.

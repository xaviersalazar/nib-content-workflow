**phoenix**, in [ancient Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt) and in [Classical antiquity](https://www.britannica.com/event/Classical-antiquity), a fabulous [bird](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal) associated with the [worship](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/worship) of the [sun](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sun-worship). The [Egyptian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ancient-Egyptian-religion) phoenix was said to be as large as an eagle, with brilliant scarlet and gold plumage and a melodious cry. Only one phoenix existed at any time, and it was very long-lived—no ancient authority gave it a life span of less than 500 years. As its end approached, the phoenix fashioned a nest of aromatic boughs and spices, set it on fire, and was consumed in the flames. From the pyre miraculously sprang a new phoenix, which, after embalming its father’s ashes in an egg of myrrh, flew with the ashes to [Heliopolis](https://www.britannica.com/place/Heliopolis-ancient-city-Egypt) (“City of the Sun”) in Egypt, where it deposited them on the altar in the temple of the Egyptian god of the sun, Re. A variant of the story made the dying phoenix fly to Heliopolis and immolate itself in the altar fire, from which the young phoenix then rose.

The Egyptians associated the phoenix with [immortality](https://www.britannica.com/topic/immortality), and that symbolism had a widespread appeal in late antiquity. The phoenix was compared to undying Rome, and it appears on the coinage of the late Roman Empire as a symbol of the Eternal City. It was also widely interpreted as an [allegory](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/allegory) of [resurrection](https://www.britannica.com/topic/resurrection-religion) and life after death—ideas that also appealed to emergent Christianity.

- How is resurrection described in Christianity?







In Christianity, the resurrection is a core doctrine rooted in the belief that [Jesus Christ](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Paul-the-Apostle) was raised from the dead [on the third day](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Easter-holiday/Introduction#ref425596) after his crucifixion, signifying his victory over sin and death. The [Gospels](https://www.britannica.com/topic/resurrection-religion/Introduction#ref1) detail the discovery of his empty tomb by women disciples, followed by appearances of Jesus to his followers in Jerusalem and Galilee.



These accounts emphasize that the resurrected Jesus was not a mere resuscitated corpse or a ghost but was substantially transformed. The resurrection of Christ is viewed as a declaration of his divine sonship and the basis for Christian hope for life after death. It also serves as an encouragement for believers to live a new life. The Christian hope focuses on the [resurrection of the body](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity/The-problem-of-suffering#ref67493), with the goal of redemption being the transformation of the whole person.

**resurrection**, the rising from the dead of a divine or human being who still retains their own personhood, or individuality, though the body may or may not be changed. The belief in the resurrection of the body is usually associated with [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity), because of the doctrine of the Resurrection of [Christ](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus), but it also is associated with later [Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judaism), which provided basic ideas that were expanded in Christianity and [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam).

Ancient Middle Eastern religious thought provided a background for belief in the resurrection of a divine being (e.g., the Babylonian vegetation god [Tammuz](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tammuz-Mesopotamian-god)), but belief in personal resurrection of humans was unknown. In Greco-Roman religious thought there was a belief in the [immortality](https://www.britannica.com/topic/immortality) of the [soul](https://www.britannica.com/topic/soul-religion-and-philosophy), but not in the resurrection of the body. Symbolic resurrection, or rebirth of the spirit, occurred in the Hellenistic mystery religions, such as the religion of the goddess [Isis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Isis-Egyptian-goddess), but postmortem corporeal resurrection was not recognized.

The expectation of the resurrection of the dead is found in several biblical works. In [the Book of Ezekiel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Book-of-Ezekiel), there is an anticipation that the righteous [Israelites](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Israelite) will rise from the dead. [The Book of Daniel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Book-of-Daniel-Old-Testament) further developed the hope of resurrection with both the righteous and unrighteous Israelites being raised from the dead, after which will occur a judgment, with the righteous participating in an eternal messianic kingdom and the unrighteous being excluded. In some intertestamental literature, such as _The Syriac Apocalypse of Baruch,_ there is an expectation of a universal resurrection at the advent of the [Messiah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/messiah-religion).

The Resurrection of Christ, a central doctrine of Christianity, is based on the belief that [Jesus Christ](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus) was raised from the dead on the third day after his [Crucifixion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crucifixion-capital-punishment) and that through his conquering of death all believers will subsequently share in his victory over “sin, death, and the Devil.” The celebration of this event, called [Easter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Easter-holiday), or the Festival of the Resurrection, is the major feast day of the church. The accounts of the Resurrection of Jesus are found in the four [Gospels](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gospel-New-Testament)— [Matthew](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gospel-According-to-Matthew), [Mark](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gospel-According-to-Mark), [Luke](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gospel-According-to-Luke), and [John](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gospel-According-to-John)—and various theological expressions of the early church’s universal conviction and consensus that Christ rose from the dead are found throughout the rest of the [New Testament](https://www.britannica.com/topic/New-Testament), especially in the letters of [St. Paul the Apostle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Paul-the-Apostle) (e.g., 1 [Corinthians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Letter-of-Paul-to-the-Corinthians) 15).

According to the [Gospel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gospel-New-Testament) accounts, certain woman disciples went to the tomb of Jesus, which was located in the garden of Joseph of Arimathea, a member of the [Sanhedrin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sanhedrin) (the supreme Jewish religious court) and a secret disciple of Jesus. They found the stone sealing the tomb moved and the tomb empty, and they informed [Peter](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Peter-the-Apostle) and other disciples that the body of Jesus was not there. Later, various disciples saw Jesus in Jerusalem, even entering a room that was locked; he was also seen in Galilee. (Accounts of the locations and occasions of the appearances differ in various Gospels.) Other than such appearances noted in the Gospels, the account of the resurrected Lord’s walking the Earth for 40 days and subsequently ascending into heaven is found only in the book of [the Acts of the Apostles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Acts-of-the-Apostles-New-Testament).

[Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam) also teaches a doctrine of the resurrection. First, at Doomsday, all humans will die and then be raised from the dead. Second, each person will be judged according to the record of their life that is kept in two books, one listing the good deeds, the other the evil deeds. After the Judgment the unbelievers will be placed in hell and the faithful Muslims will go to paradise, a place of happiness and bliss.

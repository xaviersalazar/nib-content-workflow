**unicorn**, mythological animal resembling a [horse](https://www.britannica.com/animal/horse) or a [goat](https://www.britannica.com/animal/goat) with a single [horn](https://www.britannica.com/science/horn-zoology) on its forehead. The unicorn appeared in early [Mesopotamian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mesopotamia-historical-region-Asia) artworks, and it also was referred to in the ancient myths of [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India) and [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China). The earliest description in [Greek literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/Greek-literature) of a single-horned (Greek _monokerōs_, Latin _unicornis_) animal was by the historian [Ctesias](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ctesias) (c. 400 bce), who related that the Indian [wild ass](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ass) was the size of a horse, with a white body, purple head, and blue eyes, and on its forehead was a cubit-long horn coloured red at the pointed tip, black in the middle, and white at the base. Those who drank from its horn were thought to be protected from stomach trouble, [epilepsy](https://www.britannica.com/science/epilepsy), and [poison](https://www.britannica.com/science/poison-biochemistry). It was very fleet of foot and difficult to capture. The actual animal behind Ctesias’s description was probably the [Indian rhinoceros](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Indian-rhinoceros).

Certain poetical passages of the [Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible) refer to a strong and splendid horned animal called _reʾem_. This word was translated “unicorn” or “rhinoceros” in many versions of the Bible, but many modern translations prefer “wild ox” ( [aurochs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/aurochs)), which is the correct meaning of the Hebrew _reʾem_. As a biblical animal, the unicorn was interpreted allegorically in the early [Christian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity) church. One of the earliest such interpretations appears in the ancient Greek [bestiary](https://www.britannica.com/art/bestiary-medieval-literary-genre) known as the _[Physiologus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Physiologus-Greek-scientific-text)_, which states that the unicorn is a strong, fierce animal that can be caught only if a virgin maiden is placed before it. The unicorn leaps into the virgin’s lap, and she suckles it and leads it to the king’s palace. Medieval writers thus likened the unicorn to [Christ](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus), who raised up a horn of salvation for mankind and dwelt in the womb of the [Virgin Mary](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mary-mother-of-Jesus). Other legends tell of the unicorn’s combat with the [elephant](https://www.britannica.com/animal/elephant-mammal), whom it finally spears to death with its horn, and of the unicorn’s purifying of poisoned waters with its horn so that other animals may drink.

Cups reputedly made of unicorn horn—but actually made of [rhinoceros](https://www.britannica.com/animal/rhinoceros-mammal) horn or [narwhal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/narwhal) tusk—were highly valued by important persons in the [Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages) as a protection against poisoned drinks. Many fine representations of the hunt of the unicorn survive in medieval art, not only in Europe but also in the [Islamic world](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-world) and in China.

**fantasy**, imaginative [fiction](https://www.britannica.com/art/fiction-literature) that relies on strangeness of setting (such as other worlds or times) and of characters (such as [supernatural](https://www.britannica.com/topic/supernaturalism) or unnatural beings). Examples include [William Shakespeare](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Shakespeare)’s _[A Midsummer Night’s Dream](https://www.britannica.com/topic/A-Midsummer-Nights-Dream-play-by-Shakespeare)_ (1600), [Jonathan Swift](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jonathan-Swift)’s _[Gulliver’s Travels](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gullivers-Travels)_ (1726), [J.R.R. Tolkien](https://www.britannica.com/biography/J-R-R-Tolkien)’s _[The Lord of the Rings](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Lord-of-the-Rings)_ (1954-55), [T.H. White](https://www.britannica.com/biography/T-H-White)’s _[The Once and Future King](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Once-and-Future-King)_ (1958), and [Sarah J. Maas](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sarah-J-Maas)’s _A Court of Thorns and Roses_ (2015).

[Science fiction](https://www.britannica.com/art/science-fiction) can be seen as a form of fantasy, but the terms are not interchangeable, as science fiction usually is set in the future and is based on some aspect of [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science) or [technology](https://www.britannica.com/technology/technology), while fantasy is set in an imaginary world and features the magic of mythical beings. “Romantasy” is a subgenre that blends fantasy with elements of the [romance novel](https://www.britannica.com/art/romance-novel) genre; it became popular in the 21st century, pioneered by such writers as Maas and [Rebecca Yarros](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rebecca-Yarros).

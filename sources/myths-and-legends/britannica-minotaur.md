**Minotaur**, in [Greek mythology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-mythology), a fabulous monster of [Crete](https://www.britannica.com/place/Crete) that had the body of a man and the head of a bull. It was the [offspring](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/offspring) of [Pasiphae](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pasiphae-Greek-mythology), the wife of [Minos](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Minos), and a snow-white bull sent to Minos by the god [Poseidon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Poseidon) for sacrifice. Minos, instead of sacrificing it, kept it alive; Poseidon as a punishment made Pasiphae fall in love with it. Her child by the bull was shut up in the Labyrinth created for Minos by [Daedalus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Daedalus-Greek-mythology).

A son of Minos, Androgeos, was later killed by the Athenians; to avenge his death, Minos demanded that seven Athenian youths and seven maidens should be sent every ninth year (or, according to another version, every year) to be devoured by the Minotaur. When the third time of sacrifice came, the Athenian hero [Theseus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Theseus-Greek-hero) volunteered to go, and, with the help of [Ariadne](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ariadne-Greek-mythology), daughter of Minos and Pasiphae, he killed the monster and ended the [tribute](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/tribute). Theseus escaped with Ariadne. ### Who are some of the major figures of Greek mythology?

Greek myth takes many forms, from religious myths of origin to folktales and legends of heroes. In terms of gods, the Greek pantheon consists of 12 deities who were said to reside at [Mount Olympus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Olympus-mountain-Greece): [Zeus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zeus), [Hera](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hera), [Aphrodite](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Aphrodite-Greek-mythology), [Apollo](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Apollo-Greek-mythology), [Ares](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ares-Greek-mythology), [Artemis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Artemis-Greek-goddess), [Athena](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Athena-Greek-mythology), [Demeter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Demeter), [Dionysus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dionysus), [Hephaestus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hephaestus), [Hermes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hermes-Greek-mythology), and [Poseidon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Poseidon). (This list sometimes also includes [Hades](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hades-Greek-mythology) or [Hestia](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hestia)). Other major figures of Greek myth include the heroes [Odysseus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Odysseus), [Orpheus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Orpheus-Greek-mythology), and [Heracles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Heracles); the [Titans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Titan-Greek-mythology); and the nine [Muses](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Muse-Greek-mythology).

### What are some major works in Greek mythology?

Some of the most important and well-known works of Greek mythology are the epic poems of [Homer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Homer-Greek-poet): the [_Iliad_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Iliad-epic-poem-by-Homer) and the [_Odyssey_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Odyssey-epic-by-Homer). In these, many of the characteristics of the Olympian gods and notable heroes are outlined. The fullest and most important source of myths about the origin of the gods is the _Theogony_ of [Hesiod](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hesiod), which also includes folktales and etiological myths. Hesiod also contributed [_Works and Days_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Works-and-Days), an epic poem about the agricultural arts which includes elements of myth.

### When did Greek mythology start?

It is difficult to know when Greek mythology started, as it is believed to have stemmed from centuries of oral tradition. It is likely that Greek myths evolved from stories told in the [Minoan civilization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Minoan-civilization) of Crete, which flourished from about 3000 to 1100 bce.

**Greek mythology**, body of stories concerning the gods, heroes, monsters, and rituals of the [ancient Greeks](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Greece) and [Classical antiquity](https://www.britannica.com/event/Classical-antiquity). The [myths](https://www.britannica.com/topic/myth) and [legends](https://www.britannica.com/art/legend-literature) often explained natural events, such as seasonal change, helped determine social and [moral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/moral) values, and deeply influenced ancient art and literature. In the popular piety of the Greeks, the [myths](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myths) were viewed as true though complex accounts and formed the basis of religious beliefs and practices.Greek mythology has subsequently had extensive influence on [the arts](https://www.britannica.com/topic/the-arts) and [literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/literature) of Western civilization, which fell heir to much of Greek [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture). Poets and artists from ancient times to the present have derived inspiration from Greek mythology and have discovered contemporary significance and relevance in classical mythological themes.

The Greek Pantheon: Who Are the 12 Olympians?

Meet the [gods and goddesses](https://www.britannica.com/list/12-greek-gods-and-goddesses) who lived on [Mount Olympus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Olympus-mountain-Greece).

- **[Zeus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zeus):** Chief deity, god of weather

- **[Hera](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hera):** Queen of the Olympians, goddess of marriage and the life of women

- **[Athena](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Athena-Greek-mythology):** Goddess of war (representing the intellectual and strategic side of war), handicraft, and practical reason

- **[Aphrodite](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Aphrodite-Greek-mythology):** Goddess of sexual love and beauty

- **[Poseidon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Poseidon):** God of the sea, earthquakes, and horses

- **[Hermes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hermes-Greek-mythology):** God of fertility, travelers, merchants, and thieves, protector of cattle, divine messenger

- **[Hades](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hades-Greek-mythology):** God of the underworld, only one of the 12 not to reside on [Mount](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Mount) Olympus

- **[Dionysus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dionysus):** God of fruitfulness and vegetation, wine, and ecstasy

- **[Demeter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Demeter):** Goddess of agriculture

- **[Apollo](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Apollo-Greek-mythology):** God of many functions, associated with prophecy, music, archery, and healing

- **[Ares](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ares-Greek-mythology):** God of war (representing the distasteful aspects of brutal warfare and slaughter)

- **[Artemis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Artemis-Greek-goddess):** Goddess of wild animals, the hunt, and vegetation and of chastity and childbirth


## Sources of myths: Literary and archaeological

## The [Homeric poems](https://www.britannica.com/art/Homeric-epics): The _[Iliad](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Iliad-epic-poem-by-Homer)_ and the _[Odyssey](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Odyssey-epic-by-Homer)_

The 5th-century-bce Greek historian [Herodotus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Herodotus-Greek-historian) remarked that [Homer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Homer-Greek-poet) and [Hesiod](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hesiod) gave to the Olympian gods—the 12 gods who lived on [Mount Olympus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Olympus-mountain-Greece) and presided over every facet of human life—their familiar characteristics. In the first book of the _Iliad_, the god [Apollo](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Apollo-Greek-mythology), unnamed but described as the son of [Zeus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zeus) and [Leto](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Leto), was instantly identifiable to the Greek reader, as were the Greek kings [Agamemnon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Agamemnon-Greek-mythology) and [Menelaus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Menelaus-Greek-mythology) when described as the sons of [Atreus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Atreus).

In both cases, the audience is expected to have knowledge of the myths that preceded their literary rendering. Little is known to suggest that the Greeks treated Homer, or any other source of Greek myths, as mere entertainment.

## The works of [Hesiod](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hesiod): _[Theogony](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Theogony-by-Hesiod)_ and _[Works and Days](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Works-and-Days)_

The First Woman

Hesiod relates the story of [Pandora](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pandora-Greek-mythology), the first woman in Greek mythology, in the _Theogony_ and in _Works and Days_. The first epic chronicles her creation by Zeus after [Prometheus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Prometheus-Greek-god) gives fire to mortals. The second epic records the tale of Pandora’s jar of evils (16th-century translations of the epic changed the jar to the more well-known box).

[![Charles Edward Perugini: Pandora](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/131663-050-173DA80F/Artist-box-Pandora-interpretation-evil-misery.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/131663-050-173DA80F/Artist-box-Pandora-interpretation-evil-misery.jpg)

[Charles Edward Perugini: _Pandora_](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/131663-050-173DA80F/Artist-box-Pandora-interpretation-evil-misery.jpg) _Pandora_, artwork by Charles Edward Perugini, 1893; in a private collection.(more)

The fullest and most important source of myths about the origin of the gods is the _Theogony_ of [Hesiod](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hesiod) (c. 700 bce). The elaborate genealogies are accompanied by folktales and etiological myths (myths that explain origins). The _Works and Days_ shares some of these myths in the narrative framework of a farming almanac and uses them to deliver homilies on [justice](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/justice) and [morality](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/morality). The orthodox view treats the two poems as quite different in theme and treats the _Works and Days_ as a [theodicy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/theodicy-theology) (an explanation of why God permits evil). It is possible, however, to treat the two poems as a diptych, each part dependent on the other. The _Theogony_ declares the identities and alliances of the gods, while the _Works and Days_ gives advice on the best way to succeed in a dangerous world, and Hesiod urges that the most reliable—though by no means certain—way is to be just.

## Other literary works

[![Achilles and Patroclus](https://cdn.britannica.com/22/129722-050-8F5C2135/Achilles-scene-Patroclus-Homers-Iliad.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/22/129722-050-8F5C2135/Achilles-scene-Patroclus-Homers-Iliad.jpg)

[Achilles and Patroclus](https://cdn.britannica.com/22/129722-050-8F5C2135/Achilles-scene-Patroclus-Homers-Iliad.jpg) The Greek hero Achilles bandaging his friend Patroclus in a scene from Homer's _Iliad_.(more)

Fragmentary post-Homeric epics of varying date and authorship filled the gaps in the accounts of the [Trojan War](https://www.britannica.com/event/Trojan-War) recorded in the _Iliad_ and _Odyssey_. The [_Homeric Hymns_](https://www.britannica.com/art/Homeric-Hymns)—a series of 34 poems addressing the gods by unknown authors but [ascribed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/ascribed) to Homer—are the source of several important religious myths. Many of the [lyric](https://www.britannica.com/art/lyric) poets preserved various myths, but the odes of [Pindar](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pindar) of [Thebes](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thebes-Greece) (flourished 6th–5th century bce) are particularly rich in [myth](https://www.britannica.com/topic/myth) and [legend](https://www.britannica.com/art/legend-literature). The works of the three tragedians— [Aeschylus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aeschylus-Greek-dramatist) (author of the [_Oresteia_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Oresteia) and [_Prometheus Bound_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Prometheus-Bound-play-by-Aeschylus)), [Sophocles](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sophocles) (author of [_Antigone_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Antigone-Greek-mythology) and [_Oedipus Rex_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Oedipus-Rex-play-by-Sophocles)), and [Euripides](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Euripides) (author of [_Medea_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Medea-play-by-Euripides) and [_Andromache_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Andromache-play-by-Euripides)), all of the 5th century bce—are remarkable for the variety of the traditions they preserve.

In [Hellenistic](https://www.britannica.com/event/Hellenistic-Age) times (323–30 bce) [Callimachus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Callimachus-Greek-poet-and-scholar), a 3rd-century-bce poet and scholar in [Alexandria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alexandria-Egypt), recorded many obscure myths; his contemporary, the mythographer [Euhemerus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Euhemerus-Greek-mythographer), suggested that the gods were originally human, a view known as [Euhemerism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Euhemerism). [Apollonius of Rhodes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Apollonius-of-Rhodes), another scholar of the 3rd century bce, preserved the fullest account of [Jason](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jason-Greek-mythology) and the [Argonauts](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Argonaut-Greek-mythology) in search of the Golden Fleece.

In the period of the [Roman Empire](https://www.britannica.com/art/Latin-literature), the _Geography_ of [Strabo](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Strabo) (1st century bce), the _Library_ (attributed to a 2nd-century-ce scholar), the antiquarian writings of the Greek biographer [Plutarch](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Plutarch), and the works of [Pausanias](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pausanias-Greek-geographer), a 2nd-century-ce historian, as well as the _Latin Genealogies_ of Hyginus, a 2nd-century-ce mythographer, have provided valuable sources in Latin of later Greek mythology.

## Archaeological discoveries

[![Crete: Palace of King Minos](https://cdn.britannica.com/67/196067-050-00889BAD/entrance-palace-ruins-King-Minos-Knossos-Crete.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/67/196067-050-00889BAD/entrance-palace-ruins-King-Minos-Knossos-Crete.jpg)

[Crete: Palace of King Minos](https://cdn.britannica.com/67/196067-050-00889BAD/entrance-palace-ruins-King-Minos-Knossos-Crete.jpg) The entrance of the ancient palace of King Minos at the ruins of Knossos on Crete has been partially restored. (more)

The discovery of the [Mycenaean civilization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mycenaean-civilization) by [Heinrich Schliemann](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Heinrich-Schliemann), a 19th-century [German](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany) amateur archaeologist, and the discovery of the [Minoan civilization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Minoan-civilization) in [Crete](https://www.britannica.com/place/Crete) (from which the [Mycenaean](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mycenaean) ultimately derived) by [Sir Arthur Evans](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Arthur-Evans), a 20th-century [English](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) archaeologist, are essential to the 21st-century understanding of the development of [myth](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myth) and [ritual](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ritual) in the Greek world. Such discoveries [illuminated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/illuminated) aspects of Minoan culture from about 2200 to 1450 bce and Mycenaean culture from about 1600 to 1200 bce; those eras were followed by a Dark Age that lasted until about 800 bce. Much of the evidence about myth and ritual at Mycenaean and Minoan sites is derived from the surviving architecture and monuments, because the [Linear B](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Linear-A) script (an ancient form of [Greek](https://www.britannica.com/art/Greek-pottery) found in both Crete and Greece) was mainly used to record inventories.

Geometric designs on pottery of the 8th century bce depict scenes from the [Trojan](https://www.britannica.com/event/Trojan-War) cycle, as well as the adventures of [Heracles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Heracles). The extreme formality of the style, however, renders much of the identification difficult, and there is no inscriptional evidence accompanying the designs to assist scholars in identification and interpretation. In the succeeding [Archaic](https://www.britannica.com/art/Archaic-period) (c. 750–c. 500 bce), Classical (c. 480–323 bce), and Hellenistic periods, Homeric and various other mythological scenes appear to [supplement](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/supplement) the existing literary evidence.

### What is a werewolf?

In European [folklore](https://www.britannica.com/art/folk-literature), a werewolf is a man who turns into a [wolf](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wolf) at night and devours animals, people, or corpses but returns to human form by day.

### Where does werewolf lore come from?

Stories of men turning into beasts go back to antiquity. In parts of ancient [Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Greece), werewolf [myths](https://www.britannica.com/topic/myth), presumably stemming from prehistoric times, became linked with the [Olympian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-mythology) religion. In [ancient Rome](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Rome), anyone who was supposed to have been turned into a wolf by means of magic spells or herbs was called _versipellis_ (“turnskin”).

### What does a werewolf look like?

Werewolves have been depicted in different ways throughout history. In ancient [Arcadian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arcadia-region-Greece) legends, human beings transform fully into, and associate with, [wolves](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wolf). In other traditions, particularly Hollywood horror films, werewolves are humanoid, often featuring a canine head, claws, and a fur-covered human body.

**werewolf**, in popular [legend](https://www.britannica.com/art/legend-literature), a human who can shape-shift into a [wolf](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wolf) or a hybrid wolf-human form. In many werewolf stories this transformation occurs at night, often under the influence of a [full moon](https://www.britannica.com/science/full-Moon-lunar-phase). Belief in werewolves has waxed and waned over millennia, although “sightings” are still sometimes reported. In modern times werewolves feature prominently in media, from [books](https://www.britannica.com/topic/book-publication) and [films](https://www.britannica.com/art/film) to [popular music](https://www.britannica.com/art/popular-music) and [video games](https://www.britannica.com/topic/role-playing-video-game).

## Characteristics

[![Lucas Cranach the Elder: The Werewolf or the Cannibal](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/263713-050-459C0F29/The-werewolf-or-cannibal-woodcut-Lucas-Cranach-the-elder-1500s.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/263713-050-459C0F29/The-werewolf-or-cannibal-woodcut-Lucas-Cranach-the-elder-1500s.jpg)

[Lucas Cranach the Elder: _The Werewolf or the Cannibal_](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/263713-050-459C0F29/The-werewolf-or-cannibal-woodcut-Lucas-Cranach-the-elder-1500s.jpg) _The Werewolf or the Cannibal_, woodcut by Lucas Cranach the Elder, 16th century; in the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York City.(more)

According to legend, most werewolves transform fully from human form to wolf form. Indeed, werewolves are typically described as looking much like real wolves, with fur-covered bodies, pointed ears, long snouts, razor-sharp fangs and claws, and bushy tails. Like real wolves, werewolves howl, growl, and hunt for prey. Most stories depict werewolves as monstrous, devouring animals, people, and corpses alike. In European [folklore](https://www.britannica.com/topic/folklore-academic-discipline), the human form of a werewolf is said to have certain telltale characteristics, such as a unibrow (i.e., eyebrows that join together at the center), curved fingernails, or hair growing beneath the person’s tongue. Similarly, some werewolves in modern fiction possess hybrid or in-between forms that retain both wolf and human characteristics or become more monstrous than either form on its own.

In some stories, a human becomes a werewolf because of a curse or because of having been bitten or scratched by another werewolf. Alternatively, the person may have drunk water from a wolf’s paw print, been conceived during a new moon, or made a pact with the [Devil](https://www.britannica.com/topic/devil). Other stories tell of people who are able to assume a wolf’s form through the use of a magical cloak or a sash made from a wolf’s pelt. According to folklorist Carol Rose, in ancient [Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece) it was believed that eating wolf’s meat mixed with human flesh could irreversibly change a person into a werewolf. Although in some stories the werewolf may shape-shift at will, the most well-known werewolf tales claim that the transformation happens involuntarily and only in the light of a full [moon](https://www.britannica.com/science/moon-natural-satellite). Further, when the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun) rises the next day, the person may not remember having turned into a werewolf, but any injuries sustained by the werewolf while marauding at night are typically intact.

## Methods of killing or curing a werewolf

In modern tales the most surefire way to destroy a werewolf is to use a weapon made of [silver](https://www.britannica.com/science/silver), usually a [bullet](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bullet). This belief may have been inspired by a real-life incident in [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) in 1767, when a farmer is said to have killed a ferocious “beast”—possibly a wolf or a large [dog](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dog)—using silver bullets. Another method is to find the wolf’s pelt, which some werewolves were said to have hidden during the daytime while they were in their human form, and then destroy it. This could be tricky, however, as werewolf pelts were believed to be impervious to most methods of destruction.

Some so-called werewolves were said to have been cured of their affliction by [exorcism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/exorcism) or by dubious medical treatments such as [bloodletting](https://www.britannica.com/science/bloodletting), induced [vomiting,](https://www.britannica.com/science/vomiting) or drinking [vinegar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vinegar).

## Medical theories

Some scholars think that werewolf stories may have been inspired by real-life medical conditions. The most commonly suggested conditions are [hypertrichosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/hypertrichosis), a [congenital disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/congenital-disorder) that causes excessive hair growth; [rabies](https://www.britannica.com/science/rabies), which is contracted from animal bites and can cause a person to become aggressive and foam at the mouth; and [mental illnesses](https://www.britannica.com/science/mental-disorder) such as [lycanthropy](https://www.britannica.com/science/lycanthropy) (named for the Greek legend of [Lycaon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lycaon-Greek-mythology), discussed below), the belief that one can change into a wolf.

There have also been stories of human children who were believed to have grown up in the wild and were said to have been “raised by wolves.” These so-called “ [feral children](https://www.britannica.com/topic/feral-children)” were described as exhibiting animal-like behavior such as running on all fours and foraging and hunting for food, as well as having exceptional [hearing](https://www.britannica.com/science/hearing-sense) and possessing no [language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/language). One of the most well-known of these children was a boy called “Peter the Wild Boy,” who in 1725 was found living alone in a [forest](https://www.britannica.com/science/forest) in [Hanover](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hanover-historical-state-Germany), [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany). Modern doctors believe that Peter’s behavior was the result of Pitt-Hopkins syndrome, a rare genetic condition that causes impaired [speech](https://www.britannica.com/topic/speech-language), [seizures](https://www.britannica.com/science/seizure), distinctive facial features, difficulty [breathing](https://www.britannica.com/science/breathing), poor coordination, and intellectual challenges.

## Werewolves in ancient history

Werewolf Names

The [English](https://www.britannica.com/topic/English-language) word _werewolf_ comes from the [Old English](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Old-English-language) term _wer_, meaning “man,” added to _wolf_. In [ancient Rome](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Rome) anyone who was believed to have been turned into a wolf by means of magic spells or [herbs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/spice-food) was called _versipellis_ (“turnskin”). Other names for werewolf include:

- _hombre lobo_ or _lícantropo_ ( [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain))

- _loup-garou_ ( [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France))

- _lupo mannaro_ ( [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy))

- _rougarou_ ( [Louisiana](https://www.britannica.com/place/Louisiana-state), [U.S.](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States))

- _varulv_ ( [Norway](https://www.britannica.com/place/Norway), [Sweden](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sweden), and [Denmark](https://www.britannica.com/place/Denmark))

- _vulkodlak_ ( [Russia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Russia))


Werewolves and other shape-shifters appear in folklore worldwide. The earliest known tale of a human-to-wolf transformation is found in the [_Epic of Gilgamesh_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Epic-of-Gilgamesh), a Mesopotamian poem composed between 2000 bce and about 700 bce. In the poem, the goddess [Ishtar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ishtar-Mesopotamian-goddess) is rejected by the warrior-king [Gilgamesh](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gilgamesh) because she is said to have transformed a shepherd into a wolf after having tired of his affections. [Greek mythology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-mythology) tells of a king named [Lycaon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lycaon-Greek-mythology) who angered the god [Zeus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zeus) for attempting to feed him human flesh. Lycaon was punished by being transformed into a wolf. Lycaon’s name comes from _lykos_, the [Greek](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-language) word for “wolf,” as does the word [_lycanthropy_](https://www.britannica.com/science/lycanthropy), which originally referred to a psychiatric disorder experienced by people who believed they could become a wolf. The term also refers to the fantastical shifting from human to wolf.

About 425 bce Greek historian [Herodotus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Herodotus-Greek-historian) wrote of a tribe from Scythia (in what is now [Russia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Russia)) called the Neuri, whom he claimed transformed into wolves several days a year. Werewolves also feature in early Norse [sagas](https://www.britannica.com/art/saga); in the _[Vǫlsunga saga](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Volsunga-saga)_ (c. 13th century), a father and son discover wolf pelts that give the wearers the power to transform into wolves. This story resembles that of Norse [berserkers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/berserker), fierce warriors who worshiped the god [Odin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Odin-Norse-deity) and were said to whip themselves up into a fighting frenzy by wearing the skins of [bears](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bear) and wolves, which allowed them to channel the animals’ spirits in battle.

## Werewolf trials in Europe

[![Illustration of a werewolf's crimes and his trial](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/263808-050-7FD2AB22/Werewolf-16th-century-manuscript-Johann-Jakob-Wick.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/263808-050-7FD2AB22/Werewolf-16th-century-manuscript-Johann-Jakob-Wick.jpg)

[Illustration of a werewolf's crimes and his trial](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/263808-050-7FD2AB22/Werewolf-16th-century-manuscript-Johann-Jakob-Wick.jpg) Hand-colored manuscript depicting a man being tortured and executed (left) after changing himself into a wolf (right) and eating 16 children, by Johann Jakob Wick, 1580; in _Sammlung von Nachrichten zur Zeitgeschichte aus den Jahren_ in the Zentralbibliothek Zürich.(more)

As with accusations of [vampirism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vampire) and [witchcraft](https://www.britannica.com/topic/witchcraft), accusations of lycanthropy have been made during some periods of history to denounce personal or political rivals or to otherwise punish or ostracize certain members of society, from [hermits](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hermit-religion) and beggars to suspected [pedophiles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pedophilia) and [serial killers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/serial-murder). Werewolf trials were held in parts of [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) from the 15th to the 17th century. However, because the confessions of these people were often made under duress, it is difficult to know whether they actually committed the crimes of which they were accused. In France in the 16th century, several men claimed to have possessed magical ointment that turned them into wolves. They confessed to having killed and eaten numerous children and were burned at the stake, which was considered to be one of the only effective methods for killing a werewolf.

One of the most notorious werewolf trials was that of a wealthy farmer named Peter Stubbe (or Stumpp) in Germany in 1589. Local hunters claimed that they had seen him transform from a wolf into a human. Through means of [torture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/torture), Peter confessed to having a magic belt that he used to transform into a werewolf and hunt and eat people. His gruesome execution involved—in part—being strapped to a cart wheel, beheaded, and burned at the stake.

## Werewolves in literature and popular culture

By the 19th century, tales of werewolves became popular in [literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/literature). Writers such as [Charles Maturin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Robert-Maturin) ( [_Melmoth the Wanderer_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Melmoth-the-Wanderer), 1820), [Alexandre Dumas](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alexandre-Dumas-pere) ( _The Wolf-Leader_, 1857), and [Rudyard Kipling](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rudyard-Kipling) (“ [The Mark of the Beast](https://www.kiplingsociety.co.uk/tale/the-mark-of-the-beast.htm),” 1890) penned werewolf stories or included werewolves in works about other fearsome figures. An especially influential [novel](https://www.britannica.com/art/novel) in the werewolf tradition is _The Werewolf of Paris_ (1933) by Guy Endore, the importance of which has been likened to that of [Bram Stoker](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bram-Stoker)’s [_Dracula_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dracula-novel) (1897) for [vampires](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vampire). Endore’s novel was later adapted into the film _The Curse of the Werewolf_ (1961) by renowned [horror](https://www.britannica.com/art/horror-film) studio [Hammer Films](https://www.britannica.com/money/Hammer-Films).

[![Werewolf of London](https://cdn.britannica.com/12/263712-050-BF1A26BA/Werewolf-of-London-Henry-Hull-Lester-Mathews-1935-movie.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/12/263712-050-BF1A26BA/Werewolf-of-London-Henry-Hull-Lester-Mathews-1935-movie.jpg)

One of the first big films about a werewolf was _Werewolf of London_ (1935), starring Henry Hull. Other notable werewolf films include _The Wolf Man_ (1941), starring Lon Chaney, Jr., in the titular role; John Landis’s horror-comedy _An American Werewolf in London_ (1981); and _The Howling_ (1981) and its sequels, which are based on a series of books by Gary Brandner.

The werewolf has also been used as a [metaphor](https://www.britannica.com/art/metaphor) for the drastic and sometimes unpredictable changes of [puberty](https://www.britannica.com/science/puberty), as seen in the campy [B-movie](https://www.britannica.com/art/B-film) _I Was a Teenage Werewolf_ (1957), starring [Michael Landon](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Michael-Landon); the coming-of-age comedy _Teen Wolf_ (1985), starring [Michael J. Fox](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Michael-J-Fox); and the darkly humorous horror film _Ginger Snaps_ (2000).

In [popular music](https://www.britannica.com/art/popular-music), werewolves are the star of [Warren Zevon](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Warren-Zevon)’s satirical hit song “Werewolves of London” (1978). [Michael Jackson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Michael-Jackson)’s epic music video (directed by Landis) for the song “Thriller” (1982) features a segment in which Jackson transforms into a werewolf. One of [radio](https://www.britannica.com/topic/radio)’s most famous [deejays](https://www.britannica.com/art/disc-jockey) in the 1960s and ’70s, the strangle-voiced Robert Weston Smith, went by the on-air moniker [Wolfman Jack](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wolfman-Jack-1688508). He often howled during his broadcast and had a memorable cameo in [George Lucas](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Lucas)’s film _American Graffiti_ (1973).

Contemporary literature also features its share of werewolves. [Horror](https://www.britannica.com/art/horror-story) legend [Stephen King](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Stephen-King) dipped into werewolf tales with his novel _The Cycle of the Werewolf_ (1983). In the [_Harry Potter_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Harry-Potter) series (1997–2007) by [J.K. Rowling](https://www.britannica.com/biography/J-K-Rowling), Harry’s friend and mentor Remus Lupin is revealed to be a werewolf who must drink a special potion to keep his human wits when he changes during the full moon. The main love triangle of the [_Twilight_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Twilight-Saga) series of books (2005–20) by [Stephenie Meyer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Stephenie-Meyer) revolves around protagonist Bella and her suitors Edward, a vampire, and Jacob, a werewolf.

The _Twilight_ series is one of a number of examples from modern media in which werewolves are depicted as being in opposition to vampires, as in the film _Underworld_ (2003) and its sequels and the horror mockumentary _What We Do in the Shadows_ (2014) and its series (2019–24). However, in the horror film _The Wolfman_ (2010) starring [Benicio Del Toro](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Benicio-Del-Toro) and [Anthony Hopkins](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Anthony-Hopkins), two werewolves go head to head in a fight to the death.

Werewolves have also appeared in [video games](https://www.britannica.com/topic/electronic-game) such as [_World of Warcraft_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/World-of-Warcraft) (2004), [_The Legend of Zelda: Twilight Princess_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Legend-of-Zelda) (2006), and _The Wolf Among Us_ (2013), as well as the tabletop role-playing games [_Dungeons & Dragons_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dungeons-and-Dragons) and _Werewolf: The Apocalypse_.

**lycanthropy**, (from Greek _lykos,_ “wolf ”; _anthropos,_ “man”), [mental disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/mental-disorder) in which the patient believes that he is a wolf or some other nonhuman animal. Undoubtedly [stimulated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/stimulated) by the once widespread superstition that lycanthropy is a supernatural condition in which men actually assume the physical form of werewolves or other animals, the [delusion](https://www.britannica.com/science/delusion) has been most likely to occur among people who believe in reincarnation and the transmigration of souls. Usually, a person is deemed to take the form of the most dangerous beast of prey of the region: the wolf or bear in Europe and northern Asia, the hyena or leopard in Africa, and the tiger in India, China, Japan, and elsewhere in Asia; but other animals are mentioned too. Both the superstition and the psychiatric disorder are linked with belief in animal guardian spirits, vampires, totemism, witches, and werewolves. The folklore, fairy tales, and [legends](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legends) of many nations and peoples show evidence of lycanthropic belief.

Stories of men turning into beasts go back to antiquity. In parts of [ancient Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Greece), werewolf [myths](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myths), presumably stemming from prehistoric times, became linked with the Olympian religion. In [Arcadia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arcadia-region-Greece), a region plagued by wolves, there was a cult of the Wolf-Zeus. Mount Lycaeus was the scene of a yearly gathering at which the priests were said to prepare a sacrificial feast that included meat mixed with human parts. According to [legend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legend), whoever tasted it became a wolf and could not turn back into a man unless he abstained from human flesh for nine years.

The Romans also knew of this superstition. Anyone who was supposed to have been turned into a wolf by means of magic spells or herbs was called _versipellis_ (“turnskin”) by the Romans.

Stories about the werewolf (in French, _loup-garou_) were widely believed in [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) during the [Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages). Outlaws and bandits played on these superstitions by sometimes wearing wolfskins over their armour. At that time people were unusually prone to develop the [delusion](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/delusion) that they themselves were wolves; suspected lycanthropists were burned alive if convicted. Only rarely was their condition recognized as a psychological disturbance. Although the superstition is no longer common, traces still linger in some primitive and isolated areas. _See also_ [werewolf](https://www.britannica.com/art/werewolf).

**hydroelectric power**, [electricity](https://www.britannica.com/science/electricity) produced from generators driven by [turbines](https://www.britannica.com/technology/turbine) that [convert](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/convert) the [potential energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/potential-energy) of falling or fast-flowing [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) into [mechanical energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/mechanical-energy). In the early 21st century, hydroelectric power was the most widely utilized form of [renewable energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/renewable-energy); in 2019 it accounted for more than 18 percent of the world’s total [power](https://www.britannica.com/science/power-physics) generation capacity.

- **1984**: [Itaipú Dam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Itaipu-Dam) becomes the world’s largest hydroelectric power station

- **2000**: Islay LIMPET in Scotland becomes one of the world’s first commercial [wave-power](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-power) stations

- **2003**: [Three Gorges Dam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Three-Gorges-Dam) begins operation

- **2012**: Three [Gorges](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Gorges) Dam becomes the world’s largest hydroelectric power station

- **2020s**: Hydropower remains the leading source of [renewable](https://www.britannica.com/science/renewable-energy) electricity worldwide


## Dams and hydroelectricity

[![hydroelectric turbine generators](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/192598-050-C2D10202/turbine-generators.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/192598-050-C2D10202/turbine-generators.jpg)

[hydroelectric turbine generators](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/192598-050-C2D10202/turbine-generators.jpg)(more)

For the generation of hydroelectric power at [dams](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dam-engineering), water is collected or stored at a higher elevation and led downward through large pipes or tunnels (penstocks) to a lower elevation; the difference in these two elevations is known as the [head](https://www.britannica.com/technology/head-hydroelectric-power). At the end of its passage down the pipes, the falling water causes [turbines](https://www.britannica.com/technology/turbine) to rotate. The turbines in turn drive [generators](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electric-generator), which convert the turbines’ mechanical energy into electricity. [Transformers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/transformer-electronics) are then used to convert the alternating voltage suitable for the generators to a higher voltage suitable for long-distance transmission. The structure that houses the turbines and generators, and into which the pipes or penstocks feed, is called the powerhouse.

[![Hoover Dam](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/156458-050-68649A2F/Hoover-Dam-border-Colorado-River-Arizona-Nevada.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/156458-050-68649A2F/Hoover-Dam-border-Colorado-River-Arizona-Nevada.jpg)

[Hoover Dam](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/156458-050-68649A2F/Hoover-Dam-border-Colorado-River-Arizona-Nevada.jpg) Hoover Dam, on the Colorado River at the Arizona-Nevada border, U.S.(more)

Hydroelectric power plants are usually located in [dams](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dam-engineering) that impound [rivers](https://www.britannica.com/science/river), thereby raising the level of the water behind the [dam](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dam-engineering) and creating as high a head as is [feasible](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/feasible). The potential power that can be derived from a volume of water is directly proportional to the working head, so that a high-head installation requires a smaller volume of water than a low-head installation to produce an equal amount of power. In some dams, the powerhouse is constructed on one flank of the dam, part of the dam being used as a [spillway](https://www.britannica.com/technology/spillway-engineering) over which excess water is discharged in times of flood. Where the river flows in a narrow steep gorge, the powerhouse may be located within the dam itself.

In most [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) the demand for [electric power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electric-power) varies considerably at different times of the day. To even the load on the generators, [pumped-storage](https://www.britannica.com/science/pumped-storage-system) hydroelectric stations are occasionally built. During off-peak periods, some of the extra power available is supplied to the generator operating as a motor, driving the [turbine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/turbine) to [pump](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pump) water into an elevated [reservoir](https://www.britannica.com/technology/reservoir). Then, during periods of peak demand, the water is allowed to flow down again through the turbine to generate electrical [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy). Pumped-storage systems are efficient and provide an economical way to meet peak loads.

## Tidal and wave power

![Tidal power](https://cdn.britannica.com/49/121649-050-9F923E19/Diagram-power-barrage.jpg)

[Tidal power](https://cdn.britannica.com/49/121649-050-9F923E19/Diagram-power-barrage.jpg) Diagram of a tidal power barrage.(more)

In certain coastal areas, such as the [Rance River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Rance-River) [estuary](https://www.britannica.com/science/estuary) in [Brittany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brittany-region-France), [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), hydroelectric power plants have been constructed to take advantage of the rise and fall of [tides](https://www.britannica.com/science/tide). When the tide comes in, water is impounded in one or more [reservoirs](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/reservoirs). At low tide, the water in these reservoirs is released to drive hydraulic turbines and their coupled electric generators ( _see_ [tidal power](https://www.britannica.com/science/tidal-power)).

Similarly, [wave power](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-power) generates electricity by harnessing the up-and-down motion of ocean waves. Wave power is typically produced by floating turbine platforms or buoys that rise and fall with the swells. The world’s first operational wave power generator is located off the coast of Aguçadora, Portugal, and produces as much as 2.25 megawatts from three huge jointed tubes that float on the surface of the [Atlantic Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean) ( _see_ [wave power](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-power)).

Falling water is one of the three principal sources of energy used to generate electric power, the other two being [fossil fuels](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil-fuel) and [nuclear fuels](https://www.britannica.com/science/nuclear-energy). Hydroelectric power has certain advantages over these other sources. It is continually renewable owing to the recurring nature of the [hydrologic cycle](https://www.britannica.com/science/water-cycle). It does not produce thermal [pollution](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollution-environment). (However, some [dams](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/dams) can produce [methane](https://www.britannica.com/science/methane) from the decomposition of vegetation under water.) Hydroelectric power from dams is a preferred energy source in areas with heavy rainfall and with hilly or mountainous regions that are in reasonably close proximity to the main load centers. Some large hydro sites that are remote from load centers may be sufficiently attractive to justify the long high-voltage transmission lines. Small local hydro sites may also be economical, particularly if they combine storage of water during light loads with electricity production during peaks.

Many of the negative environmental impacts of hydroelectric power come from the associated dams, which can interrupt the migrations of spawning [fish](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fish), such as [salmon](https://www.britannica.com/animal/salmon), and permanently submerge or displace ecological and human communities as the reservoirs fill. A movement to decommission and remove hydroelectric dams in sensitive ecological areas has grown in the 21st century. In addition, hydroelectric dams are [vulnerable](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vulnerable) to [water scarcity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/water-scarcity). In August 2021 [Oroville Dam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Oroville-Dam), one of the largest hydroelectric power plants in California, was forced to shut down due to historic [drought](https://www.britannica.com/science/drought) conditions in the region.

### What is renewable energy?

Renewable energy is usable energy derived from sources that are naturally replenished, such as [solar energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-energy) from the Sun, [wind power](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind-power) from wind, [hydroelectric power](https://www.britannica.com/science/renewable-energy/Introduction#ref1) from rivers, [geothermal energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/renewable-energy/Introduction#ref1) from hot springs, [tidal power](https://www.britannica.com/science/renewable-energy/Introduction#ref1) from tides, and [biofuels](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biofuel). Often referred to as clean energy, it comes from natural sources or processes that are constantly replenished within human lifetimes. Wind and solar power are virtually nonpolluting, which sharply contrasts with burning fossil fuels.

### How is renewable energy different from nonrenewable energy?

[Renewable energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/renewable-energy) comes from sources that are naturally replenished, such as solar, wind, and hydroelectric power. Nonrenewable energy sources, such as [fossil fuels](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil-fuel) (coal, oil, and natural gas), are finite and cannot be easily replenished. The use of fossil fuels has negative environmental impacts, including air pollution and [global warming](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil-fuel/Introduction#ref368639). Renewable energy is often referred to as clean energy because it generates little pollution.

### What are the main types of renewable energy?

The major types of renewable energy are:

- [Solar power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/solar-power): Uses sunlight to generate electricity or heat through photovoltaic panels and solar thermal technologies

- [Wind power](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind-power): Generates electricity using wind turbines driven by moving air

- [Hydroelectric power](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydroelectric-power): Produces electricity by harnessing the energy of flowing or falling water

- [Geothermal power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/geothermal-power): Uses heat from the Earth’s interior for electricity generation and direct heating applications

- [Biofuels](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biofuel): Converts plant material, agricultural waste, and other organic matter into heat, electricity, or transportation fuels


### Is nuclear power considered renewable?

Although [nuclear power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/nuclear-power) is a low-carbon alternative to traditional [fossil fuels](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil-fuel), it is generally not considered renewable because it relies on finite supplies of [uranium](https://www.britannica.com/science/uranium) and other fissile fuels that must be mined and processed. Unlike sunlight, wind, flowing water, and geothermal heat, these resources are not naturally replenished on human timescales.

**renewable energy**, usable [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) [derived](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/derived) from sources that are naturally replenished, including the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun) ( [solar power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/solar-power)), [wind](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind) ( [wind power](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind-power)), [rivers](https://www.britannica.com/science/river) ( [hydroelectric power](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydroelectric-power)), hot springs ( [geothermal energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/geothermal-energy)), tides ( [tidal power](https://www.britannica.com/science/tidal-power)), and [biomass](https://www.britannica.com/science/biomass) ( [biofuels](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biofuel)). Unlike [fossil fuels](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil-fuel), including [natural gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-gas), [coal](https://www.britannica.com/science/coal-fossil-fuel), and [petroleum](https://www.britannica.com/science/petroleum), which are finite and require millions of years to form, renewable energy sources are continually renewed on human timescales. Because they generally produce lower emissions of [greenhouse gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/greenhouse-gas) and [air pollutants](https://www.britannica.com/science/air-pollution) than fossil fuels, renewable-energy technologies have become increasingly important in efforts to combat the growing climate crisis and to meet global energy demands while reducing environmental impacts.

In the early 21st century, renewable energy emerged as one of the fastest-growing sectors of the world energy economy. [Hydroelectric power](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydroelectric-power), [solar power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/solar-power), and [wind power](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind-power) account for most renewable electricity generation, with other forms contributing in regions where local conditions are favorable, such as geothermal energy in volcanically active areas and tidal power along suitable coastlines. The expansion of renewable energy has been driven by technological [innovation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/innovation), declining costs, energy-security concerns, and efforts to limit [climate change](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-change).

## The problems of fossil fuels

[![The Transition to Renewable Energy Explained by Phil the Fixer](https://cdn.britannica.com/10/222610-138-F0268996/britannica-insights-Phil-the-Fixer-renewable-energy.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/britannica-insights-Phil-the-Fixer-renewable-energy/-255686)

The Transition to Renewable Energy Explained by Phil the FixerLearn more about climate change and the transition to renewable energy in this interview with Phil the Fixer.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/science/renewable-energy/images-videos)

At the beginning of the 21st century, about 80 percent of the world’s energy supply was derived from [fossil fuels](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil-fuel) such as [coal](https://www.britannica.com/science/coal-fossil-fuel), [petroleum](https://www.britannica.com/science/petroleum), and [natural gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-gas). Fossil fuel [combustion](https://www.britannica.com/science/combustion) has a number of negative environmental [consequences](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/consequences). Furthermore, the burning of fossil fuels releases [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide) (CO2), one of the main [greenhouse gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/greenhouse-gas) that cause [global warming](https://www.britannica.com/science/global-warming). Carbon dioxide released from fossil fuel combustion had been stored underground for millions of years in coal, petroleum, and natural gas deposits. Burning these fuels transfers that stored carbon rapidly into the atmosphere, altering Earth’s [carbon cycle](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-cycle) and contributing to the [greenhouse effect](https://www.britannica.com/science/greenhouse-effect).

Fossil-fueled power plants emit air pollutants such as [sulfur dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur-oxide), [particulate matter](https://www.britannica.com/science/particulate-matter), [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen) oxides, and toxic heavy metals such as [mercury](https://www.britannica.com/science/mercury-chemical-element), [chromium](https://www.britannica.com/science/chromium), and [arsenic](https://www.britannica.com/science/arsenic). Fossil-fueled vehicles emit nitrogen oxides, [carbon monoxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-monoxide), and particulate matter. Exposure to these pollutants can cause [heart disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart-disease), [asthma](https://www.britannica.com/science/asthma), and other human health problems. In addition, emissions from fossil fuel combustion are responsible for [acid rain](https://www.britannica.com/science/acid-rain), which has led to the acidification of many lakes and consequent damage to aquatic life, leaf damage in many forests, and the production of [smog](https://www.britannica.com/science/smog) in or near many urban areas.

Fossil fuels are finite resources; most estimates suggest that the proven reserves of oil are large enough to meet global demand at least until the middle of the 21st century. Depleted reserves of conventional oil fields have caused some producers to turn to [heavy oil and tar sands](https://www.britannica.com/science/heavy-oil), the recovery and refinement of which carry significant ecological and economic costs. Although the petroleum [industry](https://www.britannica.com/money/industry) has long minimized the ecological and climate impacts of fossil fuel combustion, the depletion of conventional sources of [crude oil](https://www.britannica.com/science/crude-oil) and other fossil fuels remains an incentive to invest in [alternative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alternative) energies. _See also_ [peak oil theory](https://www.britannica.com/topic/peak-oil-theory).

In a historic first, renewable energy surpassed coal as the world’s leading source of electricity in the first half of 2025.

Renewable energy sources accounted for nearly 20 percent of global energy [consumption](https://www.britannica.com/money/consumption) at the beginning of the 21st century, largely from traditional uses of biomass such as wood for heating and [cooking](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cooking). Some energy analysts consider [nuclear power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/nuclear-power) to be a form of renewable energy because of its low [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element) emissions; nuclear power generated 10.6 percent of the world’s electricity in 2015.

Growth in wind power [exceeded](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/exceeded) 20 percent and photovoltaics grew at 30 percent annually in the 1990s, and renewable energy technologies continued to expand throughout the early 21st century. Between 2001 and 2017 world total installed wind power capacity increased by a factor of 22, growing from 23,900 to 539,581 megawatts. Photovoltaic capacity also expanded, increasing by 50 percent in 2016 alone. The [European Union](https://www.britannica.com/topic/European-Union) (EU), which produced an estimated 6.38 percent of its energy from renewable sources in 2005, adopted a goal in 2007 to raise that figure to 20 percent by 2020. By 2016 some 17 percent of the EU’s energy came from renewable sources. The goal also included plans to cut emissions of carbon dioxide by 20 percent and to ensure that 10 percent of all fuel [consumption](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consumption) comes from [biofuels](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biofuel). The EU was well on its way to achieving those targets by 2017. Between 1990 and 2016 the countries of the EU reduced carbon emissions by 23 percent and increased [biofuel](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biofuel) production to 5.5 percent of all fuels consumed in the region. In the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) numerous states have responded to concerns over climate change and reliance on imported [fossil fuels](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil-fuel) by setting goals to increase renewable energy over time. For example, [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state) required its major utility companies to produce 20 percent of their electricity from renewable sources by 2010, and by the end of that year California [utilities](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/utilities) were within 1 percent of the goal. In 2008 California increased this requirement to 33 percent by 2020, and in 2017 the state further increased its renewable-use target to 50 percent by 2030.

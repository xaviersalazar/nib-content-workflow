- What is an antibiotic?







An antibiotic is a chemical compound that inhibits or kills microorganisms. Specifically, it refers to antibacterial substances like penicillin, cephalosporin, and ciprofloxacin, used to treat or prevent bacterial infections. These can be administered orally, topically, or by injection. While primarily effective against bacteria, antibiotics are sometimes used for protozoal infections. The term "antibiotic" strictly applies to drugs derived from microorganisms or their synthetic forms, though some definitions include purely synthetic compounds. The discovery of penicillin in 1928 by Alexander Fleming marked a significant advancement in treating bacterial infections.

- How do antibiotics work inside the body?







Antibiotics work by targeting specific mechanisms within bacteria that are essential for their survival and reproduction. Many antibiotics, such as penicillins and cephalosporins, inhibit the synthesis of the bacterial cell wall, leading to cell death. Others interfere with protein synthesis, a vital process for bacterial growth. Some antibiotics disrupt the bacterial cell membrane, causing essential components to leak out. Additionally, certain antibiotics act as competitive inhibitors in the synthesis of folic acid, a crucial step for bacterial nucleic acid production. It is important to note that antibiotics are ineffective against viruses.

- What are some examples of common antibiotics?







Common antibiotics include penicillins, cephalosporins, macrolides, and fluoroquinolones. Penicillins, like amoxicillin and penicillin G, are derived from _Penicillium_ mold and inhibit bacterial cell wall synthesis. Cephalosporins, such as cephalexin and ceftriaxone, also target cell wall synthesis and are often used for patients allergic to penicillin. Macrolides, including erythromycin, azithromycin, and clarithromycin, inhibit bacterial protein synthesis. Fluoroquinolones, like ciprofloxacin, interfere with bacterial DNA replication. These broad-spectrum antibiotics are crucial in treating a wide range of bacterial infections.

- Why is it important to take antibiotics exactly as prescribed?







It is crucial to take antibiotics exactly as prescribed to prevent the development of antibiotic resistance. When bacteria are exposed to antibiotics, resistant strains can survive and multiply, making infections untreatable and potentially life-threatening. This resistance can arise through genetic mutations or the acquisition of new genetic material by bacteria. Completing the full course of antibiotics ensures that all bacteria causing the infection are eliminated, minimizing the opportunity for resistant strains to emerge and spread.

- What can happen if bacteria become resistant to antibiotics?







If bacteria become resistant to antibiotics, infections may become untreatable and life-threatening. This resistance occurs when bacteria develop mutations or acquire new genetic material that allows them to withstand the effects of antibiotic drugs.



Mechanisms of resistance include:


\\* Producing enzymes that inactivate the antibiotic.


\\* Altering the target of the drug within the bacterial cell.


\\* Activating pumps that expel the drug from the cell.


\\* Modifying cell walls to prevent drug entry.



The overuse and improper use of antibiotics accelerate the evolution of these resistant strains, making infections like multidrug-resistant tuberculosis (MDR-TB) particularly dangerous and difficult to treat.

**antibiotic**, chemical substance produced by a living organism, generally a microorganism, that is [detrimental](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/detrimental) to other microorganisms. Antibiotics commonly are produced by [soil](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil) microorganisms and probably represent a means by which organisms in a complex [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment), such as soil, control the growth of competing microorganisms. Microorganisms that produce antibiotics useful in preventing or treating disease include the [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria) and the [fungi](https://www.britannica.com/science/fungus).

Antibiotics came into worldwide prominence with the introduction of [penicillin](https://www.britannica.com/science/penicillin) in 1941. Since then they have revolutionized the [treatment](https://www.britannica.com/science/therapeutics) of bacterial infections in humans and other animals. They are, however, ineffective against [viruses](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus).

## The first antibiotics

In 1928 Scottish bacteriologist [Alexander Fleming](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alexander-Fleming) noticed that colonies of bacteria growing on a [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) plate had been unfavourably affected by a [mold](https://www.britannica.com/science/mold-fungus), _Penicillium notatum_, which had contaminated the culture. A decade later British biochemist [Ernst Chain](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Boris-Chain), Australian pathologist [Howard Florey](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Howard-Florey), and others isolated the ingredient responsible, penicillin, and showed that it was highly effective against many serious bacterial infections. Toward the end of the 1950s scientists experimented with the addition of various chemical groups to the core of the penicillin molecule to generate semisynthetic versions. A range of penicillins thus became available to treat diseases caused by different types of bacteria, including [staphylococci](https://www.britannica.com/science/Staphylococcus), [streptococci](https://www.britannica.com/science/Streptococcus), [pneumococci](https://www.britannica.com/science/pneumococcus), gonococci, and the spirochaetes of [syphilis](https://www.britannica.com/science/syphilis).

Conspicuously unaffected by penicillin was the [tubercle bacillus](https://www.britannica.com/science/Mycobacterium-tuberculosis) ( _Mycobacterium tuberculosis_). This organism, however, turned out to be highly sensitive to [streptomycin](https://www.britannica.com/science/streptomycin), an antibiotic that was isolated from _Streptomyces griseus_ in 1943. As well as being dramatically effective against [tuberculosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/tuberculosis), streptomycin demonstrated activity against many other kinds of bacteria, including the [typhoid fever](https://www.britannica.com/science/typhoid-fever) bacillus. In the 1950s researchers discovered the [cephalosporins](https://www.britannica.com/science/cephalosporin), which are related to penicillins but are produced by the mold _Cephalosporium acremonium_. The following decade scientists discovered a class of antibiotics known as quinolones. Quinolones interrupt the replication of [DNA](https://www.britannica.com/science/DNA)—a crucial step in bacterial reproduction—and have proven useful in treating [urinary tract infections](https://www.britannica.com/science/urinary-tract-infection), infectious [diarrhea](https://www.britannica.com/science/diarrhea), and various other infections involving elements such as [bones](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy) and [white blood cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/white-blood-cell).

## Use and administration of antibiotics

The principle governing the use of antibiotics is to ensure that the patient receives one to which the target bacterium is sensitive, at a high enough concentration to be effective but not cause side effects, and for a sufficient length of time to ensure that the infection is totally [eradicated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/eradicated). Antibiotics vary in their range of action. Some are highly specific. Others, such as the [tetracyclines](https://www.britannica.com/science/tetracycline), act against a broad spectrum of different bacteria. These are particularly useful in combating mixed infections and in treating infections when there is no time to conduct sensitivity tests. While some antibiotics, such as the semisynthetic penicillins and the quinolones, can be taken orally, others must be given by intramuscular or intravenous injection.

## Categories of antibiotics

Antibiotics can be categorized by their spectrum of activity—namely, whether they are narrow-, broad-, or extended-spectrum agents. Narrow-spectrum agents (e.g., penicillin G) affect primarily [gram-positive bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/Gram-positive-bacterium). Broad-spectrum antibiotics, such as tetracyclines and [chloramphenicol](https://www.britannica.com/science/chloramphenicol), affect both gram-positive and some [gram-negative bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/Gram-negative-bacterium). An extended-spectrum antibiotic is one that, as a result of chemical modification, affects additional types of bacteria, usually those that are gram-negative. (The terms _gram-positive_ and _gram-negative_ are used to distinguish between bacteria that have cell walls consisting of a thick meshwork of peptidoglycan \[a peptide-sugar polymer\] and bacteria that have cell walls with only a thin peptidoglycan layer, respectively.)

## Mechanisms of action

Antibiotics produce their effects through a variety of mechanisms of action. A large number work by [inhibiting](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibiting) bacterial [cell wall](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-wall-plant-anatomy) synthesis; these agents are referred to generally as β-lactam antibiotics. Production of the bacterial cell wall involves the partial assembly of wall components inside the cell, transport of these structures through the [cell membrane](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-membrane) to the growing wall, assembly into the wall, and finally cross-linking of the strands of wall material. Antibiotics that [inhibit](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibit) the synthesis of the cell wall have a specific effect on one or another phase. The result is an alteration in the cell wall and shape of the organism and eventually the death of the bacterium.

Other antibiotics, such as the [aminoglycosides](https://www.britannica.com/science/aminoglycoside), chloramphenicol, [erythromycin](https://www.britannica.com/science/erythromycin), and clindamycin, inhibit [protein](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) synthesis in bacteria. The basic process by which bacteria and [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) cells synthesize proteins is similar, but the proteins involved are different. Those antibiotics that are selectively toxic utilize these differences to bind to or inhibit the function of the proteins of the bacterium, thereby preventing the synthesis of new proteins and new bacterial cells.

Antibiotics such as [polymyxin](https://www.britannica.com/science/polymyxin) B and polymyxin E (colistin) bind to [phospholipids](https://www.britannica.com/science/phospholipid) in the cell membrane of the bacterium and interfere with its function as a selective barrier; this allows essential macromolecules in the cell to leak out, resulting in the death of the cell. Because other cells, including human cells, have similar or identical phospholipids, these antibiotics are somewhat toxic.

Some antibiotics, such as the [sulfonamides](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfonamide), are competitive inhibitors of the synthesis of [folic acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/folic-acid) (folate), which is an essential preliminary step in the synthesis of [nucleic acids](https://www.britannica.com/science/nucleic-acid). Sulfonamides are able to inhibit folic acid synthesis because they are similar to an intermediate [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) (para-aminobenzoic acid) that is converted by an [enzyme](https://www.britannica.com/science/enzyme) to folic acid. The similarity in structure between these [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds) results in competition between [para-aminobenzoic acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/para-aminobenzoic-acid) and the [sulfonamide](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfonamide) for the enzyme responsible for converting the intermediate to folic acid. This reaction is reversible by removing the chemical, which results in the inhibition but not the death of the microorganisms. One antibiotic, [rifampin](https://www.britannica.com/science/rifampin), interferes with [ribonucleic acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/RNA) (RNA) synthesis in bacteria by binding to a subunit on the bacterial enzyme responsible for duplication of RNA. Since the [affinity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/affinity) of rifampin is much stronger for the bacterial enzyme than for the human enzyme, the human cells are unaffected at therapeutic doses.

# [Antibiotic resistance](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibiotic-resistance)

A problem that has plagued antibiotic [therapy](https://www.britannica.com/science/therapeutics) from the earliest days is the resistance that [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria) can develop to the [drugs](https://www.britannica.com/science/drug-chemical-agent). An antibiotic may kill virtually all the bacteria causing a disease in a patient, but a few bacteria that are genetically less [vulnerable](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vulnerable) to the effects of the [drug](https://www.britannica.com/science/drug-chemical-agent) may survive. These go on to reproduce or to transfer their resistance to others of their species through processes of [gene](https://www.britannica.com/science/gene) exchange. With their more vulnerable competitors wiped out or reduced in numbers by antibiotics, these resistant strains proliferate. The end result is bacterial infections in humans that are untreatable by one or even several of the antibiotics customarily effective in such cases. The [indiscriminate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/indiscriminate) and inexact use of antibiotics encourages the spread of such bacterial resistance. ( _See_ [antibiotic resistance](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibiotic-resistance).)

Researchers are continually working to discover new antibiotics as a means of overcoming antibiotic resistance. Some potentially effective [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds) that have been discovered include certain bacterial toxins and antimicrobial peptides. Novel treatment strategies, such as combining synergistic antibiotics to boost the killing of bacteria, are also under investigation. It may be possible to introduce compounds into bacterial populations that effectively resensitize the bacteria to existing antibiotic drugs.

## Major antibiotics

Each type of antibiotic has a specific application in [medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/medicine) and can serve as a useful model for exploring the various mechanisms by which antibiotics exert their effects. The following sections focus on the penicillins and cephalosporins, imipenem, the antituberculosis antibiotics, and the agents aztreonam, bacitracin, and vancomycin. These agents and groups of agents further illustrate the chemical and functional [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity) found among the antibiotics.

## [Penicillins](https://www.britannica.com/science/penicillin)

The penicillins have a unique structure, a β-lactam ring, that is responsible for their antibacterial activity. The β-lactam ring interacts with proteins in the bacterial cell responsible for the final step in the assembly of the [cell wall](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-wall-plant-anatomy).

The penicillins can be divided into two groups: the naturally occurring penicillins (penicillin G, [penicillin](https://www.britannica.com/science/penicillin) V, and benzathine penicillin) and the semisynthetic penicillins. The semisynthetic penicillins are produced by growing the mold _[Penicillium](https://www.britannica.com/science/Penicillium)_ under conditions whereby only the basic molecule (6-aminopenicillanic acid) is produced. By adding certain chemical groups to this molecule, several different semisynthetic penicillins are produced that vary in resistance to [degradation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/degradation) by β-lactamase ( [penicillinase](https://www.britannica.com/science/penicillinase)), an enzyme that specifically breaks the β-lactam ring, thereby inactivating the antibiotic. In addition, the antibacterial spectrum of activity and pharmacological properties of the natural penicillins can be changed and improved by these chemical modifications. The addition of a β-lactamase inhibitor, such as clavulanic acid, to a penicillin dramatically improves the effectiveness of the antibiotic. Several naturally occurring inhibitors have been isolated, and others have been chemically synthesized.

The naturally occurring penicillins remain the drugs of choice for treating [streptococcal](https://www.britannica.com/science/Streptococcus) [sore throat](https://www.britannica.com/science/sore-throat), [tonsillitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/tonsillitis), [endocarditis](https://www.britannica.com/science/endocarditis) caused by some streptococci, [syphilis](https://www.britannica.com/science/syphilis), and meningococcal infections. Several bacteria, most notably _Staphylococcus_, developed resistance to the naturally occurring penicillins, which led to the production of the penicillinase-resistant penicillins ( [methicillin](https://www.britannica.com/science/methicillin), oxacillin, nafcillin, cloxacillin, and dicloxacillin). The use of several of these agents, however, has been severely limited by resistance; methicillin is no longer used, because of the emergence of [methicillin-resistant _Staphylococcus aureus_](https://www.britannica.com/science/methicillin) (MRSA).

To extend the usefulness of the penicillins to the [treatment](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/treatment) of [infections](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection) caused by gram-negative rods, the broad-spectrum penicillins ( [ampicillin](https://www.britannica.com/science/ampicillin), amoxicillin, carbenicillin, and ticarcillin) were developed. These penicillins are sensitive to penicillinase, but they are useful in treating urinary tract infections caused by gram-negative rods as well as in treating typhoid and enteric fevers.

The extended-spectrum agents (mezlocillin and piperacillin) are unique in that they have greater activity against gram-negative bacteria, including _Pseudomonas aeruginosa_, a bacterium that often causes serious infection in people whose [immune systems](https://www.britannica.com/science/immune-system) have been weakened. They have decreased activity, however, against penicillinase-producing _[Staphylococcus aureus](https://www.britannica.com/science/Staphylococcus-aureus)_, a common bacterial agent in [food poisoning](https://www.britannica.com/science/food-poisoning).

The penicillins are the safest of all antibiotics. The major [adverse](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/adverse) reaction associated with their use is [hypersensitivity](https://www.britannica.com/science/allergy), with reactions ranging from a rash to bronchospasm and [anaphylaxis](https://www.britannica.com/science/anaphylaxis). The more serious reactions are uncommon.

## [Cephalosporins](https://www.britannica.com/science/cephalosporin)

The [cephalosporins](https://www.britannica.com/science/cephalosporin) have a mechanism of action identical to that of the penicillins. However, the basic chemical structure of the penicillins and cephalosporins differs in other respects, resulting in some difference in the spectrum of antibacterial activity. Modification of the basic molecule (7-aminocephalosporanic acid) produced by _Cephalosporium acremonium_ resulted in four generations of cephalosporins.

The first-generation cephalosporins (cefazolin, cephalothin, and cephalexin) have a range of antibacterial activity similar to the broad-spectrum penicillins described above. For instance, they are effective against most staphylococci and streptococci as well as penicillin-resistant pneumococci.

The second-generation cephalosporins ( [cefamandole](https://www.britannica.com/science/cefamandole), cefaclor, cefotetan, [cefoxitin](https://www.britannica.com/science/cefoxitin), and cefuroxime) have an extended antibacterial spectrum that includes greater activity against additional species of gram-negative rods. Thus, these drugs are active against [_Escherichia coli_](https://www.britannica.com/science/E-coli) and [_Klebsiella_](https://www.britannica.com/science/Klebsiella) and _Proteus_ species (though several strains of these organisms have developed resistance). Cefamandole is active against many strains of _Haemophilus influenzae_ and [_Enterobacter_](https://www.britannica.com/science/Enterobacter), while cefoxitin is particularly active against most strains of _Bacteroides fragilis_. Second-generation cephalosporins have decreased activity, however, against gram-positive bacteria.

The third-generation cephalosporins (ceftriaxone, cefixime, and ceftazidime) have increased activity against the gram-negative organisms compared with the second-generation agents. Most _Enterobacter_ species are [susceptible](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/susceptible) to these drugs, as are _H. influenzae_ and various species of _Neisseria_. The antibacterial spectrum of the fourth-generation compounds (cefepime) is similar to that of the third-generation drugs, but the fourth-generation drugs have more resistance to β-lactamases.

Like the penicillins, the cephalosporins are relatively nontoxic. Because the structure of the cephalosporins is similar to that of penicillin, hypersensitivity reactions can occur in penicillin-hypersensitive patients.

## Imipenem

Imipenem is a β-lactam antibiotic that works by interfering with cell wall synthesis. It is highly resistant to hydrolysis by most β-lactamases. This drug must be given by intramuscular injection or intravenous infusion because it is not absorbed from the [gastrointestinal tract](https://www.britannica.com/science/gastrointestinal-tract). Imipenem is hydrolyzed by an enzyme present in the renal tubule; therefore, it is always administered with cilastatin, an inhibitor of this enzyme. Neurotoxicity and seizures have limited the use of imipenem.

## Antituberculosis antibiotics

[Isoniazid](https://www.britannica.com/science/isoniazid), [ethambutol](https://www.britannica.com/science/ethambutol), [pyrazinamide](https://www.britannica.com/science/pyrazinamide), and ethionamide are [synthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/synthetic) chemicals used in treating [tuberculosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/tuberculosis). Isoniazid, ethionamide, and pyrazinamide are similar in structure to nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD), a coenzyme essential for several physiological processes. Ethambutol prevents the synthesis of mycolic acid, a [lipid](https://www.britannica.com/science/lipid) found in the tubercule bacillus. All these drugs are absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract and penetrate tissues and cells. An isoniazid-induced [hepatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/hepatitis) can occur, particularly in patients 35 years of age or older. [Cycloserine](https://www.britannica.com/science/cycloserine), an antibiotic produced by _Streptomyces orchidaceus_, is also used in the treatment of tuberculosis. A structural [analog](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analog) of the [amino acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/amino-acid) d-alanine, it interferes with enzymes necessary for incorporation of d-alanine into the bacterial cell wall. It is rapidly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract and penetrates most tissues quite well; high levels are found in urine. Rifampin, a semisynthetic agent, is absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract, penetrates tissue well (including the [lung](https://www.britannica.com/science/lung)), and is used in the treatment of tuberculosis. Rifampin administration is associated with several side effects, mostly gastrointestinal in nature. The drug can turn [urine](https://www.britannica.com/science/urine), [feces](https://www.britannica.com/science/feces), [saliva](https://www.britannica.com/science/saliva), sweat, and tears red-orange in colour.

## Aztreonam, bacitracin, and [vancomycin](https://www.britannica.com/science/vancomycin)

Aztreonam is a synthetic antibiotic that works by [inhibiting](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibiting) cell wall synthesis, and it is naturally resistant to some β-lactamases. Aztreonam has a low [incidence](https://www.britannica.com/science/incidence-epidemiology) of toxicity, but it must be administered parenterally.

Bacitracin is produced by a special strain of _[Bacillus subtilis](https://www.britannica.com/science/Bacillus-subtilis)_. Because of its severe toxicity to [kidney](https://www.britannica.com/science/kidney) cells, its use is limited to the topical treatment of skin infections caused by _Streptococcus_ and _Staphylococcus_ and for [eye](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-eye) and ear infections.

Vancomycin, an antibiotic produced by _Streptomyces orientalis_, is poorly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract and is usually given by intravenous injection. It is used for the treatment of serious staphylococcal infections caused by strains resistant to the various penicillins. Its use against MRSA led to the emergence of vancomycin-resistant _Staphylococcus aureus_ (VRSA).

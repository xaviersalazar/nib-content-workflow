- What is a transplant in surgery?







In surgery, a transplant involves removing tissue or an organ from its original location and moving it to a new spot within the same person or to a different individual. Transplants are done for various reasons, such as saving lives, improving functions by correcting deformities, or enhancing appearance for psychological benefits.



Organ transplants, such as [kidney transplants](https://www.britannica.com/science/kidney-transplant/Introduction#ref1), have become routine procedures, while heart and liver transplants are also well-established. Bone marrow transplants are also performed, but they carry risks such as increased susceptibility to infection and graft-versus-host disease (GVHD).

- What parts of the body can be transplanted?







Many parts of the human body can be transplanted via surgery, including the [kidneys](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Organ-transplants), [heart](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Organ-transplants), [liver](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Organ-transplants), [lungs](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Organ-transplants), and [pancreas](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Organ-transplants). Grafts of corneas, which are structurally a modified form of transparent skin, can restore sight to blind eyes, and [skin grafts](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Introduction#ref14496) can save lives in the case of severe burns. Additionally, [bone marrow cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Introduction#ref14496) can be transplanted into the bloodstream.



Organ transplantation is highly regulated, especially concerning [organ donation](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Special-legal-and-ethical-problems#ref14521). It raises important ethical considerations regarding potential donors' death diagnoses and how far resuscitation should be continued.

- Why might someone need a transplant operation?







A transplant operation may be necessary when a person's organ(s) are damaged or failing. Kidney transplants are common for those with kidney disease, where the kidneys can no longer remove waste and excess water, leading to severe health issues. Liver transplants are performed to treat nonmalignant destructive diseases, such as cirrhosis, and primary liver cancer. Heart and lung transplants address severe lung fibrosis and right-side heart failure.



The body's immune system can see a transplanted organ as foreign and attack it, leading to rejection. Immunosuppressive drugs help prevent this rejection.

- How do doctors match donors and patients for a transplant?







Doctors consider several factors to match donors and patients for transplants. The [immune system](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Special-legal-and-ethical-problems#ref14527) recognizes and attacks foreign materials, including transplanted organs, potentially leading to rejection. Matching [transplantation antigens](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Special-legal-and-ethical-problems#ref14527) between donor and recipient is crucial to prevent this. [Tissue typing](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Special-legal-and-ethical-problems#ref14527) identifies an individual's HLA (human leukocyte antigens), and a direct crossmatch is performed between the recipient’s serum and donor lymphocytes as a final precaution.



Traits such as [blood groups and tissue markers](https://www.britannica.com/science/kidney-transplant/Introduction#ref1) help the immune system distinguish between the body’s own and foreign tissues. In the U.S., the [Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN)](https://www.britannica.com/topic/organ-donation/Introduction#ref285845) is used to match donated organs with prospective recipients.

- What are some risks and challenges after a transplant surgery?







After transplant surgery, the greatest risk is rejection, where the body's [immune system](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Special-legal-and-ethical-problems#ref14531) attacks the transplanted tissue or organ. Immunosuppressive drugs are essential to prevent rejection, but they also bring risks, such as increased susceptibility to [infections](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Special-legal-and-ethical-problems#ref14531).



Corticosteroids, while helpful in preventing rejection, can cause side effects like complexion problems, weight gain, diabetes, stunted growth in children, and brittle bones. Cyclosporine, another immunosuppressant, can cause kidney damage and increased hair growth and requires careful monitoring to avoid toxicity. Close monitoring is needed to detect early signs of rejection or infection.

- How has transplant surgery improved over time?







Transplant surgery has improved significantly over time through key advances. The introduction of [cyclosporine](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Special-legal-and-ethical-problems#ref14534) in the 1970s, along with other immunosuppressive drugs, has greatly improved the success of organ transplantation by preventing graft rejection. Kidney transplants have become routine, with one-year graft survival rates of 90% or higher. Heart and liver transplants are also well-established, and combined heart-lung transplants have shown promising results. Lung transplants, initially difficult, saw success with single and double lung transplants in the 1980s. These advancements have transformed transplantation into a viable treatment for end-stage organ diseases, offering patients a chance at a normal life.

**transplant**, in [medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/medicine), a section of [tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/tissue) or a complete [organ](https://www.britannica.com/science/organ-biology) that is removed from its original natural site and transferred to a new position in the same person or in a separate individual. The term, like the synonym _graft_, was borrowed from [horticulture](https://www.britannica.com/science/horticulture). Both words imply that success will result in a healthy and flourishing graft or transplant, which will gain its nourishment from its new environment.

## Transplants and grafts

Transplants of animal tissue have figured prominently in mythology since the legend of the creation of Eve from one of Adam’s [ribs](https://www.britannica.com/science/rib-bone). Historical accounts of surgical tissue grafting as part of the cure of patients date back to the early Hindu surgeons who, about the beginning of the 6th century bce, developed techniques for reconstructing [noses](https://www.britannica.com/science/nose) from [skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skin) flaps taken from the patient’s arm. This method was introduced into Western medicine by Italian surgeon Gaspare Tagliacozzi in the 16th century. The flap was left attached to the arm for two to three weeks until new [blood vessels](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-vessel) had grown into it from the nose remnant. The flap was then severed and the arm freed from the reconstructed nose.

It was found that extremely thin pieces of skin could be cut free and would obtain enough nourishment from the serum in the graft bed to stay alive while new blood vessels were being formed. This free grafting of skin, together with the flap techniques already mentioned, constituted the main therapeutic devices of the [plastic surgeon](https://www.britannica.com/science/plastic-surgery) in the correction of various types of defects. Skilled manipulations of such grafts produced surprising improvements in the appearance of those born with malformed faces and in the disfigurements resulting from severe [burns](https://www.britannica.com/science/burn). [Cornea](https://www.britannica.com/science/cornea), which structurally is a modified form of transparent skin, can also be free grafted, and corneal grafts have restored sight to countless blind eyes.

[Blood transfusion](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-transfusion) can be regarded as a form of tissue graft. The blood-forming tissues— [bone marrow](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-marrow) cells—can also be transplanted. If these cells are injected into the bloodstream, they home to the marrow cavities and can become established as a vital lifesaving graft in patients suffering from defective marrow.

The chief distinguishing feature of organ and [limb](https://www.britannica.com/science/limb) grafts is that the tissues of the organ or limb can survive only if blood vessels are rapidly joined (anastomosed) to blood vessels of the recipient. This provides the graft with a [blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-disease) supply before it dies from lack of oxygen and nourishment and from the accumulation of poisonous waste products.

As can be seen from the examples cited, living-tissue grafts may be performed for a variety of reasons. [Skin grafts](https://www.britannica.com/science/skin-graft) can save life in severe [burns](https://www.britannica.com/science/burn), can improve function by correcting deformity, or can improve appearances in a cosmetic sense, with valuable psychological benefits. Organ grafts can supply a missing function and save life in cases of fatal disease of vital organs, such as the [kidney](https://www.britannica.com/science/kidney).

A tissue removed from one part of the body and transplanted to another site in the same individual is called an autograft. Autografts cannot be rejected. Similarly, grafts between identical twins or highly inbred animals—isografts—are accepted by the recipients indefinitely. Grafts from a donor to a recipient of the same species—allografts or [homografts](https://www.britannica.com/science/allograft)—are usually rejected unless special efforts are made to prevent this. Grafts between individuals of different species—xenografts or [heterografts](https://www.britannica.com/science/heterotransplant)—are usually destroyed very quickly by the recipient. (The methods used to prevent rejection are discussed in full [below](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Special-legal-and-ethical-problems#ref14525).)

Tissue or organ grafts may be transplanted to their normal situation in the recipient and are then known as orthotopic—for example, skin to the surface of the body. Alternatively, they may be transplanted to an abnormal situation and are then called heterotopic—for example, kidneys are usually grafted into the lower part of the abdomen instead of into the loin (the back between the ribs and the pelvis), as this is more convenient. If an extra organ is grafted, it is called auxiliary or accessory—for example, a heterotopic [liver](https://www.britannica.com/science/liver) graft may be inserted without removal of the recipient’s own liver.

Grafts are usually performed for long-term effects. Occasionally, the limited acceptance of a skin allograft may be lifesaving by preventing loss of fluid and [protein](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) from extensive burned surface in severely ill patients. The graft also provides a [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria)-proof covering so that infection cannot occur. When the allograft is removed or rejected, the patient may be sufficiently recovered to receive permanent autografts.

Certain tissues, including [bone](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy), [cartilage](https://www.britannica.com/science/cartilage), [tendons](https://www.britannica.com/science/tendon), [fascia](https://www.britannica.com/technology/fascia-architecture), [arteries](https://www.britannica.com/science/artery), and heart valves, can be implanted even if their cells are dead at the time of implantation or will be rejected shortly thereafter. These are structural implants rather than true grafts or transplants. They are more akin to the stick to which a rose is attached for support—although their support is essential, their function does not depend on biological processes. In fact, xenografts or inert manufactured devices may often be equally suitable substitutes.

# [Tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/tissue) transplants

## [Skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/skin-graft)

Most [skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skin) grafting is with autografts; the special indication for skin allografts in severely burned patients has been mentioned. Skin allografts seem to be rejected more aggressively than any other tissue, and there are many experimental situations in which skin grafts between two inbred strains of animal fail, although kidney grafts between the same strains survive indefinitely. With autografts, the donor skin is limited to what the patient has available. If allografts were not rejected, skin from [cadavers](https://www.britannica.com/science/cadaver) could be used for coverage of burned areas without the need for subsequent autografting.

## Flaps

Flaps as used by Tagliacozzi are particularly valuable if [adipose (fat) tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/adipose-tissue) as well as skin has been lost. This is because flaps are thicker than skin grafts; they consist of the full thickness of skin, with fascia and fat, or may also contain muscle. The procedure of raising a flap and keeping the donor site adjacent to the recipient bed can be complicated and uncomfortable for the patient. The cosmetic results are good, and the fat and other tissues under the skin contained in the flap can be used to cover exposed bone or to allow movement in a contracted joint or to fashion a new nose.

## [Full-thickness free-skin grafts](https://www.britannica.com/science/full-thickness-free-skin-graft)

Full-thickness free-skin grafts, which include dermis and epidermis, are the maximum thickness that can survive without a [blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-biochemistry) supply; they are therefore in some danger of failure to survive. These grafts produce good cosmetic appearances and are especially useful on the face. The main defect of a full-thickness free-skin graft is that, unless it is very small, the donor site from which it comes becomes a defect that needs to be closed in its own right and may itself need skin grafting.

## [Split or partial-thickness skin grafts](https://www.britannica.com/science/split-skin-graft)

Split, or partial-thickness, skin grafts are by far the most commonly used grafts in [plastic surgery](https://www.britannica.com/science/plastic-surgery). Superficial slices of skin the thickness of tissue paper are cut with a hand or mechanical razor. The graft, which contains living [cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology), is so thin that it usually gains adequate nourishment directly from the raw surface to which it is applied, and the risk of failure to take (that is, to survive in the new location) is therefore much less than with full-thickness grafts. Another major advantage is that the donor site is not badly damaged. It is tender for only two or three weeks, and it resembles a superficial graze both in appearance and in the fact that healing takes place from the deep layer of the skin left behind. Split skin grafts can be taken quickly from large areas to cover big defects. They tend to have an abnormal shiny reddish appearance that is not as satisfactory cosmetically as the other types of skin graft.

## Other tissue transplants

## Cornea

There are certain forms of [blindness](https://www.britannica.com/science/blindness-medicine) in which the [eye](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-eye) is entirely normal apart from opacity of the front window, or [cornea](https://www.britannica.com/science/cornea). The opacity may be the result of disease or injury, but, if the clouded cornea is removed and replaced by a corneal transplant, normal vision can result. Since cells of the cornea remain viable for some 12 hours after death, a cornea can be grafted if it is removed within that period. Cooling will slow the process of deterioration, although the sooner the section of cornea is transplanted the better. The graft bed to which a cornea is transplanted has no blood supply. Nourishment comes directly by diffusion from the tissues. Because most rejection factors are carried in the bloodstream, the lack of [blood vessels](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-vessel) permits most corneal allografts to survive indefinitely without rejection. Rejection can occur if, as sometimes happens, blood vessels grow into the graft.

## Blood vessels

By far the most satisfactory [blood-vessel](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-vessel) transplant is an autograft, similar in principle to skin autografts. Blood-vessel grafts are frequently used to bypass [arteries](https://www.britannica.com/science/artery) that have become blocked or dangerously narrowed by fatty deposits, a condition caused by degenerative [atherosclerosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/atherosclerosis) (hardening of the arteries). Such atherosclerotic deposits in the coronary and carotid arteries are responsible, respectively, for most [heart attacks](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart-attack) and [strokes](https://www.britannica.com/science/stroke-medical-condition). If atherosclerosis affects the main artery of the leg, the result is first [pain](https://www.britannica.com/science/pain) in the calves and then [gangrene](https://www.britannica.com/science/gangrene) of the foot, necessitating [amputation](https://www.britannica.com/science/amputation) of the leg. If dealt with early, the effects of the arterial blockage can often be overcome by removing a nonessential superficial [vein](https://www.britannica.com/science/vein-blood-vessel) from the leg, reversing it so that the valves will not obstruct blood flow, and then joining this graft to the affected artery above and below the block—thus bypassing the obstruction. Grafting for [coronary artery bypass](https://www.britannica.com/science/coronary-artery-bypass) has become one of the most common surgical operations in developed countries.

Vein or arterial allografts are far less successful. In time the walls tend to degenerate, and the vessels either dilate, with the danger of bursting, or become obstructed.

## Heart valves

Valvular diseases of the [heart](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart) can be dangerous, since both a blocked [valve](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart-valve) and a valve that allows blood to leak backward create a strain on the heart that can lead to [heart failure](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart-failure). If the valve is seriously damaged, it can be replaced with a xenograft valve or a manufactured mechanical valve. Neither is ideal. Xenograft valves have a normal central blood flow, but after a few years they may become rigid and cease to function. Plastic valves—usually of the ball-valve or trapdoor types—force blood to flow around the surface of the ball or trapdoor flap, and this tends to damage [red blood cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/red-blood-cell) and cause [anemia](https://www.britannica.com/science/anemia). Synthetic heart valves require ongoing [anticoagulation](https://www.britannica.com/science/anticoagulant) therapy.

## Bone

When [fractures](https://www.britannica.com/science/fracture-of-bone) fail to unite, autografts of [bone](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy) can be extremely valuable in helping the bone to heal. Bone allografts can be used for similar purposes, but they are not as satisfactory, since the bone cells are either dead when grafted or are rejected. Thus, the graft is merely a structural scaffold that, although useful as such, cannot partake actively in healing.

## Fascia

[Fascia](https://www.britannica.com/technology/fascia-architecture), sheets of strong [connective tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/connective-tissue) that surround muscle bundles, may be used as autografts to repair [hernias](https://www.britannica.com/science/hernia). The principle of use is like that for skin.

## [Nerves](https://www.britannica.com/science/nerve-anatomy)

Nerves outside the [brain](https://www.britannica.com/science/brain) and [spinal cord](https://www.britannica.com/science/spinal-cord) can regenerate if damaged. If the delicate sheaths containing the nerves are cut, however, as must happen if a nerve is partially or completely severed, [regeneration](https://www.britannica.com/science/regeneration-biology) may not be possible. Even if regeneration occurs it is unlikely to be complete, since most nerves are mixed motor and sensory paths and there is no control ensuring that regenerating fibres take the correct path. Thus, there will always be some fibres that end in the wrong destination and are therefore unable to function. Defective nerve regeneration is the main reason why limb grafts usually are unsatisfactory. A mechanical artificial limb is likely to be of more value to the patient.

## [Blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-blood)

[Blood transfusion](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-transfusion) has been one of the most important factors in the development of modern surgery. There are many lifesaving surgical procedures that are possible only because the blood loss inevitable in the operation can be made up by transfusion. Blood transfusion is of value in saving life following major injury, bleeding [ulcers](https://www.britannica.com/science/ulcer), [childbirth](https://www.britannica.com/science/birth), and many other conditions involving dangerous loss of blood. Purified blood components can be transfused to treat specific defects; for example, [platelets](https://www.britannica.com/science/platelet) are used to correct a low platelet count, and clotting factor VIII is given to counteract the clotting defect in classic [hemophilia](https://www.britannica.com/science/hemophilia).

## [Bone marrow](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-marrow-transplant)

![autologous bone marrow transplant](https://cdn.britannica.com/16/67716-050-907198F0/bone-marrow-stem-cells-cancer-radiation-chemotherapy.jpg)

[autologous bone marrow transplant](https://cdn.britannica.com/16/67716-050-907198F0/bone-marrow-stem-cells-cancer-radiation-chemotherapy.jpg) In an autologous bone marrow transplant, hematopoietic stem cells are harvested from the blood or bone marrow of a patient before the patient undergoes treatment for cancer. In order to remove tumour cells that may have been collected with the stem cells, the sample is incubated with antibodies that bind only to stem cells. The stem cells are then isolated and stored for later use, when they are reinfused into the patient.(more)

Diseases in which the [bone marrow](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-marrow) is defective, such as [aplastic anemia](https://www.britannica.com/science/aplastic-anemia), may be treated by marrow grafting. Some forms of [leukemia](https://www.britannica.com/science/leukemia) can be cured by destroying the patient’s bone marrow—the site of the cancerous cells—with [drugs](https://www.britannica.com/science/drug-chemical-agent) and irradiation. Marrow grafting is then necessary to rescue the patient. There is a tendency for the patient to reject the allografted marrow, and there is an additional hazard because immune cells in a marrow graft can react against the patient’s tissues, causing serious and sometimes fatal [graft-versus-host disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/graft-versus-host-disease). To avoid these complications, special [immunosuppressive](https://www.britannica.com/science/immunosuppressant) treatment is given. The use of monoclonal [antibodies](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibody) ( _see below_ [Monoclonal antibodies](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery/Special-legal-and-ethical-problems#ref14533)) to selectively remove harmful [lymphocytes](https://www.britannica.com/science/lymphocyte) from the donor marrow has produced encouraging results in preventing graft-versus-host disease.

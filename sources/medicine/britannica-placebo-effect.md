- Why do researchers use placebos in experiments?







Researchers use placebos in experiments to accurately test new treatments, especially for neurological and psychiatric conditions. In these trials, some participants unknowingly receive the actual treatment, while others get a placebo. This method helps prevent bias, as patients' knowledge of their treatment could influence the results. By comparing outcomes between the treatment and placebo groups, researchers can determine if the new intervention's effect is greater than that of the placebo itself. Placebos can also be prescribed if a doctor thinks a patient’s symptoms are psychological, hoping the mind can cure them.



However, the use of placebos raises ethical questions, particularly when effective treatments already exist. The [World Medical Association's Declaration of Helsinki](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Declaration-of-Helsinki/Introduction#ref323999) addresses these concerns, allowing placebo-controlled trials only when scientifically necessary or for minor health conditions.

- How can a patient's expectations influence the placebo effect?







The placebo effect is a psychological or psychophysiological improvement attributed to therapy using an inert substance or sham procedure. A person’s expectations about treatment can influence the placebo effect, rather than the treatment itself having a direct effect.



For instance, test subjects who took an inert substance labeled as a potent pain medication, marketed under a brand name, and sold at a relatively high price experienced greater pain tolerance following mild electrical shock compared to people who took an inert substance marketed as a generic pain medication and sold at a comparatively low cost. Notably, the brand-name placebo and the generic placebo were the same substance.



In clinical trials, understanding how expectations and cultural beliefs influence the placebo effect has important implications for design. A specific degree of uncertainty communicated verbally can potentially heighten the placebo effect, and by limiting this uncertainty, the effect may be controlled for the purposes of clinical trials.

**placebo effect**, psychological or psychophysiological improvement attributed to therapy with an inert substance or a simulated (sham) procedure. There is no clear explanation for why some persons experience measurable improvement when given an inert substance for treatment. Research has indicated that the effect may be caused by the person’s expectations about the treatment rather than being a direct effect of the treatment itself.

One of the first doctors to deliberately prescribe [placebos](https://www.britannica.com/science/placebo), or inert treatments, was Scottish physician [William Cullen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Cullen), who mentioned in a lecture series in 1772 having given placebos to patients to appease them, not to cure their conditions. Despite Cullen’s observations that placebos appeared to produce beneficial effects in some patients, the term _placebo effect_ was not introduced into [medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/medicine) until the early 20th century.

In modern medicine, placebos, including inert drugs and sham procedures, are frequently used in [clinical trials](https://www.britannica.com/science/clinical-trial) that are designed to test new treatments, particularly those developed for neurological and psychiatric conditions. In placebo-controlled trials, enrolled patients are randomly and unknowingly (blindly) assigned to receive either the new medical intervention being tested or a placebo. This prevents patients from knowing what treatment they received, which could cause them to influence study results, and it allows researchers to determine whether the new intervention produces an effect greater than that of the placebo.

The use of placebos in clinical trials has raised important questions in medicine and [bioethics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bioethics). The [World Medical Association](https://www.britannica.com/topic/World-Medical-Association)’s (WMA’s) Declaration of Helsinki, which provides a set of ethical guidelines for medical experimentation on humans, traditionally prohibited the use of placebos in trials when effective therapies or interventions already existed. In 2001, however, the WMA revised its guidelines to allow placebo-controlled trials under certain circumstances, such as when scientific methodology required the use of a placebo or when a new intervention was tested for a relatively minor health condition.

A significant proportion of new treatments and interventions routinely fail to demonstrate a benefit greater than that of placebos in clinical trials. This has been most notable for certain types of [antidepressants](https://www.britannica.com/science/antidepressant) and for the application of [ultrasound](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultrasound) in the healing of soft tissue injury. In addition, investigations of inert substances have found that the colour, the size, and the price of a pill can affect expectations of drug effectiveness. For example, in a report published in 2008, researchers found that test subjects who took an inert substance labeled as a potent [pain](https://www.britannica.com/science/pain) medication, marketed under a brand name, and sold at a relatively high price experienced greater pain tolerance following mild electrical shock to the wrist than people who took an inert substance marketed as a generic pain medication and sold at a comparatively low cost; the brand-name placebo and the generic placebo were the same substance.

Understanding the physiological and psychological basis of how factors such as expectations and cultural beliefs influence the placebo effect has important implications for the design of clinical trials. Studies have shown that the release of the neurotransmitter [dopamine](https://www.britannica.com/science/dopamine) in a region of the [brain](https://www.britannica.com/science/brain) known as the ventral striatum is a major determinant of expectation in the placebo effect. Patients with chronic illness who frequently experience positive outcomes from their medications often strongly anticipate therapeutic benefit, a phenomenon that has been demonstrated in research on persons with [Parkinson disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/Parkinson-disease). In one study researchers found that, in response to previously having taken medications such as [levodopa](https://www.britannica.com/science/levodopa) and then being presented with these medications, Parkinson patients experienced dopamine release in the dorsal striatum of the basal ganglia. However, patients who were told that they had a 75 percent chance of receiving a new active drug, which was actually a placebo, produced significant amounts of dopamine in the ventral striatum. By comparison, patients who were told that they had a 25, 50, or 100 percent chance of receiving the new drug released relatively small amounts of dopamine in the ventral striatum. In addition to isolating the ventral striatum and dopamine as central to the placebo effect in this patient subset, the findings also suggested that a specific degree of uncertainty communicated verbally can potentially heighten the placebo effect and that by limiting this uncertainty the effect may be controlled for the purposes of clinical trials.

- What is mind-body dualism?







Mind-body dualism, in its most radical form, is the philosophical view that the mind and body are fundamentally distinct kinds of substances or natures. - How does mind-body dualism explain the relationship between the mind and the body?







Mind-body dualism posits that the mind and body are fundamentally distinct entities. In its original form, called substance dualism, the mind is an immaterial, nonextended substance capable of rational thought, imagining, and feeling, while the body is a material, extended substance governed by the laws of physics.



[René Descartes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mind-body-dualism/Introduction#ref1) argued that the mind and body causally interact, but this interaction raises the question of how it is possible. Alternative dualistic theories, such as occasionalism and parallelism, avoid direct causal interaction by attributing apparent links between mental and physical events to God's constant causal action or a preestablished harmony. Critics of substance dualism question the nature of an immaterial mental substance, leading some to favor monistic theories.

- What are some arguments for and against mind-body dualism?







Mind-body dualism posits that the mind and body are distinct entities, differing fundamentally in nature. Arguments for this view often emphasize the subjective experience of consciousness, which seems irreducible to physical explanations. Dualists argue that mental states possess unique qualities, such as intentionality and qualia, absent in physical matter.



However, dualism faces challenges, notably the interaction problem: how can an immaterial mind causally interact with a physical body? Critics also argue that dualism complicates understanding consciousness's origins and development and that simpler, monistic views can adequately explain the mind-body relationship.

- How does mind-body dualism influence modern debates about consciousness and artificial intelligence?







Mind-body dualism, the belief that the mind and body are distinct entities, significantly influences modern debates on [consciousness](https://www.britannica.com/topic/philosophy-of-mind/Explanatory-gaps) and [artificial intelligence](https://www.britannica.com/topic/philosophical-anthropology/Artificial-intelligence#ref279225) (AI). Dualism poses challenges to materialism, the view that all phenomena, including mental ones, are physical.



The rise of AI, which seeks to replicate human intelligence in machines, challenges traditional dualistic views by suggesting that mental processes can be replicated through material means. Critics of dualism, like Gilbert Ryle, argue that the mind is simply the behavior of the body, dismissing the idea of a "ghost in the machine". Contemporary philosophers and neuroscientists grapple with reconciling subjective experiences with the physical workings of the brain, reflecting the enduring impact of Cartesian dualism on understanding consciousness.

**mind-body dualism**, in its original and most radical formulation, the philosophical view that [mind](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mind) and body (or [matter](https://www.britannica.com/science/matter)) are fundamentally distinct kinds of substances or natures. That version, now often called [substance dualism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/substance-dualism), implies that _mind_ and _body_ not only differ in meaning but refer to different kinds of [entities](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/entities). Thus, a mind-body (substance) dualist would oppose any theory that identifies mind with the [brain](https://www.britannica.com/science/brain), conceived as a physical [mechanism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mechanism-philosophy).

A brief treatment of mind-body dualism follows. For fuller discussion, _see_ [Philosophy of mind](https://www.britannica.com/topic/philosophy-of-mind): Dualism; and [Metaphysics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/metaphysics): Mind and body.

The modern problem of the relationship of mind to body stems from the [thought](https://www.britannica.com/topic/thought) of the 17th-century French philosopher and mathematician [René Descartes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rene-Descartes), who gave dualism its classical formulation. Beginning from his famous dictum _[cogito, ergo sum](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cogito-ergo-sum)_ (Latin: “I think, therefore I am”), Descartes developed a theory of mind as an immaterial, nonextended substance that engages in various activities or undergoes various states such as rational thought, imagining, feeling ( [sensation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sensation)), and [willing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/free-will). Matter, or extended substance, conforms to the laws of [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science) in mechanistic fashion, with the important exception of the [human body](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-body), which Descartes believed is causally affected by the human mind and which causally produces certain mental events. For example, willing the arm to be raised causes it to be raised, whereas being hit by a hammer on the finger causes the mind to feel [pain](https://www.britannica.com/science/pain). This part of Descartes’s dualistic theory, known as [interactionism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/interactionism), raises one of the chief problems faced by Descartes and his followers: the question of how this causal interaction is possible.

This problem gave rise to other varieties of substance dualism, such as [occasionalism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/occasionalism) and some forms of [parallelism](https://www.britannica.com/science/psychophysical-parallelism) that do not require direct causal interaction. Occasionalism maintains that apparent links between mental and physical events are the result of God’s constant causal action. Parallelism also rejects causal interaction but without constant divine intervention. [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gottfried-Wilhelm-Leibniz), a 17th-century German [rationalist](https://www.britannica.com/topic/rationalism) and mathematician, saw mind and body as two perfectly [correlated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/correlated) series, synchronized like two clocks at their origin by God in a [preestablished harmony](https://www.britannica.com/topic/preestablished-harmony).

Another substance-dualistic theory is [epiphenomenalism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/epiphenomenalistic-materialism), which agrees with other theories in holding that mental events and physical events are different. The epiphenomenalist holds, however, that the only true causes are physical events, with mind as a by-product. Mental events seem causally efficacious because certain mental events occur just before certain physical events and because humans are ignorant of the events in the brain that truly cause them.

Among other difficulties faced by substance dualism is the [inherent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inherent) obscurity in conceiving of what sort of thing a mental substance—an immaterial, thinking “stuff”—might be. Such [criticisms](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criticisms) have led some thinkers to abandon substance dualism in favour of various monistic theories, including the [identity theory](https://www.britannica.com/topic/identity-theory), according to which every mental state or event is identical to some physical (i.e., brain) state or event, and the [dual-aspect](https://www.britannica.com/topic/double-aspect-theory) theory, also called [neutral monism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/neutral-monism), according to which mental and physical states and events [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) different aspects or properties of a single underlying substance, which is neither mental nor physical.

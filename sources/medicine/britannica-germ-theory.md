### What is germ theory?

Germ theory, in medicine, is the scientific principle that [infectious diseases](https://www.britannica.com/science/infectious-disease) are caused by microorganisms—such as bacteria, [viruses](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus), [fungi](https://www.britannica.com/science/fungus), and [protozoa](https://www.britannica.com/science/protozoan)—that invade and multiply within the body. These organisms are too small to be seen without a microscope and can spread between individuals through various forms of contact or environmental exposure. Germ theory established that specific pathogens cause specific diseases, forming the basis for modern practices such as sanitation, vaccination, and [infection](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection) control.

### Who were key figures in the development of germ theory?

Key figures in the development of germ theory include [Louis Pasteur](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-Pasteur), [Joseph Lister](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Lister-Baron-Lister-of-Lyme-Regis), and [Robert Koch](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Koch). Pasteur demonstrated that microorganisms cause processes such as [fermentation](https://www.britannica.com/science/fermentation) and decay, helping to disprove [spontaneous generation](https://www.britannica.com/science/spontaneous-generation). Lister applied these ideas to medicine by introducing antiseptic surgical techniques to reduce infection, while Koch established that specific microorganisms cause specific diseases and developed a framework, known as Koch’s postulates, to link pathogens to illnesses.

### What are Koch’s postulates?

Koch’s postulates, developed by German physician [Robert Koch](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Koch), are a set of criteria used to establish a causal relationship between a specific microorganism and a specific disease.

Koch’s postulates

- 1\. A specific microorganism is always associated with a specific disease.

- 2\. The microorganism can be isolated from the diseased animal and grown in pure culture in the laboratory.

- 3\. The cultured microorganism will cause the same disease when transferred to a healthy animal.

- 4\. The microorganism can be isolated from the newly infected animal and confirmed as being identical to the original causative organism.


### How is germ theory applied in modern medicine?

Germ theory is applied in modern medicine through practices that prevent, diagnose, and treat infectious diseases by targeting microorganisms. It underlies the use of antimicrobial drugs, such as [antibiotics](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibiotic) and [antiviral drugs](https://www.britannica.com/science/antiviral-drug), as well as the use of [vaccines](https://www.britannica.com/science/vaccine) that prepare the [immune system](https://www.britannica.com/science/immune-system) to recognize and fight infections. In clinical settings, it guides the use of sterile techniques, including hand hygiene and instrument sterilization, and the use of protective equipment to reduce the spread of disease. Germ theory also informs diagnostic methods, such as microbial cultures and molecular tests, and supports [public health](https://www.britannica.com/topic/public-health) measures such as the development of sanitation systems and disease surveillance.

**germ theory**, in [medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/medicine), is the scientific principle that [infectious diseases](https://www.britannica.com/science/infectious-disease) are caused by specific microorganisms (pathogens) that invade and reproduce within the body, disrupting normal physiological functions. These microorganisms include [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria), [viruses](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus), [fungi](https://www.britannica.com/science/fungus), and [protozoa](https://www.britannica.com/science/protozoan), which are too small to be seen except through a [microscope](https://www.britannica.com/technology/microscope).

Germ theory serves a central role in explaining how diseases are [transmitted](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/transmitted), including through direct contact, contaminated surfaces, airborne particles, and vectors. Understanding these transmission pathways allows for the development of strategies to prevent [infection](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection), such as vaccination, sanitation, and [quarantine](https://www.britannica.com/science/quarantine-preventive-medicine). In modern [biology](https://www.britannica.com/science/biology), germ theory is closely linked to the study of the [immune system](https://www.britannica.com/science/immune-system), which defends the body against invading pathogens through innate and adaptive responses.

## Early discoveries

[![Louis Pasteur](https://cdn.britannica.com/00/3400-050-0819DB14/Louis-Pasteur.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/00/3400-050-0819DB14/Louis-Pasteur.jpg)

[Louis Pasteur](https://cdn.britannica.com/00/3400-050-0819DB14/Louis-Pasteur.jpg) French chemist and microbiologist Louis Pasteur.(more)

French chemist and microbiologist [Louis Pasteur](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-Pasteur), English surgeon [Joseph Lister](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Lister-Baron-Lister-of-Lyme-Regis), and German physician [Robert Koch](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Koch) are given much of the credit for development and acceptance of the theory. In the mid-19th century Pasteur showed that [fermentation](https://www.britannica.com/science/fermentation) and putrefaction are caused by organisms in the air; his experiments disproved the idea of [spontaneous generation](https://www.britannica.com/science/spontaneous-generation) and provided evidence that microorganisms arise from other microorganisms.

In the 1860s Lister revolutionized [surgical](https://www.britannica.com/science/surgery-medicine) practice by utilizing [carbolic acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbolic-acid) (phenol) to exclude atmospheric germs and thus prevent putrefaction in [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) fractures of [bones](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy); this work demonstrated that controlling microbial exposure reduces infection rates. In the 1880s Koch identified the organisms that cause [tuberculosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/tuberculosis) and [cholera](https://www.britannica.com/science/cholera). He also formulated Koch’s postulates, a set of [criteria](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criteria) used to establish a causal relationship between a specific microorganism and a specific [disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/disease).

[![Joseph Lister](https://cdn.britannica.com/30/10230-050-F15519DE/Joseph-Lister.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/30/10230-050-F15519DE/Joseph-Lister.jpg)

[Joseph Lister](https://cdn.britannica.com/30/10230-050-F15519DE/Joseph-Lister.jpg) English surgeon Joseph Lister, undated photograph.(more)

Although the germ theory has long been considered proved, its full [implications](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implications) for medical practice were not immediately apparent; bloodstained frock coats were considered suitable operating-room attire even in the late 1870s, and surgeons operated without masks or head coverings as late as the 1890s. This delay in adoption highlights the role of scientific communication and [paradigm](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/paradigm) shifts in the acceptance of new scientific ideas. Over time, germ theory led to major advances in antiseptic and aseptic techniques, sterilization, and public health measures.

## Modern applications

Modern applications of germ theory are central to medicine and [public health](https://www.britannica.com/topic/public-health), guiding efforts to prevent and treat infectious diseases. In particular, it underlies the development of vaccines and antimicrobial drugs, as well as infection-control practices such as hand hygiene, sterilization, and the use of protective equipment. Germ theory also informs public health measures, including the development of modern sanitation systems, food safety standards, and disease tracking methods such as contact tracing.

The ease with which pathogens can spread via hands, especially in medical settings, has made hand hygiene an especially critical method for reducing infection. Standardized practices such as routine handwashing with soap, the use of antiseptics and alcohol-based [hand sanitizers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hand-sanitizer), and hygiene [protocols](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/protocols) in hospitals and public spaces remain some of the most effective strategies for preventing the spread of infectious diseases. This simple means of infection control is routinely applied in a variety of settings, from day-care centers and schools to hospitals and health care clinics and from supermarkets to cruise ships.

- What kinds of germs can cause infectious diseases?







Infectious diseases are caused by several types of germs, including [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria/Bacteria-in-medicine), [viruses](https://www.britannica.com/science/infectious-disease/Rickettsias), [fungi](https://www.britannica.com/science/infectious-disease/Fungi), and [parasites](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection/Introduction#ref436288).



  - **Bacteria** Pathogenic bacteria, such as _Escherichia coli_, _Staphylococcus aureus_, and _Streptococcus_, can cause illnesses ranging from gastroenteritis and pneumonia to strep throat.
  - **Viruses** Viruses like influenza, HIV, and SARS-CoV-2 spread rapidly, replicating inside host cells.
  - **Fungi** Fungal infections include athlete’s foot, ringworm, and systemic conditions like candidiasis, common in immunocompromised people.
  - **Parasites** Protozoa, helminths, and ectoparasites like the malaria parasite (Plasmodium), tapeworms, and lice can cause parasitic infections, drawing resources from their host and sometimes leading to chronic illness.
  - **Prions** Prions are abnormal proteins that can cause neurodegenerative diseases.

- How do infectious diseases spread from one person to another?







Infectious diseases spread through various routes. Airborne transmission occurs via infectious particles in droplets or aerosols, such as with [tuberculosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection/Introduction#ref436289) or [influenza](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection/Introduction#ref436289). Direct contact involves physical touch, sexual contact, or bodily fluids, while indirect transmission involves contaminated surfaces or objects.



Some infections spread through vectors like mosquitoes carrying [malaria](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection/Introduction#ref436289) or ticks carrying [Lyme disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection/Introduction#ref436289). Vertical transmission passes infection from mother to child, and transfusion-transmitted infections occur through blood transfusions. Respiratory droplets from coughs or sneezes can also spread diseases like [COVID-19](https://www.britannica.com/story/questions-about-covid-19-answered/Introduction#ref423973).

- How can people prevent getting infectious diseases?







To prevent infectious diseases, maintain proper [hygiene](https://www.merriam-webster.com/thesaurus/hygiene), ensure [safe food](https://www.britannica.com/science/nutritional-disease/Foodborne-illnesses#ref247899) and water, and follow guidelines for [handwashing](https://www.britannica.com/science/bloodborne-disease/Introduction#ref333306) and [vaccination](https://www.britannica.com/science/immune-system/Immunity-against-cancer#ref215592). Public health strategies, including surveillance, quarantine during outbreaks, and vector control, limit transmission. [Medical interventions](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection/Introduction#ref436290) using antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals, and antiparasitic drugs are also essential for treating infections.



Humans' social habits influence the spread of infectious agents. Overcrowding facilitates the passage of disease-causing organisms from person to person.

- How do doctors diagnose and treat infectious diseases?







Doctors diagnose infectious diseases through observation of symptoms and identification of the pathogen. Identification methods include microscopy, serology and immunology, molecular biology, and lab instrumentation. Serological tests detect antibodies or antibody-like substances in blood serum associated with certain diseases.



Treatment aims to restore a normal physiological state and remove the causative agent, supplementing the body's natural defenses. Treatments include drugs such as antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals, and antiparasitic drugs. Some drugs kill microorganisms, while others inhibit their proliferation, allowing host defenses to function effectively. For viral infections, treatment is mainly directed toward relieving discomfort and pain.

**infectious disease**, in [medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/medicine), a process caused by an agent, often a type of [microorganism](https://www.britannica.com/science/microorganism), that impairs a person’s [health](https://www.britannica.com/topic/health). In many cases, [infectious](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/infectious) disease can be spread from person to person, either directly (e.g., via [skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skin) contact) or indirectly (e.g., via contaminated food or water).

![Basic reproduction number of select viral diseases](https://cdn.britannica.com/61/269561-050-7347DFDC/illustration-how-contagious-a-selection-of-viruses-are-maximum-ro-values.jpg)

[Basic reproduction number of select viral diseases](https://cdn.britannica.com/61/269561-050-7347DFDC/illustration-how-contagious-a-selection-of-viruses-are-maximum-ro-values.jpg) The contagiousness of infectious pathogens is measured by R0 (R naught), or the basic reproduction number, which is the number of individuals likely to become infected by a single case.(more)

An infectious disease can differ from simple [infection](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection), which is the invasion of and replication in the body by any of various agents—including [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria), [viruses](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus), [fungi](https://www.britannica.com/science/fungus), [protozoans](https://www.britannica.com/science/protozoan), and [worms](https://www.britannica.com/animal/worm)—as well as the reaction of tissues to their presence or to the toxins that they produce. When health is not altered, the process is called a subclinical infection. Thus, a person may be infected but not have an infectious disease. This principle is illustrated by the use of [vaccines](https://www.britannica.com/science/vaccine) for the prevention of infectious diseases. For example, a [virus](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus) such as that which causes [measles](https://www.britannica.com/science/measles) may be [attenuated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/attenuated) (weakened) and used as an immunizing agent. The [immunization](https://www.britannica.com/science/immunization) is designed to produce a measles infection in the recipient but generally causes no discernible alteration in the state of health. It produces [immunity](https://www.britannica.com/science/immunity-biology) to measles without producing a clinical [illness](https://www.britannica.com/science/disease) (an infectious disease).

The most important barriers to invasion of the human host by infectious agents are the skin and mucous membranes (the tissues that line the nose, mouth, and upper respiratory tract). When these tissues have been broken or affected by earlier disease, invasion by infectious agents may occur. These infectious agents may produce a local infectious disease, such as [boils](https://www.britannica.com/science/boil-skin-infection), or may invade the bloodstream and be carried throughout the body, producing generalized bloodstream infection ( [septicemia](https://www.britannica.com/science/septicemia)) or localized infection at a distant site, such as [meningitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/meningitis) (an infection of the coverings of the brain and spinal cord). Infectious agents swallowed in food and drink can attack the wall of the intestinal tract and cause local or general disease. The [conjunctiva](https://www.britannica.com/science/conjunctiva), which covers the front of the eye, may be penetrated by viruses that cause a local [inflammation](https://www.britannica.com/science/inflammation) of the eye or that pass into the bloodstream and cause a severe general disease, such as [smallpox](https://www.britannica.com/science/smallpox). Infectious agents can enter the body through the genital tract, causing the [acute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/acute) inflammatory reaction of [gonorrhea](https://www.britannica.com/science/gonorrhea) in the genital and pelvic organs or spreading out to attack almost any organ of the body with the more chronic and destructive lesions of [syphilis](https://www.britannica.com/science/syphilis). Even before [birth](https://www.britannica.com/science/birth), viruses and other infectious agents can pass through the [placenta](https://www.britannica.com/science/placenta-human-and-animal) and attack developing cells, so that an infant may be diseased or deformed at birth.

## [Bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria)

[![MRSA and neutrophil](https://cdn.britannica.com/26/192126-050-B29B1FBB/neutrophils-Staphylococcus-aureus-bacteria-human-white-blood.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/26/192126-050-B29B1FBB/neutrophils-Staphylococcus-aureus-bacteria-human-white-blood.jpg)

[MRSA and neutrophil](https://cdn.britannica.com/26/192126-050-B29B1FBB/neutrophils-Staphylococcus-aureus-bacteria-human-white-blood.jpg) Four methicillin-resistant _Staphylococcus aureus_ (MRSA) bacteria (purple) being engulfed by neutrophils (blue), which are a type of human white blood cell.(more)

[Bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria) can survive within the body but outside individual cells. Some bacteria, classified as [aerobes](https://www.britannica.com/science/aerobe), require oxygen for growth, while others, such as those normally found in the [small intestine](https://www.britannica.com/science/small-intestine) of healthy persons, grow only in the absence of oxygen and, therefore, are called [anaerobes](https://www.britannica.com/science/anaerobe). Most bacteria are surrounded by a capsule that appears to play an important role in their ability to produce disease. Also, a number of bacterial [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) give off toxins that in turn may damage tissues. Bacteria are generally large enough to be seen under a light [microscope](https://www.britannica.com/technology/microscope). Streptococci, the bacteria that cause [scarlet fever](https://www.britannica.com/science/scarlet-fever), are about 0.75 μm (0.00003 inch) in [diameter](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/diameter). The [spirochetes](https://www.britannica.com/science/spirochete), which cause [syphilis](https://www.britannica.com/science/syphilis), [leptospirosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/leptospirosis), and [rat-bite fever](https://www.britannica.com/science/rat-bite-fever), are 5 to 15 μm long. Bacterial infections can be treated with [antibiotics](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibiotic).

Bacterial infections are commonly caused by [pneumococci](https://www.britannica.com/science/pneumococcus), [staphylococci](https://www.britannica.com/science/Staphylococcus), and [streptococci](https://www.britannica.com/science/Streptococcus), all of which are often commensals (that is, organisms living harmlessly on their hosts) in the upper [respiratory tract](https://www.britannica.com/science/respiratory-system) but that can become virulent and cause serious conditions, such as pneumonia, septicemia (blood poisoning), and meningitis. The [pneumococcus](https://www.britannica.com/science/pneumococcus) is the most common cause of lobar [pneumonia](https://www.britannica.com/science/pneumonia), the disease in which one or more lobes, or segments, of the lung become solid and airless as a result of inflammation. [Staphylococci](https://www.britannica.com/science/Staphylococcus) affect the lungs either in the course of staphylococcal septicemia—when bacteria in the circulating blood cause scattered abscesses in the lungs—or as a complication of a viral infection, commonly [influenza](https://www.britannica.com/science/influenza)—when these organisms invade the damaged lung cells and cause a life-threatening form of pneumonia. Streptococcal pneumonia is the least common of the three and occurs usually as a complication of influenza or other lung disease.

Pneumococci often enter the bloodstream from inflamed lungs and cause septicemia, with continued [fever](https://www.britannica.com/science/fever) but no other special [symptoms](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/symptoms). Staphylococci produce a type of septicemia with high spiking fever; the bacteria can reach almost any organ of the body—including the brain, the bones, and especially the lungs—and destructive abscesses form in the infected areas. [Streptococci](https://www.britannica.com/science/Streptococcus) also cause septicemia with fever, but the organisms tend to cause inflammation of surface lining cells rather than abscesses—for example, [pleurisy](https://www.britannica.com/science/pleurisy) (inflammation of the chest lining) rather than lung [abscess](https://www.britannica.com/science/abscess), and [peritonitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/peritonitis) (inflammation of the membrane lining the abdomen) rather than liver abscess. In the course of either of the last two forms of septicemia, organisms may enter the [nervous system](https://www.britannica.com/science/nervous-system) and cause streptococcal or staphylococcal [meningitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/meningitis), but these are rare conditions. Pneumococci, on the other hand, often spread directly into the [central nervous system](https://www.britannica.com/science/central-nervous-system), causing one of the common forms of meningitis.

Staphylococci and streptococci are common causes of skin diseases. Boils and [impetigo](https://www.britannica.com/science/impetigo) (in which the skin is covered with blisters, pustules, and yellow crusts) may be caused by either. Staphylococci also can cause a severe skin infection that strips the outer skin layers off the body and leaves the underlayers exposed, as in severe burns, a condition known as toxic epidermal necrolysis. Streptococcal organisms can cause a severe condition known as [necrotizing fasciitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/necrotizing-fasciitis), commonly referred to as flesh-eating disease, which has a fatality rate between 25 and 75 percent. Streptococci can be the cause of the red [cellulitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/cellulitis) of the skin known as [erysipelas](https://www.britannica.com/science/erysipelas).

Some staphylococci produce an intestinal [toxin](https://www.britannica.com/science/toxin) and cause [food poisoning](https://www.britannica.com/science/food-poisoning). Certain streptococci settling in the throat produce a reddening toxin that speeds through the bloodstream and produces the symptoms of [scarlet fever](https://www.britannica.com/science/scarlet-fever). Streptococci and staphylococci also can cause [toxic shock syndrome](https://www.britannica.com/science/toxic-shock-syndrome), a potentially fatal disease. Streptococcal toxic shock syndrome (STSS) is fatal in some 35 percent of cases.

[Meningococci](https://www.britannica.com/science/meningococcus) are fairly common inhabitants of the throat, in most cases causing no illness at all. As the number of healthy carriers increases in any population, however, there is a tendency for the meningococcus to become more invasive. When an opportunity is presented, it can gain access to the bloodstream, invade the central nervous system, and cause meningococcal meningitis (formerly called cerebrospinal meningitis or spotted fever). Meningococcal meningitis, at one time a dreaded and still a very serious disease, usually responds to [treatment](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/treatment) with [penicillin](https://www.britannica.com/science/penicillin) if diagnosed early enough. When meningococci invade the bloodstream, some gain access to the skin and cause bloodstained spots, or [purpura](https://www.britannica.com/science/purpura). If the condition is diagnosed early enough, antibiotics can clear the bloodstream of the bacterium and prevent any from getting far enough to cause meningitis. Sometimes the septicemia takes a mild, chronic, relapsing form with no tendency toward meningitis; this is curable once it is diagnosed. The meningococcus also can cause one of the most fulminating of all forms of septicemia, meningococcemia, in which the body is rapidly covered with a purple rash, purpura fulminans; in this form the [blood pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-pressure) becomes dangerously low, the heart and blood [vessels](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/vessels) are affected by [shock](https://www.britannica.com/science/shock-physiology), and the infected person dies within a matter of hours. Few are saved, despite treatment with appropriate drugs.

_[Haemophilus influenzae](https://www.britannica.com/science/Haemophilus-influenzae)_ is a microorganism named for its occurrence in the sputum of patients with [influenza](https://www.britannica.com/science/influenza)—an occurrence so common that it was at one time thought to be the cause of the disease. It is now known to be a common inhabitant of the nose and throat that may invade the bloodstream, producing meningitis, pneumonia, and various other diseases. In children it is the most common cause of acute [epiglottitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/epiglottitis), an infection in which [tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/tissue) at the back of the tongue becomes rapidly swollen and obstructs the airway, creating a potentially fatal condition. _H. influenzae_ also is the most common cause of meningitis and pneumonia in children under five years of age, and it is known to cause bronchitis in adults. The [diagnosis](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diagnosis) is established by [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) of blood, [cerebrospinal fluid](https://www.britannica.com/science/cerebrospinal-fluid), or other tissue from sites of infection. Antibiotic therapy is generally effective, although death from [sepsis](https://www.britannica.com/science/sepsis) or meningitis is still common. In developed countries where _H. influenza_ vaccine is used, there has been a great decrease in serious infections and deaths.

## Chlamydial organisms

[![Chlamydia](https://cdn.britannica.com/44/166544-050-326DA6EE/Chlamydia-trachomatis-Pap-smear-specimen-cause-sexually.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/44/166544-050-326DA6EE/Chlamydia-trachomatis-Pap-smear-specimen-cause-sexually.jpg)

[Chlamydia](https://cdn.britannica.com/44/166544-050-326DA6EE/Chlamydia-trachomatis-Pap-smear-specimen-cause-sexually.jpg) A Pap smear specimen showing the bacterium _Chlamydia trachomatis_, the cause of the sexually transmitted disease chlamydia. (Bacteria appear within vacuoles.)(more)

[_Chlamydia_](https://www.britannica.com/science/Chlamydia-microorganism) are intracellular organisms found in many vertebrates, including birds and humans and other mammals. Clinical illnesses are caused by the species _[C. trachomatis](https://www.britannica.com/science/Chlamydia-trachomatis)_, which is a frequent cause of genital infections in women. If an infant passes through an infected birth canal, it can produce disease of the eye ( [conjunctivitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/conjunctivitis)) and pneumonia in the newborn. Young children sometimes develop ear infections, [laryngitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/laryngitis), and upper respiratory tract disease from _Chlamydia_. Such infections can be treated with [erythromycin](https://www.britannica.com/science/erythromycin).

Another chlamydial organism, _[Chlamydophila psittaci](https://www.britannica.com/science/Chlamydia-psittaci)_, produces [psittacosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/psittacosis), a disease that results from exposure to the [discharges](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/discharges) of infected birds. The illness is characterized by high fever with chills, a slow [heart rate](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart-rate), pneumonia, [headache](https://www.britannica.com/science/headache), weakness, [fatigue](https://www.britannica.com/science/fatigue-physiology), muscle pains, [anorexia](https://www.britannica.com/science/anorexia), nausea, and vomiting. The diagnosis is usually suspected if the patient has a history of exposure to birds. It is confirmed by blood tests. Mortality is rare, and specific antibiotic treatment is available.

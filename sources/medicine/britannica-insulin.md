- What is insulin?







[Insulin](https://www.britannica.com/science/insulin/Introduction#ref1) is a hormone produced by beta cells in the [pancreas](https://www.britannica.com/science/insulin/Introduction#ref1). It regulates blood sugar (glucose) levels, increasing glucose uptake in fat, muscle, and liver tissues to maintain energy balance.



When blood glucose rises, insulin is secreted; when it falls, insulin secretion stops, and the liver releases glucose. Insulin stimulates glucose transporters to move to the cell membrane, facilitating glucose uptake. Insufficient insulin production leads to [diabetes mellitus](https://www.britannica.com/science/diabetes-mellitus/Introduction#ref285756), requiring insulin injections, which are now primarily human insulin produced using recombinant DNA technology.

- Where in the body is insulin made?

- What is the main function of insulin in the body?







The primary role of [insulin](https://www.britannica.com/science/insulin/Introduction#ref1) is to regulate [glucose](https://www.britannica.com/science/diabetes-mellitus/Introduction#ref285756) levels in the blood. Produced by the pancreas' beta cells, insulin prompts cells to absorb glucose from the blood for energy or storage as glycogen.



After a meal, as blood glucose rises, insulin is released; conversely, insulin secretion decreases as blood glucose levels fall. Insulin stimulates glucose uptake in adipose (fat), muscle, and liver tissues, which is crucial for nutrient metabolism and storage. Insufficient insulin production leads to [diabetes mellitus](https://www.britannica.com/science/diabetes-mellitus), requiring insulin therapy to maintain normal blood glucose levels.

- What happens if the body does not make enough insulin?







When the body doesn't produce enough [insulin](https://www.britannica.com/science/insulin), a hormone that regulates blood sugar (glucose) levels, it leads to a condition called [diabetes mellitus](https://www.britannica.com/science/diabetes-mellitus). Insulin is secreted by beta cells in the pancreas and is essential for cells to uptake glucose from the blood for energy.



Insufficient insulin results in [hyperglycemia](https://www.britannica.com/science/diabetes-mellitus/Gestational-diabetes#ref285759), or high blood sugar, as glucose can't enter cells effectively. This can cause symptoms such as increased thirst and frequent urination as the body tries to eliminate excess glucose through urine. Over time, diabetes can lead to serious health complications, including cardiovascular disease, nerve damage, kidney failure, and vision loss. Treatment typically involves insulin therapy to help regulate blood glucose levels.

- How is insulin used as a treatment for diabetes?







[Insulin](https://www.britannica.com/science/insulin/Introduction#ref1) is a hormone that regulates blood sugar levels, and inadequate insulin production leads to diabetes mellitus. Insulin therapy is essential for diabetics who do not produce it themselves.



Before insulin was discovered in the 1920s, most patients died shortly after the onset of diabetes. Traditional insulin therapy involves regular hormone injections, tailored to individual needs. Modern treatments use recombinant DNA technology to produce human insulin, which can be short-acting or long-acting.



The goal is to mimic the natural insulin secretion pattern: a constant low level plus a surge after each meal. This can be achieved with a daily long-acting insulin shot and a rapid-acting one before meals. Insulin pumps offer more control over administration rates. Patients on insulin may need to adjust their food intake based on activity levels, needing less insulin and more food as exercise increases.

**insulin**, [hormone](https://www.britannica.com/science/hormone) that regulates the level of sugar ( [glucose](https://www.britannica.com/science/glucose)) in the blood and that is produced by the [beta](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/beta) cells of the [islets of Langerhans](https://www.britannica.com/science/islets-of-Langerhans) in the [pancreas](https://www.britannica.com/science/pancreas). Insulin is secreted when the level of blood glucose rises—as after a meal. When the level of blood glucose falls, [secretion](https://www.britannica.com/science/secretion) of insulin stops, and the [liver](https://www.britannica.com/science/liver) releases glucose into the blood. Insulin was first reported in pancreatic extracts in 1921, having been identified by Canadian scientists [Frederick G. Banting](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Frederick-Banting) and [Charles H. Best](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-H-Best) and by Romanian physiologist [Nicolas C. Paulescu](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Nicolas-C-Paulescu), who was working independently and called the substance “pancrein.” After Banting and Best isolated insulin, they began work to obtain a purified extract, which they accomplished with the help of Scottish physiologist [J.J.R. Macleod](https://www.britannica.com/biography/J-J-R-Macleod) and Canadian chemist James B. Collip. Banting and Macleod shared the 1923 [Nobel Prize](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nobel-Prize) for Physiology or Medicine for their work.

![Hormones secreted from adipose tissue, the gastrointestinal tract, and the pancreatic islets of Langerhans regulate a variety of physiological processes.](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/116188-050-64AB502D/Hormones-adipose-tissue-gastrointestinal-tract-islets-variety.jpg)

[Hormones secreted from adipose tissue, the gastrointestinal tract, and the pancreatic islets of Langerhans regulate a variety of physiological processes.](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/116188-050-64AB502D/Hormones-adipose-tissue-gastrointestinal-tract-islets-variety.jpg)(more)

[Insulin](https://www.britannica.com/science/insulin) is a [protein](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) composed of two chains, an A chain (with 21 [amino acids](https://www.britannica.com/science/amino-acid)) and a B chain (with 30 amino acids), which are linked together by [sulfur](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur) atoms. Insulin is derived from a 74-amino-acid prohormone [molecule](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) called [proinsulin](https://www.britannica.com/science/proinsulin). Proinsulin is relatively inactive, and under normal conditions only a small amount of it is secreted. In the [endoplasmic reticulum](https://www.britannica.com/science/endoplasmic-reticulum) of beta cells the proinsulin molecule is [cleaved](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cleaved) in two places, yielding the A and B chains of insulin and an intervening, biologically inactive C [peptide](https://www.britannica.com/science/peptide). The A and B chains become linked together by two sulfur-sulfur (disulfide) bonds. Proinsulin, insulin, and C peptide are stored in granules in the beta cells, from which they are released into the [capillaries](https://www.britannica.com/science/capillary) of the islets in response to appropriate stimuli. These capillaries empty into the [portal vein](https://www.britannica.com/science/portal-vein), which carries blood from the [stomach](https://www.britannica.com/science/stomach), [intestines](https://www.britannica.com/science/intestine), and pancreas to the liver. The pancreas of a normal adult contains approximately 200 units of insulin, and the average daily secretion of insulin into the [circulation](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/circulation) in healthy individuals ranges from 30 to 50 units.

Several factors stimulate insulin secretion, but by far the most important is the concentration of glucose in the arterial (oxygenated) blood that perfuses the islets. When blood glucose concentrations increase (i.e., following a meal), large amounts of glucose are taken up and metabolized by the beta cells, and the secretion of insulin increases. Conversely, as blood glucose concentrations decrease, the secretion of insulin decreases; however, even during [fasting](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fasting), small amounts of insulin are secreted. The secretion of insulin may also be stimulated by certain amino acids, [fatty acids](https://www.britannica.com/science/fatty-acid), keto acids (products of fatty acid oxidation), and several hormones secreted by the [gastrointestinal tract](https://www.britannica.com/science/gastrointestinal-tract). The secretion of insulin is [inhibited](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibited) by [somatostatin](https://www.britannica.com/science/somatostatin) and by activation of the [sympathetic nervous system](https://www.britannica.com/science/sympathetic-nervous-system) (the branch of the [autonomic nervous system](https://www.britannica.com/science/autonomic-nervous-system) responsible for the [fight-or-flight response](https://www.britannica.com/science/fight-or-flight-response)).

Insulin acts primarily to stimulate glucose uptake by three tissues— [adipose](https://www.britannica.com/science/adipose-cell) (fat), [muscle](https://www.britannica.com/science/muscle), and [liver](https://www.britannica.com/science/liver)—that are important in the [metabolism](https://www.britannica.com/science/metabolism) and storage of nutrients. Like other protein hormones, insulin binds to specific [receptors](https://www.britannica.com/science/receptor-nerve-ending) on the outer membrane of its target cells, thereby activating metabolic processes within the cells. A key action of insulin in these cells is to stimulate the translocation of glucose transporters (molecules that mediate cell uptake of glucose) from within the cell to the [cell membrane](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-membrane).

In [adipose tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/adipose-tissue), insulin [stimulates](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/stimulates) glucose uptake and utilization. The presence of glucose in adipose cells in turn leads to increased uptake of fatty acids from the circulation, increased synthesis of fatty acids in the cells, and increased esterification (when an acid molecule binds to an alcohol) of fatty acids with [glycerol](https://www.britannica.com/science/glycerol) to form [triglycerides](https://www.britannica.com/science/triglyceride), the storage form of [fat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fat). In addition, insulin is a potent inhibitor of the breakdown of triglycerides (lipolysis). This prevents the release of fatty acids and glycerol from fat cells, saving them for when they are needed by the body (e.g., when exercising or fasting). As serum insulin concentrations decrease, lipolysis and fatty acid release increase.

In muscle tissue, insulin stimulates the transport of glucose and amino acids into muscle cells. The glucose is stored as [glycogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/glycogen), a storage molecule that can be broken down to supply energy for muscle contraction during exercise and to supply energy during fasting. The amino acids transported into muscle cells in response to insulin stimulation are utilized for the synthesis of protein. In contrast, in the absence of insulin the protein of muscle cells is broken down to supply amino acids to the liver for transformation into glucose.

Insulin is not required for the transport of glucose into liver cells, but it has profound effects on glucose metabolism in these cells. It stimulates the formation of glycogen, and it [inhibits](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibits) the breakdown of glycogen ( [glycogenolysis](https://www.britannica.com/science/glycogenolysis)) and the synthesis of glucose from amino acids and glycerol ( [gluconeogenesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/gluconeogenesis)). Therefore, the overall effect of insulin is to increase glucose storage and to decrease glucose production and release by the liver. These actions of insulin are opposed by [glucagon](https://www.britannica.com/science/glucagon), another pancreatic hormone produced by cells in the islets of Langerhans.

### What is the molecular formula of glucose?

C6H12O6

### Where is glucose commonly found?

Glucose is commonly found in [fruits](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body) and [honey](https://www.britannica.com/topic/honey).

### What is the role of glucose in cell function?

Glucose serves as the source of [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) in [cell](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology) function.

### How does the liver help maintain blood glucose levels?

The [liver](https://www.britannica.com/science/liver) stores excess glucose as [glycogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/glycogen). It then releases glycogen when [blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-biochemistry) glucose levels drop. The liver also produces glucose from non-carbohydrate sources through [gluconeogenesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/gluconeogenesis).

### What are some compounds composed of glucose?

[Starch](https://www.britannica.com/science/starch) and [cellulose](https://www.britannica.com/science/cellulose) are examplas of major compounds composed of glucose.

**glucose**, one of a group of [carbohydrates](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbohydrate) known as simple sugars ( [monosaccharides](https://www.britannica.com/science/monosaccharide)). Glucose (from Greek _glykys_; “sweet”) has the molecular formula C6 [H](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) 12 [O](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) 6. It is found in [fruits](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body) and [honey](https://www.britannica.com/topic/honey) and is the major free [sugar](https://www.britannica.com/science/sugar-chemical-compound) circulating in the [blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-biochemistry) of higher animals.

Glucose is the source of energy in [cell](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology) function. The regulation of its [metabolism](https://www.britannica.com/science/metabolism) is of great importance and is relevant in various metabolic processes, examples being [fermentation](https://www.britannica.com/science/fermentation) and [gluconeogenesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/gluconeogenesis). Molecules of [starch](https://www.britannica.com/science/starch), the major energy-reserve [carbohydrate](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbohydrate) of [plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant), consist of thousands of linear glucose units. Another major [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) composed of glucose is [cellulose](https://www.britannica.com/science/cellulose), which is also linear. Dextrose is the [molecule](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) d-glucose.

The maintenance of the glucose content of [vertebrate](https://www.britannica.com/animal/vertebrate) blood requires glucose 6-phosphate to be converted to glucose. This process occurs in the [kidney](https://www.britannica.com/science/kidney), in the lining of the [intestine](https://www.britannica.com/science/intestine), and most importantly in the [liver](https://www.britannica.com/science/liver). The liver stores excess glucose as [glycogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/glycogen), a reserve carbohydrate, and releases it when blood glucose levels drop, thereby preventing [hypoglycemia](https://www.britannica.com/science/hypoglycemia). In addition, the liver can produce glucose from non-carbohydrate sources through gluconeogenesis, which helps ensure a steady supply of glucose for the body.

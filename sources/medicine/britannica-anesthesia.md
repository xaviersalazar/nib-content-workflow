**anesthesia**, loss of physical [sensation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sensation), with or without loss of [consciousness](https://www.britannica.com/topic/consciousness), as artificially induced by the administration of [drugs](https://www.britannica.com/science/drug-chemical-agent), inhalant gases, or other agents. The use of [anesthetic](https://www.britannica.com/science/anesthetic) gases in [surgery](https://www.britannica.com/science/surgery-medicine) was first proposed by British chemist [Sir Humphrey Davy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sir-Humphry-Davy-Baronet) in 1798, following his observation that inhalation of [nitrous oxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrous-oxide) relieved [pain](https://www.britannica.com/science/pain). However, surgical anesthesia was not demonstrated with success publicly until 1846, when American surgeon [William Morton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Thomas-Green-Morton) used [ether](https://www.britannica.com/science/ether-chemical-compound) during an operation to remove a jaw [tumour](https://www.britannica.com/science/tumor) at Massachusetts General Hospital in [Boston](https://www.britannica.com/place/Boston). Another American surgeon, [Crawford Williamson Long](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Crawford-Williamson-Long), had used [ether](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ether) four years earlier but did not make his findings public until 1849. An attempt at public demonstration of dental extraction under anesthesia with nitrous oxide was made by American dentist [Horace Wells](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Horace-Wells) in 1845. Morton witnessed Wells’s attempt, which proved unsuccessful when the patient cried out during the extraction procedure.

[![intravenous drip](https://cdn.britannica.com/56/130456-050-D973A6D7/anesthetics-drip.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/56/130456-050-D973A6D7/anesthetics-drip.jpg)

[intravenous drip](https://cdn.britannica.com/56/130456-050-D973A6D7/anesthetics-drip.jpg) Some anesthetics are administered via intravenous drip.(more)

Early anesthetic agents were usually gases such as nitrous oxide or vapours from such volatile liquids as ether or [chloroform](https://www.britannica.com/science/chloroform). Injectable anesthetics were introduced in 1872 by French surgeon Pierre-Cyprien Oré, who used [chloral hydrate](https://www.britannica.com/science/chloral-hydrate) to produce general anesthesia. [Cocaine](https://www.britannica.com/science/cocaine) was used as a local anesthetic beginning in 1884 and as a spinal anesthetic by German surgeon August Karl Gustav Bier in 1898. [Synthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Synthetic) agents began to be introduced in the middle of the 20th century, beginning with [trichloroethylene](https://www.britannica.com/science/trichloroethylene) in 1941.

[![anesthesia](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/183141-050-BD55C5AB/patient-anesthesia.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/183141-050-BD55C5AB/patient-anesthesia.jpg)

[anesthesia](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/183141-050-BD55C5AB/patient-anesthesia.jpg) Medications for anesthesia was one of Zeneca Group's main specialties.(more)

Anesthetics are strong systemic poisons that can cause permanent injury or [death](https://www.britannica.com/science/death) if dosage is not carefully controlled, so cardiac and respiratory functions must be closely monitored during surgery under anesthesia. The first anesthesia was administered by simple devices consisting of glass or metal containers for sponges soaked in ether and allowed no control of dosage. Modern inhalant anesthetics are given in carefully measured doses through endotracheal tubes, usually following premedication with injectable drugs, such as [sedatives](https://www.britannica.com/science/sedative-hypnotic-drug) or [analgesic](https://www.britannica.com/science/analgesic) agents, to reduce patient anxiety and ease [induction](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/induction) of anesthesia.

The mechanisms by which anesthetics produce their effects are not entirely understood. These agents have been shown to block the transmission of signals across nerve junctions ( [synapses](https://www.britannica.com/science/synapse)) in the central [nervous system](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-nervous-system) without affecting [peripheral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/peripheral) nerves. There is evidence that inhalation anesthetics block synapses that participate in excitatory neuronal transmission, whereas some intravenous anesthetics primarily stimulate the activity of inhibitory neuronal transmission. Analgesic drugs, such as [morphine](https://www.britannica.com/science/morphine), [codeine](https://www.britannica.com/science/codeine), and [fentanyl](https://www.britannica.com/science/fentanyl), act on natural [receptors](https://www.britannica.com/science/receptor-nerve-ending) that already exist for the opioids.

- What is surgery in medicine?







Surgery, a branch of medicine, addresses injuries, diseases, and disorders through manual and instrumental methods. It manages acute injuries and illnesses, as well as chronic diseases requiring operations.



Contemporary surgery uses monitoring devices during and after procedures to track blood pressure, pulse rate, heart contractions, brain waves, oxygen levels, and respiratory exchange. Asepsis, crucial for preventing contamination, involves sterilizing instruments and the surgical field.



Surgical techniques include wound treatment, extirpative surgery (removal of diseased tissue), reconstructive surgery, and transplantation surgery. Lasers, stereotaxic surgery, cryosurgery, and endoscopy supplement traditional methods. The major specialties encompass general, plastic, orthopedic, neurosurgery, and other types of surgery.

- Why do people sometimes need surgery?







People sometimes need surgery for various reasons, broadly categorized as wound treatment, removal of diseased tissue, reconstruction, or transplantation.



  - **Wound Treatment** Aims to promote healing and prevent infection.
  - **Extirpative Surgery** Involves removing diseased organs or tissue, such as in cancer surgeries like mastectomy or hysterectomy.
  - **Reconstructive Surgery** Focuses on replacing lost tissues due to burns, fractures, or diseases, often using grafts or artificial devices like metal hip replacements or plastic heart valves.
  - **Transplantation Surgery** Replaces diseased organs with healthy ones from other bodies; kidneys are the most commonly transplanted organs.

Other reasons for surgery can include injury, infection, tumors, diabetes, or insufficient blood supply. In some cases, amputation, the removal of a body part, becomes necessary.

- What are some common types of surgery?







Surgery is a broad field, with procedures falling into various categories. Here are some common types of surgery:



  - **Extirpative Surgery** This involves removing diseased tissue or organs. Examples include mastectomy (removal of the breast), cholecystectomy (removal of the gallbladder), and hysterectomy (removal of the uterus).

  - **Reconstructive Surgery** This type focuses on replacing lost tissues resulting from fractures, burns, or degenerative diseases. Plastic and orthopedic surgery often involve reconstructive procedures, using grafts or artificial devices (prostheses) to replace damaged tissues or organs. Hip joint replacements using metal and heart valve replacements using plastic valves are common examples.

  - **Transplantation Surgery** This involves replacing diseased organs with organs from other bodies. Kidney transplants are the most common type of organ transplant.

  - **Open-heart surgery** Surgical procedures that require an incision into the heart, exposing one or more of the cardiac chambers, or those needing a heart-lung machine for maintaining blood circulation and oxygenation outside the body, are categorized as open-heart surgeries. These are commonly performed to repair valvular disease, correct congenital heart defects, and treat severe coronary artery disease.

  - **Aesthetic surgery** Also known as cosmetic surgery, aesthetic surgery enhances normal structures that have unusual features that are distressing to the patient or are subject to age-related changes.

- What tools and equipment are used during surgery?







Contemporary surgical therapy relies on various tools and equipment, including monitoring devices that track blood pressure, pulse rate, heart contractions, brain waves, and oxygen levels.



Asepsis is crucial, so sterilization is achieved using autoclaves, chemical sterilization, and antiseptic soaps.



During surgery, hemostasis (arresting bleeding) is achieved using [hemostats](https://www.britannica.com/science/surgery-medicine/Introduction#ref6993), clamps, and suture materials. Sponges absorb blood, and [electrocautery](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/electrosurgery) devices burn tissue to control bleeding. Common instruments include scalpels, forceps, retractors, and clamps.



Wound closure involves sutures, with absorbable materials like catgut or synthetic substitutes and nonabsorbable options such as nylon or steel staples. Lasers are used to destroy tumors and coagulate blood vessels. Robotic surgery enhances precision and enables minimally invasive procedures.

- How do doctors make sure patients recover safely after surgery?







To ensure patient safety and recovery after surgery, doctors focus on restoring patients to their normal physiological state through careful preoperative and postoperative care.



During surgery, monitoring devices track [blood pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-pressure), pulse rate, heart contractions, brain waves, oxygen levels, carbon dioxide partial pressure, and respiratory exchange. Aseptic techniques, including sterilization of instruments and the surgical field, are employed to prevent infection.



Postoperative care includes blood transfusions, intravenous fluids, and measures to prevent complications like [lung infections](https://www.britannica.com/science/lung-disease) and blood clotting.

## History

[![Ether anesthesia](https://cdn.britannica.com/25/131925-050-87501C61/ether-anesthesia-demonstration-William-Thomas-Green-Morton.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/25/131925-050-87501C61/ether-anesthesia-demonstration-William-Thomas-Green-Morton.jpg)

[Ether anesthesia](https://cdn.britannica.com/25/131925-050-87501C61/ether-anesthesia-demonstration-William-Thomas-Green-Morton.jpg) American dental surgeon William Thomas Green Morton administering ether anesthesia during the first successful public demonstration of its use in surgery, undated engraving.(more)

Surgery is as old as humanity, for anyone who has ever stanched a wound has acted as a surgeon. In some ancient civilizations surgery reached a rather high level of development, as in [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), China, Egypt, and Hellenistic Greece. In [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) during the [Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages), the practice of surgery was not taught in most universities, and ignorant barbers instead wielded the knife, either on their own responsibility or upon being called into cases by physicians. The organization of the United Company of Barber Surgeons of London in 1540 marked the beginning of some control of the qualifications of those who performed operations. This guild was the [precursor](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/precursor) of the Royal College of Surgeons of England.

In the 18th century, with increasing knowledge of [anatomy](https://www.britannica.com/science/anatomy), such operative procedures as [amputations](https://www.britannica.com/science/amputation) of the extremities, excision of [tumours](https://www.britannica.com/science/tumor) on the surface of the body, and removal of stones from the [urinary bladder](https://www.britannica.com/science/urinary-bladder) had helped to firmly establish surgery in the medical curriculum. Accurate anatomical knowledge enabled surgeons to operate more rapidly; patients were sedated with [opium](https://www.britannica.com/science/opium) or made drunk with alcohol, tied down, and a leg [amputation](https://www.britannica.com/science/amputation), for example, could then be done in three to five minutes. The [pain](https://www.britannica.com/science/pain) involved in such procedures, however, continued to limit expansion of the field until the introduction of [ether](https://www.britannica.com/science/ether-chemical-compound) [anesthesia](https://www.britannica.com/science/anesthesia) in 1846. The number of operations thereafter increased markedly, but only to [accentuate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/accentuate) the frequency and severity of “surgical [infections](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection).”

[![Joseph Lister](https://cdn.britannica.com/30/10230-050-F15519DE/Joseph-Lister.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/30/10230-050-F15519DE/Joseph-Lister.jpg)

[Joseph Lister](https://cdn.britannica.com/30/10230-050-F15519DE/Joseph-Lister.jpg) English surgeon Joseph Lister, undated photograph.(more)

In the mid-19th century the French microbiologist [Louis Pasteur](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-Pasteur) developed an understanding of the relationship of bacteria to infectious diseases, and the application of this theory to wound sepsis by the British surgeon [Joseph Lister](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Lister-Baron-Lister-of-Lyme-Regis) from 1867 resulted in the technique of [antisepsis](https://www.britannica.com/science/antiseptic), which brought about a remarkable reduction in the mortality rate from wound infections after operations. The twin emergence of anesthesia and antisepsis marked the beginning of modern surgery.

[![Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen](https://cdn.britannica.com/20/130320-050-57EDD079/Wilhelm-Conrad-Rontgen.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/20/130320-050-57EDD079/Wilhelm-Conrad-Rontgen.jpg)

[Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen](https://cdn.britannica.com/20/130320-050-57EDD079/Wilhelm-Conrad-Rontgen.jpg)(more)

[Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wilhelm-Rontgen)’s discovery of [X-rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray) at the turn of the 20th century added an important diagnostic tool to surgery, and the discovery of [blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-biochemistry) types in 1901 by Austrian biologist [Karl Landsteiner](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Karl-Landsteiner) made transfusions safer. New techniques of [anesthesia](https://www.britannica.com/science/anesthesiology) involving not only new agents for inhalation but also regional anesthesia accomplished by [nerve](https://www.britannica.com/science/nerve-anatomy) blocking (spinal and local anesthesia) were also introduced. The use of positive pressure and controlled [respiration](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/respiration) techniques (to prevent the [lung](https://www.britannica.com/science/lung) from collapsing when the pleural cavity was opened) made chest surgery practical and relatively safe for the first time. The intravenous administration (injection into the [veins](https://www.britannica.com/science/vein-blood-vessel)) of anesthetic agents was also adopted. In the period from the 1930s to the 1960s, the replenishment of body fluids by intravenous infusion, the introduction of chemicals and [antibiotics](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibiotic) to fight infection and to treat the metabolically disturbed body, and the development of [heart-lung machines](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart-lung-machine) helped bring surgery to a state in which every body cavity, system, organ, and area could safely be operated on.

## Present-day surgery

Contemporary surgical therapy is greatly helped by [monitoring](https://www.britannica.com/technology/control-system) devices that are used during surgery and during the postoperative period. [Blood pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-pressure) and [pulse](https://www.britannica.com/science/pulse-physiology) rate are monitored during an operation because a fall in the former and a rise in the latter give evidence of a critical loss of blood. Other items monitored are the heart [contractions](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/contractions) as indicated by [electrocardiograms](https://www.britannica.com/science/electrocardiography); tracings of [brain](https://www.britannica.com/science/brain) waves recorded by [electroencephalograms](https://www.britannica.com/science/electroencephalography), which reflect changes in brain function; the [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) level in [arteries](https://www.britannica.com/science/artery) and veins; [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide) partial pressure in the circulating blood; and respiratory volume and exchange. Intensive monitoring of the patient usually continues into the critical postoperative stage.

[Asepsis](https://www.britannica.com/science/asepsis), the freedom from contamination by pathogenic organisms, requires that all instruments and dry goods coming in contact with the surgical field be [sterilized](https://www.britannica.com/science/sterilization-biochemistry). This is accomplished by placing the materials in an [autoclave](https://www.britannica.com/technology/autoclave), which subjects its contents to a period of steam under pressure. Chemical [sterilization](https://www.britannica.com/science/sterilization-medicine) of some instruments is also used. The patient’s skin is sterilized by chemicals, and members of the surgical team scrub their hands and forearms with [antiseptic](https://www.britannica.com/science/antiseptic) or [disinfectant](https://www.britannica.com/technology/disinfectant) soaps. Sterilized gowns, caps, and masks that filter the team’s exhaled air and sterilized gloves of disposable plastic complete the picture. Thereafter, attention to avoiding contact with nonsterilized objects is the basis of maintaining asepsis.

During an operation, [hemostasis](https://www.britannica.com/science/hemostasis) (the arresting of bleeding) is achieved by use of the hemostat, a clamp with ratchets that grasps [blood vessels](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-vessel) or tissue; after application of hemostats, suture materials are tied around the [bleeding](https://www.britannica.com/science/bloodletting) vessels. Absorbent sterile napkins called sponges, made of a variety of natural and [synthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/synthetic) materials, are used for drying the field. Bleeding may also be controlled by electrocautery, the use of an instrument heated with an [electric current](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-current) to cauterize, or burn, vessel tissue. The most commonly used instruments in surgery are still the scalpel (knife), hemostatic forceps, flexible tissue-holding forceps, wound retractors for exposure, crushing and noncrushing clamps for intestinal and vascular surgery, and the curved needle for working in depth.

The most common method of closing wounds is by [sutures](https://www.britannica.com/science/suture-surgery). There are two basic types of suture materials; absorbable ones such as [catgut](https://www.britannica.com/technology/catgut) (which comes from sheep intestine) or synthetic substitutes; and nonabsorbable materials, such as nylon sutures, steel [staples](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/staples), or adhesive tissue tape. Catgut is still used extensively to tie off small blood vessels that are bleeding, and since the body absorbs it over time, no foreign materials are left in the wound to become a focus for disease organisms. Nylon stitches and steel staples are removed when sufficient healing has taken place.

There are three general techniques of wound treatment; primary intention, in which all tissues, including the [skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skin), are closed with suture material after completion of the operation; secondary intention, in which the [wound](https://www.britannica.com/science/wound) is left open and closes naturally; and third intention, in which the [wound](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/wound) is left open for a number of days and then closed if it is found to be clean. The third technique is used in badly contaminated wounds to allow drainage and thus avoid the entrapment of microorganisms. Military surgeons use this technique on wounds contaminated by shell fragments, pieces of clothing, and dirt.

The 20th and 21st centuries witnessed several new surgical technologies to supplement the techniques of manual incision. [Lasers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/laser) became widely used to destroy tumours and other pigmented lesions, some of which are inaccessible by conventional surgery. They are also used to surgically weld detached [retinas](https://www.britannica.com/science/retina) back in place and to coagulate blood vessels to stop them from bleeding. [Stereotaxic surgery](https://www.britannica.com/science/stereotaxic-surgery) uses a three-dimensional system of coordinates obtained by X-ray photography to accurately focus high-intensity radiation, cold, heat, or chemicals on tumours located deep in the brain that could not otherwise be reached. [Cryosurgery](https://www.britannica.com/science/cryosurgery) uses extreme cold to destroy warts and precancerous and cancerous skin [lesions](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/lesions) and to remove [cataracts](https://www.britannica.com/science/cataract-eye). Some traditional techniques of open surgery were replaced by the use of a thin flexible fibre-optic tube equipped with a light and a video connection; the tube, or [endoscope](https://www.britannica.com/science/endoscopy), is inserted into various bodily passages and provides views of the interior of hollow organs or vessels. Accessories added to the endoscope allow small surgical procedures to be executed inside the body without making a major incision.

Preoperative and postoperative care both have the same object: to restore patients to as near their normal physiologic state as possible. [Blood transfusions](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-transfusion), intravenous administration of fluids, and the use of measures to prevent common complications such as lung infection and [blood clotting](https://www.britannica.com/science/coagulation-of-blood) in the legs are the principal features of postoperative care.

There are four major categories of surgery: (1) [wound](https://www.britannica.com/science/wound) [treatment](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/treatment), (2) extirpative surgery, (3) reconstructive surgery, and (4) [transplantation](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery) surgery. The technical aspects of wound surgery, already partly discussed, centre on procuring good healing and the avoidance of infection. [Extirpative surgery](https://www.britannica.com/science/extirpative-surgery) involves the removal of diseased tissue or organs. [Cancer](https://www.britannica.com/science/cancer-disease) surgery usually falls into this category, with [mastectomy](https://www.britannica.com/science/mastectomy) (removal of the breast), cholecystectomy (removal of the [gallbladder](https://www.britannica.com/science/gallbladder)), and [hysterectomy](https://www.britannica.com/science/hysterectomy) (removal of the [uterus](https://www.britannica.com/science/uterus)) among the most frequent procedures. [Reconstructive surgery](https://www.britannica.com/science/reconstructive-surgery) deals with the replacement of lost tissues, whether from fractures, burns, or degenerative-disease processes, and is especially prominent in the practice of [plastic surgery](https://www.britannica.com/science/plastic-surgery) and orthopedic surgery. [Grafts](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Grafts) from the patient or from others are frequently used to replace lost tissues. Reconstructive surgery also uses artificial devices ( [prostheses](https://www.britannica.com/science/prosthesis)) to replace damaged or diseased organs or tissues. Common examples are the use of metal in reconstructing hip joints and the use of plastic valves to replace heart valves. [Transplantation](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery) surgery involves the use of organs transplanted from other bodies to replace diseased organs in patients. [Kidneys](https://www.britannica.com/science/kidney) are the most commonly transplanted organs.

The major medical specialties involving surgery are general surgery, plastic surgery, orthopedic surgery, [obstetrics and gynecology](https://www.britannica.com/science/obstetrics), neurosurgery, thoracic surgery, colon and rectal surgery, [otolaryngology](https://www.britannica.com/science/otolaryngology), [ophthalmology](https://www.britannica.com/science/ophthalmology), and [urology](https://www.britannica.com/science/urology). General surgery is the parent specialty and now centres on operations involving the [stomach](https://www.britannica.com/science/stomach), intestines, breast, blood [vessels](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/vessels) in the extremities, endocrine glands, tumours of soft tissues, and amputations. [Plastic surgery](https://www.britannica.com/science/plastic-surgery) is concerned with the bodily surface and with reconstructive work of the face and exposed parts. [Orthopedic surgery](https://www.britannica.com/science/orthopedics) deals with the bones, tendons, ligaments, and muscles; fractures of the extremities and congenital skeletal defects are common targets of treatment. Obstetricians perform [cesarean sections](https://www.britannica.com/science/cesarean-section), while gynecologists operate to remove tumours from the uterus and [ovaries](https://www.britannica.com/science/ovary-animal-and-human). Neurosurgeons operate to remove brain tumours, treat injuries to the brain resulting from skull fractures, and treat ruptured intravertebral disks that affect the [spinal cord](https://www.britannica.com/science/spinal-cord). Thoracic surgeons treat disorders of the lungs; the subspecialty of cardiovascular surgery is concerned with the [heart](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart) and its major blood vessels and has become a major field of surgical endeavour. Colon and rectal surgery deals with disorders of the [large intestine](https://www.britannica.com/science/large-intestine). Otolaryngologic surgery is performed in the area of the [ear](https://www.britannica.com/science/ear), [nose](https://www.britannica.com/science/nose), and [throat](https://www.britannica.com/science/pharynx) (e.g., tonsillectomy), while ophthalmologic surgery deals with disorders of the [eyes](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-eye). Urologic surgery treats diseases of the urinary tract and, in males, of the genital [apparatus](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/apparatus).

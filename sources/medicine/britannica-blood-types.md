- What is a blood group?







Blood groups are classifications of blood based on inherited characteristics of red blood cells, specifically the presence or absence of certain antigens on the cell surfaces. The ABO system, identified in 1901, is a primary classification, dividing blood into types A, B, AB, and O, depending on the presence of A and B antigens. The Rh system, discovered later, classifies blood based on the presence or absence of the Rh factor. Blood typing is essential for safe transfusions and to prevent conditions like [hemolytic disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-group/Paternity-testing#ref256888) in newborns due to blood group incompatibility between mother and fetus.

- What are the main types of blood groups?







Human blood is classified into different groups based on the presence or absence of certain antigens on the surface of red blood cells. The ABO blood group system, identified by Karl Landsteiner in 1901, is a primary classification. This system categorizes blood into four main types: A, B, AB, and O.



Another significant system is the Rh blood group system, also discovered by Landsteiner in 1940. This system classifies blood based on the presence or absence of the Rh antigen (Rh factor). Those with the antigen are Rh-positive, while those lacking it are Rh-negative.



Besides ABO and Rh, other blood group systems exist, including Kell, Diego, Lutheran, Duffy, and Kidd. These systems were identified later through the study of antibodies in patients.

- How are blood groups determined?







Blood groups are determined by the presence or absence of specific [antigens](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-group/The-importance-of-antigens-and-antibodies#ref256882) on the surface of red blood cells. These antigens are inherited and represent genetic variations. The ABO blood group system classifies blood into four types: A, B, AB, and O, based on the presence or absence of A and B antigens. The Rh blood group system adds another layer, classifying blood as positive or negative based on the presence or absence of the Rh factor. These variations have evolved to influence immune responses and, in some instances, provide resistance to disease. Blood typing is essential for safe [transfusions](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-typing/Introduction#ref1), as incompatible blood can cause serious reactions.

- What happens if someone receives the wrong blood group during a transfusion?







Receiving the wrong blood group during a transfusion can trigger a rapid and severe immune response, known as an intravascular hemolytic reaction. This occurs because the recipient's antibodies attack the antigens on the donor's red blood cells, leading to the destruction of the transfused cells.



This type of incompatibility in ABO blood groups can be fatal and may arise from misidentification of blood samples or patients, or mislabeling of blood components. Even if a reaction isn't due to red cell antigen-antibody reactions, it could stem from antibodies to the donor’s platelets or white cells.



To prevent these reactions, donated blood is tested, and compatibility tests are performed before transfusion to identify any potential interactions between the donor's cells and the recipient's serum.

- What is the Rh factor in blood groups?







The Rh factor is an antigen on red blood cells, also known as the Rh antigen. The Rh blood group system classifies blood groups based on whether this antigen is present. If you have the Rh antigen, you are Rh-positive; if not, you are Rh-negative.



If an Rh-negative person receives Rh-positive blood, their immune system may react and produce anti-Rh antibodies. These antibodies can cause agglutination and hemolysis if Rh-positive blood is given again, which can result in severe illness or death. During pregnancy, if an Rh-negative mother carries an Rh-positive fetus, the mother can produce antibodies that attack subsequent Rh-incompatible fetuses, leading to erythroblastosis fetalis. This condition can be prevented by vaccinating the mother with Rh immunoglobulin.

- Why is it important to know your blood group for medical situations?







Knowing your blood group is vital in medical situations, especially when blood transfusions or organ transplants are necessary. The ABO and Rh systems are critical for matching blood types to prevent adverse reactions.



\

During transfusions, incompatible blood can cause a hemolytic transfusion reaction, which can be fatal. Similarly, in organ transplants, ABO-compatible matches are preferred to minimize the risk of rejection. In emergencies, knowing your blood type ensures you receive the correct blood, and in the case of women, it is especially important to know the Rh factor to prevent [erythroblastosis fetalis](https://www.britannica.com/science/Rh-blood-group-system/Introduction#ref1) in newborns.

## Historical background

[![Gloved hands hold a blood sample tube above a rack of vials in a laboratory, illustrating blood analysis in human body science.](https://cdn.britannica.com/94/253594-138-6D2D3DBC/How-much-blood-is-in-the-human-body.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/How-much-blood-is-in-the-human-body/-299667)

English physician [William Harvey](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Harvey) announced his observations on the [circulation](https://www.britannica.com/science/circulatory-system) of the blood in 1616 and published his famous monograph titled _Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis et Sanguinis in Animalibus_ ( _[The Anatomical Exercises Concerning the Motion of the Heart and Blood in Animals](https://www.britannica.com/topic/An-Anatomical-Disquisition-On-the-Motion-of-the-Heart-and-Blood-in-Animals)_) in 1628. His discovery, that blood circulates around the body in a closed system, was an essential prerequisite of the concept of [transfusing](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-transfusion) [blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-transfusion) from one animal to another of the same or different species. In England, experiments on the transfusion of blood were pioneered in dogs in 1665 by physician [Richard Lower](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Richard-Lower). In November 1667 Lower transfused the blood of a lamb into a man.

Meanwhile, in France, [Jean-Baptiste Denis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Baptiste-Denis), court physician to [King Louis XIV](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-XIV-king-of-France), had also been transfusing lambs’ blood into human subjects and described what is probably the first recorded account of the signs and [symptoms](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/symptoms) of a hemolytic transfusion reaction. Denis was arrested after a fatality, and the procedure of transfusing the blood of other animals into humans was prohibited, by an act of the Chamber of Deputies in 1668, unless sanctioned by the Faculty of Medicine of Paris. Ten years later, in 1678, the British Parliament also prohibited transfusions. Little advance was made in the next 150 years.

In England in the 19th century, interest was reawakened by the activities of obstetrician James Blundell, whose humanitarian instincts had been aroused by the frequently fatal outcome of [hemorrhage](https://www.britannica.com/science/hemorrhage) occurring after childbirth. He insisted that it was better to use human blood for transfusion in such cases.

In 1875 German physiologist Leonard Landois showed that, if the red blood cells of an animal belonging to one species are mixed with [serum](https://www.britannica.com/science/serum) taken from an animal of another species, the red cells usually clump and sometimes burst—i.e., [hemolyze](https://www.britannica.com/science/hemolysis). He [attributed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/attributed) the appearance of black urine after transfusion of heterologous blood (blood from a different species) to the [hemolysis](https://www.britannica.com/science/hemolysis) of the incompatible red cells. Thus, the dangers of transfusing blood of another species to humans were established scientifically.

The human [ABO blood groups](https://www.britannica.com/science/ABO-blood-group-system) were discovered by Austrian-born American biologist [Karl Landsteiner](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Karl-Landsteiner) in 1901. Landsteiner found that there are substances in the blood, [antigens](https://www.britannica.com/science/antigen) and [antibodies](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibody), that induce clumping of red cells when red cells of one type are added to those of a second type. He recognized three groups—A, B, and O—based on their reactions to each other. A fourth group, AB, was identified a year later by another research team. Red cells of the A group clump with donor blood of the B group; those of the B group clump with blood of the A group; those of the AB group clump with those of the A or the B group because AB cells contain both A and B antigens; and those of the O group do not generally clump with any group, because they do not contain either A or B antigens. The application of knowledge of the ABO system in blood transfusion practice is of enormous importance, since mistakes can have fatal [consequences](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/consequences).

The discovery of the [Rh system](https://www.britannica.com/science/Rh-blood-group-system) by Landsteiner and Alexander Wiener in 1940 was made because they tested human red cells with [antisera](https://www.britannica.com/science/antiserum) developed in rabbits and guinea pigs by immunization of the animals with the red cells of the [rhesus monkey](https://www.britannica.com/animal/rhesus-monkey) ( _Macaca mulatta_).

Other blood groups were identified later, such as [Kell](https://www.britannica.com/science/Kell-blood-group-system), [Diego](https://www.britannica.com/science/Diego-blood-group-system), [Lutheran](https://www.britannica.com/science/Lutheran-blood-group-system), [Duffy](https://www.britannica.com/science/Duffy-blood-group-system), and [Kidd](https://www.britannica.com/science/Kidd-blood-group-system). The remaining blood group systems were first described after antibodies were identified in patients. Frequently, such discoveries resulted from the search for the explanation of an unexpected unfavorable reaction in a recipient after a transfusion with formerly compatible blood. In such cases the antibodies in the recipient were produced against previously unidentified antigens in the donor’s blood. In the case of the Rh system, for example, the presence of antibodies in the maternal [serum](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/serum) directed against antigens present on the child’s red cells can have serious consequences because of antigen-antibody reactions that produce [erythroblastosis fetalis](https://www.britannica.com/science/erythroblastosis-fetalis), or hemolytic disease of the newborn. Some of the other blood group systems—for example, the Kell and Kidd systems—were discovered because an infant was found to have erythroblastosis fetalis even though mother and child were compatible as far as the Rh system was concerned.

# The importance of [antigens](https://www.britannica.com/science/antigen) and antibodies

[![Close-up of a medical blood bag filled with red blood, showing attached tubes and connectors against a blurred background.](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/253595-138-811BABAC/Why-is-blood-red.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/Why-is-blood-red/-300798)

Why Is Blood Red?Are you “red-y” to find out?(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-group/images-videos)

The red cells of an individual contain [antigens](https://www.britannica.com/science/antigen) on their surfaces that correspond to their blood group and [antibodies](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibody) in the [serum](https://www.britannica.com/science/serum) that identify and combine with the [antigen](https://www.britannica.com/science/antigen) sites on the surfaces of red cells of another type. The reaction between red cells and corresponding antibodies usually results in clumping— [agglutination](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agglutination-grammar)—of the red cells; therefore, antigens on the surfaces of these red cells are often referred to as agglutinogens.

Antibodies are classified by molecular size and weight and by several other biochemical properties. Most [blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-biochemistry) group antibodies are classified as either immunoglobulin G (IgG) or immunoglobulin M (IgM), and occasionally the immunoglobulin A (IgA) class may exhibit blood group [specificity](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/specificity). Naturally occurring antibodies are the result of immunization by substances in nature that have structures similar to human blood groups. These antibodies are present in an individual despite the fact that there has been no previous exposure to the corresponding red [cell](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology) antigens—for example, anti-A in the [plasma](https://www.britannica.com/science/plasma-biology) of people of blood group B and anti-B in the plasma of people of blood group A.

Immune antibodies are evoked by exposure to the corresponding red cell antigen. [Immunization](https://www.britannica.com/science/immunization) (i.e., the production of antibodies in response to antigen) against blood group antigens in humans can occur as a result of [pregnancy](https://www.britannica.com/science/pregnancy), [blood transfusion](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-transfusion), or deliberate immunization. The combination of pregnancy and transfusion is a particularly [potent](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/potent) stimulus. Individual blood group antigens vary in their antigenic potential; for example, some of the antigens belonging to the Rh and ABO systems are strongly immunogenic (i.e., capable of inducing antibody formation), whereas the antigens of the Kidd and Duffy blood group systems are much weaker immunogens.

The blood group antigens are not restricted solely to red cells or even to hematopoietic tissues. The antigens of the ABO system are widely distributed throughout the tissues and have been unequivocally identified on platelets and white cells (both lymphocytes and polymorphonuclear leukocytes) and in [skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skin), the epithelial (lining) cells of the [gastrointestinal tract](https://www.britannica.com/science/gastrointestinal-tract), the [kidney](https://www.britannica.com/science/kidney), the urinary tract, and the lining of the [blood vessels](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-vessel). Evidence for the presence of the antigens of other blood group systems on cells other than red cells is less well [substantiated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/substantiated). Among the red cell antigens, only those of the ABO system are regarded as [tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/tissue) antigens and therefore need to be considered in organ [transplantation](https://www.britannica.com/science/transplant-surgery).

## Chemistry of the blood group substances

The exact chemical structure of some blood groups has been identified, as have the [gene](https://www.britannica.com/science/gene) products (i.e., those molecules synthesized as a result of an inherited [genetic code](https://www.britannica.com/science/genetic-code) on a gene of a [chromosome](https://www.britannica.com/science/chromosome)) that assist in synthesizing the antigens on the red cell surface that determine the blood type. Blood group [antigens](https://www.britannica.com/science/antigen) are present on [glycolipid](https://www.britannica.com/science/glycolipid) and glycoprotein molecules of the red [cell membrane](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-membrane). The [carbohydrate](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbohydrate) chains of the membrane glycolipids are oriented toward the external surface of the red cell membrane and carry antigens of the [ABO](https://www.britannica.com/science/ABO-blood-group-system), Hh, Ii, and P systems. [Glycoproteins](https://www.britannica.com/science/glycoprotein), which [traverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/traverse) the red cell membrane, have a polypeptide backbone to which carbohydrates are attached. An abundant glycoprotein, band 3, is a site of ABO(H) antigens. Another [integral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integral) membrane glycoprotein, [glycophorin A](https://www.britannica.com/science/glycophorin-A), contains large numbers of sialic acid molecules and MN blood group structures; another, [glycophorin B](https://www.britannica.com/science/glycophorin-B), contains Ss and U antigens.

The genes responsible for inheritance of ABH and Lewis antigens are glycosyltransferases (a group of [enzymes](https://www.britannica.com/science/enzyme) that catalyze the addition of specific sugar residues to the core [precursor](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/precursor) substance). For example, the _H_ gene codes for the production of a specific glycosyltransferase that adds l-fucose to a core precursor substance, resulting in the H antigen; the _L_ _e_ gene codes for the production of a specific glycosyltransferase that adds l-fucose to the same core precursor substance, but in a different place, forming the Lewis antigen; the _A_ gene adds _N_-acetyl-d-galactosamine (H must be present), forming the A antigen; and the _B_ gene adds d-galactose (H must be present), forming the B antigen. The P system is [analogous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogous) to the ABH and Lewis blood groups in the sense that the P antigens are built by the addition of sugars to precursor globoside and paragloboside glycolipids, and the genes responsible for these antigens must produce glycosyltransferase enzymes.

The genes that code for MNSs glycoproteins change two amino acids in the sequence of the glycoprotein to account for different antigen specificities. Additional analysis of red cell membrane glycoproteins has shown that in some cases the absence of blood group antigens is associated with an absence of minor membrane glycoproteins that are present normally in antigen-positive persons.

## Methods of [blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-analysis) grouping

## Identification of blood groups

The basic technique in identification of the [antigens](https://www.britannica.com/science/antigen) and [antibodies](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibody) of blood groups is the [agglutination test](https://www.britannica.com/science/agglutination-test). [Agglutination](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agglutination-grammar) of red cells results from antibody cross-linkages established when different specific combining sites of one antibody react with antigen on two different red cells. By mixing red cells (antigen) and [serum](https://www.britannica.com/science/serum) (antibody), either the type of antigen or the type of antibody can be determined depending on whether a cell of known antigen [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) or a serum with known antibody specificity is used.

In its simplest form, a volume of serum containing antibody is added to a thin suspension (2–5 percent) of red cells suspended in physiological saline solution in a small tube with a narrow diameter. After incubation at the appropriate temperature, the red cells will have settled to the bottom of the tube. These sedimented red cells are examined macroscopically (with the naked eye) for agglutination, or they may be spread on a slide and viewed through a low-power [microscope](https://www.britannica.com/technology/microscope).

An antibody that agglutinates red cells when they are suspended in saline solution is called a complete antibody. With powerful complete antibodies, such as anti-A and anti-B, agglutination reactions visible to the naked eye take place when a drop of antibody is placed on a slide together with a drop containing red cells in [suspension](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/suspension). After stirring, the slide is rocked, and agglutination is visible in a few minutes. It is always necessary in blood grouping to include a positive and a negative control for each test.

An antibody that does not clump red cells when they are suspended in saline solution is called incomplete. Such antibodies block the antigenic sites of the red cells so that subsequent addition of complete antibody of the same antigenic specificity does not result in agglutination. Incomplete antibodies will agglutinate red cells carrying the appropriate antigen, however, when the cells are suspended in media containing [protein](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein). [Serum albumin](https://www.britannica.com/science/serum-albumin) from the blood of cattle is a substance that is frequently used for this purpose. Red cells may also be rendered specifically agglutinable by incomplete antibodies after [treatment](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/treatment) with such protease enzymes as trypsin, papain, ficin, or bromelain.

After such infections as [pneumonia](https://www.britannica.com/science/pneumonia), red cells may become agglutinable by almost all normal sera because of exposure of a hidden antigenic site (T) as a result of the action of bacterial enzymes. When the patient recovers, the blood also returns to normal with respect to agglutination. It is unusual for the red cells to reflect antigenicity other than that determined by the individual’s genetic makeup. The presence of an acquired B antigen on the red cells has been described occasionally in diseases of the colon, thus allowing the red cell to express an antigenicity other than that genetically determined. Other diseases may alter immunoglobulins; for example, some may [induce](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/induce) the production of antibodies directed against the person’s own blood groups (autoimmune hemolytic [anemia](https://www.britannica.com/science/anemia)) and thus may interfere with blood grouping. In other diseases a defect in antibody synthesis may cause the absence of anti-A and anti-B antibody.

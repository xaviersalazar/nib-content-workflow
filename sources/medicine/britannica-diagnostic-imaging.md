- What is diagnostic imaging in medicine?







Diagnostic imaging in medicine uses technologies like [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging/Introduction#ref1), [sound waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging/Introduction#ref1), and [optical instruments with cameras](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging/Introduction#ref1) to create images of internal body structures for accurate diagnoses. This field, similar to radiology, includes X-rays, which are effective for imaging bones, but technologies like [computerized axial tomography (CAT)](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnosis/Mental-examination#ref301326), [magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnosis/Mental-examination#ref301326), and [positron emission tomography (PET)](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnosis/Mental-examination#ref301326) have largely replaced traditional X-ray methods. Diagnostic imaging is indispensable in virtually every branch of medicine.

- Why do doctors use diagnostic imaging?







Doctors use diagnostic imaging to produce images of internal body structures for accurate diagnoses. Diagnostic imaging technologies include [X-rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnosis/Mental-examination#ref301326), [ultrasound](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging/Introduction#ref1), [endoscopy](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging/Introduction#ref1), [magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-resonance-imaging/Introduction#ref1), [positron emission tomography (PET)](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnosis/Mental-examination#ref301326), and [computerized axial tomography (CAT)](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnosis/Mental-examination#ref301326). These non-invasive procedures help visualize tissues, detect abnormalities, and assess organ function.



Diagnostic imaging can detect tumors, examine fetuses, and visualize arteries and veins.

- How does an X-ray work to create images of the body?







X-rays create images by passing radiation through the body, which darkens photographic film. Dense materials, such as [bones](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging/Introduction#ref340116), absorb more X-rays, preventing them from reaching the film, while soft tissues absorb fewer rays.



To enhance contrast between soft tissues of similar densities, contrast media can be injected or ingested. These media are either radiopaque or radiotransparent. This allows for the [X-ray imaging](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging/Introduction#ref340116) of specific internal structures, such as blood vessels or the urinary tract.



X-ray tubes, invented by William D. Coolidge, produce X-rays by accelerating electrons to high speeds and colliding them with a target, typically made of [tungsten](https://www.britannica.com/technology/X-ray-tube/Introduction#ref1). Only a small fraction of the electron energy converts to X-rays.

- What is an MRI, and how is it different from an X-ray?







An MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) is a 3D diagnostic technique that uses a strong magnetic field and radio waves to create detailed images of the organs and structures within the body. It is particularly valuable for visualizing soft tissues, the brain, spinal cord, and cancellous bone.



X-rays use electromagnetic radiation to create images based on the differential absorption of X-ray photons by body structures. Bones absorb more radiation than soft tissues, creating shadows that reveal their structure.



Unlike X-rays, MRI does not use radiation. MRI is better for visualizing soft tissues, while X-rays are typically used for bone imaging.

- What is a CT scan and when might a doctor order one?







A [computed tomography (CT) scan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computed-tomography/Introduction#ref1) is a diagnostic imaging method that uses X-rays to create detailed, cross-sectional images of the body. During a CT scan, a narrow X-ray beam rotates around the patient, and detectors measure the strength of the X-rays from different angles. This data is then processed by a computer to generate a 3D image of the internal body part.



Doctors may order a CT scan to evaluate [stroke](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computed-tomography/Introduction#ref1), [abdominal tumors](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computed-tomography/Introduction#ref1), abscesses, or other abnormalities. CT scans offer more precise visualization of anatomical structures compared to conventional X-rays, often detecting lesions without exploratory surgery.

- How does ultrasound imaging work, and what are its common uses in medicine?







Ultrasound imaging, or ultrasonography, uses high-frequency sound waves to visualize internal body structures. A device emits ultrasonic waves that reflect off tissues, creating echoes. These echoes are processed to form images, displayed as static or moving pictures.



Common uses include fetal examinations, imaging organs (heart, liver, kidneys, gallbladder, breasts, eyes), diagnosing tumors, guiding biopsies, drainage procedures, and intrauterine surgery. Doppler ultrasound assesses blood flow, detecting arterial diseases and heart valve defects.



It is quick, relatively inexpensive, and, within the diagnostic range, has no known biological hazards. Though less precise than CAT scans or MRIs, ultrasound offers advantages like real-time imaging and good soft tissue contrast.

**diagnostic imaging**, the use of [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation) and certain other technologies to produce images of internal structures of the body for the purpose of accurate [diagnosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnosis). [Diagnostic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Diagnostic) imaging is roughly equivalent to [radiology](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiology), the branch of [medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/medicine) that uses radiation to diagnose and treat diseases. However, other technologies—including [ultrasound](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultrasound), which employs [sound](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics) waves to visualize [tissues](https://www.britannica.com/science/tissue), and [endoscopy](https://www.britannica.com/science/endoscopy) and similar methods in which a flexible optical instrument is equipped with a camera for imaging—may also be used.

## X-ray imaging

[X-rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray), used since 1895, were the first type of radiation to provide images of the interior of the body. X-rays pass through bodily tissues and also have the property of darkening photographic film when they strike it. As they penetrate tissues, the X-rays are absorbed differentially, with denser objects such as [bones](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy) absorbing more of the rays and thus preventing them from reaching the film. Soft tissues, on the other hand, absorb fewer rays; the result is that in an X-ray photograph of the interior of the body, bones show up as lighter areas and soft tissues show up as darker ones on the exposed film.

A [limiting factor](https://www.britannica.com/science/density-dependent-factor) in X-rays when used alone is the inability to distinguish between [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent), [differentiated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/differentiated) soft tissues of roughly the same density (i.e., it is not possible to produce contrasting tones between such objects on the exposed film). To obtain this contrast, a [contrast medium](https://www.britannica.com/science/contrast-medium)—a liquid or gaseous substance that is comparatively [opaque](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/opaque) to X-rays (radiopaque) or comparatively transparent to them—is injected into the body. Contrast-medium fluids can be injected into naturally occurring body cavities, injected into the bloodstream and lymphatic vessels, swallowed or introduced by [enema](https://www.britannica.com/science/enema) for study of the [digestive tract](https://www.britannica.com/science/gastrointestinal-tract), or injected around organs to show their external [contour](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contour). Different contrast media thus allow the X-ray imaging of particular types of soft internal structures, such as the [arteries](https://www.britannica.com/science/artery) and [veins](https://www.britannica.com/science/vein-blood-vessel) in [angiography](https://www.britannica.com/science/angiography), the passage of blood through the heart in [angiocardiography](https://www.britannica.com/science/angiocardiography), the [gallbladder](https://www.britannica.com/science/gallbladder) and biliary channels in [cholecystography](https://www.britannica.com/science/cholecystography), the [spinal cord](https://www.britannica.com/science/spinal-cord) in [myelography](https://www.britannica.com/science/myelography), and the urinary tract in [urography](https://www.britannica.com/science/urography). Virtually any part of the body can be examined for physiological disturbances of the normal structures by X-ray analysis. X-ray motion-picture films can record the body processes as the contrast media enter and leave parts of the body.

Other imaging techniques have been developed using X-rays. In [tomography](https://www.britannica.com/science/tomography), X-ray images of deep internal structures can be obtained by focusing the rays on a specific plane within the body. A more complex variation of this technique is [computed tomography](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computed-tomography), known as a CT scan.

## Nuclear medicine

The scanning of radioactive [isotopes](https://www.britannica.com/science/isotope) that have been injected into the tissues is a medical specialty called [nuclear medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/nuclear-medicine). Both isotope scanning and X-ray photography are used in [brain scanning](https://www.britannica.com/science/brain-scanning). An imaging technique related to isotope scanning is [positron emission tomography](https://www.britannica.com/topic/positron-emission-tomography). Another type of diagnostic imaging is [nuclear magnetic resonance](https://www.britannica.com/science/nuclear-magnetic-resonance), which creates images of thin slices of the body using very-high-frequency radio waves. [Ultrasound](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultrasonics) is a technique in which high-frequency sound waves are used for detecting abnormalities in internal organs. The varieties of radiation that are used in diagnostic imaging continues to expand, along with the techniques for using them.

## Endoscopy and related procedures

Procedures such as [endoscopy](https://www.britannica.com/science/endoscopy), [laparoscopy](https://www.britannica.com/science/laparoscopy), and [colposcopy](https://www.britannica.com/science/colposcopy) make use of generally flexible optical instruments that can be inserted through openings, either natural or surgical in origin, in the body. Many scope instruments are fitted with small video cameras that enable the physician or surgeon to view the tissues being examined on a large monitor. A number of scopes also are designed to enable [tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/tissue) [biopsy](https://www.britannica.com/science/biopsy), in which a small sample of tissue is collected for histological study, to be performed in [conjunction](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conjunction) with visual analysis.

- What is radiology in medicine?







Radiology is a branch of medicine that uses radiation to diagnose and treat diseases. It utilizes various forms of radiation, including X-rays, gamma rays, and nonionizing radiation like ultrasound waves and nuclear magnetic resonance.



Radiology's diagnostic capabilities stem from the ability of X-rays to penetrate tissues and create images based on varying densities. This allows doctors to visualize internal structures, identify abnormalities, and monitor conditions without surgery. While radiology offers significant benefits, efforts are made to minimize unnecessary radiation exposure.

- How do X-rays help doctors see inside the body?







X-rays enable doctors to visualize the body's interior by sending electromagnetic radiation through tissues. This technique helps doctors diagnose and monitor various diseases and abnormalities.

- What is the difference between a CT scan and an MRI?







Both [CT scans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computed-tomography/Introduction#ref1) and [MRIs](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-resonance-imaging/Introduction#ref1) are invaluable diagnostic imaging techniques that visualize internal organs and structures. CT scans use X-rays to create cross-sectional images, while MRI uses magnetic fields and radio waves. MRI excels in visualizing soft tissues, like the brain and spinal cord, and detecting subtle changes in the body. CT scans are the preferred method for evaluating stroke, abdominal tumors, and abscesses.



The clear distinction between black, gray, and white areas of the image allow pathological diagnosis in many cases. MRI does not visualize bone well.

- How do radiologists use images to detect diseases?







Radiologists use various imaging techniques to detect diseases by observing the internal structures of the body. These techniques include X-rays, which utilize differential absorption to create images. In this process, tissues absorb radiation based on their densities, allowing radiologists to visualize structures.



Computerized tomography (CT) scanning uses X-rays from different angles to create cross-sectional images, which helps in identifying abnormalities. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) uses magnetic fields and radio waves to produce detailed images of soft tissues. Ultrasound uses high-frequency sound waves to create images, especially useful for examining soft tissues and fetuses.

- What safety precautions are taken in radiology to protect patients from radiation?







In radiology, several safety measures are implemented to protect patients from the potential risks associated with radiation exposure. These include:



  - **Limiting exposure** Efforts are made to avoid unnecessary exposure to ionizing radiation in medicine. Each use of radiation is evaluated for its merits on a case-by-case basis.

  - **Risk assessment** Procedures involving radiation are considered to entail some degree of risk, and activities are assessed to ensure that the benefits outweigh the risks.

  - **Protective measures** Protective measures are taken to limit exposure.

  - **Newer techniques** Recent radiological techniques like CAT, NMR, and PET scanning provide safer means of examining internal body structures, yielding precise and clear images while diminishing the margin of error in therapeutic measures.

**radiology**, branch of medicine using radiation for the diagnosis and treatment of disease. Radiology originally involved the use of [X-rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray) in the diagnosis of disease and the use of X-rays, gamma rays, and other forms of ionizing radiation in the treatment of disease. In more recent years radiology has come also to embrace diagnosis by a method of organ scanning with the use of radioactive isotopes and also with nonionizing radiation, such as ultrasound waves and nuclear magnetic resonance. Similarly, the scope of radiotherapy has extended to include, in the treatment of cancer, such agents as hormones and chemotherapeutic drugs.

## Diagnosis

[![German physicist Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/20964-004-80386569/Wilhelm-Conrad-Rontgen.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/20964-004-80386569/Wilhelm-Conrad-Rontgen.jpg)

[German physicist Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/20964-004-80386569/Wilhelm-Conrad-Rontgen.jpg) The first X-ray was captured by Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen.(more)

X-rays were discovered by [Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wilhelm-Rontgen), a German professor of physics, in his laboratory in the University of Würzburg on Nov. 8, 1895. Early on, in [radiodiagnosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging), use was made of three of the properties of X-rays—their ability to penetrate the tissues, their photographic effect, and their ability to cause certain substances to fluoresce. In penetrating the tissues, the [radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiation) is absorbed differentially, depending on the densities of the tissues being penetrated. The radiation emerging from tissues thus produces on a photographic film or a fluorescent screen an image of the structures of differing densities within the body. The limiting factor in this method of diagnosis is the similarity between the densities of adjacent soft tissues within the body, with a resultant failure to produce a notable contrast between the images of adjacent structures or organs.

[![A mobile radiological unit](https://cdn.britannica.com/23/135723-050-6DA0CA01/automobile-Marie-Curie-Renault-vehicles-unit-soldiers-1914.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/23/135723-050-6DA0CA01/automobile-Marie-Curie-Renault-vehicles-unit-soldiers-1914.jpg)

[A mobile radiological unit](https://cdn.britannica.com/23/135723-050-6DA0CA01/automobile-Marie-Curie-Renault-vehicles-unit-soldiers-1914.jpg) Marie Curie driving a car converted into a mobile radiological unit used to treat wounded soldiers during World War I, 1914.(more)

During the first two decades following their discovery, X-rays were used largely for the diagnosis and control of treatment of fractures and for the localization of foreign bodies, such as bullets, during World War I. The physicians using these methods introduced artificial [contrast](https://www.britannica.com/science/contrast-medium) agents, such as a paste consisting of [barium sulfate](https://www.britannica.com/science/barium-sulfate), which is inert and nontoxic when taken by mouth. When a contrast agent is taken by mouth or introduced by [enema](https://www.britannica.com/science/enema), the various parts of the alimentary tract can be demonstrated and examined. Refinements of this technique continue to the present day, and radiological examination of the alimentary tract is an elegant and precise aid to diagnosis. Eventually a number of other contrast media were produced that could be injected into blood vessels. The media could thus be used either to demonstrate those vessels (whether arteries or veins) or, after their selective concentration and excretion by the kidneys, to show the urinary tract.

Within the first few months after Röntgen’s discovery, attempts were made to produce films of moving objects; thus, it was soon realized that radiology might be able to depict function and so demonstrate dynamic physiological functions rather than just static anatomy. Technical difficulties and the hazards of a high dose of radiation to the patient prevented the proper development of this technique.

In the 1950s an electronic method was devised to intensify the image, the so-called [image intensifier](https://www.britannica.com/technology/image-intensifier), which made possible the overcoming of the technical difficulties, and cineradiography became routine. During the whole period of the development of radiology, [photographic](https://www.britannica.com/technology/photography) techniques were also continually being improved. Single-coated photographic plates were used at first, and then double-coated [photographic films](https://www.britannica.com/technology/film-photography); photographic emulsions have now been developed to such a point that high speed can be provided with good definition and little intrusion of photographic grain into the image. Similarly, processing methods have been improved; automatic processors now can deliver a fully processed dry film in 90 seconds.

[Allan MacLeod Cormack](https://cdn.britannica.com/18/68918-050-F7F19B05/Allan-MacLeod-Cormack.jpg)(more)

A new form of X-ray imaging, [computerized axial tomography](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computed-tomography) (CAT scanning), was devised by [Godfrey Hounsfield](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Godfrey-Newbold-Hounsfield) of Great Britain and [Allan Cormack](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Allan-MacLeod-Cormack) of the United States during the 1970s. This method measures the attenuation of X-rays entering the body from many different angles. From these measurements a computer reconstructs the organ under study in a series of cross sections or planes. The technique allows soft tissues such as the liver and kidney to be clearly differentiated in the images reconstructed by the computer. This procedure adds enormously to the diagnostic information that can be provided by conventional X-rays. CAT scanners are now in use in many large hospitals and medical centres throughout the world.

A still more recently developed technique is [nuclear magnetic resonance](https://www.britannica.com/science/nuclear-magnetic-resonance) (NMR) scanning (also called magnetic resonance imaging, or MRI), in which radio waves are beamed into an individual who is subjected to a powerful magnetic field. Different atoms in the body absorb radio waves at different frequencies under the influence of the magnetic field. The way in which absorption takes place is measured and used by a computer to construct images of internal structures.

Another recent technique is [positron emission tomography](https://www.britannica.com/topic/positron-emission-tomography), or PET scanning, which involves the emission of particles of antimatter by compounds injected into the body being scanned. These particles, positrons, are neutralized by their opposites, electrons, and energy is released in the form of radiation as matter and antimatter annihilate each other. Detectors arranged around the body pick up the energy released and use it to follow the movements of the injected compound and its metabolism.

These relatively new radiological techniques provide much safer means of examining internal body structures. They also yield precise and clear images for the physician and diminish the margin of error in therapeutic measures.

## Therapy

In radiotherapy, use is made of the biological effects of [ionizing radiations](https://www.britannica.com/science/ionizing-radiation-injury). The early workers noted that large doses of radiation would cause, after some delay, reddening of the skin, which might lead to blistering and ulceration. Even small repeated doses, if occurring often enough, might produce serious skin lesions. It was argued, then, that a phenomenon producing such damage to normal tissues might be directed toward abnormal and undesirable tissues, such as cancer. Research into the fundamental nature of the biological action of radiation continues to the present day, and a new type of scientist, the radiobiologist, has emerged. About the same time as the uses of X-rays were first being applied to medicine, radium was discovered, and also the importance of the time factor as a modifier of the reaction of tissue to radiation was established. Thus was born the art of radiotherapy, at first based entirely on an empirical approach. It was soon noted that ionizing radiations also have the effect of alleviating pain, and so in the period of development of this form of treatment it was used rather extensively in the treatment of painful forms of arthritis, swellings of the salivary glands, herpes zoster or shingles, overgrowth of adenoids in children, and several other benign conditions. As knowledge of the possible harmful effects of radiation has grown, many of these applications have been discarded, except in special circumstances and under strict supervision.

The vast bulk of the practice of radiotherapy has to do with [cancer](https://www.britannica.com/science/cancer-disease), and it is here that the great advances have been made. The first step was to establish a unit of measurement. Since the early days physicians practicing treatment with ionizing radiations have worked in close collaboration with physicists, and much of the fundamental research has been undertaken by radiation physicists working alongside their medical colleagues. The establishment of an internationally accepted unit of measurement at Stockholm in 1925, the [roentgen](https://www.britannica.com/science/roentgen) unit, enabled physicists to undertake similar calibration in different centres and provided a means of devising a system of [dosimetry](https://www.britannica.com/science/dosimeter).

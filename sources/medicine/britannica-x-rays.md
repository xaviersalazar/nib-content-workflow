- What is an X-ray radiation beam?







An X-ray radiation beam is a stream of electromagnetic radiation with short wavelengths and high frequency. X-rays are produced when charged particles are accelerated or decelerated, such as in an X-ray tube or synchrotron particle accelerator.



In medicine, an X-ray beam is used to create images of the body's interior. The beam is directed through the patient, and a detector records the rays that pass through. Different body structures absorb varying amounts of X-ray photons, creating shadows that form an image. Bones, for example, absorb more photons than soft tissues, resulting in sharper shadows.



X-rays are a form of ionizing radiation, meaning they can remove electrons from neutral atoms. This process deposits energy in the matter and can cause biochemical changes in living tissue, which can be harmful.

- How is an X-ray radiation beam produced?







X-rays are produced through the acceleration of charged particles, atomic transitions between discrete energy levels, and the radioactive decay of some atomic nuclei.



In an X-ray tube, high-energy electrons bombard a solid target, causing them to decelerate abruptly. This deceleration results in the emission of bremsstrahlung, a continuous spectrum of electromagnetic radiation with a peak intensity in the X-ray region. Additionally, characteristic radiation is produced when the target atoms are excited by collisions with fast-moving electrons, leading to the emission of X-ray photons. The [Duane–Hunt law](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Duane-Hunt-law/Introduction#ref1) defines the relationship between the voltage applied to an X-ray tube and the maximum frequency of the emitted X-rays.

- What are X-ray radiation beams used for?







X-ray radiation beams have diverse applications across various fields because of their ability to penetrate optically opaque materials.



In medicine, they are indispensable for diagnostic imaging, detecting bone fractures, dental cavities, and cancerous conditions. Radiation therapies utilize X-rays to treat cancerous tumors and blood disorders like leukemia.



Industrially, X-rays detect flaws in castings and are used to examine airline baggage contents. They also determine crystal structures in various materials.



However, X-rays can cause biochemical changes in living cells and pose health risks.

- How does an X-ray radiation beam pass through different materials?







X-ray radiation, a form of [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation) with short wavelengths and high frequencies, interacts with materials through ionization. When X-rays pass through a substance, they can cause atoms to eject electrons, depositing energy in the material.



The degree to which X-rays penetrate materials depends on factors such as the energy of the X-ray photons and the density and composition of the material. Denser materials, like bone, absorb more X-rays, while less dense materials, such as soft tissue, allow more X-rays to pass through. This differential absorption is the basis for X-ray imaging.



The [Compton effect](https://www.britannica.com/science/Compton-effect/Introduction#ref1), where X-rays scatter off electrons and lose energy, also plays a role in how X-rays interact with matter.

- What safety measures are taken when using X-ray radiation beams?







When using X-ray radiation beams, several safety measures are taken to minimize risk. Because any radiation exposure carries some risk of mutagenic or carcinogenic effects, procedures are carefully evaluated to ensure benefits outweigh risks.



Protective measures to limit exposure were implemented early on, after the recognition of radiation burns. Limits have been placed on the amounts of radioactivity and radiation doses that different body tissues can accumulate for both radiation workers and the general public. Each use of radiation in medicine and dentistry is evaluated on a case-by-case basis to avoid unnecessary exposure.

**X-ray**, [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation) of extremely short [wavelength](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength) and high frequency, with wavelengths ranging from about 10−8 to 10−12 metre and corresponding frequencies from about 1016 to 1020 hertz (Hz).

X-rays are commonly produced by accelerating (or decelerating) charged particles; examples include a beam of [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) striking a metal plate in an [X-ray tube](https://www.britannica.com/technology/X-ray-tube) and a circulating beam of electrons in a [synchrotron](https://www.britannica.com/technology/synchrotron) [particle accelerator](https://www.britannica.com/technology/particle-accelerator) or storage ring. In addition, highly excited [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) can emit X-rays with discrete wavelengths characteristic of the energy level spacings in the atoms. The X-ray region of the electromagnetic spectrum falls far outside the range of visible wavelengths. However, the passage of X-rays through materials, including biological tissue, can be recorded with photographic films and other detectors. The analysis of X-ray images of the body is an extremely valuable medical diagnostic tool.

X-rays are a form of ionizing radiation—when interacting with matter, they are energetic enough to cause neutral atoms to eject electrons. Through this ionization process the energy of the X-rays is deposited in the matter. When passing through living tissue, X-rays can cause harmful biochemical changes in [genes](https://www.britannica.com/science/gene), [chromosomes](https://www.britannica.com/science/chromosome), and other cell components. The biological effects of ionizing radiation, which are complex and highly dependent on the length and intensity of exposure, are still under active study ( _see_ [radiation injury](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiation-injury)). X-ray [radiation therapies](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiation-therapy) take advantage of these effects to combat the growth of malignant tumours.

X-rays were discovered in 1895 by German physicist [Wilhelm Konrad Röntgen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wilhelm-Rontgen) while investigating the effects of [electron beams](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron-beam) (then called [cathode rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/cathode-ray)) in electrical discharges through low-pressure gases. Röntgen uncovered a startling effect—namely, that a screen coated with a [fluorescent](https://www.britannica.com/science/fluorescence) material placed outside a discharge tube would glow even when it was shielded from the direct visible and [ultraviolet](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultraviolet-radiation) light of the gaseous discharge. He deduced that an invisible radiation from the tube passed through the air and caused the screen to [fluoresce](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray-fluorescence). Röntgen was able to show that the radiation responsible for the fluorescence originated from the point where the electron beam struck the glass wall of the discharge tube. Opaque objects placed between the tube and the screen proved to be transparent to the new form of radiation; Röntgen dramatically demonstrated this by producing a photographic image of the bones of the human hand. His discovery of so-called Röntgen rays was met with worldwide scientific and popular excitement, and, along with the discoveries of [radioactivity](https://www.britannica.com/science/radioactivity) (1896) and the [electron](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) (1897), it ushered in the study of the atomic world and the era of modern [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science).

# Fundamental characteristics

## Wave nature

X-rays are a form of electromagnetic radiation; their basic physical properties are identical to those of the more familiar components of the electromagnetic spectrum—visible [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light), [infrared radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/infrared-radiation), and [ultraviolet radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultraviolet-radiation). As with other forms of electromagnetic radiation, X-rays can be described as coupled waves of [electric](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-field) and [magnetic fields](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-field) traveling at the speed of light (about 300,000 km, or 186,000 miles, per second). Their characteristic wavelengths and frequencies can be demonstrated and measured through the [interference](https://www.britannica.com/science/interference-physics) effects that result from the overlap of two or more waves in space. X-rays also exhibit particle-like properties; they can be described as a flow of [photons](https://www.britannica.com/science/photon) carrying discrete amounts of [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) and [momentum](https://www.britannica.com/science/momentum). This dual nature is a property of all forms of radiation and matter and is comprehensively described by the theory of [quantum mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-mechanics-physics).

Though it was immediately suspected, following Röntgen’s discovery, that X-rays were a form of electromagnetic radiation, this proved very difficult to establish. X-rays are distinguished by their very short wavelengths, typically 1,000 times shorter than the wavelengths of visible light. Because of this, and because of the practical difficulties of producing and detecting the new form of radiation, the nature of X-rays was only gradually unraveled in the early decades of the 20th century.

In 1906 the British physicist [Charles Glover Barkla](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Glover-Barkla) first demonstrated the wave nature of X-rays by showing that they can be “polarized” by [scattering](https://www.britannica.com/science/scattering) from a solid. [Polarization](https://www.britannica.com/science/polarization-physics) refers to the orientation of the oscillations in a transverse wave; all electromagnetic waves are transverse oscillations of electric and magnetic fields. The very short wavelengths of X-rays, hinted at in early [diffraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/diffraction) studies in which the rays were passed through narrow slits, was firmly established in 1912 by the pioneering work of the German physicist [Max von Laue](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Max-von-Laue) and his students Walter Friedrich and Paul Knipping. Laue suggested that the ordered arrangements of atoms in [crystals](https://www.britannica.com/science/crystal) could serve as natural three-dimensional [diffraction gratings](https://www.britannica.com/technology/diffraction-grating). Typical atomic spacings in crystals are approximately 1 [angstrom](https://www.britannica.com/science/angstrom) (1 × 10−10 metre), ideal for producing diffraction effects in electromagnetic radiation of comparable wavelength. Friedrich and Knipping verified Laue’s predictions by photographing diffraction patterns produced by the passage of X-rays through a crystal of zinc sulfide. These experiments demonstrated that X-rays have wavelengths of about 1 angstrom and confirmed that the atoms in crystals are arranged in regular structures.

In the following year, the British physicist [William Lawrence Bragg](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Lawrence-Bragg) devised a particularly simple model of the [scattering of X-rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray-scattering) from the parallel layers of atoms in a crystal. The [Bragg law](https://www.britannica.com/science/Bragg-law) shows how the angles at which X-rays are most efficiently diffracted from a crystal are related to the X-ray wavelength and the distance between the layers of atoms. Bragg’s physicist father, [William Henry Bragg](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Bragg), based his design of the first X-ray spectrometer on his son’s analysis. The pair used their X-ray spectrometer in making seminal studies of both the distribution of wavelengths in X-ray beams and the crystal structures of many common solids—an achievement for which they shared the Nobel Prize for Physics in 1915.

## Particle nature

In the early 1920s, experimental studies of the scattering of X-rays from solids played a key role in establishing the particle nature of electromagnetic radiation. In 1905 German physicist [Albert Einstein](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Albert-Einstein) had proposed that electromagnetic radiation is granular, consisting of quanta (later called [photons](https://www.britannica.com/science/photon)) each with an energy _h_ _f_, where _h_ is [Planck’s constant](https://www.britannica.com/science/Plancks-constant) (about 6.6 × 10−34 joule∙second) and _f_ is the frequency of the radiation. Einstein’s hypothesis was strongly supported in subsequent studies of the [photoelectric effect](https://www.britannica.com/science/photoelectric-effect) and by the successes of Danish physicist [Niels Bohr](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Niels-Bohr)’s model of the hydrogen atom and its characteristic emission and [absorption spectra](https://www.britannica.com/science/absorption-spectrum) ( _see_ [Bohr atomic model](https://www.britannica.com/science/Bohr-model)). Further verification came in 1922 when American physicist [Arthur Compton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Arthur-Holly-Compton) successfully treated the scattering of X-rays from the atoms in a solid as a set of collisions between X-ray photons and the loosely bound outer electrons of the atoms.

Adapting the relation between momentum and energy for a classical electromagnetic wave to an individual photon, Compton used [conservation of energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/conservation-of-energy) and [conservation of momentum](https://www.britannica.com/science/conservation-of-momentum) arguments to derive an expression for the wavelength shift of scattered X-rays as a function of their scattering angle. In the so-called [Compton effect](https://www.britannica.com/science/Compton-effect), a colliding photon transfers some of its energy and momentum to an electron, which [recoils](https://www.britannica.com/science/recoil-electron). The scattered photon must thus have less energy and momentum than the incoming photon, resulting in scattered X-rays of slightly lower frequency and longer wavelength. Compton’s careful measurements of this small effect, coupled with his successful theoretical treatment (independently derived by the Dutch scientist [Peter Debye](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Peter-Debye)), provided convincing evidence for the existence of photons. The approximate wavelength range of the X-ray portion of the electromagnetic spectrum, 10−8 to 10−12 metre, corresponds to a range of photon energies from about 100 eV ( [electron volts](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron-volt)) to 1 MeV (million electron volts).

## Applications

The defining characteristics of X-rays—their ability to penetrate optically opaque materials, their wavelengths of atomic dimension, the high energy of individual X-ray photons—lead to a wide range of industrial, medical, and scientific applications. Specialized X-ray sources, detectors, and analysis techniques have been developed to address a range of questions from the study of the interactions of the simplest molecules to the structure of the human brain.

X-ray images of the body are an indispensable diagnostic tool in modern medicine. Medical imaging allows for the nonintrusive detection of dental cavities, bone fractures, foreign objects, and diseased conditions such as cancer. Standard X-ray images easily differentiate between bone and soft tissue; additional contrast between different areas of soft tissue is afforded by the injection of a contrast medium—a liquid or gas that is comparatively opaque to X-rays ( _see_ [diagnostic imaging](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging)). In the 1970s a powerful new X-ray imaging technique, [computed tomography](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computed-tomography) (CT), was developed. Now in widespread use, CT scans produce detailed high-resolution cross-sectional images of internal organs and structures; they are far more sensitive to small density variations than conventional X-ray images.

As with other forms of ionizing radiation, X-rays cause biochemical changes in living cells. A high-energy X-ray photon deposits its energy by liberating electrons from atoms and molecules. These free electrons may themselves ionize additional neutral species. Through this process, reactive ions and free radicals are formed, leading to further chemical reactions. The resulting radiation-induced chemistry can break the molecular bonds needed for cell growth and can induce genetic damage ( _see_ [radiation injury](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiation-injury)). While there are significant health risks associated with exposure to X-rays, radiation therapies exploit the above effects to treat cancerous tumours and blood disorders such as [leukemia](https://www.britannica.com/science/leukemia). X-rays (and higher-energy [gamma rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/gamma-ray)) are directed at target tissues; the consequent molecular damage blocks the growth of the diseased cells. Nearby normal cells, also exposed to the ionizing X-rays, are typically more capable of repair. In a related application, in agricultural industries the irradiation of some foods with X-rays and gamma rays is used to inhibit selectively the growth of bacteria ( _see_ [food preservation: Food irradiation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food-preservation/Food-irradiation#ref50578)).

X-rays are a powerful diagnostic tool for revealing the structure and composition of [materials](https://www.britannica.com/technology/materials-testing). The great utility of X-ray images derives from the differential [absorption](https://www.britannica.com/science/absorption-physics) of X-rays by materials of different density, composition, and homogeneity. In a common application, X-rays are used for quick examination of the contents of airline baggage. In industry, X-ray images are used to detect flaws nondestructively in castings that are inaccessible to direct observation. X-ray microscopes are capable of magnifying X-ray absorption images so as to resolve features on scales as small as about 40 nanometres (nm; billionths of a metre), or roughly 400 atomic diameters. This resolution, about five times greater than that achieved by the best visible light microscopes, is possible because of the small diffraction effects associated with the very short wavelengths of X-rays. X-ray microscopes usually operate with “soft” X-rays (wavelengths in the 1- to 10-nm range) and rely on reflective optics ( _see_ [spectroscopy: X-ray optics](https://www.britannica.com/science/spectroscopy/Production-methods#ref80648)) or “zone plates” ( _see_ [optics: Filtering](https://www.britannica.com/science/optics/Optics-and-information-theory#ref37994)) to achieve focusing. Because water is relatively transparent in the soft X-ray region, these microscopes are ideal for studying biological materials in an aqueous environment. Another sophisticated absorption technique, called EXAFS (“extended X-ray absorption fine structure”), is capable of identifying the short-range ordering of atoms and molecules in unstable samples of crystals and [amorphous solids](https://www.britannica.com/science/amorphous-solid).

[X-ray diffraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray-diffraction) techniques (or “X-ray crystallography”) allow for the determination of crystal structures in inorganic, organic, and biological materials. The detailed atomic structure of the double-helix polymer deoxyribonucleic acid ( [DNA](https://www.britannica.com/science/DNA)) was famously revealed by [James Watson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Dewey-Watson) and [Francis Crick](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-Crick) via the X-ray crystallography studies of [Maurice Wilkins](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Maurice-Wilkins). X-ray fluorescence is a complementary method for the quantitative analysis of the composition of materials. In this technique, a sample is exposed to either an electron beam or a beam of primary X-rays; the resulting atomic excitations lead to [X-ray emissions](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-analysis/Emitted-radiation#ref80804) with wavelengths characteristic of the elements in the sample. The electron microprobe uses this process to identify the constituents of sample regions as small as a few micrometres (millionths of a metre). X-ray fluorescence and diffraction techniques are valuable methods for the nondestructive analysis of art objects. Brushstroke techniques and the arrangements of painted-over pigments in oil paintings, the presence of coatings and varnishes, and the compositions of glasses, porcelain, and enamels are revealed through X-ray analysis.

Many of the above techniques are enhanced by the exceptionally high X-ray intensities produced in modern synchrotron light facilities. Extremely bright, short X-ray pulses, tuned to selected wavelength regions, are used to probe chemical reactions on surfaces, the electronic structures of semiconductors and magnetic materials, and the structure and function of proteins and biological macromolecules. Another promising source of high-intensity X-rays is the X-ray [laser](https://www.britannica.com/technology/laser). [Coherent](https://www.britannica.com/science/coherence) X-rays (a signature of lasing) at the longer-wavelength end of the spectral region have been produced in the laboratory. In 2009 lasing was achieved at the Linac Coherent Light Source facility in Menlo Park, Calif., at a wavelength of 0.15 nm, but construction of a practical device at such short wavelengths remained a difficult technological challenge.

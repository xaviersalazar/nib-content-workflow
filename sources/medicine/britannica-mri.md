- What is magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)?







Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is a 3D diagnostic technique for visualizing internal organs and structures without X-rays or radiation. It is valuable for detailed anatomical images, detecting structural abnormalities, and observing changes over time.



During the procedure, the patient lies inside a strong, cylindrical magnet, exposing them to a powerful magnetic field. Different atoms resonate at different frequencies of magnetic fields. MRI primarily detects oscillations in hydrogen atoms, which possess a small magnetic field due to their spinning proton nucleus. A second magnetic field pulses on and off, causing hydrogen atoms to resonate and align with it. As they swing back into alignment with the background field, they create a signal that is converted into an image.



MRI is particularly valuable in imaging the brain, spinal cord, pelvic organs, and cancellous bone.

- How does an MRI machine work?







An MRI machine uses a strong, steady [magnetic field](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-resonance-imaging/Introduction#ref1) to visualize organs and structures inside the body. The machine exposes the patient to radio waves, exciting protons that form the nuclei of hydrogen atoms. The protons then emit measurable electrical energy, which a computer uses to construct a tissue map. MRI excels at imaging the brain, spinal cord, and pelvic organs because it poorly visualizes bone. This technique can detect structural abnormalities that appear during a disease, and it can reveal the extent of tumors rapidly and vividly.

- Why is MRI safer than some other imaging techniques?







MRI is generally considered safer than some other imaging techniques because it doesn't use [ionizing radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation/Ultraviolet-radiation#ref59186) like X-rays or [computed tomography](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation/Ultraviolet-radiation#ref59186) (CT) scans. Instead, MRI uses strong magnetic fields and radio waves to generate images of the body's internal structures. Because it avoids ionizing radiation, MRI doesn't carry the same risks of cell damage and increased cancer risk associated with techniques like X-ray and CT.

- What do doctors look for in an MRI scan?







During an [MRI](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-resonance-imaging/Introduction#ref1) scan, doctors look for abnormalities and changes in tissue structure, which can indicate disease. MRI is particularly valuable for visualizing the brain, spinal cord, and pelvic organs.



MRI can detect the extent of tumors and early signs of damage from stroke. The images are created based on the varying amounts of hydrogen in different tissues, with high-hydrogen tissues appearing bright and low-hydrogen tissues appearing black.

- What are some limitations or challenges of using MRI in medicine?







MRI offers detailed anatomical images without radiation, but it has limitations. It poorly visualizes bone and can induce anxiety due to the narrow tube, especially for those with claustrophobia.



MRI scans take longer than other imaging techniques like CAT scans, making them susceptible to motion artifacts, which reduces their effectiveness for chest or abdominal scans. The strong magnetic field means MRI is unsafe for patients with pacemakers or metal in critical areas like the eye or brain. Although contrast agents such as gadodiamide can improve image quality, MRI sensitivity remains relatively limited.

**magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)**, three-dimensional [diagnostic imaging](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging) technique used to visualize organs and structures inside the body without the need for [X-rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray) or other [radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiation). MRI is valuable for providing detailed anatomical images and can reveal minute changes that occur over time. It can be used to detect structural abnormalities that appear in the course of a disease as well as how these abnormalities affect subsequent development and how their progression correlates with mental and emotional aspects of a disorder. Since MRI poorly visualizes [bone](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy), excellent images of the intracranial and intraspinal contents are produced.

[![magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/80578-050-BF193014/Magnetic-resonance-imaging-organs-structures-X-rays-radiation.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/80578-050-BF193014/Magnetic-resonance-imaging-organs-structures-X-rays-radiation.jpg)

[magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/80578-050-BF193014/Magnetic-resonance-imaging-organs-structures-X-rays-radiation.jpg) During magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), a patient lies inside a hollow cylindrical magnet and is exposed to a powerful magnetic field.(more)

During an MRI procedure, the patient lies inside a massive hollow cylindrical [magnet](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnet) and is exposed to a powerful steady [magnetic field](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-field). Different [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) in the portion of the body being scanned [resonate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/resonate) to different frequencies of magnetic fields. MRI is used primarily to detect the oscillations of [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) atoms, which contain a [proton](https://www.britannica.com/science/proton-subatomic-particle) nucleus that spins and thus can be thought of as possessing a small magnetic field. In MRI a background magnetic field lines up all the hydrogen atoms in the tissue being imaged. A second magnetic field, oriented differently from the background field, is turned on and off many times per second; at certain pulse rates, the hydrogen atoms resonate and line up with this second field. When the second field is turned off, the atoms that were lined up with it swing back to align with the background field. As they swing back, they create a signal that can be picked up and converted into an image.

Tissue that contains a large amount of hydrogen, which occurs abundantly in the [human body](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-body) in the form of [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water), produces a bright image, whereas tissue that contains little or no hydrogen (e.g., bone) appears black. The brightness of an MRI image is [facilitated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/facilitated) by the use of a contrast agent such as gadodiamide, which patients ingest or are injected with prior to the procedure. Although these agents can improve the quality of images from MRI, the procedure remains relatively limited in its sensitivity. Techniques to improve the sensitivity of MRI are being developed. The most promising of these techniques involves the use of para-hydrogen, a form of hydrogen with unique molecular [spin](https://www.britannica.com/science/spin-atomic-physics) properties that are highly sensitive to magnetic fields.

- What is diagnostic imaging in medicine?

Diagnostic imaging is indispensable in virtually every branch of medicine.

- How does an X-ray work to create images of the body?







X-rays create images by passing radiation through the body, which darkens photographic film. Dense materials, such as [bones](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging/Introduction#ref340116), absorb more X-rays, preventing them from reaching the film, while soft tissues absorb fewer rays.



To enhance contrast between soft tissues of similar densities, contrast media can be injected or ingested. These media are either radiopaque or radiotransparent. This allows for the [X-ray imaging](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnostic-imaging/Introduction#ref340116) of specific internal structures, such as blood vessels or the urinary tract.



X-ray tubes, invented by William D. Coolidge, produce X-rays by accelerating electrons to high speeds and colliding them with a target, typically made of [tungsten](https://www.britannica.com/technology/X-ray-tube/Introduction#ref1). Only a small fraction of the electron energy converts to X-rays.

- What is an MRI, and how is it different from an X-ray?







An MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) is a 3D diagnostic technique that uses a strong magnetic field and radio waves to create detailed images of the organs and structures within the body. It is particularly valuable for visualizing soft tissues, the brain, spinal cord, and cancellous bone.



X-rays use electromagnetic radiation to create images based on the differential absorption of X-ray photons by body structures. Bones absorb more radiation than soft tissues, creating shadows that reveal their structure.



Unlike X-rays, MRI does not use radiation. MRI is better for visualizing soft tissues, while X-rays are typically used for bone imaging.

- What is a CT scan and when might a doctor order one?







A [computed tomography (CT) scan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computed-tomography/Introduction#ref1) is a diagnostic imaging method that uses X-rays to create detailed, cross-sectional images of the body. During a CT scan, a narrow X-ray beam rotates around the patient, and detectors measure the strength of the X-rays from different angles. This data is then processed by a computer to generate a 3D image of the internal body part.



Doctors may order a CT scan to evaluate [stroke](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computed-tomography/Introduction#ref1), [abdominal tumors](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computed-tomography/Introduction#ref1), abscesses, or other abnormalities. CT scans offer more precise visualization of anatomical structures compared to conventional X-rays, often detecting lesions without exploratory surgery.

- How does ultrasound imaging work, and what are its common uses in medicine?







Ultrasound imaging, or ultrasonography, uses high-frequency sound waves to visualize internal body structures. A device emits ultrasonic waves that reflect off tissues, creating echoes. These echoes are processed to form images, displayed as static or moving pictures.



Common uses include fetal examinations, imaging organs (heart, liver, kidneys, gallbladder, breasts, eyes), diagnosing tumors, guiding biopsies, drainage procedures, and intrauterine surgery. Doppler ultrasound assesses blood flow, detecting arterial diseases and heart valve defects.



It is quick, relatively inexpensive, and, within the diagnostic range, has no known biological hazards. Though less precise than CAT scans or MRIs, ultrasound offers advantages like real-time imaging and good soft tissue contrast.

**diagnostic imaging**, the use of [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation) and certain other technologies to produce images of internal structures of the body for the purpose of accurate [diagnosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/diagnosis). [Diagnostic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Diagnostic) imaging is roughly equivalent to [radiology](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiology), the branch of [medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/medicine) that uses radiation to diagnose and treat diseases. However, other technologies—including [ultrasound](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultrasound), which employs [sound](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics) waves to visualize [tissues](https://www.britannica.com/science/tissue), and [endoscopy](https://www.britannica.com/science/endoscopy) and similar methods in which a flexible optical instrument is equipped with a camera for imaging—may also be used.

## X-ray imaging

[X-rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray), used since 1895, were the first type of radiation to provide images of the interior of the body. X-rays pass through bodily tissues and also have the property of darkening photographic film when they strike it. As they penetrate tissues, the X-rays are absorbed differentially, with denser objects such as [bones](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy) absorbing more of the rays and thus preventing them from reaching the film. Soft tissues, on the other hand, absorb fewer rays; the result is that in an X-ray photograph of the interior of the body, bones show up as lighter areas and soft tissues show up as darker ones on the exposed film.

A [limiting factor](https://www.britannica.com/science/density-dependent-factor) in X-rays when used alone is the inability to distinguish between [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent), [differentiated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/differentiated) soft tissues of roughly the same density (i.e., it is not possible to produce contrasting tones between such objects on the exposed film). To obtain this contrast, a [contrast medium](https://www.britannica.com/science/contrast-medium)—a liquid or gaseous substance that is comparatively [opaque](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/opaque) to X-rays (radiopaque) or comparatively transparent to them—is injected into the body. Contrast-medium fluids can be injected into naturally occurring body cavities, injected into the bloodstream and lymphatic vessels, swallowed or introduced by [enema](https://www.britannica.com/science/enema) for study of the [digestive tract](https://www.britannica.com/science/gastrointestinal-tract), or injected around organs to show their external [contour](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contour). Different contrast media thus allow the X-ray imaging of particular types of soft internal structures, such as the [arteries](https://www.britannica.com/science/artery) and [veins](https://www.britannica.com/science/vein-blood-vessel) in [angiography](https://www.britannica.com/science/angiography), the passage of blood through the heart in [angiocardiography](https://www.britannica.com/science/angiocardiography), the [gallbladder](https://www.britannica.com/science/gallbladder) and biliary channels in [cholecystography](https://www.britannica.com/science/cholecystography), the [spinal cord](https://www.britannica.com/science/spinal-cord) in [myelography](https://www.britannica.com/science/myelography), and the urinary tract in [urography](https://www.britannica.com/science/urography). Virtually any part of the body can be examined for physiological disturbances of the normal structures by X-ray analysis. X-ray motion-picture films can record the body processes as the contrast media enter and leave parts of the body.

## Nuclear medicine

The scanning of radioactive [isotopes](https://www.britannica.com/science/isotope) that have been injected into the tissues is a medical specialty called [nuclear medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/nuclear-medicine). Both isotope scanning and X-ray photography are used in [brain scanning](https://www.britannica.com/science/brain-scanning). An imaging technique related to isotope scanning is [positron emission tomography](https://www.britannica.com/topic/positron-emission-tomography). Another type of diagnostic imaging is [nuclear magnetic resonance](https://www.britannica.com/science/nuclear-magnetic-resonance), which creates images of thin slices of the body using very-high-frequency radio waves. [Ultrasound](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultrasonics) is a technique in which high-frequency sound waves are used for detecting abnormalities in internal organs. The varieties of radiation that are used in diagnostic imaging continues to expand, along with the techniques for using them.

## Endoscopy and related procedures

Procedures such as [endoscopy](https://www.britannica.com/science/endoscopy), [laparoscopy](https://www.britannica.com/science/laparoscopy), and [colposcopy](https://www.britannica.com/science/colposcopy) make use of generally flexible optical instruments that can be inserted through openings, either natural or surgical in origin, in the body. Many scope instruments are fitted with small video cameras that enable the physician or surgeon to view the tissues being examined on a large monitor. A number of scopes also are designed to enable [tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/tissue) [biopsy](https://www.britannica.com/science/biopsy), in which a small sample of tissue is collected for histological study, to be performed in [conjunction](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conjunction) with visual analysis.

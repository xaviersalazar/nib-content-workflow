### What is football?

Football, also called association football or soccer, is a game involving two teams of 11 players who try to maneuver the ball into the other team’s goal without using their hands or arms. The team that scores more goals wins. Football is the world’s most popular ball game in numbers of participants and spectators.

### What is the origin of football?

Modern football originated in Britain in the 19th century. Though “folk football” had been played since medieval times with varying rules, the game began to be standardized when it was taken up as a winter game at public schools. The [Football Association](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Football-Association), formed in 1863, codified the rules of the game and hosted the first cup competition between regional football clubs in Britain.

### How many people play football?

The Fédération Internationale de Football Association, or FIFA, estimated that at the turn of the 21st century there were approximately 250 million football players worldwide.

### What is football’s premier tournament?

The [World Cup](https://www.britannica.com/sports/World-Cup-football) is football’s premier tournament. It is a quadrennial tournament and is likely the most popular sporting event in the world, drawing billions of television viewers every tournament.

### How long does a red card last?

When a player receives a red card during a match, they are ejected from the game and must leave the pitch. Players earn a red card when they are given a second yellow card (a caution card for a serious foul) by a referee during the course of the contest or for a foul involving flagrant violence or unsportsmanlike conduct (such as fighting, spitting, or conducting themselves in an abusive manner toward other players and officials). A player receiving a red card cannot return for the duration of the match, and the team cannot replace the ejected player. In addition, the player may not participate in the next match.

### What happens when a goalkeeper (or goalie) receives a red card?

Should a goalkeeper (or “goalie”) receive a red card during the course of the match, they are immediately ejected from the match in the same manner that they would be if they played any other position, and the offending team must finish the contest one player down. The team can either elect to play the remainder of the match without a goalkeeper or it may insert a backup goalkeeper. If the team chooses the latter option, a player from another position must be taken out for the remainder of the match.

### How far away from the goal are penalty kicks taken?

According to the International Football Association Board (the sport’s governing body that determines the its rules and regulations), penalty kicks are taken from the penalty mark, a dot placed in the pitch located 11 meters (about 36 feet) away from the center of the goal line. A penalty kick is a direct free kick that is awarded to the attacking side after a defending player commits a foul within their own penalty area. (The penalty areas are the large boxes adjacent to the semicircular arcs at either end of the field.) The penalty kick involves the kicker, who attempts to kick the ball into the goal, and the goalkeeper, who attempts to block the attempt.

### Is it a handball in soccer if it hits your arm?

It depends. No handball foul is called if the point of contact between the player and the ball occurs on the arm above the point in line with the bottom of the armpit (in other words, the shoulder and slightly below the shoulder). Likewise, no foul is called if contact is made below that point on the arm, the referee judges that contact to be incidental, and the contact does not result in the ball going into the opponent’s goal. However, any time a player, who is not the goalkeeper, deliberately touches the ball with his hand or arm, the player has committed a handball foul. Although the goalkeeper can use their hands to play the ball, this power can only occur within their team’s penalty area. Otherwise,the referee may call the foul on the goalkeeper.

### Can a goalkeeper (or “goalie”) score a goal in soccer?

Yes. A goalkeeper can score a goal on the opposing team at any time during the match as long as the ball is played with the feet. However, a goal will not count if the goalkeeper either throws or punches the ball into the opponent’s goal directly. (In the unlikely event that a goalkeeper manages to hurl the ball such a distance, it must make contact with another player first before it enters the opponent’s goal to count.) During the waning moments of a match, goalkeepers often move forward to support their team during an attack, and some have kicked the ball in directly or have helped to set up another player who heads the ball into the goal.

Modern football originated in Britain in the 19th century. Since before [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) times, “ [folk football](https://www.britannica.com/sports/folk-football)” games had been played in towns and villages according to local customs and with a minimum of rules. [Industrialization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/industrialization) and [urbanization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/urbanization), which reduced the amount of [leisure](https://www.britannica.com/topic/leisure) time and space available to the working class, combined with a history of legal prohibitions against particularly violent and destructive forms of folk football to undermine the game’s status from the early 19th century onward. However, football was taken up as a winter game between residence houses at public (independent) schools such as [Winchester College](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Winchester-College), [Charterhouse](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Charterhouse), and [Eton College](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eton-College). Each school had its own rules; some allowed limited handling of the ball and others did not. The variance in rules made it difficult for public schoolboys entering university to continue playing except with former schoolmates. As early as 1843 an attempt to standardize and [codify](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/codify) the rules of play was made at the [University of Cambridge](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Cambridge), whose students joined most public schools in 1848 in adopting these “Cambridge rules,” which were further spread by Cambridge graduates who formed football clubs. In 1863 a series of meetings involving clubs from metropolitan [London](https://www.britannica.com/place/London) and surrounding counties produced the printed rules of football, which prohibited the carrying of the ball. Thus, the “handling” game of [rugby](https://www.britannica.com/sports/rugby) remained outside the newly formed [Football Association](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Football-Association) (FA). Indeed, by 1870 all handling of the ball except by the goalkeeper was prohibited by the FA.

The new rules were not universally accepted in Britain, however; many clubs retained their own rules, especially in and around [Sheffield](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sheffield-England). Although this northern English city was the home of the first [provincial](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/provincial) club to join the FA, in 1867 it also gave birth to the Sheffield Football Association, the forerunner of later county associations. Sheffield and London clubs played two matches against each other in 1866, and a year later a match pitting a club from [Middlesex](https://www.britannica.com/place/Middlesex-historical-county) against one from [Kent](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kent-county-England) and [Surrey](https://www.britannica.com/place/Surrey-county-England) was played under the revised rules. In 1871 15 FA clubs accepted an invitation to enter a cup competition and to contribute to the purchase of a trophy. By 1877 the associations of Great Britain had agreed upon a uniform code, 43 clubs were in competition, and the London clubs’ initial dominance had diminished.

# [Professionalism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/professionalism)

The development of modern football was closely tied to processes of industrialization and urbanization in Victorian Britain. Most of the new working-class inhabitants of Britain’s industrial towns and cities gradually lost their old [bucolic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/bucolic) pastimes, such as badger-baiting, and sought fresh forms of [collective](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/collective) [leisure](https://www.britannica.com/topic/leisure). From the 1850s onward, industrial workers were increasingly likely to have Saturday afternoons off work, and so many turned to the new game of football to watch or to play. Key urban institutions such as churches, trade unions, and schools organized working-class boys and men into recreational football teams. Rising adult literacy spurred press coverage of organized [sports](https://www.britannica.com/sports/sports), while transport systems such as the railways or urban trams enabled players and spectators to travel to football games. Average attendance in [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-Kingdom) rose from 4,600 in 1888 to 7,900 in 1895, rising to 13,200 in 1905 and reaching 23,100 at the outbreak of [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I). Football’s popularity eroded public interest in other sports, notably cricket.

Leading clubs, notably those in [Lancashire](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lancashire-county-England), started charging admission to spectators as early as the 1870s and so, despite the [FA’s](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Football-Association) amateurism rule, were in a position to pay [illicit](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/illicit) wages to attract highly skilled working-class players, many of them hailing from [Scotland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Scotland). Working-class players and northern English clubs sought a professional system that would provide, in part, some financial reward to cover their “broken time” (time lost from their other work) and the risk of injury. The FA remained staunchly elitist in sustaining a policy of [amateurism](https://www.britannica.com/sports/amateur-sport) that protected upper and upper-middle class influence over the game.

The issue of professionalism reached a crisis in England in 1884, when the FA expelled two clubs for using professional players. However, the payment of players had become so commonplace by then that the FA had little option but to [sanction](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/sanction) the practice a year later, despite initial attempts to restrict professionalism to reimbursements for broken time. The consequence was that northern clubs, with their large supporter bases and capacity to attract better players, came to prominence. As the influence of working-class players rose in football, the upper classes took refuge in other sports, notably [cricket](https://www.britannica.com/sports/cricket-sport) and [rugby union](https://www.britannica.com/sports/rugby). Professionalism also sparked further modernization of the game through the establishment of the [Football League](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Football-League), which allowed the leading dozen teams from the North and Midlands to compete systematically against each other from 1888 onward. A lower, second division was introduced in 1893, and the total number of teams increased to 28. The Irish and Scots formed leagues in 1890. The Southern League began in 1894 but was absorbed by the Football League in 1920. Yet football did not become a major profit-making business during this period. Professional clubs became [limited liability](https://www.britannica.com/money/limited-liability) companies primarily to secure land for gradual development of [stadium](https://www.britannica.com/technology/stadium) facilities. Most clubs in England were owned and controlled by businessmen but shareholders received very low, if any, dividends; their main reward was an [enhanced](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhanced) public status through [running](https://www.britannica.com/sports/running-athletics) the local club.

Later national leagues overseas followed the British model, which included league championships, at least one annual cup competition, and a [hierarchy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hierarchy) of leagues that sent clubs finishing highest in the tables (standings) up to the next higher division (promotion) and clubs at the bottom down to the next lower division (relegation). A league was formed in [the Netherlands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Netherlands) in 1889, but professionalism arrived only in 1954. [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany) completed its first national championship season in 1903, but the [Bundesliga](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bundesliga), a [comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive) and fully professional national league, did not evolve until 60 years later. In [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), where the game was introduced in the 1870s, a professional league did not begin until 1932, shortly after professionalism had been adopted in the South American countries of [Argentina](https://www.britannica.com/place/Argentina) and [Brazil](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brazil).

## International organization

By the early 20th century, football had spread across Europe, but it was in need of [international organization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/international-organization). A solution was found in 1904, when representatives from the football associations of [Belgium](https://www.britannica.com/place/Belgium), [Denmark](https://www.britannica.com/place/Denmark), [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), [the Netherlands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Netherlands), [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain), [Sweden](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sweden), and [Switzerland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Switzerland) founded the [Fédération Internationale de Football Association](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Federation-Internationale-de-Football-Association) (FIFA).

Although Englishman Daniel Woolfall was elected FIFA president in 1906 and all of the home nations ( [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England), [Scotland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Scotland), [Ireland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ireland), and [Wales](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wales)) were admitted as members by 1911, British football associations were disdainful of the new body. FIFA members accepted British control over the rules of football via the International Board, which had been established by the home nations in 1882. Nevertheless, in 1920 the British associations resigned their FIFA memberships after failing to persuade other members that [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany), [Austria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Austria), and [Hungary](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hungary) should be expelled following World War I. The British associations rejoined FIFA in 1924 but soon after insisted upon a very rigid definition of amateurism, notably for [Olympic](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Olympic-Games) football. Other nations again failed to follow their lead, and the British resigned once more in 1928, remaining outside FIFA until 1946. When FIFA established the [World Cup](https://www.britannica.com/sports/World-Cup-football) championship, British [insouciance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/insouciance) toward the international game continued. Without membership in FIFA, the British national teams were not invited to the first three competitions (1930, 1934, and 1938). For the next competition, held in 1950, FIFA ruled that the two best finishers in the British home nations [tournament](https://www.britannica.com/sports/tournament-medieval-military-games) would qualify for World Cup play; England won, but Scotland (which finished second) chose not to compete for the World Cup.

Despite sometimes fractious [international relations](https://www.britannica.com/topic/international-relations), football continued to rise in popularity. It made its official [Olympic](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Olympic-Games) [debut](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/debut) at the [London Games in 1908](https://www.britannica.com/event/London-1908-Olympic-Games), and it has since been played in each of the [Summer Games](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Summer-Olympic-Games) (except for the [1932 Games in Los Angeles](https://www.britannica.com/event/Los-Angeles-1932-Olympic-Games)). FIFA also grew steadily—especially in the latter half of the 20th century, when it strengthened its standing as the game’s global authority and regulator of competition. Guinea became FIFA’s 100th member in 1961; at the turn of the 21st century, more than 200 nations were registered FIFA members, which is more than the number of countries that belong to the [United Nations](https://www.britannica.com/topic/United-Nations).

[Carli Lloyd](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/183231-050-8D8EB720/Carli-Lloyd-penalty-goal-semifinal-match-Germany-2015.jpg) Carli Lloyd (left) of the United States scoring a penalty goal during the semifinal match against Germany in the FIFA Women's World Cup at the Olympic Stadium in Montreal, June 30, 2015.(more)

The World Cup finals remain football’s premier tournament, but other important tournaments have emerged under FIFA guidance. Two different tournaments for young players began in 1977 and 1985, and these became, respectively, the World Youth Championship (for those 20 years old and younger) and the Under-17 World Championship. Futsal, the world indoor five-a-side championship, started in 1989. Two years later the first [Women’s World Cup](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Womens-World-Cup) was played in [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China). In 1992 FIFA opened the Olympic football tournament to players aged under 23 years, and four years later the first women’s Olympic football tournament was held. The World Club Championship debuted in Brazil in 2000. The Under-19 [Women](https://www.britannica.com/topic/women)’s World Championship was inaugurated in 2002.

FIFA membership is open to all national associations. They must accept FIFA’s authority, observe the laws of football, and possess a suitable football [infrastructure](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infrastructure) (i.e., facilities and internal organization). FIFA statutes require members to form continental confederations. The first of these, the [Confederación Sudamericana de Fútbol](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Confederacion-Sudamericana-de-Futbol) (commonly known as CONMEBOL), was founded in [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) in 1916. In 1954 the [Union of European Football Associations](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Union-of-European-Football-Associations) (UEFA) and the Asian Football Confederation (AFC) were established. Africa’s governing body, the Confédération Africaine de Football (CAF), was founded in 1957. The [Confederation of North, Central American and Caribbean Association Football](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Confederation-of-North-Central-American-and-Caribbean-Association-Football) (CONCACAF) followed four years later. The Oceania Football Confederation (OFC) appeared in 1966. These confederations may organize their own club, international, and youth tournaments, elect representatives to FIFA’s Executive Committee, and promote football in their specific continents as they see fit. In turn, all football players, agents, leagues, national associations, and confederations must recognize the authority of FIFA’s Arbitration Tribunal for Football, which effectively functions as football’s supreme court in serious disputes.

Until the early 1970s, control of FIFA (and thus of world football) was firmly in the hands of northern Europeans. Under the presidencies of the Englishmen Arthur Drewry (1955–61) and Stanley Rous (1961–74), FIFA adopted a rather [conservative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conservative) patrician relationship to the national and continental bodies. It survived on modest income from the World Cup finals, and relatively little was done to promote football in developing countries or to explore the game’s business potential within the West’s postwar economic boom. FIFA’s leadership was more concerned with matters of regulation, such as confirming amateur status for Olympic competition or banning those associated with illegal transfers of players with existing contracts. For example, [Colombia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Colombia) (1951–54) and [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia) (1960–63) were suspended temporarily from FIFA after permitting clubs to recruit players who had broken contracts elsewhere in the world.

Growing African and Asian membership within FIFA [undermined](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/undermined) European control. In 1974 Brazilian [João Havelange](https://www.britannica.com/money/Joao-Havelange) was elected president, gaining large support from developing nations. Under Havelange, FIFA was transformed from an international gentlemen’s club into a global corporation: billion-dollar television deals and partnerships with major transnational corporations were established during the 1980s and ’90s. While some earnings were reinvested through FIFA development projects—primarily in Asia, Africa, and Central America—the biggest political reward for developing countries has been the expansion of the World Cup finals to include more countries from outside Europe and South America.

Greater professionalization of sports also forced FIFA to [intercede](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/intercede) in new areas as a governing body and competition regulator. The use of [performance-enhancing drugs](https://www.britannica.com/science/doping) by teams and individual players had been suspected since at least the 1930s; FIFA introduced [drug tests](https://www.britannica.com/science/drug-testing) in 1966, and occasionally drug users were uncovered, such as Willie Johnston of Scotland at the 1978 World Cup finals. But FIFA regulations were tightened in the 1980s after the sharp rise in offenses among Olympic athletes, the appearance of new drugs such as the [steroid](https://www.britannica.com/science/anabolic-steroid) nandrolone, and the use of drugs by stars such as Argentina’s [Diego Maradona](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Diego-Maradona) in 1994. While FIFA has authorized lengthy worldwide bans of players who fail drug tests, discrepancies remain between nations and confederations over the intensity of testing and the legal status of specific drugs.

[![Sepp Blatter](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/182913-050-BC4C7684/Sepp-Blatter-FIFA-2006.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/182913-050-BC4C7684/Sepp-Blatter-FIFA-2006.jpg)

[Sepp Blatter](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/182913-050-BC4C7684/Sepp-Blatter-FIFA-2006.jpg) FIFA president Sepp Blatter, 2006.(more)

As the sport moved into the 21st century, FIFA came under pressure to respond to some of the major consequences of [globalization](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/globalization) for international football. During the corrupt [tenure](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/tenure) of Switzerland’s [Sepp Blatter](https://www.britannica.com/money/Sepp-Blatter) as president from 1998 to 2015, the political bargaining and wrangling among world football’s officials gained greater media and public attention. Direct conflicts of interest among football’s various groups have also arisen: players, agents, television networks, competition sponsors, clubs, national bodies, continental associations, and FIFA all have divergent views regarding the staging of football tournaments and the distribution of football’s income. Regulation of player representatives and transfers is also [problematic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/problematic). In UEFA countries, players move freely when not under contract. On other continents, notably Africa and Central and South America, players tend to be tied into long-term contracts with clubs that can control their entire careers. FIFA now requires all agents to be licensed and to pass written examinations held by national associations, but there is little global consistency regarding the control of agent powers. In Europe, agents have played a key role in promoting wage inflation and higher player mobility. In Latin America, players are often partially “owned” by agents who may decide on whether transfers proceed. In parts of Africa, some European agents have been compared to slave traders in the way that they [exercise](https://www.britannica.com/topic/exercise-physical-fitness) [authoritarian](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/authoritarian) control over players and profit hugely from transfer fees to Western leagues with little thought for their clients’ well-being. In this way, the ever-widening inequalities between developed and developing nations are reflected in the uneven growth and variable regulations of world football.

Top Questions


### What is basketball?

Basketball is a [game](https://www.britannica.com/topic/game-recreation) played between two teams, typically of five players each, on a rectangular court. Each team tries to score by shooting, laying up, or dunking the [ball](https://www.britannica.com/sports/ball-sports) through the opponent’s goal, an elevated horizontal hoop and net called a basket.

### When was basketball invented?

Basketball was invented by [James Naismith](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Naismith) on December 21, 1891, at the International [Young Men’s Christian Association](https://www.britannica.com/topic/YMCA) (YMCA) Training School, [Springfield](https://www.britannica.com/place/Springfield-Massachusetts), [Massachusetts](https://www.britannica.com/place/Massachusetts), where Naismith was an instructor in [physical education](https://www.britannica.com/topic/physical-education). Basketball is the only major sport strictly of U.S. origin (although Naismith was born in Canada).

### How does basketball exercise your body?

Basketball is a dynamic sport that builds stamina from the short sprints required of running up and down the length of the court. Movements distinct to basketball, such as jumping to take a shot or to grab a rebound, require frequent muscle contractions, which can build muscular endurance. Additional weight training is recommended for basketball players in order to improve their performance on the court.

### Where is basketball popular outside of the United States?

The success of international basketball was greatly advanced by the inclusion of men’s basketball in the [Olympic Games](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Olympic-Games) beginning in 1936. Basketball has caught on particularly well in [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy), and [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain) has several basketball leagues. The other major center of European basketball is eastern Europe, particularly in the [Balkans](https://www.britannica.com/place/Balkans).

### What was the influence of television on basketball?

Basketball grew steadily but slowly in popularity and importance in the United States and internationally in the first three decades after [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II) (1939–45) as a result of [television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/television-technology) exposure. However, with the advent of [cable television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cable-television), the game’s popularity exploded at all levels, especially during the 1980s.

**basketball**, [game](https://www.britannica.com/topic/game-recreation) played between two teams, typically of five players each, on a rectangular court. Each team tries to score by shooting, laying up, or dunking the [ball](https://www.britannica.com/sports/ball-sports) through the opponent’s goal, an elevated horizontal hoop and [net](https://www.britannica.com/technology/net) called a basket. The only major sport strictly of U.S. origin, basketball was invented by [James Naismith](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Naismith) in 1891 and within a decade had spread around the world. Over the course of the 20th century, it became one of the leading competitive [sports](https://www.britannica.com/sports/sports), organized at the international level by [FIBA](https://www.britannica.com/topic/FIBA) (founded 1932) and further popularized by the growth of professional leagues, most notably the [NBA](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Basketball-Association) (formed in 1949) and the [WNBA](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Womens-National-Basketball-Association) (founded in 1997). Meanwhile, basketball also became a beloved recreational sport, and informal games between two or more players can be found year-round on courts in public parks, municipal, school, and church gyms, and in family driveways.

## History

## The early years

[![James Naismith](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/9877-050-15EEA839/James-Naismith-ball-peach-basket-basketball-equipment.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/9877-050-15EEA839/James-Naismith-ball-peach-basket-basketball-equipment.jpg)

[James Naismith](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/9877-050-15EEA839/James-Naismith-ball-peach-basket-basketball-equipment.jpg) Basketball inventor James Naismith holding a ball and a peach basket, the first basketball equipment.(more)

James Naismith (1861–1939) was working as an instructor in [physical education](https://www.britannica.com/topic/physical-education) at the International [Young Men’s Christian Association](https://www.britannica.com/topic/YMCA) (YMCA) Training School (now Springfield College), [Springfield](https://www.britannica.com/place/Springfield-Massachusetts), [Massachusetts](https://www.britannica.com/place/Massachusetts), when he was asked to [devise](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/devise) an indoor game that could replace the boring or dangerous exercises used at the school during the winter. For his new game Naismith selected features of [football (soccer)](https://www.britannica.com/sports/football-soccer), [American football](https://www.britannica.com/sports/American-football), [field hockey](https://www.britannica.com/sports/field-hockey), and other outdoor sports, and sought to eliminate bodily contact between players.

_[(Read James Naismith’s 1929 Britannica essay on his invention of basketball.)](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Naismith-on-basketball-2215550)_

For that first game of basketball, held on December 21, 1891, Naismith used as goals two half-bushel [peach](https://www.britannica.com/plant/peach) baskets, which gave the sport its name. The students were enthusiastic. After much [running](https://www.britannica.com/sports/running-athletics) and [shooting](https://www.britannica.com/sports/shooting), William R. Chase made a midcourt shot—the only score in that historic contest. Word spread about the newly invented game, and numerous associations wrote Naismith for a copy of the rules, which were published in the January 15, 1892, issue of the _Triangle_, the YMCA Training School’s campus paper.

[![Outdoor basketball game in 1892](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/60176-050-018D3ECA/Players-peach-basket-basketball-game-1892.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/60176-050-018D3ECA/Players-peach-basket-basketball-game-1892.jpg)

[Outdoor basketball game in 1892](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/60176-050-018D3ECA/Players-peach-basket-basketball-game-1892.jpg) Players shooting into a closed-bottom peach basket in an outdoor game of basketball, 1892.(more)

In the early years the number of players on a team varied according to the number in the class and the size of the playing area. In 1894 teams began to play with five on a side when the playing area was less than 1,800 square feet (167.2 square meters); the number rose to seven when the [gymnasium](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gymnasium-sports) measured from 1,800 to 3,600 square feet (334.5 square meters) and up to nine when the playing area exceeded that. In 1895 the number was occasionally set at five by mutual consent; the rules [stipulated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/stipulated) five players two years later, and this number has remained ever since.

Since Naismith and five of his original players were Canadians, it is not surprising that [Canada](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canada) was the first country outside the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) to play the game. Basketball was introduced in [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) in 1893, in [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) in 1894, in [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia), [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China), and [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India) soon thereafter, and in [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) in 1900.

While basketball helped swell the membership of YMCAs because of the availability of their [gyms](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gymnasium-German-school), within five years the game was outlawed by various associations because gyms that had been occupied by classes of 50 or 60 members were now monopolized by only 10 to 18 players. The banishment of the game [induced](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/induced) many members to terminate their YMCA membership and to hire halls to play the game, thus paving the way to the professionalization of the sport.

Originally, players wore one of three styles of uniforms: knee-length football trousers; jersey tights, as commonly worn by wrestlers; or short padded pants, forerunners of today’s uniforms, plus knee guards. The courts often were of irregular shape with occasional obstructions such as pillars, stairways, or offices that interfered with play. In 1903 it was ruled that all boundary lines must be straight. In 1893 the Narragansett Machinery Co. of [Providence](https://www.britannica.com/place/Providence-Rhode-Island), [Rhode Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Rhode-Island-state), marketed a hoop of iron with a hammock style of basket. Originally a ladder, then a pole, and finally a chain fastened to the bottom of the net was used to retrieve a ball after a goal had been scored. Nets open at the bottom were adopted in 1912–13. In 1895–96 the points for making a basket (goal, or field goal) were reduced from three to two, and the points for making a free throw (shot uncontested from a line in front of the basket after a foul had been committed) were reduced from three to one.

[![Indoor basketball game](https://cdn.britannica.com/11/253711-050-2C77805F/Female-students-playing-basketball-in-a-gymnasium.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/11/253711-050-2C77805F/Female-students-playing-basketball-in-a-gymnasium.jpg)

[Indoor basketball game](https://cdn.britannica.com/11/253711-050-2C77805F/Female-students-playing-basketball-in-a-gymnasium.jpg) Female students playing basketball in a gymnasium, Western High School, Washington, D.C., circa 1899.(more)

Baskets were frequently attached to balconies, making it easy for spectators behind a basket to lean over the railings and deflect the ball to favor one side and [hinder](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/hinder) the other; in 1895 teams were urged to provide a 4-by-6-foot (1.2-by-1.8-meter) screen for the purpose of eliminating interference. Soon after, wooden [backboards](https://www.britannica.com/sports/backboard) proved more suitable. Glass backboards were legalized by the professionals in 1908–09 and by colleges in 1909–10. In 1920–21 the backboards were moved 2 feet (0.6 meter), and in 1939–40 4 feet, in from the end lines to reduce frequent stepping out-of-bounds. Fan-shaped backboards were made legal in 1940–41.

A soccer ball (football) was used for the first two years. In 1894 the first basketball was marketed. It was laced, measured close to 32 inches (81 cm), or about 4 inches (10 cm) larger than the soccer ball, in circumference, and weighed less than 20 ounces (567 grams). By 1948–49, when the laceless molded ball was made official, the size had been set at about 30 inches (76 cm).

The first college to play the game was either Geneva College (Beaver Falls, [Pennsylvania](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pennsylvania-state)) or the [University of Iowa](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Iowa). C.O. Bemis heard about the new sport at Springfield and tried it out with his students at Geneva in 1892. At Iowa, H.F. Kallenberg, who had [attended](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/attended) Springfield in 1890, wrote Naismith for a copy of the rules and also presented the game to his students. At Springfield, Kallenberg met [Amos Alonzo Stagg](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Amos-Alonzo-Stagg), who became athletic director at the new [University of Chicago](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Chicago) in 1892. The first college basketball game with five on a side was played between the University of Chicago and the University of Iowa in [Iowa City](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iowa-City) on January 18, 1896. The University of Chicago won, 15–12, with neither team using a substitute. Kallenberg refereed that game—a common practice in that era—and some of the spectators took exception to some of his decisions.

The colleges formed their own rules committee in 1905, and by 1913 there were at least five sets of rules: [collegiate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/collegiate), YMCA–Amateur Athletic Union, those used by state militia groups, and two varieties of professional rules. Teams often agreed to play under a different set for each half of a game. To establish some measure of uniformity, the colleges, Amateur Athletic Union, and YMCA formed the Joint Rules Committee in 1915. This group was renamed the National Basketball Committee (NBC) of the United States and Canada in 1936 and until 1979 served as the game’s sole amateur rule-making body. In that year, however, the colleges broke away to form their own rules committee, and during the same year the [National Federation of State High School Associations](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Federation-of-State-High-School-Associations) likewise assumed the task of establishing separate playing rules for the high schools. The [National Collegiate Athletic Association](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Collegiate-Athletic-Association) (NCAA) Rules Committee for men is a 12-member board representing all three NCAA divisions. It has six members from Division I schools and three each from Divisions II and III. It has [jurisdiction](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/jurisdiction) over colleges, junior colleges, the National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics (NAIA), and Armed Forces basketball. There is a similar body for women’s play.

In addition to collegiate competition, many grammar schools, youth groups, municipal recreation centers, churches, and other organizations conduct basketball programs for youngsters of less than high school age. Jay Archer, of [Scranton](https://www.britannica.com/place/Scranton-Pennsylvania), [Pennsylvania](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pennsylvania-state), introduced “biddy” basketball in 1950 for boys and girls under 12 years of age, the court and equipment being adjusted for size.

## Growth of the game

[![Julius Erving](https://cdn.britannica.com/14/198714-050-2883B189/Julius-Erving.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/14/198714-050-2883B189/Julius-Erving.jpg)

[Julius Erving](https://cdn.britannica.com/14/198714-050-2883B189/Julius-Erving.jpg)“Dr. J” led the 76ers to the NBA finals four times in seven years, including their 1983 championship win.(more)

Basketball grew steadily but slowly in popularity and importance in the United States and internationally in the first three decades after [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II). Interest in the game deepened as a result of television exposure, but with the advent of [cable television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cable-television), especially during the 1980s, the game’s popularity exploded at all levels. Given a [timely](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/timely) mix of spectacular players—such as [Earvin (“Magic”) Johnson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Magic-Johnson), [Julius Erving](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Julius-Erving) (“Dr. J”), [Larry Bird](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Larry-Bird), and [Michael Jordan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Michael-Jordan)—and the greatly increased exposure, basketball moved quickly to the forefront of the American sporting scene, alongside such traditional leaders as [baseball](https://www.britannica.com/sports/baseball) and football. Four areas of the game developed during this period: U.S. high school and college basketball, professional basketball, women’s basketball, and international basketball.

# U.S. high school and college basketball

Basketball at the [high school](https://www.britannica.com/topic/high-school) and college levels developed from a structured, rigid game in the early days to one that is often fast-paced and high-scoring. Individual skills improved markedly, and, although basketball continued to be regarded as the ultimate team game, individualistic, one-on-one performers came to be not only accepted but used as an effective means of winning games.

In the early years games were frequently won with point totals of less than 30, and the game, from the spectator’s viewpoint, was slow. Once a team [acquired](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/acquired) a modest lead, the popular tactic was to stall the game by passing the [ball](https://www.britannica.com/sports/ball-sports) without trying to score, in an attempt to run out the clock. The NBC, seeing the need to discourage such slowdown tactics, instituted a number of rule changes. In 1932–33 a line was drawn at midcourt, and the offensive team was required to advance the ball past it within 10 seconds or lose possession. Five years later, in 1937–38, the center jump following each field goal or free throw was eliminated. Instead, the defending team was permitted to inbound the ball from the out-of-bounds line underneath the basket. Decades passed before another alteration of like magnitude was made in the college game. After experimentation, the NCAA Rules Committee installed a 45-second [shot clock](https://www.britannica.com/sports/shot-clock) in 1985 (reduced to 35 seconds in 1993), restricting the time a team could control the ball before [shooting](https://www.britannica.com/sports/shooting), and one year later it [implemented](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implemented) a three-point shot rule for baskets made beyond a distance of 19.75 feet (6.0 meters). In 2008 the three-point line was moved to 20.75 feet (6.3 meters) from the basket, and in 2019 it was again extended, to 22.15 feet (6.75 meters).

More noticeable alteration in the game came at both the playing and coaching levels. [Stanford University’s](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Stanford-University) [Hank Luisetti](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hank-Luisetti) was the first to use and popularize the one-hand shot in the late 1930s. Until then the only outside attempts were two-handed push shots. In the 1950s and ’60s a shooting style evolved from Luisetti’s push-off one hander to a jump shot, which is released at the top of the jump. [West Virginia University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/West-Virginia-University) guard [Jerry West](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jerry-West) and Purdue University’s Rick [Mount](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Mount) were two players who demonstrated the devastating effectiveness of this shot.

Coaching strategy changed appreciably over the years. Frank W. Keaney, coach at the [University of Rhode Island](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Rhode-Island) from 1921 to 1948, is credited with introducing the concept of “ [fast break](https://www.britannica.com/sports/fast-break)” basketball, in which the offensive team rushes the ball upcourt hoping to get a good shot before the defense can get set. Another man who contributed to a quicker pace of play, particularly through the use of the pressure defense, was [Adolph Rupp](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adolph-Rupp), who became the [University of Kentucky](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Kentucky)’s coach in 1931 and turned its program into one of the most storied in basketball history.

Defensive coaching philosophy, similarly, has undergone change. Whereas pioneer coaches such as Henry Iba of Oklahoma A&M University (now [Oklahoma State University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Oklahoma-State-University)) or Long Island University’s Clair Bee taught strictly a man-to-man defense, the [zone defense](https://www.britannica.com/sports/zone-defense), developed by Cam Henderson of [Marshall University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Marshall-University) in [West Virginia](https://www.britannica.com/place/West-Virginia), later became an [integral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integral) part of the game.

Over the years one of the rules makers’ chief concerns was to neutralize the advantage of taller players. At 6 feet 5 inches (1.96 meters) [Joe Lapchick](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joe-Lapchick) was considered very tall when he played for the Original Celtics in the 1920s, but, as even taller players appeared, rules were changed in response. To prevent tall players from stationing themselves near the basket, a rule was instituted in 1932–33 prohibiting the player with the ball from standing inside the foul lane with his back to the basket for more than three seconds; the three-second rule later applied to any attacking player in the foul lane. In 1937–38 a new rule forbade any player from touching the ball when it was in the basket or on its rim (basket interference), and in 1944–45 it became illegal for any defending player to touch the ball on its downward flight toward the basket (goaltending).

Nevertheless, with each passing decade, the teams with the tallest players tended to dominate. Bob Kurland (7 feet \[2.13 meters\]) led [Oklahoma A&M](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Oklahoma-State-University) to two NCAA championships in the 1940s and led the nation in scoring in 1945–46. In the same era [George Mikan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Mikan) (6 feet 10 inches \[2.08 meters\]) scored more than 550 points in each of his final two seasons at [DePaul University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/DePaul-University) before going on to play nine professional seasons in which he scored more than 11,000 points. Mikan was an outstanding player, not only because of his size but because of his ability to shoot sweeping hook shots with both hands.

[![Wilt Chamberlain and Bill Russell](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/7289-050-8F468B0A/Wilt-Chamberlain-Bill-Russell-1965.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/7289-050-8F468B0A/Wilt-Chamberlain-Bill-Russell-1965.jpg)

[Wilt Chamberlain and Bill Russell](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/7289-050-8F468B0A/Wilt-Chamberlain-Bill-Russell-1965.jpg) American basketball player Wilt Chamberlain (right) battling Bill Russell (center), 1965.(more)

In the 1950s [Bill Russell](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bill-Russell) (6 feet 9 inches \[2.06 meters\]) led the [University of San Francisco](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-San-Francisco) to two NCAA championships before going on to become one of the greatest centers in professional basketball history. [Wilt Chamberlain](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wilt-Chamberlain) (7 feet 1 inch \[2.16 meters\]) played at the [University of Kansas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Kansas) before turning professional in the late 1950s and is regarded as the greatest all-around big man ever to play. It remained, however, for Lew Alcindor (later [Kareem Abdul-Jabbar](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kareem-Abdul-Jabbar)), standing 7 feet 2 inches (2.18 meters), to most influence the rules. After his sophomore year (1966–67) at the [University of California](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-California) at [Los Angeles](https://www.britannica.com/place/Los-Angeles-California) (UCLA), the [dunk shot](https://www.britannica.com/sports/dunk-shot) was banned from [collegiate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/collegiate) basketball, ostensibly because the rules committee felt, again, that the big men had too great an advantage. The rule was [rescinded](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/rescinded) beginning with the 1976–77 season, and the dunk shot became an important part of the game, electrifying both fans and players.

[![Pistol Pete](https://cdn.britannica.com/93/255393-050-BB5666BD/Pete-Maravich-LSU-Tigers-1970.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/93/255393-050-BB5666BD/Pete-Maravich-LSU-Tigers-1970.jpg)

[Pistol Pete](https://cdn.britannica.com/93/255393-050-BB5666BD/Pete-Maravich-LSU-Tigers-1970.jpg) Pete Maravich running the offense for the LSU Tigers during a 1970 game against the Kentucky Wildcats.(more)

So too have the small- and medium-size players affected the game’s development. [Bob Cousy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bob-Cousy), playing at Holy Cross College and later for the [Boston Celtics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Boston-Celtics), was regarded as one of the game’s first great playmakers. He was among the first to use the behind-the-back pass and between-the-legs dribble as effective offensive maneuvers. Later such smaller players as [Providence College’s](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Providence-College) Ernie DiGregorio, the University of North Carolina’s Phil Ford, and [Indiana’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indiana-state) [Isiah Thomas](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isiah-Thomas) proved the importance of their role. Between those two extremes are players such as [Louisiana State University’s](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Louisiana-State-University-system-Louisiana) [Pete Maravich](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pete-Maravich), who set an all-time collegiate scoring record of 44.5 points per game in the 1969–70 season; [Magic Johnson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Magic-Johnson), the point guard who led [Michigan State University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Michigan-State-University) to a championship in 1979 and the [Los Angeles Lakers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Los-Angeles-Lakers) to several [NBA](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Basketball-Association) championships; [Oscar Robertson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Oscar-Robertson), a dominating performer for the [University of Cincinnati](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Cincinnati) in the late 1950s and for the [Milwaukee Bucks](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Milwaukee-Bucks) in the 1970s; [Larry Bird](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Larry-Bird) of [Indiana State University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indiana-State-University), a forward of exceptional versatility who led the Boston Celtics to several championships; and Michael Jordan, a great all-around player with the [University of North Carolina](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-North-Carolina) in the 1980s who is widely considered the best professional player in the history of the sport.

Nothing influenced the college game’s growth more than [television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/television-technology), however. The [NCAA](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Collegiate-Athletic-Association) championship games were televised nationally from 1963, and by the 1980s all three major television networks were telecasting intersectional college games during the November-to-March season. Rights fees for these games soared from a few million dollars to well over $50 million by the late 1980s. As for broadcasting the NCAA finals, a television contract that began in 2003 gave the NCAA an average of $545 million per year for the television rights; this [exponential](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/exponential) growth in broadcast fees reflected the importance of these games to both networks and advertisers.

Profits such as these inevitably attract gamblers, and in the evolution of college basketball the darkest hours have been related to gambling scandals. But, as the game began to draw more attention and generate more income, the pressure to win intensified, resulting in an outbreak of rules violations, especially with regard to recruitment of star players.

The most identifiable phase of college basketball in America is the postseason [tournament](https://www.britannica.com/sports/tournament-medieval-military-games) held in March—popularly known as [March Madness](https://www.britannica.com/sports/March-Madness). Interest in the NCAA tournament paralleled the growth of the game. The first basketball tournament was staged by the [Amateur](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Amateur) Athletic Union in 1897 and was won by [New York City’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City) 23rd Street [YMCA](https://www.britannica.com/topic/YMCA), later to become a traveling professional team known as the [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state) Wanderers. Although the YMCA was prominently identified with the game in its early years, it did not hold its first national tournament until 1923, and that event took place until 1962. The first national tournament for colleges was held in 1937 and was conducted by an organization in Kansas City, Missouri, that later became the NAIA.

New York City basketball writers organized the first [National Invitation Tournament](https://www.britannica.com/sports/National-Invitation-Tournament) (NIT) in 1938, but a year later the New York City colleges took control of the event. Until the early 1950s the NIT was considered the most prestigious American tournament, but, with the growth of the college-run NCAA championship, the NIT became a [consolation](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/consolation) event for teams that failed to make the NCAA selections.

[![NIT quarterfinals](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/240757-050-3F38448C/Kentucky-vs-Loyola-Chicago.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/240757-050-3F38448C/Kentucky-vs-Loyola-Chicago.jpg)

[NIT quarterfinals](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/240757-050-3F38448C/Kentucky-vs-Loyola-Chicago.jpg) American basketball player Ben Bluitt of Loyola University Chicago foiling a basket attempt by Wallace Jones of the University of Kentucky Wildcats during the National Invitation Tournament (NIT) quarterfinals at Madison Square Garden, New York City, March 14, 1949.(more)

The first NCAA tournament was played in 1939, and its growth took place in three stages. The first era ran through 1964, when it was essentially a tournament for champions of various conferences. There were just eight teams in the 1939 field, and by 1963 it had been expanded to 25 teams, all champions of their respective conferences, plus several successful independent teams. The most outstanding teams of the 1940s and ’50s participated in both the NCAA and NIT tournaments, but, after the gambling scandals that followed the 1950 NIT championship, a rule was passed prohibiting a team from playing in both. Afterward the NCAA tournament progressively outgrew the NIT.

In 1964 the second era dawned as the UCLA Bruins, coached by [John Wooden](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Wooden), began a period of domination over the NCAA field. From that season until 1975 Wooden led his teams to 10 NCAA championships. Only championships won by Texas Western University (now [University of Texas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Texas) at El Paso) in 1966 and [North Carolina](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Carolina-state) State in 1974 interrupted UCLA’s reign. In the eyes of many, the UCLA dynastic period probably had a regressive effect on the game’s growth; a sport with such high predictability lost some of its attractiveness.

The third growth stage came with the end of UCLA’s dominance. Champions began to emerge from all sections of the country. From the field of 25 in 1974, the NCAA tournament expanded to 64 participants in 1985, to 65 in 2001, and to 68 in 2011 (corresponding “play-in games” were added in 2001 and 2011), including not only [conference](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conference) championship teams but other outstanding teams from the same conferences as well. Three weeks of play culminate with the Final Four weekend, an event now comparable in general public interest and media attention to the [Super Bowl](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Super-Bowl) and [World Series](https://www.britannica.com/sports/World-Series). Championships at the Division II, Division III, and NAIA levels also continued to grow in interest, reaping some of the fallout from the popularity of Division I.

About 17,000 high schools in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) have basketball teams. All 50 states conduct statewide tournaments annually.

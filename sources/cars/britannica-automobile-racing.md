**automobile racing**, professional and [amateur](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/amateur) automobile sport practiced throughout the world in a variety of forms on roads, tracks, or closed circuits. It includes [Grand Prix racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Grand-Prix-racing), [speedway racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/speedway-racing), [stock-car racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/stock-car-racing), [sports-car racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/sports-car-racing), [drag racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/drag-racing), [midget-car racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/midget-car-racing), and [karting](https://www.britannica.com/sports/karting), as well as hill climbs and trials ( _see_ [hill climb](https://www.britannica.com/sports/hill-climb); _see also_ [rally driving](https://www.britannica.com/sports/rally-automobile-racing); [gymkhana](https://www.britannica.com/sports/gymkhana)). Local, national, and international governing bodies, the most notable of which is the [Fédération Internationale de l’Automobile](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Federation-Internationale-de-l-Automobile) (FIA), divide racing cars into various classes and subclasses and supervise competitions.

## Early history

Automobile racing began soon after the invention of the [gasoline](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gasoline-fuel)\- (petrol-) fueled [internal-combustion engine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/internal-combustion-engine) in the 1880s. The first organized automobile competition, a reliability test in 1894 from Paris to [Rouen](https://www.britannica.com/place/Rouen), [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), a distance of about 80 km (50 mi), was won with an average speed of 16.4 kph (10.2 mph). In 1895 the first true race was held, from [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris) to [Bordeaux](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bordeaux), France, and back, a distance of 1,178 km. The winner made an average speed of 24.15 kph. Organized automobile racing began in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) with an 87-km race from [Chicago](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chicago) to [Evanston](https://www.britannica.com/place/Evanston-Illinois), [Illinois](https://www.britannica.com/place/Illinois-state), and back on [Thanksgiving Day](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Thanksgiving-Day) in 1895. Both early races were [sponsored](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/sponsored) by [newspapers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/newspaper) for promotional purposes. In [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe), town-to-town races in [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), or from France to other countries, became the norm until 1903 when authorities stopped the Paris-to-Madrid race at Bordeaux because of the large number of accidents. The first closed-circuit [road race](https://www.britannica.com/sports/road-race), the Course de Périgueux, was run in 1898, a distance of 145 km on one lap. Such racing, governed by the [Automobile Club de France](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Automobile-Club-de-France) (founded in 1895), came to prevail in Europe except for [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England), [Wales](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wales), and [Scotland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Scotland). By 1900 racers had achieved speeds of more than 80.46 kph. Danger to spectators, racers, and [livestock](https://www.britannica.com/animal/livestock) on roads not built for the automobile, let alone racing, ultimately caused road races to decrease in number. A notable exception was the [Mille Miglia](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Mille-Miglia), which was not stopped until 1957.

International racing in the modern sense began after [James Gordon Bennett](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Gordon-Bennett-American-editor-1795-1872), owner of _The New York Herald_, offered a [trophy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/trophy) to be competed for annually by national automobile clubs, racing three cars each that had been built of parts made in the respective countries. The Automobile Club de France organized the first Bennett Trophy races in 1901, 1902, and 1903. The event was later held at the Circuit of [Ireland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ireland) (1903), the Taunus Rundstrecke in [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany) (1904), and the Circuit d’Auvergne (1905). The unwillingness of French manufacturers to be limited to three cars led to their [boycott](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/boycott) of the Bennett Trophy Race in 1906 and the establishment of the first French Grand Prix Race at [Le Mans](https://www.britannica.com/place/Le-Mans) in that year, the cars being raced by manufacturers’ teams. The first Targa Florio was run in [Sicily](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sicily) the same year and thereafter except during wartime at distances varying from 72 to 1,049 km.

William K. Vanderbilt, the [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state) sportsman, established a trophy raced for on [Long Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Long-Island-New-York) from 1904 through 1909 (except for 1907) at distances ranging from 450 to 482 km. Thereafter the race was run at [Savannah](https://www.britannica.com/place/Savannah-Georgia), [Georgia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Georgia-state); [Milwaukee](https://www.britannica.com/place/Milwaukee); [Santa Monica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Santa-Monica), [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state); and [San Francisco](https://www.britannica.com/place/San-Francisco-California) until its [discontinuance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/discontinuance) in 1916. Later [Vanderbilt Cup races](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Vanderbilt-Cup-race) were run in 1936 and 1937 at Roosevelt Raceway, [Long Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Long-Island-Cumberland-Islands-Queensland), [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state).

In early racing, in both [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) and the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), competing race cars were usually [prototypes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/prototypes) of the following year’s models. After [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I), racing became too specialized for the use of production cars, though occasionally high-performance touring cars were stripped of their bodies and fitted with special seats, fuel tanks, and [tires](https://www.britannica.com/technology/tire) for racing. Still later stock-car racing in 1939 started with standard models modified for racing.

## [Speedway racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/speedway-racing)

The first speedway purpose-built for automobile racing was constructed in 1906 at Brooklands, near Weybridge, [Surrey](https://www.britannica.com/place/Surrey-county-England), [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England). The track was a 4.45 km circuit, 30 m (100 ft) wide, with two curves banked to a height of 8.5 m. Sprint, [relay](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/relay), endurance, and [handicap](https://www.britannica.com/sports/handicap-sports) races were run at Brooklands, as well as long-distance runs (1,600 km) in 1932. Twenty-four hour races were held in 1929–31. Brooklands closed in 1939. The first road racing allowed in [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) was at Donington Park, Lancashire, in 1932, but the circuit did not survive [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II). Oval, banked speedways on the Continent included Monza (outside [Milan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Milan-Italy), 1922) and Montlhéray (outside [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris), 1924), both of which were attached to road circuits, using only half the track as part of Grand Prix racing. Montlhéray was also the site of many long-distance speed records.

# American, European, and international racing

After the first Grand Prix race in [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) in 1906 and the first Indianapolis 500 race in 1911, automobile racing was essentially different in [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) and in [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) until in the 1950s Grand Prix racing was organized [worldwide](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/worldwide). Racing in the United States was essentially speedway track racing, the tracks varying from half-mile dirt tracks to the 2 1/2-mi track for the Indianapolis 500. [Stock-car racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/stock-car-racing) arose in the 1930s on the beach at [Daytona Beach](https://www.britannica.com/place/Daytona-Beach), [Florida](https://www.britannica.com/place/Florida), then moved to tracks, and the major governing body, the [National Association for Stock Car Auto Racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/NASCAR) (NASCAR), was founded in 1947. [Hot-rod](https://www.britannica.com/sports/hot-rod) racing, particularly [drag racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/drag-racing), a rapid-acceleration contest on a quarter-mile strip, originated in the United States in the 1930s in the southern [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state) desert. Hot-rod cars originally were modified stock cars, but they ultimately became, like other racing cars, highly specialized. Hot-rod racing spread rapidly after [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II), and in 1951 the National Hot Rod Association was founded. The sport spread to [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia), [New Zealand](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Zealand), [Canada](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canada), [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England), [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany), [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy), [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan), and [Sweden](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sweden) and in 1965 was recognized by the FIA. R [acing with midget cars](https://www.britannica.com/sports/midget-car-racing) began in the United States in the 1940s and with even smaller cars, called [karts](https://www.britannica.com/sports/karting), in the 1950s. Karts were also later raced in England, throughout the rest of [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe), and in Australia, New Zealand, and Japan, with international competition from the 1960s. [Sports-car racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/sports-car-racing), both [amateur](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/amateur) and professional, became popular in the United States in the late 1930s, the earliest cars being European-made. The U.S. governing body, the Sports Car Club of America (founded 1944), and the Canadian Automobile Sports Committee (founded 1951) cooperate closely. Amateur members mainly compete in local [rallies](https://www.britannica.com/sports/rally-automobile-racing) and [gymkhanas](https://www.britannica.com/sports/gymkhana), but general public interest is mainly in the professional races. [Off-road racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/offroad-racing), held in the western deserts of the United States from the 1960s and in [Baja California](https://www.britannica.com/place/Baja-California-state-Mexico), [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico), is notable for the Baja 500 and the Mexican 1000 (mile) races.

Unlike most European and other countries, the United States has no single automobile racing body. The governing bodies noted above for various kinds of racing are members of the Automobile Competition Committee for the United States-FIA, basically an advisory and [liaison](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/liaison) organization.

## [Grand Prix racing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Grand-Prix-racing)

After the first French Grand Prix race of 1906 at [Le Mans](https://www.britannica.com/place/Le-Mans), a frequent early [venue](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/venue) and also the site of [24 Hours of Le Mans](https://www.britannica.com/sports/24-Hours-of-Le-Mans), run from 1923, the race was run in 1907 and 1908 and then not again until 1912. The first Italian Grand Prix was run in 1908. When racing resumed after [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I), the French and Italian Grand Prix were held in 1921. The Belgian Grand Prix began in 1925, the German in 1926, and that at [Monaco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Monaco) in 1929. The national clubs had formed a governing body in 1904, the Association Internationale des Automobiles Clubs Reconnus (renamed the Fédération Internationale de l’Automobile in 1946). The cars of each nation were all painted one colour for easy identification: French, blue; Italian, red; German, white; and British, green. Entries were made by manufacturers, usually two or three cars, and [drivers](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/drivers) were professional. Races were on closed circuits of 5 or 6 km to a lap with total distances of from 250 to 650 km. Through 1934 French and Italian manufacturers won most frequently, but throughout the rest of the 1930s, German manufacturers dominated. Racing resumed in 1947, and from the late 1950s British-made cars were dominant. In 1950 a world championship for drivers was instituted, usually involving point tallying for some fifteen [Grand Prix](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Grand-Prix-racing) races, including those of [Monaco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Monaco), [Belgium](https://www.britannica.com/place/Belgium), [the Netherlands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Netherlands), [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), [Great Britain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Britain-island-Europe), [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany), [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy), [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico), [South Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Africa), [Canada](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canada), and the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States). A championship for [Formula I](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Formula-One-automobile-racing) [car](https://www.britannica.com/technology/automobile) manufacturers was begun in 1955.

## [Rally driving](https://www.britannica.com/sports/rally-automobile-racing)

Racing over specified routes, the [driver](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/driver) being kept on course by a navigator between checkpoints, began in 1907 with a Peking-to-Paris race of about 12,000 km. The Monte-Carlo Rally from various starting points began in 1911 and continued thereafter except for wartime interruptions. [Rallies](https://www.britannica.com/sports/rally-automobile-racing) became very popular after [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II) in [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) and elsewhere with European and international championships being instituted by the FIA. Weekend rallies came to be common worldwide, ranging from those held by local auto clubs to those sponsored by larger organizations.

## Speed

In almost all kinds of racing, speed has been the preeminent goal, although concern for safety by governing bodies has prevented a steady climb in speeds. Nevertheless, speed has risen from 120.04 kph in the 1911 [Indianapolis 500](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Indianapolis-500) to nearly 260 kph in the late 1970s. In Grand Prix racing, where the [terrain](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/terrain) and number of curves vary, speeds are somewhat lower.

In the 1920s, land-speed record attempts deserted the tracks and courses for special desert or beach strips, and cars were designed for the record alone. Jet engines later came into use, and in one case a three-wheeled vehicle attempting a new record had to be certified by the Fédération Internationale Motorcycliste, the FIA having refused certification.

**internal-combustion engine**, any of a group of devices in which the reactants of [combustion](https://www.britannica.com/science/combustion) (oxidizer and fuel) and the products of combustion serve as the working fluids of the engine. Such an engine gains its [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) from [heat](https://www.britannica.com/science/heat) released during the combustion of the nonreacted working fluids, the oxidizer-fuel mixture. This process occurs within the engine and is part of the [thermodynamic](https://www.britannica.com/science/thermodynamics) cycle of the device. Useful work generated by an internal-combustion (IC) engine results from the hot gaseous products of combustion acting on moving surfaces of the engine, such as the face of a [piston](https://www.britannica.com/technology/piston-and-cylinder), a [turbine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/turbine) blade, or a nozzle.

[![Automobile plow](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/198339-050-98661AFB/Henry-Ford-iron-wheeled-Fordson-internal-combustion-engine-automobile-1907.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/198339-050-98661AFB/Henry-Ford-iron-wheeled-Fordson-internal-combustion-engine-automobile-1907.jpg)

[Automobile plow](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/198339-050-98661AFB/Henry-Ford-iron-wheeled-Fordson-internal-combustion-engine-automobile-1907.jpg) Henry Ford's iron-wheeled “Fordson” was unveiled in 1907 and powered by an internal-combustion engine.(more)

Internal-combustion engines are the most broadly applied and widely used power-generating devices currently in existence. Examples include [gasoline engines](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gasoline-engine), [diesel engines](https://www.britannica.com/technology/diesel-engine), [gas-turbine engines](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gas-turbine-engine), and [rocket](https://www.britannica.com/technology/rocket-jet-propulsion-device-and-vehicle)-propulsion systems.

![gasoline engines](https://cdn.britannica.com/11/311-050-A6346399/types-gasoline-engines.jpg)

[gasoline engines](https://cdn.britannica.com/11/311-050-A6346399/types-gasoline-engines.jpg) Gasoline engine types include (A) opposed-piston engines, (B) Wankel rotary engines, (C) in-line engines, and (D) V-8 engines.(more)

Internal-combustion engines are divided into two groups: continuous-combustion engines and intermittent-combustion engines. The continuous-combustion engine is characterized by a steady flow of fuel and oxidizer into the engine. A stable flame is maintained within the engine (e.g., [jet engine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/jet-engine)). The intermittent-combustion engine is characterized by periodic ignition of [air](https://www.britannica.com/science/air) and fuel and is commonly referred to as a [reciprocating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reciprocating) engine. Discrete volumes of air and fuel are processed in a cyclic manner. [Gasoline](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gasoline-fuel) [piston](https://www.britannica.com/technology/piston-and-cylinder) engines and [diesel engines](https://www.britannica.com/technology/diesel-engine) are examples of this second group.

Internal-combustion engines can be [delineated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/delineated) in terms of a series of thermodynamic events. In the continuous-combustion engine, the thermodynamic events occur simultaneously as the oxidizer and fuel and the products of combustion flow steadily through the engine. In the intermittent-combustion engine, by contrast, the events occur in succession and are repeated for each full cycle.

![air-breathing engines](https://cdn.britannica.com/42/63142-050-14461F36/air-turbofan-compressor-engine-rest-turboprop-engines.jpg)

[air-breathing engines](https://cdn.britannica.com/42/63142-050-14461F36/air-turbofan-compressor-engine-rest-turboprop-engines.jpg) Some air taken in by the turbofan (top) goes to the compressor; the rest bypasses the main engine. In turboprop engines (bottom) the hot gases drive a turbine, which powers the compressor and propeller, and provide jet thrust.(more)

With the exception of rockets (both solid rocket motors and liquid-propellant rocket engines), internal-combustion engines ingest [air](https://www.britannica.com/science/air), then either compress the air and introduce fuel into the air or introduce fuel and compress the air-fuel mixture. Then, common to all internal-combustion engines, the air-fuel mixture is burned, [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics) is [extracted](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/extracted) from the expansion of the hot gaseous products of combustion, and ultimately the products of combustion are released through the exhaust system. Their operation can be contrasted with that of external-combustion engines (e.g., [steam engines](https://www.britannica.com/technology/steam-engine)), in which the working fluid does not chemically react and energy gain is achieved solely through [heat transfer](https://www.britannica.com/science/heat-transfer) to the working fluid by way of a [heat exchanger](https://www.britannica.com/technology/heat-exchanger).

The most common internal-combustion engine is the four-stroke, [gasoline](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gasoline-fuel)-powered, homogeneous-charge, spark-ignition engine. This is because of its outstanding performance as a prime mover in the ground transportation industry. Spark-ignition engines also are used in the aeronautics industry; however, aircraft gas turbines have become the prime movers in this sector because of the [emphasis](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/emphasis) of the aeronautics industry on range, speed, and passenger comfort. The domain of internal-combustion engines also includes such exotic devices as supersonic combustion [ramjet](https://www.britannica.com/technology/ramjet) engines (scramjets), such as those proposed for hypersonic aircraft, and sophisticated rocket engines and motors, such as those used on U.S. [space shuttles](https://www.britannica.com/technology/space-shuttle) and other space vehicles.

**automotive industry**, all those companies and activities involved in the manufacture of motor vehicles, including most components, such as engines and bodies, but [excluding](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/excluding) tires, batteries, and fuel. The [industry’s](https://www.britannica.com/money/industry) principal products are passenger automobiles and light trucks, including pickups, vans, and sport utility vehicles. Commercial vehicles (i.e., delivery trucks and large transport trucks, often called semis), though important to the industry, are secondary. The design of modern automotive vehicles is discussed in the articles [automobile](https://www.britannica.com/technology/automobile), [truck](https://www.britannica.com/technology/truck-vehicle), [bus](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bus-vehicle), and [motorcycle](https://www.britannica.com/technology/motorcycle); automotive engines are described in [gasoline engine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gasoline-engine) and [diesel engine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/diesel-engine). The development of the automobile is covered in [transportation, history of: The rise of the automobile](https://www.britannica.com/technology/automobile/History-of-the-automobile#ref64377).

_[(Read Lee Iacocca’s Britannica entry on Chrysler.)](https://www.britannica.com/money/Chrysler)_

The history of the automobile industry, though brief compared with that of many other industries, has exceptional interest because of its effects on history from the 20th century. Although the automobile originated in [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) in the late 19th century, the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) completely dominated the world industry for the first half of the 20th century through the invention of [mass production](https://www.britannica.com/technology/mass-production) techniques. In the second half of the century the situation altered sharply as western European countries and [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) became major producers and exporters.

## History

Although [steam-powered](https://www.britannica.com/science/steam-power) road vehicles were produced earlier, the origins of the automotive industry are rooted in the development of the [gasoline engine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gasoline-engine) in the 1860s and ’70s, principally in [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) and [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany). By the beginning of the 20th century, German and French manufacturers had been joined by [British](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-Kingdom), [Italian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy), and American makers.

## Developments before World War I

Most early automobile companies were small shops, hundreds of which each produced a few handmade cars, and nearly all of which abandoned the business soon after going into it. The handful that survived into the era of large-scale production had certain [characteristics](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characteristics) in common. First, they fell into one of three well-defined categories: they were makers of [bicycles](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bicycle), such as [Opel](https://www.britannica.com/money/Opel-AG) in Germany and [Morris](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Morris-Motors) in Great Britain; builders of horse-drawn vehicles, such as Durant and Studebaker in the United States; or, most frequently, machinery manufacturers. The kinds of machinery included stationary gas engines ( [Daimler](https://www.britannica.com/money/Daimler-AG) of Germany, Lanchester of [Britain](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-Kingdom), Olds of the United States), marine engines ( [Vauxhall](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Vauxhall-British-company) of Britain), machine tools (Leland of the United States), sheep-shearing machinery (Wolseley of Britain), washing machines (Peerless of the United States), sewing machines (White of the United States), and woodworking and milling machinery (Panhard and Levassor of France). One American company, Pierce, made birdcages, and another, Buick, made [plumbing](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/plumbing) fixtures, including the first enameled cast-iron bathtub. Two notable exceptions to the general pattern were [Rolls-Royce](https://www.britannica.com/money/Rolls-Royce-PLC) in Britain and [Ford](https://www.britannica.com/money/Ford-Motor-Company) in the United States, both of which were founded as carmakers by partners who combined engineering talent and business skill.

In the United States almost all of the producers were assemblers who put together components and parts that were manufactured by separate firms. The assembly technique also lent itself to an advantageous method of financing. It was possible to begin building motor vehicles with a minimal investment of capital by buying parts on credit and selling the finished cars for cash; the cash sale from manufacturer to dealer has been [integral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integral) in the [marketing](https://www.britannica.com/money/marketing) of motor vehicles in the United States ever since. European automotive firms of this period tended to be more self-sufficient.

The pioneer automobile manufacturer not only had to solve the technical and financial problems of getting into production but also had to make a basic decision about what to produce. After the first success of the gasoline engine, there was widespread experimentation with steam and electricity. For a brief period the [electric automobile](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electric-car) actually enjoyed the greatest acceptance because it was quiet and easy to operate, but the limitations imposed by battery capacity proved competitively fatal. Especially popular with women, electric cars remained in limited production well into the 1920s. One of the longest-surviving makers, Detroit Electric Car Company, operated on a regular basis through 1929.

Steam power, a more serious [rival](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/rival), was aided by the general adoption, after 1900, of the so-called flash [boiler](https://www.britannica.com/technology/boiler), in which steam could be raised rapidly. The steam car was easy to operate because it did not require an elaborate transmission. On the other hand, high steam pressures were needed to make the engine light enough for use in a road vehicle; suitable engines required expensive [construction](https://www.britannica.com/technology/construction) and were difficult to maintain. By 1910 most manufacturers of steam vehicles had turned to gasoline power. The [Stanley brothers](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Stanley-Francis-Edgar-and-Stanley-Freelan-O) in the United States, however, continued to manufacture steam automobiles until the early 1920s.

As often happens with a new [technology](https://www.britannica.com/technology/technology), the automotive industry experienced [patent](https://www.britannica.com/topic/patent) controversies in its early years. Most notable were two long, drawn-out court cases in Britain and the United States, in each of which a promoter sought to gain control of the new industry by filing [comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive) patents. In Britain the claim was rejected by the courts in 1901, five years after the patent application. In the United States there was a legal battle between Ford and the Association of Licensed Automobile Manufacturers over the [Selden](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-B-Selden) patent, which the association claimed as a basic patent on the gasoline-powered car. In 1911 the courts held the patent “valid but not infringed” by Ford. The main consequence of the decision was the formation of the predecessor of the Alliance of Automobile Manufacturers to supervise an agreement for cross-licensing patents, which was ratified in 1915.

## [Mass production](https://www.britannica.com/technology/mass-production)

The outstanding contribution of the automotive industry to technological advance was the introduction of full-scale [mass production](https://www.britannica.com/technology/mass-production), a process combining precision, [standardization](https://www.britannica.com/technology/standardization), interchangeability, synchronization, and [continuity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/continuity). Mass production was an American [innovation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/innovation). The United States, with its large population, high [standard of living](https://www.britannica.com/money/standard-of-living), and long distances, was the natural birthplace of the technique, which had been partly explored in the 19th century. Although Europe had shared in the experimentation, the American role was emphasized in the popular description of standardization and interchangeability as “the [American system](https://www.britannica.com/technology/American-System-industry) of manufacture.” The fundamental techniques were known, but they had not previously been applied to the manufacture of a mechanism as complex as a motor vehicle ( _see_ [work, history of the organization of](https://www.britannica.com/money/history-of-the-organization-of-work)).

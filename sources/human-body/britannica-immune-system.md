Top Questions


### How do skin and mucous membranes act as barriers to infection?

The [skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skin), with its tough outer layer, acts as a mechanical barrier against [infection](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection). It also secretes substances that can kill [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria). [Mucous membranes](https://www.britannica.com/science/mucous-membrane) trap particles with [mucus](https://www.britannica.com/science/mucus) and use [cilia](https://www.britannica.com/science/cilium) to expel them, while also containing protective [antibodies](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibody).

### What role do interferons play in the immune system?

[Interferons](https://www.britannica.com/science/interferon) are [proteins](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) that inhibit the replication of [viruses](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus). Infected cells produce interferons, signaling other cells to resist viral growth, and they regulate the expression of [molecules](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) on cell membranes.

### How do macrophages and granulocytes differ in their role in the immune system?

[Granulocytes](https://www.britannica.com/science/granulocyte) ingest and destroy microorganisms quickly. [Macrophages](https://www.britannica.com/science/macrophage), derived from [monocytes](https://www.britannica.com/science/monocyte), move more slowly, live longer, and play a role in stimulating specific immune responses.

### What is the function of T cells and B cells in acquired immunity?

[T cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/T-cell) attack invaders directly and are involved in cell-mediated immunity. [B cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/B-cell) secrete antibodies that bind to [antigens](https://www.britannica.com/science/antigen), providing humoral immunity. Both types of cells recognize foreign molecules through [receptor](https://www.britannica.com/science/receptor-nerve-ending) molecules.

**immune system**, the complex group of defense responses found in humans and other advanced [vertebrates](https://www.britannica.com/animal/vertebrate) that helps repel disease-causing organisms (pathogens). [Immunity](https://www.britannica.com/science/immunity-biology) from [disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/disease) is actually [conferred](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conferred) by two cooperative defense systems, called nonspecific, innate immunity and specific, acquired (or adaptive) immunity. Nonspecific protective mechanisms repel all microorganisms equally, while the specific immune responses are tailored to particular types of invaders. Both systems work together to thwart organisms from entering and proliferating within the body. These immune mechanisms also help eliminate abnormal [cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology) of the body that can develop into [cancer](https://www.britannica.com/science/cancer-disease).

## Mechanisms of the immune system

## [Nonspecific, innate immunity](https://www.britannica.com/science/nonspecific-immunity)

![Clonal selection of a B cell](https://cdn.britannica.com/97/20897-004-7F47EF6C/antigen-selection-B-cell-plasma-cells-binding.jpg)

[Clonal selection of a B cell](https://cdn.britannica.com/97/20897-004-7F47EF6C/antigen-selection-B-cell-plasma-cells-binding.jpg) Activated by the binding of an antigen to a specific matching receptor on its surface, a B cell proliferates into a clone. Some clonal cells differentiate into plasma cells, which are short-lived cells that secrete antibody against the antigen. Others form memory cells, which are longer-lived and which, by proliferating rapidly, help to mount an effective defense upon a second exposure to the antigen. (more)

Most microorganisms encountered in daily life are repelled before they cause detectable signs and symptoms of disease. These potential pathogens, which include [viruses](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus), [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria), [fungi](https://www.britannica.com/science/fungus), [protozoans](https://www.britannica.com/science/protozoan), and [worms](https://www.britannica.com/animal/worm), are quite [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse), and therefore a nonspecific defense system that diverts all types of this varied microscopic horde equally is quite useful to an organism. The innate immune system provides this kind of nonspecific protection through a number of defense mechanisms, which include physical barriers such as the [skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skin), chemical barriers such as antimicrobial proteins that harm or destroy invaders, and cells that attack foreign cells and body cells harboring infectious agents. The details of how these mechanisms operate to protect the body are described in the following sections.

## External barriers to [infection](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection)

The skin and the [mucous membrane](https://www.britannica.com/science/mucous-membrane) linings of the respiratory, gastrointestinal, and genitourinary tracts provide the first line of defense against invasion by microbes or [parasites](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/parasites).

## [Skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/skin)

Human skin has a tough outer layer of cells that produce [keratin](https://www.britannica.com/science/keratin). This layer of cells, which is constantly renewed from below, serves as a mechanical barrier to [infection](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection). In addition, glands in the skin secrete oily substances that include [fatty acids](https://www.britannica.com/science/fatty-acid), such as [oleic acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/oleic-acid), that can kill some bacteria; skin glands also secrete [lysozyme](https://www.britannica.com/science/lysozyme), an [enzyme](https://www.britannica.com/science/enzyme) (also present in tears and saliva) that can break down the outer wall of certain bacteria. Victims of severe [burns](https://www.britannica.com/science/burn) often fall prey to infections from normally harmless bacteria, illustrating the importance of intact, healthy skin to a healthy immune system.

## [Mucous membranes](https://www.britannica.com/science/mucous-membrane)

Like the outer layer of the skin but much softer, the mucous membrane linings of the respiratory, [gastrointestinal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/gastronomy), and genitourinary tracts provide a mechanical barrier of cells that are constantly being renewed. The lining of the [respiratory tract](https://www.britannica.com/science/respiratory-system) has cells that [secrete](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/secrete) [mucus](https://www.britannica.com/science/mucus) (phlegm), which traps small particles. Other cells in the wall of the respiratory tract have small hairlike projections called [cilia](https://www.britannica.com/science/cilium), which steadily beat in a sweeping movement that propels the mucus and any trapped particles up and out of the [throat](https://www.britannica.com/science/pharynx) and [nose](https://www.britannica.com/science/nose). Also present in the mucus are protective [antibodies](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibody), which are products of specific immunity. Cells in the lining of the [gastrointestinal tract](https://www.britannica.com/science/gastrointestinal-tract) secrete mucus that, in addition to aiding the passage of food, can trap potentially harmful particles or prevent them from attaching to cells that make up the lining of the gut. Protective antibodies are [secreted](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/secreted) by cells underlying the gastrointestinal lining. Furthermore, the [stomach](https://www.britannica.com/science/stomach) lining secretes [hydrochloric acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrochloric-acid) that is strong enough to kill many microbes.

## Chemical barriers to infection

Some microbes penetrate the body’s protective barriers and enter the internal tissues. There they encounter a variety of chemical substances that may prevent their growth. These substances include chemicals whose protective effects are incidental to their primary function in the body, chemicals whose principal function is to harm or destroy invaders, and chemicals produced by naturally occurring bacteria.

## Chemicals with incidental protective effects

Some of the chemicals involved in normal body processes are not directly involved in defending the body against disease. Nevertheless, they do help repel invaders. For example, chemicals that [inhibit](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibit) the potentially damaging digestive [enzymes](https://www.britannica.com/science/enzyme) released from body cells which have died in the natural course of events also can inhibit similar enzymes produced by bacteria, thereby limiting bacterial growth. Another substance that provides protection against microbes incidentally to its primary cellular role is the [blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-biochemistry) protein [transferrin](https://www.britannica.com/science/transferrin). The normal function of transferrin is to bind molecules of iron that are absorbed into the bloodstream through the gut and to deliver the iron to cells, which require the mineral to grow. The protective benefit transferrin confers results from the fact that bacteria, like cells, need free iron to grow. When bound to transferrin, however, iron is unavailable to the invading microbes, and their growth is stemmed.

## Antimicrobial proteins

## [Complement](https://www.britannica.com/science/complement-immune-system-component)

A number of [proteins](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) contribute directly to the body’s nonspecific defense system by helping to destroy invading microorganisms. One group of such [proteins](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) is called [complement](https://www.britannica.com/science/complement-immune-system-component) because it works with other defense mechanisms of the body, complementing their efforts to [eradicate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/eradicate) invaders. Many microorganisms can activate complement in ways that do not involve specific immunity. Once activated, complement proteins work together to lyse, or break apart, harmful infectious organisms that do not have protective coats. Other microorganisms can evade these mechanisms but fall prey to scavenger cells, which engulf and destroy infectious agents, and to the mechanisms of the specific immune response. Complement cooperates with both nonspecific and specific defense systems.

# [Interferons](https://www.britannica.com/science/interferon)

Another group of proteins that provide protection are the [interferons](https://www.britannica.com/science/interferon), which [inhibit](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibit) the replication of many—but not all—viruses. Cells that have been infected with a [virus](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus) produce interferon, which sends a signal to other cells of the body to resist viral growth. When first discovered in 1957, interferon was thought to be a single substance, but since then several types have been discovered, each produced by a different type of cell. [Alpha interferon](https://www.britannica.com/science/alpha-interferon) is produced by [white blood cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/white-blood-cell) other than [lymphocytes](https://www.britannica.com/science/lymphocyte), [beta interferon](https://www.britannica.com/science/beta-interferon) by [fibroblasts](https://www.britannica.com/science/fibroblast), and [gamma interferon](https://www.britannica.com/science/gamma-interferon) by natural killer cells and cytotoxic T lymphocytes (killer T cells). All interferons inhibit viral replication by interfering with the transcription of viral [nucleic acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/nucleic-acid). Interferons exert additional inhibitory effects by regulating the extent to which lymphocytes and other cells express certain important molecules on their surface membranes.

## Proteins from naturally occurring [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria)

In the [small](https://www.britannica.com/science/small-intestine) and [large intestines](https://www.britannica.com/science/large-intestine) the growth of invading bacteria can be [inhibited](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibited) by naturally gut-dwelling bacteria that do not cause [disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/disease). These gut-dwelling microorganisms secrete a variety of proteins that [enhance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhance) their own survival by [inhibiting](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibiting) the growth of the invading bacterial species.

If an infectious agent is not successfully repelled by the chemical and physical barriers described above, it will encounter [cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology) whose function is to eliminate foreign substances that enter the body. These cells are the nonspecific effector cells of the innate immune response. They include scavenger cells—i.e., various cells that attack infectious agents directly—and natural killer cells, which attack cells of the body that harbor infectious organisms. Some of these cells destroy infectious agents by engulfing and destroying them through the process of [phagocytosis](https://www.britannica.com/science/phagocytosis), while other cells resort to [alternative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alternative) means. As is true of other components of innate [immunity](https://www.britannica.com/science/immunity-biology), these cells interact with components of acquired immunity to fight infection.

## Scavenger cells

![Macrophage structure](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/20889-050-9137C72F/Macrophages-components-immune-system-bacteria-particles.jpg)

[Macrophage structure](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/20889-050-9137C72F/Macrophages-components-immune-system-bacteria-particles.jpg) The principal phagocytic (cell-engulfing) components of the immune system are known as macrophages. Macrophages ingest and destroy foreign particles such as bacteria.(more)

All higher animals and many lower ones have scavenger cells—primarily [leukocytes](https://www.britannica.com/science/white-blood-cell) (white [blood](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-biochemistry) cells)—that destroy infectious agents. Most vertebrates, including all birds and mammals, possess two main kinds of scavenger cells. Their importance was first recognized in 1884 by Russian biologist [Élie Metchnikoff](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Elie-Metchnikoff), who named them microphages and macrophages, after Greek words meaning “little eaters” and “big eaters.”

## [Granulocytes](https://www.britannica.com/science/granulocyte)

Microphages are now called either [granulocytes](https://www.britannica.com/science/granulocyte), because of the numerous chemical-containing granules found in their [cytoplasm](https://www.britannica.com/science/cytoplasm), or polymorphonuclear leukocytes, because of the oddly shaped [nucleus](https://www.britannica.com/science/nucleus-biology) these cells contain. Some granules contain digestive enzymes capable of breaking down proteins, while others contain bacteriocidal (bacteria-killing) proteins. There are three classes of granulocytes— [neutrophils](https://www.britannica.com/science/neutrophil), [eosinophils](https://www.britannica.com/science/eosinophil), and [basophils](https://www.britannica.com/science/basophil)—which are distinguished according to the shape of the nucleus and the way in which the granules in the cytoplasm are stained by dye. The differences in staining characteristics reflect differences in the chemical makeup of the granules. Neutrophils are the most common type of [granulocyte](https://www.britannica.com/science/granulocyte), making up about 60 to 70 percent of all white blood cells. These granulocytes ingest and destroy microorganisms, especially bacteria. Less common are the eosinophils, which are particularly effective at damaging the cells that make up the cuticle (body wall) of larger parasites. Fewer still are the basophils, which release [heparin](https://www.britannica.com/science/heparin) (a substance that [inhibits](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibits) blood coagulation), [histamine](https://www.britannica.com/science/histamine), and other substances that play a role in some allergic reactions ( _see_ [immune system disorder: Allergies](https://www.britannica.com/science/immune-system-disorder/Deficiencies-caused-by-malnutrition#ref215500)). Very similar in structure and function to basophils are the tissue cells called [mast cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/mast-cell), which also contribute to immune responses.

Granulocytes, which have a life span of only a few days, are continuously produced from [stem (i.e., precursor) cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/stem-cell) in the [bone marrow](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-marrow). They enter the bloodstream and circulate for a few hours, after which they leave the [circulation](https://www.britannica.com/science/circulation-anatomy-and-physiology) and die. Granulocytes are mobile and are attracted to foreign materials by chemical signals, some of which are produced by the invading microorganisms themselves, others by damaged tissues, and still others by the interaction between microbes and proteins in the blood [plasma](https://www.britannica.com/science/plasma-biology). Some microorganisms produce toxins that poison granulocytes and thus escape phagocytosis; other microbes are indigestible and are not killed when ingested. By themselves, then, granulocytes are of limited effectiveness and require [reinforcement](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/reinforcement) by the mechanisms of specific immunity.

### What does DNA do?

Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) is an organic chemical that contains genetic information and instructions for [protein synthesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/translation-genetics). It is found in most [cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology) of every organism. DNA is a key part of [reproduction](https://www.britannica.com/science/reproduction-biology) in which [genetic heredity](https://www.britannica.com/science/heredity-genetics) occurs through the passing down of DNA from parent or parents to offspring.

### What is DNA made of?

DNA is made of [nucleotides](https://www.britannica.com/science/nucleotide). A nucleotide has two components: a backbone, made from the sugar [deoxyribose](https://www.britannica.com/science/deoxyribose) and [phosphate](https://www.britannica.com/science/phosphate) groups, and [nitrogenous](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen) bases, known as [cytosine](https://www.britannica.com/science/cytosine), [thymine](https://www.britannica.com/science/thymine), [adenine](https://www.britannica.com/science/adenine), and [guanine](https://www.britannica.com/science/guanine). [Genetic code](https://www.britannica.com/science/genetic-code) is formed through different arrangements of the bases.

### Who discovered the structure of DNA?

The discovery of DNA’s double-helix structure is credited to the researchers [James Watson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Dewey-Watson) and [Francis Crick](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-Crick), who, with fellow researcher [Maurice Wilkins](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Maurice-Wilkins), received a Nobel Prize in 1962 for their work. Many think that [Rosalind Franklin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rosalind-Franklin) should also be given credit, since she made the revolutionary photo of DNA’s double-helix structure, which was used as evidence without her permission.

### Can you edit DNA?

[Gene editing](https://www.britannica.com/science/gene-editing) today is mostly done through a technique called Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR), adopted from a [bacterial](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria) mechanism that can cut out specific sections in DNA. One use of CRISPR is the creation of [genetically modified organism (GMO)](https://www.britannica.com/science/genetically-modified-organism) crops.

### What’s the difference between DNA and RNA?

DNA is the master blueprint for [life](https://www.britannica.com/science/life) and constitutes the [genetic material](https://www.britannica.com/science/gene) in all free-living organisms. RNA uses DNA to code for the structure of [proteins](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) synthesized in [cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology). [Learn more about how DNA and RNA differ.](https://www.britannica.com/story/what-is-the-difference-between-dna-and-rna)

**DNA**, organic chemical of complex [molecular](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecular-biology) structure that is found in all [prokaryotic](https://www.britannica.com/science/prokaryote) and [eukaryotic](https://www.britannica.com/science/eukaryote) [cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology) and in many [viruses](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus). ## Discovery

Key events in the discovery of DNA

- DNA was first isolated in 1869 by Swiss chemist [Friedrich Miescher](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Friedrich-Miescher), who extracted a previously unknown substance from the nuclei of [white blood cells](https://www.britannica.com/science/white-blood-cell) and called it “nuclein”; its significance was not yet understood.

- In 1944, bacteriologist [Oswald Avery](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Oswald-Avery) and colleagues [Maclyn McCarty](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Maclyn-McCarty) and Colin MacLeod demonstrated that DNA, not protein, was the substance responsible for transferring genetic traits in [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria).

- In the late 1940s, biochemist Erwin Chargaff showed that the amount of [adenine](https://www.britannica.com/science/adenine) in DNA roughly equals the amount of [thymine](https://www.britannica.com/science/thymine), and the amount of [guanine](https://www.britannica.com/science/guanine) roughly equals that of [cytosine](https://www.britannica.com/science/cytosine)—findings that later helped scientists determine DNA’s double-helical structure.

- In 1952, scientist [Rosalind Franklin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rosalind-Franklin), using [X-ray diffraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray-diffraction), captured her famous “Photo 51,” which revealed the helical shape of DNA.

- In 1953, using experimental [data](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/data) from several researchers, including Franklin and [Maurice Wilkins](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Maurice-Wilkins), biochemist [James Watson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Dewey-Watson) and biophysicist [Francis Crick](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-Crick) described DNA as two intertwined strands held together by complementary base pairing.

The chemical DNA was first discovered in 1869, but its role in genetic inheritance was not demonstrated until 1943. In 1953 [James Watson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Dewey-Watson) and [Francis Crick](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-Crick), aided by the work of biophysicists [Rosalind Franklin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rosalind-Franklin) and [Maurice Wilkins](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Maurice-Wilkins), determined that the structure of DNA is a double-helix [polymer](https://www.britannica.com/science/polymer), a spiral consisting of two DNA strands wound around each other in opposite (antiparallel) directions. The breakthrough led to significant advances in scientists’ understanding of DNA replication and hereditary control of cellular activities.

## Structure

![The polynucleotide chain of deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA)](https://cdn.britannica.com/19/6519-050-C4091560/Portion-polynucleotide-chain-acid-inset-base-ribonucleic.jpg)

[The polynucleotide chain of deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA)](https://cdn.britannica.com/19/6519-050-C4091560/Portion-polynucleotide-chain-acid-inset-base-ribonucleic.jpg) A portion of the polynucleotide chain of deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA), with an inset showing the corresponding pentose sugar and pyrimidine base in ribonucleic acid (RNA).(more)

Each strand of a DNA [molecule](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) is composed of a long chain of [monomer](https://www.britannica.com/science/monomer) [nucleotides](https://www.britannica.com/science/nucleotide). The nucleotides of DNA consist of a [deoxyribose](https://www.britannica.com/science/deoxyribose) sugar molecule to which is attached a [phosphate](https://www.britannica.com/science/phosphate) group and one of four nitrogenous [bases](https://www.britannica.com/science/base-nucleic-acid): two [purines](https://www.britannica.com/science/purine) ( [adenine](https://www.britannica.com/science/adenine) and [guanine](https://www.britannica.com/science/guanine)) and two [pyrimidines](https://www.britannica.com/science/pyrimidine) ( [cytosine](https://www.britannica.com/science/cytosine) and [thymine](https://www.britannica.com/science/thymine)). The nucleotides are joined together by [covalent bonds](https://www.britannica.com/science/covalent-bond) between the phosphate of one [nucleotide](https://www.britannica.com/science/nucleotide) and the sugar of the next, forming a phosphate-sugar [backbone](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/backbone) linked by phosphodiester bonds, from which the nitrogenous bases protrude. One strand is held to another by [hydrogen bonds](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen-bonding) between the bases; the sequencing of this bonding is specific—i.e., adenine bonds only with thymine, and cytosine only with guanine.

The configuration of the DNA molecule is highly stable, allowing it to act as a template for the replication of new DNA molecules, as well as for the production ( [transcription](https://www.britannica.com/science/transcription-genetics)) of the related [RNA](https://www.britannica.com/science/RNA) (ribonucleic acid) molecule, which differs from DNA by containing [ribose](https://www.britannica.com/science/ribose) sugar and the nitrogenous base [uracil](https://www.britannica.com/science/uracil) instead of thymine. A segment of DNA that codes for the [cell’s](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology) [synthesis](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/synthesis) of a specific [protein](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) is called a [gene](https://www.britannica.com/science/gene).

## Replication and organization

DNA [replicates](https://www.britannica.com/science/replication) by separating into two single strands, each of which serves as a template for a new strand. The new strands are copied by the same principle of hydrogen-bond pairing between bases that exists in the double helix. Two new double-stranded molecules of DNA are produced, each containing one of the original strands and one new strand. This “semiconservative” replication is the key to the stable inheritance of genetic traits.

Within a cell, DNA is organized into dense protein-DNA complexes called [chromosomes](https://www.britannica.com/science/chromosome). In [eukaryotes](https://www.britannica.com/science/eukaryote), DNA is wrapped around proteins called [histones](https://www.britannica.com/science/histone) to form chromatin, and the chromosomes are located in the [nucleus](https://www.britannica.com/science/nucleus-biology), although DNA also is found in [mitochondria](https://www.britannica.com/science/mitochondrion) and [chloroplasts](https://www.britannica.com/science/chloroplast). In [prokaryotes](https://www.britannica.com/science/prokaryote), which do not have a membrane-bound nucleus, the DNA is found as a single circular [chromosome](https://www.britannica.com/science/chromosome) in the [cytoplasm](https://www.britannica.com/science/cytoplasm).

## Alternative DNA structures and forms

Some prokaryotes, such as [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria), and a few eukaryotes contain small, circular molecules of extrachromosomal DNA known as [plasmids](https://www.britannica.com/science/plasmid). Unlike chromosomal DNA, plasmids replicate independently within the cell and often carry genes that provide selective advantages, such as [antibiotic resistance](https://www.britannica.com/science/antibiotic-resistance) or the ability to metabolize unusual substances. Because plasmids can be transferred between cells, they play an important role in [horizontal gene transfer](https://www.britannica.com/science/horizontal-gene-transfer) (the movement of genetic material between distinct organisms), particularly among bacteria. In biotechnology and molecular biology, plasmids have been widely used as vectors in [recombinant DNA technology](https://www.britannica.com/science/recombinant-DNA-technology) to insert, replicate, and study genes, as well as to produce proteins such as [insulin](https://www.britannica.com/science/insulin) and other medically important substances.

In cellular organisms, [alternative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alternative) DNA structures can also occur under certain conditions. Among these are G-quadruplexes, four-stranded DNA configurations that form in guanine-rich areas of the genome. These structures have been identified in human DNA and are thought to play roles in regulating gene expression, DNA replication, and chromosome stability. Researchers are also investigating their possible involvement in [cancer](https://www.britannica.com/science/cancer-disease) and other diseases, since G-quadruplex formation can influence how genes are activated or suppressed.

Viral genomes vary widely in size, structure, and replication strategy. The genetic material of viruses, for example, may be single- or double-stranded DNA or RNA. [Retroviruses](https://www.britannica.com/science/retrovirus) carry their genetic material as single-stranded RNA and use an [enzyme](https://www.britannica.com/science/enzyme) known as [reverse transcriptase](https://www.britannica.com/science/reverse-transcriptase) to synthesize DNA from the RNA template after infecting a host cell. The newly formed viral DNA can then [integrate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrate) into the host genome, allowing the [virus](https://www.britannica.com/science/virus) to replicate using the host’s cellular machinery.

All [dogs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dog) are close relatives of [wolves](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wolf)—after all, domestic dogs ( _Canis lupus familiaris_) descend from [gray wolves](https://www.britannica.com/animal/gray-wolf) ( _C. lupus_). Yet, several breeds, including the [Shiba Inu](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Shiba-Inu), [Akita](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Akita-dog), [Alaskan Malamute](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Alaskan-Malamute), [Siberian Husky](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Siberian-husky), and [Chow Chow](https://www.britannica.com/animal/chow-chow), remain genetically closer to wolves than others. Because of this, they are often grouped together as the so-called ancient breeds.

[![Gray wolf](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/240180-050-E93295BC/gray-wolf-Canis-lupus.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/240180-050-E93295BC/gray-wolf-Canis-lupus.jpg)

[Gray wolf](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/240180-050-E93295BC/gray-wolf-Canis-lupus.jpg) Because domestic dogs ( _Canis lupus familiaris_) descended from gray wolves ( _C. lupus_), all dogs are closely related to wolves.(more)

Genetic evidence indicates that the domestication of dogs occurred at least 15,000–35,000 years ago. Rather than a single event, domestication probably occurred multiple times across different regions of [Eurasia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Eurasia). Wolves may even have played an active role in their own domestication, scavenging the kills of nomadic hunters and gradually forging a mutually beneficial relationship with humans. From these early interactions, the ancient breeds emerged—arising in East Asia or the Arctic long before modern kennel clubs and [selective breeding](https://www.britannica.com/science/selective-breeding) practices began to shape dogs for particular jobs or appearances.

The wolflike legacy of these breeds is still visible at the genetic level. Comparisons of mitochondrial [DNA](https://www.britannica.com/science/DNA) and whole-genome sequences consistently show that ancient breeds retain markers more closely aligned with wolves than with the majority of modern dog breeds.

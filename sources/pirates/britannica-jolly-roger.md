- How has the Jolly Roger become a symbol in modern times?







The Jolly Roger, a flag design featuring a white skull and crossbones on a black background, was historically used by pirates to intimidate their prey. Today, it has become an icon in popular culture.



Its modern use as a symbol of martial prowess began with British navy submarine crews in World War I, who created their own versions of the flag. The USS Kidd is the only U.S. naval ship authorized to fly the flag. The Jolly Roger frequently appears in fictional works about pirates, such as _Treasure Island_ and _Peter Pan_. Several American sports teams, such as the Tampa Bay Buccaneers, Las Vegas Raiders, and Pittsburgh Pirates, use variations of the flag for their team logos. The skull-and-crossbones design is also a warning symbol on poison control labels.

**Jolly Roger**, [flag](https://www.britannica.com/topic/flag-heraldry) design used by [pirates](https://www.britannica.com/topic/piracy-international-law) and [privateers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/privateer) to frighten the crews of other [ships](https://www.britannica.com/technology/ship) into submission. The Jolly Roger is most recognizable by its white skull-and-crossbones design on a black background, but the flags of individual pirates also carried other design elements for identification or to communicate specific intentions. The Jolly Roger, or some variation of it, was most notably used during the “golden age of piracy,” which spanned the late 17th to early 18th century.

The origin of the name Jolly Roger is not clear. During the golden age “Roger” or “Old Roger” was a nickname for the [devil](https://www.britannica.com/topic/devil), and “roger” was a label for vagrants and privateers (who were also known as “sea beggars”). Other sources maintain that the name could have been derived from a French phrase, _joli rouge_ (“pretty red”), which referred to a red flag commonly flown by privateers.

## Purpose and origins

The flag was not flown constantly by pirate vessels. Before hoisting the Jolly Roger, pirates would raise the flag of one country or another to trick the crew of a ship into letting them draw near so they could plunder the ship. The flag’s use followed a previous tradition of raising a plain black flag to intimidate a targeted crew into surrendering. The black flag signaled to the targeted ship that it was about to be attacked and boarded, but quarter might be given (meaning that the targeted crew might be taken prisoner instead of being executed) if the ship was surrendered immediately. If the targeted crew did not heed this warning, the black flag would be replaced with a red flag, which signaled that no quarter would be given and that the pirates would fight to the death to obtain their loot.

The origins of the flag’s design are not well known. The skull and crossbones may have originated with [Barbary pirates](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Barbary-pirate), who added a [skull](https://www.britannica.com/science/skull) to their green flag as early as the 16th century. Later an entry in a French ship’s log from 1687 refers to a red flag with a skull and crossbones carried by disembarked pirates on land.

French pirate Emanuel Wynn, who operated in the western [Atlantic Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean) near Britain’s Carolina [colonies](https://www.britannica.com/topic/American-colonies) and in the [Caribbean Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Caribbean-Sea) about 1700, is considered by many historians to have been the first to fly a flag with a skull and crossbones, but his Jolly Roger also included an [hourglass](https://www.britannica.com/technology/hourglass) symbol. British pirate Richard Worley and Irish pirate Edward England are both credited as being early adopters of better-known versions of the Jolly Roger (that is, a black flag with a white skull and crossbones) between 1717 and 1720.

On Bonnet's flag, the skull is flanked by a dagger and a heart. Although no examples have survived, this reconstruction is derived from existing evidence.(more)

There is evidence that the term Jolly Roger was used as a catchall for black pirate flags of the period. The first known appearance of the term occurs in _A General History of the Robberies and Murders of the Most Notorious Pyrates_ (1724)—a book attributed at the time to a Capt. Charles Johnson but possibly written by English novelist [Daniel Defoe](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Daniel-Defoe) or English printer-journalist Nathaniel Mist. In this work, the name Jolly Roger refers to the flags flown by Welsh pirate [Bartholomew Roberts](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bartholomew-Roberts), also known as Black Bart Roberts, and English pirate Francis Spriggs, both of whom flew several variants of the skull-and-crossbones design about the same time as Worley and England.

Roberts used a series of black flags made up of different elements, including those with a skull and a single [sword](https://www.britannica.com/technology/sword), a single [skeleton](https://www.britannica.com/science/skeleton), a pirate sharing a cup of wine with a spear-brandishing skeleton, and a pirate bearing a sword and standing atop two skulls. Likewise, Spriggs’s flags featured a red skeleton set against a black field or a white skeleton holding an hourglass and a spear with a red heart image against a black background. Other pirates, such as [Stede Bonnet](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Stede-Bonnet) (known as the Gentleman Pirate), flew their own versions of the Jolly Roger.

## Use in modern navies and popular culture

The Jolly Roger has become an icon of popular culture. Its use as a modern [symbol](https://www.britannica.com/topic/symbol) of martial prowess began in [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I) (1914−18) with the [British navy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Royal-Navy)’s [submarine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/submarine-naval-vessel) crews, who designed their own variations on the flag. The USS _Kidd_, a destroyer whose mascot is English privateer [William Kidd](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Kidd), is the only [U.S. naval](https://www.britannica.com/topic/United-States-Navy) ship authorized to fly the flag.

The Jolly Roger is frequently found in fictional works about pirates: in the novel [_Treasure Island_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Treasure-Island) (1881–82) by Scottish author [Robert Louis Stevenson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Louis-Stevenson), the main character throws the flag overboard, and in the play [_Peter Pan_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Peter-Pan-play-by-Barrie) (first staged in 1904) by Scottish playwright [J.M. Barrie](https://www.britannica.com/biography/J-M-Barrie), the ship commanded by the villainous Captain Hook is named _Jolly Roger_. Several American sports teams, such as the [Tampa Bay Buccaneers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tampa-Bay-Buccaneers) and [Las Vegas Raiders](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Las-Vegas-Raiders) (both of the [National Football League](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Football-League) \[NFL\]) and the [Pittsburgh Pirates](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pittsburgh-Pirates) (of [Major League Baseball](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Major-League-Baseball) \[MLB\]), have used variations of the flag for their team logos. The skull-and-crossbones design is also used as a warning symbol on [poison](https://www.britannica.com/science/poison-biochemistry) control labeling.

**piracy**, any [robbery](https://www.britannica.com/topic/robbery) or other violent action, for private ends and without authorization by public authority, committed on the seas or in the air outside the normal [jurisdiction](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/jurisdiction) of any state. Because piracy has been regarded as an offense against the law of nations, the public vessels of any state have been permitted to seize a pirate ship, to bring it into port, to try the crew (regardless of their nationality or domicile), and, if they are found guilty, to punish them and to confiscate the ship.

According to [international law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/international-law), piracy takes place outside the normal jurisdiction of a state, without state authority, and is private, not political, though acts of unlawful [warfare](https://www.britannica.com/topic/war), acts of insurgents and revolutionaries, [mutiny](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mutiny), and slave trading have been defined as piracy by national laws of various countries or by special treaties.

Piracy has occurred throughout history. In the ancient Mediterranean, piracy was often closely related to [maritime](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/maritime) commerce, and the Phoenicians appear to have engaged in both, as did the Greeks, the Romans, and the Carthaginians. In the Middle Ages, Vikings from the north and Moors from the south also engaged in piracy. At the conclusion of European wars during the Renaissance and after, naval vessels were routinely laid up and their crews disbanded; the unemployed crew from these ships were often drawn into the service of pirates.

A common source of piracy was the [privateer](https://www.britannica.com/technology/privateer), a privately owned and armed ship commissioned by a government to make reprisals, gain [reparation](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/reparation) for specified offenses in time of peace, or prey upon the enemy in time of war; its officers and crew were granted a share of the plunder taken from captured vessels. After a war the temptation was great to continue this profitable business without authorization. During the wars between [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) and [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain) in the late 16th century, treasure-laden Spanish [galleons](https://www.britannica.com/technology/galleon) sailing from Mexico into the Caribbean were a natural target for privateers, and the distinction between privateering and piracy became difficult to draw.

The so-called “golden age” of piracy occurred in the Caribbean and in the waters off the [American colonies](https://www.britannica.com/topic/American-colonies) in the century after 1650. This was the era of legendary figures such as Sir [Henry Morgan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-Morgan-Welsh-buccaneer), [Blackbeard](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Blackbeard), and [William Kidd](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Kidd) (“Captain Kidd”). Pirate crews came from every maritime country of Europe, and a good number of sailors were African. Among the most successful pirates of [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) was Jean-François Duclerc, a Frenchman who preyed on ships in the area around [Guanabara Bay](https://www.britannica.com/place/Guanabara-Bay) (southeastern [Brazil](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brazil) between Niterói and Rio de Janeiro). The exploits of these and other pirates later inspired a sizable [genre](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/genre) of popular [romantic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/romantic) and [children’s literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/childrens-literature), perhaps best exemplified by [Robert Louis Stevenson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Louis-Stevenson)’s _[Treasure Island](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Treasure-Island)_ (1881). In the late 20th century, pirates became the subject of serious historical inquiry. Some scholars portrayed pirate [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) as a genuinely subversive radical movement that defied the common distinctions of class and race and kept alive the dreams of 17th-century political radicals long after they had been defeated in England and elsewhere.

Piracy also flourished in other regions. From the 16th to the 18th century, after the weakening of Turkish rule had resulted in the virtual independence of the [Barbary](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Barbary-pirate) States of [North Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Africa), piracy became common in the Mediterranean. [Morocco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Morocco), [Algiers](https://www.britannica.com/place/Algiers), Tunis, and Tripoli so tolerated or even organized piracy that they came to be called pirate states. In the early 19th century these pirate states were suppressed by successive actions of American, British, and French forces.

Although piracy declined dramatically in the 19th century, the practice of [hijacking](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hijacking) ships and airplanes developed into a new form of piracy in the late 20th century. The [affinity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/affinity) between piracy and [terrorism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/terrorism) became of particular concern after the hijacking of the _Achille Lauro_ cruise liner by Palestinian militants in 1985 and after agents of [al-Qaeda](https://www.britannica.com/topic/al-Qaeda) executed the [September 11 attacks](https://www.britannica.com/event/September-11-attacks) of 2001 in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States). In the last decades of the 20th century, nautical piracy once again became prevalent in the seas of East and [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia) and [eastern Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/eastern-Africa), where acts of piracy were committed by or in cooperation with criminal organizations involved in [smuggling](https://www.britannica.com/topic/smuggling) (of guns and drugs) and other illegal activities. These pirates sometimes operated under the protection of state officials in small ports, who received a share of the illicit profits. An upsurge of attacks in waters off the coast of Africa, particularly Somalia, in 2008–09 included the hijacking of ships belonging to several countries and led to the forcible intervention of warships of several navies. These events prompted the Western news media to reexamine the special problems of international jurisdiction posed by such incidents and to address themselves once more to historical lessons learned in the 18th century—above all, the necessity of [deploying](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/deploying) [armed force](https://www.britannica.com/topic/armed-force) against pirates and their home bases.

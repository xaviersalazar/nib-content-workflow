- What did buccaneers do in the Caribbean?







Buccaneers were maritime adventurers who primarily operated in the Caribbean and along the Pacific coast of South America during the 17th century, preying on Spanish settlements and shipping. The term _buccaneer_ came from the French word _boucan_, a grill for smoking meat, which they initially used as hunters in Hispaniola.



Inspired by figures like Sir Francis Drake, they targeted Spanish interests, often with support from England and France, becoming a menace to Spanish control in the region. Though sometimes confused with [pirates](https://www.britannica.com/story/pirates-privateers-corsairs-buccaneers-whats-the-difference), buccaneers gained notoriety through memoirs and tales of their exploits, influencing literature and popular culture.

Buccaneers, who haunted the Caribbean and the Pacific seaboard of South America, were sea adventurers who preyed on Spanish settlements and shipping during the second half of the 17th century.

- What caused the decline of buccaneers and their activities?







The end of buccaneering came about due to a combination of political and economic shifts in the late 17th century.



After the Treaty of Madrid in 1670, which aimed to resolve Anglo-Spanish conflicts, the English authorities started to suppress buccaneering, though they lacked the naval resources to do so effectively. Additionally, the outbreak of the War of the Grand Alliance in 1689 led many buccaneers to become privateers in the service of their respective nations, marking the end of buccaneering as a distinct phenomenon.

**buccaneer**, English, French, or Dutch sea adventurer who haunted chiefly the [Caribbean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Caribbean-Sea) and the Pacific seaboard of [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America), preying on Spanish settlements and shipping during the second half of the 17th century. In their own day, [buccaneers](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/buccaneers) were usually called [privateers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/privateer); the word _buccaneer_ came into use after the publication, in 1684, of _Bucaniers_ \[sic\] _of America_, the English translation of _De Americaensche zee-rovers,_ by the Dutchman Alexander Esquemelin (or Exquemelin), whose work was a fecund source of tales of these men.

![piracy](https://cdn.britannica.com/75/196475-050-83AAA9BA/pirates-history.jpg)

[piracy](https://cdn.britannica.com/75/196475-050-83AAA9BA/pirates-history.jpg) Learn about pirates throughout history.(more)

The term _buccaneer_ comes from the French _boucan_, a grill for the smoking of _viande boucanée_, or dried meat, for use in ships at sea. The French called their adventurers _flibustiers_ (from the Dutch _vrijbuiter_, “freebooter”), and the Dutch called theirs _zeerovers_ (“sea robbers”); the Spaniards called them _corsarios_ (“corsairs”). The earliest buccaneers were hunters in western [Hispaniola](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hispaniola) ( [Haiti](https://www.britannica.com/place/Haiti)) in the early 17th century. They spread from there to the island of [Tortuga](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tortue-Island), the French governors of which were liberal in issuing commissions for attacks on Spanish maritime trade. [Jamaica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jamaica), after its capture by the English in 1655, also afforded a base for their activities.

[![William Dampier](https://cdn.britannica.com/01/7901-004-8BFF913E/William-Dampier.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/01/7901-004-8BFF913E/William-Dampier.jpg)

[William Dampier](https://cdn.britannica.com/01/7901-004-8BFF913E/William-Dampier.jpg)(more)

The early buccaneers were usually escaped servants, former soldiers, and logwood cutters from the [Campeche](https://www.britannica.com/place/Campeche-Mexico) coast (in present southern Mexico). They exercised a democratic [discipline](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/discipline) among themselves when they went off “on the account,” electing their captains, marooning [mutineers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mutiny), arranging for the equitable distribution of shares of plunder, and drawing up elaborate insurance schemes for injuries suffered. Because they attracted to their ranks such remarkable men as [William Dampier](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Dampier), Lionel Wafer, and Basil Ringrose, who wrote racy accounts of their adventurous cruises, they had a greater influence on later generations than was justified by their exploits.

The historical importance of the buccaneers lies chiefly in the influence that they had on the founding of the abortive Scottish colony at [Darién](https://www.britannica.com/place/Darien-region-Panama), on the [Isthmus of Panama](https://www.britannica.com/place/Isthmus-of-Panama) (1698), and on the foundation of the [South Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-China-Sea) Company and also in the way in which they inspired later and more serious [voyages](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/voyages) of exploration in the Pacific by the publicity that their writings enjoyed. Their stories also influenced such important authors as [Jonathan Swift](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jonathan-Swift), [Daniel Defoe](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Daniel-Defoe), and [Robert Louis Stevenson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Louis-Stevenson). The chief bond between these Brethren of the Coast, as they styled themselves, was hostility toward the Spaniards, who then regarded the Caribbean and the South Pacific sea lanes as their monopoly.

The buccaneers were largely inspired by the example of 16th-century seamen such as [Sir Francis Drake](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-Drake), but they are to be distinguished from genuine privateers because the commissions that they held were seldom valid. They are also to be distinguished from the outlawed [pirates](https://www.britannica.com/topic/piracy-international-law) of the 18th century, although many of the buccaneers’ actions can be called piratical.

The earliest buccaneers went under assumed names, such as L’Olonnais (Jean-David Nau) or Rock Brasiliano, a Dutchman who had lived in Brazil. With the appearance of [Sir Henry Morgan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-Morgan-Welsh-buccaneer), an outstanding leader, they began to organize themselves into powerful bands that captured [Portobelo](https://www.britannica.com/place/Portobelo) in 1668 and [Panama](https://www.britannica.com/place/Panama-City) in 1671. As the Treaty of Madrid (1670) had only recently been signed to compose Anglo-Spanish differences in those parts, the news of his success at Panama was not officially welcome. Morgan was brought back to England under arrest, but, on the renewal of trouble with Spain, he was knighted and sent out as deputy governor of Jamaica. He and his superiors attempted to suppress buccaneering, a task impossible without adequate naval patrols. The last great buccaneering enterprise was the unsuccessful attack on Panama in about 1685 by a force of about 3,000 men led by Edward Davis, John Eaton, Charles Swan, and others. On the outbreak of the [War of the Grand Alliance](https://www.britannica.com/event/War-of-the-Grand-Alliance) in 1689, these freebooters became [legitimate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legitimate) privateers in the service of their respective nations, and buccaneering came to an end.

**piracy**, any [robbery](https://www.britannica.com/topic/robbery) or other violent action, for private ends and without authorization by public authority, committed on the seas or in the air outside the normal [jurisdiction](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/jurisdiction) of any state. Because piracy has been regarded as an offense against the law of nations, the public vessels of any state have been permitted to seize a pirate ship, to bring it into port, to try the crew (regardless of their nationality or domicile), and, if they are found guilty, to punish them and to confiscate the ship.

According to [international law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/international-law), piracy takes place outside the normal jurisdiction of a state, without state authority, and is private, not political, though acts of unlawful [warfare](https://www.britannica.com/topic/war), acts of insurgents and revolutionaries, [mutiny](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mutiny), and slave trading have been defined as piracy by national laws of various countries or by special treaties.

Piracy has occurred throughout history. In the ancient Mediterranean, piracy was often closely related to [maritime](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/maritime) commerce, and the Phoenicians appear to have engaged in both, as did the Greeks, the Romans, and the Carthaginians. In the Middle Ages, Vikings from the north and Moors from the south also engaged in piracy. At the conclusion of European wars during the Renaissance and after, naval vessels were routinely laid up and their crews disbanded; the unemployed crew from these ships were often drawn into the service of pirates.

A common source of piracy was the [privateer](https://www.britannica.com/technology/privateer), a privately owned and armed ship commissioned by a government to make reprisals, gain [reparation](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/reparation) for specified offenses in time of peace, or prey upon the enemy in time of war; its officers and crew were granted a share of the plunder taken from captured vessels. After a war the temptation was great to continue this profitable business without authorization. During the wars between [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) and [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain) in the late 16th century, treasure-laden Spanish [galleons](https://www.britannica.com/technology/galleon) sailing from Mexico into the Caribbean were a natural target for privateers, and the distinction between privateering and piracy became difficult to draw.

The so-called “golden age” of piracy occurred in the Caribbean and in the waters off the [American colonies](https://www.britannica.com/topic/American-colonies) in the century after 1650. This was the era of legendary figures such as Sir [Henry Morgan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-Morgan-Welsh-buccaneer), [Blackbeard](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Blackbeard), and [William Kidd](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Kidd) (“Captain Kidd”). Pirate crews came from every maritime country of Europe, and a good number of sailors were African. Among the most successful pirates of [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) was Jean-François Duclerc, a Frenchman who preyed on ships in the area around [Guanabara Bay](https://www.britannica.com/place/Guanabara-Bay) (southeastern [Brazil](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brazil) between Niterói and Rio de Janeiro). The exploits of these and other pirates later inspired a sizable [genre](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/genre) of popular [romantic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/romantic) and [children’s literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/childrens-literature), perhaps best exemplified by [Robert Louis Stevenson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Louis-Stevenson)’s _[Treasure Island](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Treasure-Island)_ (1881). In the late 20th century, pirates became the subject of serious historical inquiry. Some scholars portrayed pirate [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) as a genuinely subversive radical movement that defied the common distinctions of class and race and kept alive the dreams of 17th-century political radicals long after they had been defeated in England and elsewhere.

Piracy also flourished in other regions. From the 16th to the 18th century, after the weakening of Turkish rule had resulted in the virtual independence of the [Barbary](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Barbary-pirate) States of [North Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Africa), piracy became common in the Mediterranean. [Morocco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Morocco), [Algiers](https://www.britannica.com/place/Algiers), Tunis, and Tripoli so tolerated or even organized piracy that they came to be called pirate states. In the early 19th century these pirate states were suppressed by successive actions of American, British, and French forces.

Although piracy declined dramatically in the 19th century, the practice of [hijacking](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hijacking) ships and airplanes developed into a new form of piracy in the late 20th century. The [affinity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/affinity) between piracy and [terrorism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/terrorism) became of particular concern after the hijacking of the _Achille Lauro_ cruise liner by Palestinian militants in 1985 and after agents of [al-Qaeda](https://www.britannica.com/topic/al-Qaeda) executed the [September 11 attacks](https://www.britannica.com/event/September-11-attacks) of 2001 in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States). In the last decades of the 20th century, nautical piracy once again became prevalent in the seas of East and [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia) and [eastern Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/eastern-Africa), where acts of piracy were committed by or in cooperation with criminal organizations involved in [smuggling](https://www.britannica.com/topic/smuggling) (of guns and drugs) and other illegal activities. These pirates sometimes operated under the protection of state officials in small ports, who received a share of the illicit profits. An upsurge of attacks in waters off the coast of Africa, particularly Somalia, in 2008–09 included the hijacking of ships belonging to several countries and led to the forcible intervention of warships of several navies. These events prompted the Western news media to reexamine the special problems of international jurisdiction posed by such incidents and to address themselves once more to historical lessons learned in the 18th century—above all, the necessity of [deploying](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/deploying) [armed force](https://www.britannica.com/topic/armed-force) against pirates and their home bases.

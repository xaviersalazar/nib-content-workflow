Top Questions


### What is Ibn Battuta known for?

Ibn Battuta was a medieval Muslim traveler who wrote one of the world’s most famous travel logs, the [Riḥlah _Riḥlah_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Travels-by-Ibn-Battutah). This work describes the people, places, and cultures he encountered in his journeys along some 75,000 miles (120,000 km) across and beyond the [Islamic world](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-world).

### How did Ibn Battuta impact the world?

Ibn Battuta’s _Riḥlah_ has tremendous documentary value because of its detailed accounts of social, cultural, and political aspects of much of the [Islamic world](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-world) during the 14th century. His unique and mostly reliable [historiography](https://www.britannica.com/topic/historiography) is valuable for the study of [history](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history).

### What was Ibn Battuta’s early life like?

Ibn Battuta was born into a family of [qadis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/qadi), Muslim judges who enjoyed significant civil authority at the time. He was educated toward this end in his hometown of [Tangier](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tangier-Morocco). His travels began when he undertook a pilgrimage ( [hajj](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hajj)) to [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mecca).

### What was Ibn Battuta’s occupation?

Ibn Battuta was trained as a [qadi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/qadi), a Muslim judge who ruled on matters both religious and civil. He worked as a qadi in various places, including [Delhi](https://www.britannica.com/place/Delhi), the [Maldives](https://www.britannica.com/place/Maldives), and possibly [Morocco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Morocco) toward the end of his life. His travels were independent of his occupation, however, and depended on the support of benefactors.

**Ibn Battuta** (born February 24, 1304, Tangier, Morocco—died 1368/69 or 1377, Morocco) was the greatest [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) Muslim [traveler](https://www.britannica.com/art/travel-literature) and the author of one of the most famous travel books, the _[Riḥlah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Travels-by-Ibn-Battutah)_ ( _Travels_). His great work describes his extensive [travels](https://www.britannica.com/topic/non-European-exploration) covering some 75,000 miles (120,000 km) in trips to almost all of the Muslim countries and as far as [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) and [Sumatra](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sumatra) (now part of [Indonesia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indonesia)).

## Early life and travels

![Ibn Baṭṭūṭah's voyages](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/163973-050-5E38FBFF/voyages-Ibn-Battutah.jpg)

[Ibn Baṭṭūṭah's voyages](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/163973-050-5E38FBFF/voyages-Ibn-Battutah.jpg) The voyages of Ibn Baṭṭūṭah.(more)

Ibn Battuta was from a family that produced a number of Muslim judges ( [qadis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/qadi)). He received the traditional juristic and literary education in his native town of [Tangier](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tangier-Morocco). In 1325, at the age of 21, he started his travels by undertaking the [pilgrimage](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pilgrimage-religion) ( [hajj](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hajj)) to [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mecca). At first his purpose was to fulfill that religious duty and to broaden his education by studying under famous scholars in [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt), [Syria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Syria), and the [Hejaz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hejaz) (western [Arabia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arabia-peninsula-Asia)). That he achieved his objectives is [corroborated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/corroborated) by long enumerations of scholars and [Sufi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sufism) (Islamic mystic) saints whom he met and also by a list of diplomas conferred on him (mainly in [Damascus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Damascus)). Those studies qualified him for judicial office, whereas the claim of being a former pupil of the then-outstanding authorities in traditional Islamic sciences greatly [enhanced](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhanced) his chances and made him thereafter a respected guest at many courts.

That renown was to follow later, however. In Egypt, where he arrived by the land route via [Tunis](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tunis) and [Tripoli](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tripoli), an irresistible passion for travel was born in his soul, and he decided to visit as many parts of the world as possible, setting as a rule “never to travel any road a second time.” His contemporaries traveled for practical reasons (such as trade, pilgrimage, and education), but Ibn Battuta did it for its own sake, for the joy of learning about new countries and new peoples. He made a living of it, benefitting at the beginning from his scholarly status and later from his increasing fame as a traveler. He enjoyed the generosity and [benevolence](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/benevolence) of numerous sultans, rulers, governors, and high dignitaries in the countries he visited, thus securing an income that enabled him to continue his wanderings.

From [Cairo](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cairo), Ibn Battuta set out via [Upper Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Upper-Egypt) to the [Red Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Red-Sea) but then returned and visited Syria, there joining a [caravan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/caravan-desert-transport) for Mecca. Having finished the pilgrimage in 1326, he crossed the [Arabian Desert](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arabian-Desert) to [Iraq](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iraq), southern [Iran](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iran), [Azerbaijan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Azerbaijan), and [Baghdad](https://www.britannica.com/place/Baghdad). There he met the last of the [Mongol](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mongol) khans of Iran, Abū Saʿīd (ruled 1316–36), and some lesser rulers. Ibn Battuta spent the years between 1327 and 1330 in [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Mecca) and [Medina](https://www.britannica.com/place/Medina-Saudi-Arabia) leading the quiet life of a devotee, but such a long stay did not suit his temperament.

Embarking on a boat in [Jiddah](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jeddah-Saudi-Arabia), he sailed with a retinue of followers down both shores of the Red Sea to [Yemen](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yemen), crossed it by land, and set sail again from [Aden](https://www.britannica.com/place/Aden). This time he navigated along the eastern African coast, visiting the trading city-states as far as [Kilwa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kilwa) (Tanzania). His return journey took him to southern Arabia, [Oman](https://www.britannica.com/place/Oman), [Hormuz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hormuz), southern Persia, and across the [Persian Gulf](https://www.britannica.com/place/Persian-Gulf) back to Mecca in 1332.

There a new, ambitious plan matured in his mind. Hearing of the [sultan of Delhi](https://www.britannica.com/place/Delhi-sultanate), [Muḥammad ibn Tughluq](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Muhammad-ibn-Tughluq) (ruled 1325–51), and his fabulous generosity to Muslim scholars, he decided to try his luck at his court. Forced by lack of communications to choose a more indirect route, Ibn Battuta turned northward, again passed Egypt and Syria, and boarded ship for Asia Minor ( [Anatolia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Anatolia)) in [Latakia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Latakia-Syria). He crisscrossed that “land of the Turks” in many directions at a time when [Anatolia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Anatolia) was divided into numerous petty sultanates. Thus, his narrative provides a valuable source for the history of that country between the end of the [Seljuq](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Seljuq) power and the rise of the house of Ottoman. Ibn Battuta was received cordially and generously by all the local rulers and heads of religious brotherhoods ( _ākhī_ s).

His journey continued across the [Black Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Black-Sea) to the [Crimean Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Crimean-Peninsula), then to the northern [Caucasus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Caucasus) and to Saray on the lower [Volga River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Volga-River), capital of the khan of the [Golden Horde](https://www.britannica.com/place/Golden-Horde), [Öz Beg](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Oz-Beg) (ruled 1312–41). According to his narrative, he undertook an excursion from Saray to Bulgary on the upper Volga and [Kama](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kama-River-Russia), but there are reasons to doubt his [veracity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/veracity) on that point. On the other hand, the narrative of his visit to Constantinople (now [Istanbul](https://www.britannica.com/place/Istanbul)) in the retinue of the khan’s wife, a [Byzantine](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Byzantine) princess, seems to be an eyewitness record, although there are some minor chronological discrepancies. Ibn Battuta’s description of the [Byzantine](https://www.britannica.com/place/Byzantine-Empire) capital is vivid and, in general, accurate. Although he shared the strong opinions of his fellow Muslims toward unbelievers, his account of the “second Rome” shows him as a rather tolerant man with a lively curiosity. Nevertheless, he always felt happier in the realm of [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam) than in non-Muslim lands, whether Christian, Hindu, or pagan.

After his return from Constantinople through the Russian steppes, he continued his journey in the general direction of India. From Saray he traveled with a [caravan](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/caravan) to [Central Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/history-of-Central-Asia-102306), visiting the ancient towns of [Bukhara](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bukhara), [Samarkand](https://www.britannica.com/place/Samarkand-Uzbekistan), and [Balkh](https://www.britannica.com/place/Balkh), all of those still showing the scars left by the Mongol invasion. He took rather complicated routes through [Khorāsān](https://www.britannica.com/place/Khorasan-historical-region-Asia) and [Afghanistan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Afghanistan), and, after crossing the [Hindu Kush](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hindu-Kush) mountain range, he arrived at the frontiers of [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India) on the [Indus River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indus-River) on September 12, 1333, by his own dating. The accuracy of that date is doubtful, as it would have been impossible to cover such enormous distances (from Mecca) in the course of only one year. Because of that discrepancy, his subsequent dating until 1348 is highly uncertain.

# Time in India and later journeys of Ibn Battuta

By that time Ibn Battuta was already a man of some importance and fame, with a large train of attendants and followers and also with his own [harem](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/harem) of legal wives and concubines. [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India) and its ruler, [Muḥammad ibn Tughluq](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Muhammad-ibn-Tughluq), lived up to Ibn Battuta’s expectations of wealth and generosity, and the traveler was received with honours and gifts and later appointed grand [qadi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/qadi) of [Delhi](https://www.britannica.com/place/Delhi), a sinecure that he held for several years.

Though he had apparently attained an easy life, it soon became clear that his new position was not without danger. Sultan Muḥammad, an extraordinary mixture of generosity and cruelty, held sway over the greater part of India with an iron hand that fell indiscriminately upon high and low, Muslim and Hindu alike. Ibn Battuta witnessed all the glories and setbacks of the [sultan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sultan-Islamic-title) and his rule, fearing daily for his life as he saw many friends fall victim to the suspicious [despot](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/despot). His portrait of Muḥammad is an unusually fine piece of psychological insight and mirrors faithfully the author’s mixed feelings of terror and sympathy. Notwithstanding all his precautions, Ibn Battuta at last fell into disgrace, and only good fortune saved his life. Gaining favour again, he was appointed the sultan’s envoy to the Chinese emperor in 1342.

He left Delhi without regrets, but his journey was full of other dangers: not far away from Delhi his party was waylaid by Hindu insurgents, and the traveler barely escaped with his life. On the [Malabar Coast](https://www.britannica.com/place/Malabar-Coast) of southwestern India he became involved in local wars and was finally shipwrecked near Calicut (now [Kozhikode](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kozhikode)), losing all his property and the gifts for the Chinese emperor. Fearing the [wrath](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/wrath) of the sultan, Ibn Battuta chose to go to the [Maldive Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Maldives), where he spent nearly two years. As a [qadi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/qadi), he was soon active in politics, married into the ruling family, and apparently even aspired to become sultan.

Finding the situation too dangerous, he set out for [Sri Lanka](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sri-Lanka), where he visited the ruler as well as the famous [Adam’s Peak](https://www.britannica.com/place/Adams-Peak). After a new shipwreck on the [Coromandel Coast](https://www.britannica.com/place/Coromandel-Coast) of southeastern India, he took part in a war led by his brother-in-law and went again to the Maldives and then to [Bengal](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bengal-region-Asia) and [Assam](https://www.britannica.com/place/Assam). At that time he decided to resume his mission to [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) and sailed for [Sumatra](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sumatra). There he was given a new ship by the Muslim sultan and started for China; his description of his itinerary contains some discrepancies.

He landed at the great Chinese port Zaytūn (identified as [Quanzhou](https://www.britannica.com/place/Quanzhou), near [Xiamen](https://www.britannica.com/place/Xiamen) \[Amoy\]) and then traveled on inland waterways as far as [Beijing](https://www.britannica.com/place/Beijing) and back. That part of his narrative is rather brief, and the itinerary, as well as the chronology, presents many problems and difficulties, not yet surmounted, that cast shadows of doubt on his [veracity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/veracity).

Equally brief is his account of the return voyage via Sumatra, Malabar, and the [Persian Gulf](https://www.britannica.com/place/Persian-Gulf) to [Baghdad](https://www.britannica.com/place/Baghdad) and [Syria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Syria). In Syria he witnessed the ravages of the [Black Death](https://www.britannica.com/event/Black-Death) of 1348, visited again many towns there and in [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt), and in the same year performed his final pilgrimage to [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mecca). At last he decided to return home, sailing from [Alexandria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alexandria-Egypt) to [Tunisia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tunisia), then to [Sardinia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sardinia-island-Italy) and [Algiers](https://www.britannica.com/place/Algiers), finally reaching [Fès](https://www.britannica.com/place/Fes), the capital of the [Marīnid](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Marinid-dynasty) sultan, Abū ʿInān, in November 1349.

But there still remained two Muslim countries not [yet](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/yet) known to him. Shortly after his return he went to the kingdom of [Granada](https://www.britannica.com/place/Granada-historical-kingdom-Spain), the last remnant of Moorish [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain), and two years later (in 1352) he set out on a journey to the western [Sudan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sudan-region-Africa). His last journey (across the [Sahara](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sahara-desert-Africa) to Western Africa) was taken unwillingly at the command of the sultan. Crossing the Sahara, he spent a year in the [empire of Mali](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mali-historical-empire-Africa), then at the height of its power under Mansa Sulaymān; his account represents one of the most important sources of that period for the history of that part of Africa.

Toward the end of 1353 Ibn Battuta returned to [Morocco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Morocco) and, at the sultan’s request, [dictated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/dictated) his reminiscences to a writer, Ibn Juzayy (died 1355), who embellished the simple prose of Ibn Battuta with an ornate style and fragments of poetry. After that he passes from sight. He is reported to have held the office of [qadi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/qadi) in a town in Morocco before his death, details of which remain uncertain. It has been suggested that he died in 1368/69 or 1377 and was buried in his native town of [Tangier](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tangier-Morocco).

## Legacy

The claim of Ibn Battuta to be “the traveler of Islam” is well founded: it is estimated that the extent of his wanderings was some 75,000 miles (120,000 km), a figure hardly surpassed by anyone before the age of [steam power](https://www.britannica.com/science/steam-power). He visited almost all Muslim countries as well as many [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) non-Muslim lands. Notably, however, he did not visit central Persia, Armenia, and Georgia. While he did not discover new or unknown lands, and his contribution to scientific geography was minimal, the documentary value of his work has given it lasting historical and geographical significance. He met at least 60 rulers and a much greater number of viziers, governors, and other dignitaries; in his book he mentioned more than 2,000 persons who were known to him personally or whose tombs he visited. The majority of those people are identifiable by independent sources, and there are surprisingly few errors in names or dates in Ibn Battuta’s material.

His _[Riḥlah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Travels-by-Ibn-Battutah)_, as his book is commonly known, is an important document shedding light on many aspects of the social, cultural, and political history of a great part of the [Muslim world](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-world). Ibn Battuta was a curious observer interested in the ways of life in various countries, and he described his experiences with a human approach rarely encountered in official historiography. His accounts of his travels in [Asia Minor](https://www.britannica.com/place/Anatolia), East and [West Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/western-Africa), the Maldives, and India form a major source for the histories of those areas, whereas the parts dealing with the [Arab](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Arab) and Persian [Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/Middle-East) are valuable for their wealth of detail on various aspects of social and cultural life.

On the whole, Ibn Battuta is reliable; only his [alleged](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alleged) journey to Bulgary was proved to be invented, and there are some doubts concerning the East Asian part of his travels. A few grave and several minor discrepancies in the chronology of his travels are due more to lapses in his memory than to intentional fabrication. A number of formerly uncertain points (such as travels in Asia Minor and the visit to Constantinople) have since been cleared away by contemporary research and the discovery of new corroborative sources.

Another interesting aspect of the _Riḥlah_ is the gradual revealing of the character of Ibn Battuta himself; in the course of the narrative the reader may learn the opinions and reactions of an average middle-class Muslim of the 14th century. He was deeply rooted in orthodox [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam) but, like many of his contemporaries, oscillated between the pursuit of its legislative formalism and an [adherence](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adherence) to the mystic path and succeeded in combining both. He did not offer any profound philosophy but accepted life as it came to him, leaving to [posterity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/posterity) a true picture of himself and his times.

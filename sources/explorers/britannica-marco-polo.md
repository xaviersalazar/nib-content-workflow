### What was Marco Polo’s family like?

The Polos were likely shrewd, alert, and courageous; they traded with the [Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/Middle-East) and acquired considerable wealth and prestige. Marco Polo’s father, Niccolò, and uncle, Maffeo, continued this legacy. Traveling east as far as Mongol emperor [Kublai Khan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kublai-Khan)’s summer residence, Shangdu, they established friendly relations with him before returning to [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) as his [ambassadors](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ambassador).

### What did Marco Polo do?

Marco Polo was 17 or 18 when he began his journey from [Venice](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venice) to the farthest reaches of the [Mongol empire](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mongol-empire). Living among the emperor’s dominions, with his father and uncle, as an advisor and emissary for 16 or 17 years, he returned to Venice by way of [Hormuz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hormuz) (aboard [ship](https://www.britannica.com/technology/ship)) and [Constantinople](https://www.britannica.com/place/Istanbul) (overland).

### Why was Marco Polo so influential?

Marco Polo’s account in _Il milione_ opened new vistas to the European mind, and, as Western horizons expanded, Polo’s legacy grew as well. The wealth of new [geographic information](https://www.britannica.com/science/geography) recorded by Polo was widely used in the late 15th and the 16th centuries, during the age of the great [European voyages of discovery](https://www.britannica.com/topic/European-exploration) and conquest.

### What were Marco Polo’s other accomplishments in Asia?

Kublai Khan sent Marco Polo on fact-finding missions to distant parts of the empire, including visits to Yunnan (and possibly Myanmar \[Burma\]) and through southeastern China to “Quinsay” (now Hangzhou). He escorted a Mongol princess, with his father and uncle, by sea to Hormuz, and by land to Khorasan, during his return voyage to Venice.

**Marco Polo** (born c. 1254, Venice \[Italy\]—died January 8, 1324, Venice) was a Venetian merchant and adventurer who traveled from [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) to [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia) in 1271–95, remaining in [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) for 17 of those years. His _Il milione_ (“The Million”), known in English as the _Travels of Marco Polo_, is a classic of travel [literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/literature).

## Travels of the Polo family

![Marco Polo](https://cdn.britannica.com/17/144417-050-53036450/Marco-Polo-travels-Asia-1271-1295.jpg)

[Marco Polo](https://cdn.britannica.com/17/144417-050-53036450/Marco-Polo-travels-Asia-1271-1295.jpg) Marco Polo's travels to Asia (1271–95), immortalized in his _Travels of Marco Polo_. Marco, his father, and his uncle set out from Venice in 1271 and reached China in 1275. The Polos spent a total of 17 years in China. (more)

Polo’s way was paved by the pioneering efforts of his ancestors, especially his father, Niccolò, and his uncle, Maffeo. The family had [traded](https://www.britannica.com/money/international-trade) with the [Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/Middle-East) for a long time, acquiring considerable wealth and [prestige](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/prestige). Although it is uncertain if the Polos were of the nobility, the matter was of little importance in [Venice](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venice), a city of republican and mercantile traditions.

The family appears to have been shrewd, alert, and courageous; about 1260 they foresaw a political change in [Constantinople](https://www.britannica.com/place/Istanbul) (e.g., the overthrow of the [Crusaders](https://www.britannica.com/event/Crusades) who had ruled since 1204 by [Michael VIII Palaeologus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Michael-VIII-Palaeologus) in 1261), liquidated their property there, invested their capital in jewels, and set off for the [Volga River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Volga-River), where [Berke Khan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Berke), [sovereign](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/sovereign) of the western territories in the [Mongol](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yuan-dynasty) Empire, held court at Sarai or [Bulgar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bolgary). The Polos apparently managed their affairs well at Berke’s court, where they doubled their assets. When political events prevented their return to Venice, they traveled eastward to [Bukhara](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bukhara) (Bokhara) and ended their journey in 1265, probably at the grand khan’s summer residence, [Shangdu](https://www.britannica.com/place/Shangdu) (immortalized as [Xanadu](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Xanadu) by English poet [Samuel Taylor Coleridge](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Samuel-Taylor-Coleridge)). Establishing friendly relations with the great [Kublai Khan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kublai-Khan), they eventually returned to Europe as his ambassadors, carrying letters asking the pope to send Kublai 100 intelligent men “acquainted with the Seven Arts”; they also [bore](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/bore) gifts and were asked to bring back oil from the lamp burning at the [Holy Sepulchre](https://www.britannica.com/place/Holy-Sepulchre) in [Jerusalem](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jerusalem).

## Polo’s journey to Asia

[![Marco Polo](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/3573-004-A4688126/Marco-Polo-Tatar-attire.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/3573-004-A4688126/Marco-Polo-Tatar-attire.jpg)

[Marco Polo](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/3573-004-A4688126/Marco-Polo-Tatar-attire.jpg) Marco Polo in Tatar attire.(more)

Little is known about Marco’s early years except that he probably grew up in [Venice](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venice). He was age 15 or 16 when his father and uncle returned to meet him and learned that the pope, [Clement IV](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Clement-IV), had recently died. [Niccolò](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Niccolo-Polo) and [Maffeo](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Maffeo-Polo) remained in Venice anticipating the election of a new pope, but in 1271, after two years of waiting, they departed with Marco for the Mongol court. In [Acre](https://www.britannica.com/place/Acre-Israel) (now in Israel) the [papal legate](https://www.britannica.com/topic/legate-Roman-Catholicism), Teobaldo of Piacenza, gave them letters for the Mongol emperor. The Polos had been on the road for only a few days when they heard that their friend Teobaldo had been elected pope as [Gregory X](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Blessed-Gregory-X). Returning to Acre, they were given proper [credentials](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/credentials), and two friars were assigned to accompany them, though they abandoned the Polos shortly after the expedition resumed.

From Acre the travelers proceeded to Ayas (“Laiazzo” in Marco’s writings, now Yumurtalik, on the Gulf of İskenderun, also called the Gulf of Alexandretta, in southeastern Turkey). During the early part of 1272, they probably passed through [Erzurum](https://www.britannica.com/place/Erzurum), in what is now eastern Turkey, and [Tabrīz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tabriz), in what is now northern [Iran](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iran), later crossing inhospitable deserts infested with brigands before reaching [Hormuz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hormuz) on the [Persian Gulf](https://www.britannica.com/place/Persian-Gulf). There the Polos decided not to risk a sea passage to India and beyond but to proceed overland to the Mongol capital.

They next traveled through deserts of “surpassing aridity” toward the [Khorasan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Khorasan-historical-region-Asia) region in what is now eastern Iran. Turning gradually to the northeast, they reached more hospitable lands; Badakhshān (“Balascian”), in [Afghanistan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Afghanistan), in particular, pleased the travelers. Marco suggests that they remained there for a year; detained, perhaps, by illness (possibly [malaria](https://www.britannica.com/science/malaria)) that was cured by the [benign](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/benign) climate of the district. It is also believed that Marco visited territories to the south (other parts of Afghanistan, [Kafiristan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nurestan) in the [Hindu Kush](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hindu-Kush), [Chitral](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chitral) in what is now Pakistan, and perhaps [Kashmir](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kashmir-region-Indian-subcontinent)) during this period. It is, however, difficult to establish which districts he [traversed](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/traversed) and which he may have described from information gathered en route.

[![Marco Polo](https://cdn.britannica.com/07/115207-050-AED27638/Marco-Polo-caravan-illustration-collection-Catalan-Atlas.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/07/115207-050-AED27638/Marco-Polo-caravan-illustration-collection-Catalan-Atlas.jpg)

[Marco Polo](https://cdn.britannica.com/07/115207-050-AED27638/Marco-Polo-caravan-illustration-collection-Catalan-Atlas.jpg) Marco Polo traveling in a caravan, illustration from the _Catalan Atlas_ (1375), in the collection of the Bibliothèque Nationale, Paris, France.(more)

Leaving Badakhshān, the Polos proceeded toward the [Pamirs](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pamirs), but the route they followed to cross these Central Asian highlands remains uncertain. Descending on the northeastern side of the chain, they reached Kashi (“Cascar”) in what is now the [Uygur Autonomous Region of Xinjiang](https://www.britannica.com/place/Xinjiang), China. By this point the Polos were on the main [Silk Road](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Silk-Road-trade-route), and they probably followed along the oases to the south and east of the [Takla Makan Desert](https://www.britannica.com/place/Takla-Makan-Desert)— [Yarkant](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yarkand) (“Yarcan”), [Hotan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hotan) (“Cotan”), Che’erchen (“Ciarcian”), and [Lop Nur](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lop-Nur) (Lop Lake). These stepping-stones led to Shazhou (“Saciu”) on the borders of China, a place now called [Dunhuang](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dunhuang).

Before reaching Shazhou, the Polos had traveled primarily among Muslim peoples, though they also [encountered](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/encountered) [Nestorian Christians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nestorianism), [Buddhists](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism), [Manichaeans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Manichaeism), and [Zoroastrians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zoroastrianism). In the vast province of [Gansu](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gansu) (called “Tangut” by Marco), an entirely different civilization—mainly Buddhist in religion but partly Chinese in culture—prevailed. The travelers probably stopped in [Suzhou](https://www.britannica.com/place/Suzhou) (“Sukchu”; now Jiuquan) and [Ganzhou](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ganzhou) (“Campiciu”; now Zhangye) before entering the [Ningxia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ningxia) area. It is not clear whether they reached the Mongol summer capital of Shangdu (“Ciandu”) directly or after a detour; in any event, sometime in 1275 (1274, according to the research of Japanese scholar Matsuo Otagi) the Polos were again at the Mongol court, presenting the sacred oil from Jerusalem and the papal letters to their [patron](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/patron), Kublai Khan.

# Sojourn in China of Marco Polo

![Yuan (Mongol) Empire c. 1300](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/64939-050-87C5101B/Yuan-empire-extent-Kublai-Khan.jpg)

[Yuan (Mongol) Empire c. 1300](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/64939-050-87C5101B/Yuan-empire-extent-Kublai-Khan.jpg) The Yuan (Mongol) Empire (c. 1300), showing the extent reached under Kublai Khan.(more)

For the next 16 or 17 years the Polos lived in the emperor’s dominions, which included, among other places, Cathay (now [North China](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-China)) and Mangi, or “Manzi” (now South China). They may have moved with the court from Shangdu, to the winter residence, Dadu, or “Taidu” (modern [Beijing](https://www.britannica.com/place/Beijing)).

Unfortunately, because Marco’s book _Il milione_ is only incidentally a biography and autobiography, it is exceedingly difficult to [ascertain](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ascertain) where the Polos went and what they did during these years. Nevertheless, it is well known that many foreigners were in the employ of the state, since the Mongol rulers did not trust their Chinese subjects; so it would have been natural for the Polos to fit in most honourably and successfully with this motley society.

The extent of their success and the specific roles they filled, however, remains an open question. The elder Polos were probably employed in some technical capacity. Once and very abruptly, a glimpse in _Il milione_ is obtained of them acting as military advisers during the [siege](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/siege) of “Saianfu” (formerly [Xiangyang](https://www.britannica.com/place/Xiangyang), now Xiangfan), a city that was finally taken, according to Marco, thanks to some “great [mangonels](https://www.britannica.com/technology/onager-weapon)” (missile-throwing engines) built according to the Polos’ specifications. The whole episode is dubious, however.

Marco was about age 20 when he reached [Cathay](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cathay-medieval-region-China). Although he knew little or no Chinese, he did speak some of the many languages then used in East Asia—most probably [Turkish](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Turkish-language) (in its Coman dialect) as spoken among the Mongols, Arabized Persian, [Uighur](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Uyghur) (Uygur), and perhaps Mongol. He was noticed very favourably by Kublai, who took great delight in hearing of strange countries and repeatedly sent him on fact-finding missions to distant parts of the empire. One such journey took Polo to [Yunnan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yunnan) in southwestern China and perhaps as far as Tagaung in [Myanmar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Myanmar) (Burma); on another occasion he visited southeastern China, later enthusiastically describing the city of “Quinsay” (now [Hangzhou](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hangzhou)) and the populous regions recently conquered by the Mongols. Apart from the missions he undertook for the emperor, Polo may have held other administrative responsibilities, including inspection of the customs duties and [revenues](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/revenues) collected from the trade in salt and other commodities. According to some versions of _Il milione_, he governed the city of [Yangzhou](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yangzhou) for three years sometime between 1282 and 1287; but this assertion seems hardly credible and hinges entirely on the interpretation of one word. There is, however, ample evidence to show that Polo considered himself an adoptive son of his new country.

## The return to Venice

Sometime around 1292 (1290 according to Otagi), a Mongol princess was to be sent to Persia to become the consort of [Arghun Khan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Arghun), and the Polos offered to accompany her. Marco wrote that Kublai had been unwilling to let them go but finally granted permission. They were [eager](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/eager) to leave, in part, because Kublai was nearly 80, and his death (and the consequent change in regime) might have been dangerous for a small group of isolated foreigners. Naturally, they also longed to see their native [Venice](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venice) and their families again.

The princess, with some 600 courtiers and sailors, and the Polos boarded 14 ships, which left the port of [Quanzhou](https://www.britannica.com/place/Quanzhou) (“Zaiton”) and sailed southward. The fleet stopped briefly at Champa (“Ciamba,” modern [Vietnam](https://www.britannica.com/place/Vietnam)) as well as a number of islands and the [Malay Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Malay-Peninsula) before settling for five months on the island of [Sumatra](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sumatra) (“Lesser Giaua”) to avoid monsoon storms. There Polo was much impressed by the fact that the [North Star](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-North-Star-American-newspaper) appeared to have dipped below the horizon. The [fleet](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/fleet) then passed near the [Nicobar Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nicobar-Islands) (“Necuveran”), touched land again in [Sri Lanka](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sri-Lanka), or Ceylon (“Seilan”), followed the west coast of India and the southern reaches of Persia, and finally anchored at [Hormuz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hormuz). The expedition then proceeded to [Khorāsān](https://www.britannica.com/place/Khorasan-historical-region-Asia), handing over the princess not to Arghun, who had died, but to his son [Maḥmūd Ghāzān](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mahmud-Ghazan).

The Polos eventually departed for Europe, but their movements at this point are unclear; possibly they stayed for a few months in [Tabrīz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tabriz). Unfortunately, as soon as they left the Mongol dominions and set foot in a Christian country, at [Trebizond](https://www.britannica.com/place/Trabzon-Turkey) in what is now Turkey, they were robbed of most of their hard-won earnings. After further [delays](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/delays), they reached Constantinople and finally Venice (1295). The story of their dramatic recognition by relatives and neighbours who had thought them long since dead is a part of Polo lore that is well known.

## Compilation of _[Il milione](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Travels-of-Marco-Polo)_

Soon after his return to Venice, Polo was taken prisoner by the [Genoese](https://www.britannica.com/place/Genoa-Italy)—great rivals of the Venetians at sea—during a skirmish or battle in the Mediterranean. He was then imprisoned in Genoa, where he had a felicitous encounter with a prisoner from [Pisa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pisa-Italy), Rustichello (or Rusticiano), a fairly well-known writer of romances and a specialist in [chivalry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/chivalry) and its lore, then a fashionable subject. Polo may have intended to write about his 25 years in Asia but possibly did not feel sufficiently comfortable in either Venetian or Franco-Italian; however, with Rustichello at hand, the traveler began dictating his [tale](https://www.britannica.com/art/travel-literature). The language employed was Franco-Italian—a strange composite tongue fashionable during the 13th and 14th centuries.

Polo was soon freed and returned to Venice. The remainder of his life can be reconstructed, in part, through the testimony of legal documents. He seems to have led a quiet existence, managing a not too [conspicuous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conspicuous) fortune and dying at age 70. His will set free a “Tatar slave” who may possibly have followed him from [East Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/East-Asia). A famous story relates how Polo was asked on his deathbed to retract the “fables” he had invented in his book; his answer was that he told barely half of what he actually saw.

## Nature and content of _Il milione_

[![Marco Polo](https://cdn.britannica.com/23/38523-004-1062ABF8/Marco-Polo-Frontispiece-travels-edition.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/23/38523-004-1062ABF8/Marco-Polo-Frontispiece-travels-edition.jpg)

[Marco Polo](https://cdn.britannica.com/23/38523-004-1062ABF8/Marco-Polo-Frontispiece-travels-edition.jpg) Frontispiece depicting Marco Polo, from an early printed edition (in German) of his travels.(more)

An instant success—“In a few months it spread throughout Italy,” wrote [Giovanni Battista Ramusio](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Giovanni-Battista-Ramusio), the 16th-century Italian geographer— _Il milione_ was apparently conceived as a vast cosmography based on firsthand experience. The book was not intended to be a collection of personal recollections, which leaves Polo’s own personality somewhat [elusive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/elusive), but _Divisament dou monde_ (“Description of the World”), as it was originally titled, was to be the book to end all books on Asia. Nonetheless, details concerning travel, distances covered, and seasons are rarely stated; the panorama is observed from an impersonal distance with a powerful wide-angle lens. In _Il milione_ Polo often branches off into descriptions of places probably visited not by himself but by his relatives or people he knew. Typical [digressions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/digressions) are those on Mesopotamia, the [Assassins](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nizari-Ismailiyyah) and their castles, [Samarkand](https://www.britannica.com/place/Samarkand-Uzbekistan), [Siberia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Siberia), [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan), [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), [Ethiopia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ethiopia), and [Madagascar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madagascar). _Il milione_ is better understood not as biography but as part of the [vernacular didactic literature](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vernacular), of which the Middle Ages offer many examples.

The work is marked by uncertainty and controversy, however. The origin of the popular title, _Il milione_, for example, is not quite clear. Although it most likely comes from Polo’s nickname, _Il Milione_, from his tendency to describe the millions of things he saw in the [Mongol empire](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mongol-empire), it may have been related to the idea of a “tall story,” or from a nickname running in the family, possibly traceable to a corruption of Aemilione (“Big Emil”). The history of the text itself is [characterized](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characterized) by similar uncertainty. There is no authentic original manuscript, and even if there were, it would likely not represent what Polo dictated since Rustichello asserted his own personality and familiar phraseology, especially in the standardized description of battles. Polo also seems to have made emendations himself on various copies of the work during the last 20 years or so of his life. Some editors—for instance, the [friar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/friar) Pipino, who made a good Latin translation of the original—found many of Polo’s descriptions or interpretations impious or dangerously near to [heresy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/heresy) and therefore heavily [bowdlerized](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thomas-Bowdler) the text. Furthermore, since all this happened long before the invention of [printing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/printing-publishing), professional scribes or amateurs made dozens of copies of the book, as well as free translations and adaptations—often adding to or subtracting from the text with little or no respect for authenticity. There were many unfamiliar names that rarely passed unchanged from one copy to another. Consequently, there are some 140 different manuscript versions of the text in three manuscript groups, in a dozen different languages and dialects—an immensely complex and controversial body of material representing one of the most [obdurate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/obdurate) philological problems inherited from the Middle Ages.

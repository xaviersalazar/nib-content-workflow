### Why is Nellie Bly important?

Nellie Bly was the most famous American woman reporter of the 19th century. Her investigation of conditions at an insane asylum sparked outrage, legal action, and improvements of the treatment of the mentally ill. Her trip around the world in 72 days brought her even further fame.

### Was Nellie Bly married?

Nellie Bly married manufacturer Robert Seaman in 1895. Seaman died in 1904, and Bly took over his firm, the Iron Clad Manufacturing Company. After the company suffered losses from embezzlement, Bly returned to journalism and reported from Europe during [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I).

### Why did Nellie Bly change her name?

When Elizabeth Cochran began in journalism in 1885, it was considered inappropriate for a woman to write under her own name. Cochran’s editor chose the name “Nelly Bly” from a [Stephen Foster](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Stephen-Foster) song. However, he also misspelled the name, and she became “Nellie Bly.”

**Nellie Bly** (born May 5, 1864, Cochran’s Mills, [Pennsylvania](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pennsylvania-state), U.S.—died January 27, 1922, [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state), New York) was an American journalist whose around-the-world race against a fictional record brought her world renown. Bly was also known for a number of exposés. Most notably, in 1887 she went undercover as a patient at a mental institution in [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City) and subsequently wrote about the [deplorable](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/deplorable) conditions.

Elizabeth Cochran (she later added a final “ _e_” to Cochran) received scant formal schooling. She began her career in 1885 in her native Pennsylvania as a reporter for the _Pittsburgh Dispatch_, to which she had sent an angry letter to the editor in response to an article the newspaper had printed entitled “What Girls Are Good For” (not much, according to the article). The editor was so impressed with her writing that he gave her a job.

It was for the _Dispatch_ that she began using the pen name “Nellie Bly,” borrowed from a popular [Stephen Foster](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Stephen-Foster) song. Her first articles, on conditions among working girls in [Pittsburgh](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pittsburgh), slum life, and other similar topics, marked her as a reporter of [ingenuity](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/ingenuity) and concern. At a time when a woman’s contribution to a newspaper was generally confined to the “women’s pages,” Cochrane was given a rare opportunity to report on wider issues. In 1886–87 she traveled for several months through Mexico, sending back reports on official corruption and the condition of the poor. Her sharply critical articles angered Mexican officials and caused her expulsion from the country. The articles were subsequently collected in _Six Months in Mexico_ (1888).

In 1887 Cochrane left Pittsburgh for New York City and went to work for [Joseph Pulitzer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Pulitzer)’s [_New York World_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/New-York-World). One of her first undertakings for that paper was to get herself committed to the [mental health](https://www.britannica.com/science/mental-health) asylum on Blackwell’s (now Roosevelt) Island by feigning insanity. Her exposé of conditions among the patients, published in the _World_ and later collected in _Ten Days in a Mad House_ (1887), precipitated a grand-jury investigation of the asylum and helped bring about needed improvements in patient care. Similar reportorial [gambits](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/gambits) took her into sweatshops, jails, and the legislature (where she exposed bribery in the lobbyist system). She was far and away the best-known woman journalist of her day.

The high point of Cochrane’s career at the _World_ began on November 14, 1889, when she sailed from New York to beat the record of [Phileas Fogg](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Phileas-Fogg), hero of [Jules Verne](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jules-Verne)’s romance _[Around the World in Eighty Days](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Around-the-World-in-Eighty-Days-by-Verne)_. The _World_ built up the story by running daily articles and a guessing contest in which whoever came nearest to naming Cochrane’s time in circling the globe would get a trip to Europe. There were nearly one million entries in the contest. Cochrane rode on ships and trains, in rickshaws and sampans, on horses and burros. On the final lap of her journey, the _World_ transported her from San Francisco to New York by special train; she was greeted everywhere by brass bands, fireworks, and like [panoply](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/panoply). Her time was 72 days 6 hours 11 minutes 14 seconds. The stunt made her famous. _Nellie Bly’s Book: Around the World in Seventy-two Days_ (1890) was a great popular success, and the name Nellie Bly became a synonym for a female star reporter.

She married millionaire Robert Seaman in 1895, but after his death she suffered financial reverses, and she returned to newspaper work on the _New York Journal_ in 1920.

### Where does the term muckraker come from?

The name _muckraker_ was pejorative when used by U.S. President [Theodore Roosevelt](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Theodore-Roosevelt) in a speech on April 14, 1906; he borrowed a passage from [John Bunyan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Bunyan)’s _[The Pilgrim’s Progress](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Pilgrims-Progress)_ that referred to “the Man with the Muckrake…who could look no way but downward.” But _muckraker_ came to take on favourable connotations of social concern and courageous exposition.

### What does the term muckraker mean?

A muckraker was any of a group of American writers identified with pre- [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I) reform and exposé writing. The muckrakers provided detailed, accurate [journalistic accounts](https://www.britannica.com/topic/journalism) of the political and economic corruption and social hardships caused by the power of big business in a rapidly [industrializing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/industrialization) [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States).

### Who was the first muckraker?

[Lincoln Steffens](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Lincoln-Steffens), [Ray Stannard Baker](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ray-Stannard-Baker), and [Ida M. McClure, [Frank A. Munsey](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Frank-Andrew-Munsey), and Peter F. Collier.

**muckraker**, any of a group of American writers identified with pre- [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I) reform and exposé [literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/literature). The intense public interest aroused by articles critical of political corruption, industrial [monopolies](https://www.britannica.com/money/monopoly-economics), and fraudulent business practices rallied journalists, novelists, and reformers of all sorts to sharpen their [criticism](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criticism) of American society. [Charles Edward Russell](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Edward-Russell) led the reform writers with exposés ranging from _The Greatest Trust in the World_ (1905) to _The Uprising of the Many_ (1907), the latter of which reported on methods being tried to extend [democracy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/democracy) in other countries. [Lincoln Steffens](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Lincoln-Steffens) wrote about corrupt city and state politics in _[The Shame of the Cities](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Shame-of-the-Cities)_ (1904). Brand Whitlock, who wrote _The Turn of the Balance_ (1907), a novel opposing [capital punishment](https://www.britannica.com/topic/capital-punishment), was also a reform mayor of [Toledo](https://www.britannica.com/place/Toledo-Ohio), [Ohio](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ohio-state). Thomas W. Lawson, a Boston financier, provided in “Frenzied Finance” ( _Everybody’s_, 1904–05) a major exposé of [stock-market](https://www.britannica.com/money/stock-exchange-finance) abuses and insurance [fraud](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fraud). [Tarbell’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ida-Tarbell) _The History of the Standard Oil Company_ (1904) exposed the corrupt practices used to form a great industrial monopoly. [Edwin Markham](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edwin-Markham)’s _Children in Bondage_ was a major attack on [child labour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/child-labour). [Upton Sinclair](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Upton-Sinclair)’s novel _[The Jungle](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Jungle-novel-by-Sinclair)_ (1906) and [Samuel Hopkins Adams](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Samuel-Hopkins-Adams)’s _The Great American Fraud_ (1906), [combined](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/combined) with the work of Harvey W. Wiley and U.S. Sen. [Albert J. Beveridge](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Albert-J-Beveridge), brought about passage of the [Meat Inspection Act](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Meat-Inspection-Act) and the [Pure Food and Drug Act](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pure-Food-and-Drug-Act). David Graham Phillips’s series “The Treason of the Senate” ( _[Cosmopolitan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Cosmopolitan-magazine),_ 1906), which inspired Pres. Roosevelt’s speech in 1906, was influential in leading to the passage of the [Seventeenth Amendment](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Seventeenth-Amendment) to the [Constitution](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Constitution-of-the-United-States-of-America), which provided for popular senatorial elections. Muckraking as a movement largely disappeared between 1910 and 1912.

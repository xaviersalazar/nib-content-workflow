### What was Zheng He best known for?

Zheng He was the best known of the [Yongle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Yongle) emperor’s diplomatic agents. His voyages had the effect of extending [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China)’s political sway over maritime [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia) for half a century. In their wake, Chinese [emigration](https://www.britannica.com/topic/emigration) increased, resulting in Chinese colonization in [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia) and the accompanying tributary trade, which lasted until the 19th century.

### Where was Zheng He raised?

He was raised in [Yunnan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yunnan), China. In 1381 Yunnan was reconquered by [Ming dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ming-dynasty-Chinese-history) forces, and he was captured, [castrated](https://www.britannica.com/topic/castration), and sent into the army. By 1390, under the command of the prince of Yan, he had distinguished himself as a junior officer—skilled in [war](https://www.britannica.com/topic/war) and [diplomacy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/diplomacy) and having made influential friends at court.

**Zheng He** (born _c._ 1371, Kunyang, near [Kunming](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kunming), Yunnan province, China—died 1433, Calicut \[now Kozhikode\], India) was an [admiral](https://www.britannica.com/topic/admiral) and diplomat who helped extend the [maritime](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/maritime) and commercial influence of [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) throughout the regions bordering the [Indian Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indian-Ocean). He commanded seven naval expeditions almost a century before the Portuguese reached [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India) by sailing around the southern tip of [Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Africa).

## Background and early years

Zheng He was from a [Hui](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hui-people) (Chinese Muslim) family. His father was a hajji, a Muslim who had made the [hajj](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hajj) (pilgrimage) to [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mecca). His family claimed descent from an early Mongol governor of [Yunnan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yunnan) province in southwestern China as well as from King Muḥammad of [Bukhara](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bukhara) (now in [Uzbekistan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Uzbekistan)). The [family name](https://www.britannica.com/topic/surname) Ma was derived from the Chinese rendition of Muḥammad.

In 1381, when he was about 10 years old, Yunnan, the last Mongol hold in China, was reconquered by Chinese forces led by generals of the [Ming dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ming-dynasty-Chinese-history), which had overthrown the [Yuan (Mongol) dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yuan-dynasty) in 1368. The young Ma Sanbao (later Ma He), as he was then known, was among the boys who were captured, castrated, and sent into the army as orderlies. By 1390, when those troops were placed under the command of the prince of Yan, Ma He had distinguished himself as a junior officer, skilled in war and [diplomacy](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/diplomacy). Ma also had made influential friends at court.

In 1400 the prince of Yan revolted against his nephew, the [Jianwen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jianwen) emperor, taking the throne in 1402 as the [Yongle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Yongle) emperor. Under the Yongle administration (1402–24), the war-devastated economy of China was soon restored. The Ming court then sought to display its naval power to bring the maritime states of South and [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia) in line.

For some 300 years the Chinese had been extending their power out to sea. An [extensive](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/extensive) seaborne commerce had developed to meet the taste of the Chinese for spices and aromatics and the need for raw industrial materials. Chinese travelers abroad, as well as Indian and Muslim visitors, widened the geographic horizon of the Chinese. Technological developments in shipbuilding and in the arts of seafaring reached new heights by the beginning of the Ming.

## Naval expeditions

Ma He quickly became a [eunuch](https://www.britannica.com/topic/eunuch) of great influence in the Yongle court. Soon after he ascended the throne, the emperor conferred on Ma the surname Zheng, and he was henceforth known as Zheng He. Zheng was then selected by the emperor to be commander in chief of what became a series of [missions](https://www.britannica.com/topic/non-European-exploration) to the “Western Oceans.” He first set sail in 1405, commanding 62 ships and 27,800 men. The [fleet](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/fleet) visited [Champa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Champa-ancient-kingdom-Indochina) (now in southern [Vietnam](https://www.britannica.com/place/Vietnam)), Siam ( [Thailand](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thailand)), Malacca ( [Melaka](https://www.britannica.com/place/Melaka-Malaysia)), and the island of [Java](https://www.britannica.com/place/Java-island-Indonesia) and then through the Indian Ocean to Calicut ( [Kozhikode](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kozhikode)) on the [Malabar Coast](https://www.britannica.com/place/Malabar-Coast) of India and Ceylon ( [Sri Lanka](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sri-Lanka)). Zheng He returned to China in 1407.

On his second voyage, in 1408–09, Zheng He again visited Calicut—stopping as well in Chochin ( [Kochi](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kochi-India)) along the coast to the south—but encountered treachery from King Alagonakkara of Ceylon. Zheng defeated Alagonakkara’s forces and took the king back to [Nanjing](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nanjing-China) as a captive. In October 1409 Zheng He set out on his third voyage. This time, going beyond the seaports of India, he sailed to [Hormuz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hormuz) on the [Persian Gulf](https://www.britannica.com/place/Persian-Gulf). On his return in 1411 he touched at Samudra, on the northern tip of Sumatra.

On his fourth [voyage](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/voyage) Zheng He left China in 1413. After stopping at the principal ports of Asia, he proceeded westward from India to Hormuz. A detachment of the fleet cruised southward down the coast of [Arabia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arabia-peninsula-Asia), visiting [Dhofar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dhofar) ( [Oman](https://www.britannica.com/place/Oman)) and [Aden](https://www.britannica.com/place/Aden) ( [Yemen](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yemen)). A Chinese mission visited Mecca and continued to [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt). The fleet visited towns along the east coast of Africa of what are now [Somalia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Somalia) and [Kenya](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kenya) and almost reached the [Mozambique Channel](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mozambique-Channel). On his return to China in 1415, Zheng He brought the envoys of more than 30 states of South and Southeast Asia to pay [homage](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tributary-system) to the Chinese emperor.

During Zheng He’s fifth voyage (1417–19), the Ming fleet revisited the Persian Gulf and the east coast of Africa. A sixth voyage was launched in 1421 to take home the foreign emissaries from China. Again he visited Southeast Asia, India, Arabia, and Africa. In 1424 the Yongle emperor died. In the shift of policy his successor, the Hongxi emperor, suspended naval expeditions abroad. Zheng He was appointed garrison commander in Nanjing, with the task of disbanding his troops.

Zheng He’s seventh and final voyage left China in the winter of 1431. He visited the states of Southeast Asia, the coast of India, the Persian Gulf, the [Red Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Red-Sea), and the east coast of Africa. Zheng died in Calicut in the spring of 1433, and the fleet returned to China that summer.

Zheng He was the best known of the Yongle emperor’s [diplomatic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/diplomatic) agents. Although some historians see no achievement in the naval expeditions other than flattering the emperor’s vanity, those missions did have the effect of extending China’s political sway over maritime Asia for half a century. Admittedly, they did not, like similar voyages of European merchant-adventurers, lead to the establishment of trading empires. Yet, in their wake, Chinese emigration increased, resulting in Chinese colonization in Southeast Asia and the accompanying tributary trade, which lasted until the 19th century.

### Who founded the Ming Dynasty?

The Ming dynasty was founded by Zhu Yuanzhang, who was of humble origins and later assumed the reign title of [Hongwu](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hongwu). The Ming became one of the most stable and one of the most autocratic of all Chinese dynasties.

### What were the two traditional forms of Ming painting?

The two main traditions in painting in the Ming period are “literati painting” (wenrenhua) of the Wu school and the “professional academics” (huayuanpai) associated with the Zhe school. But artists generally stressed independent creation, impressing their work with strong marks of their personal styles.

### What major types of decoration in ceramics emerged during the Ming Dynasty?

Three major types of decoration in ceramics emerged during the Ming dynasty. The monochromatic glazes, including celadon, red, green, and yellow, the underglaze copper-red and cobalt blue, and the overglaze, or enamel painting, sometimes combined with underglaze blue.

**Ming dynasty**, Chinese [dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dynasty) that lasted from 1368 to 1644 and provided an interval of native Chinese rule between eras of [Mongol](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mongol) and Manchu dominance, respectively. During the Ming period, [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) exerted immense cultural and political influence on [East Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/East-Asia) and the Turks to the west, as well as on [Vietnam](https://www.britannica.com/place/Vietnam) and [Myanmar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Myanmar) to the south.

## History

The Ming [dynasty](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynasty), which succeeded the [Yuan (Mongol) dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yuan-dynasty) (1206–1368), was founded by Zhu Yuanzhang. Zhu, who was of humble origins, later assumed the reign title of [Hongwu](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hongwu). The Ming became one of the most stable but also one of the most autocratic of all Chinese [dynasties](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynasties).

The basic governmental structure established by the Ming was continued by the subsequent [Qing (Manchu) dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Qing-dynasty) and lasted until the imperial institution was abolished in 1911/12. The [civil service](https://www.britannica.com/topic/civil-service) system was perfected during the Ming and then became stratified; almost all the top Ming officials entered the [bureaucracy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/bureaucracy) by passing a government examination. The Censorate (Yushitai), an office designed to investigate official misconduct and corruption, was made a separate organ of the government. Affairs in each province were handled by three agencies, each reporting to separate bureaus in the central government. The position of [prime minister](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prime-minister) was abolished. Instead, the emperor took over personal control of the government, ruling with the assistance of the especially appointed Neige, or Grand Secretariat.

Basically, the Ming incorporated the [Song dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Song-dynasty)’s policy of relying on the literati in managing state affairs. However, from the [Yongle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Yongle) emperor onward, the emperors relied increasingly on trusted [eunuchs](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/eunuchs) to contain the literati. Also introduced at that time was a system of punishment by flogging with a stick in court, which was designed to humiliate civil officials—while also making use of them to realize the emperor’s aim of maintaining practical control of the state in his own hands. By decree of the emperor, a vast spying service was organized under three special agencies.

[![Great Wall of China](https://cdn.britannica.com/05/162505-050-ECCD60F1/Portion-Ming-dynasty-Great-Wall-of-China.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/05/162505-050-ECCD60F1/Portion-Ming-dynasty-Great-Wall-of-China.jpg)

[Great Wall of China](https://cdn.britannica.com/05/162505-050-ECCD60F1/Portion-Ming-dynasty-Great-Wall-of-China.jpg) Portion of the Great Wall of China built during the Ming dynasty.(more)

Struggles with peoples of various nationalities continued throughout the Ming period. Clashes with Mongols were nearly incessant. During the first decades of the dynasty, the Mongols were driven north to Outer Mongolia (present-day [Mongolia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mongolia)), but the Ming could not claim a [decisive](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/decisive) victory. From then onward the Ming were generally able to maintain their northern border, though by the later stages of the dynasty it in effect only reached the line of the [Great Wall](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Great-Wall-of-China). On the northeast, the Juchen (Chinese: Nüzhen, or Ruzhen), who rose in the northeast around the end of the 16th century, pressed the Ming army to withdraw successively southward, and eventually the Ming made the east end of the Great Wall their last line of defense. The Ming devoted considerable resources toward maintaining and strengthening the wall, especially near [Beijing](https://www.britannica.com/place/Beijing), the dynasty’s capital.

In early Ming times, China’s [domain](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/domain) extended considerably in the south as a result of its successful invasion of northern Vietnam. But the brief occupation of Vietnam was met with determined local guerrilla resistance, and the Ming government quickly decided to restore the boundary to its original line. It never again attempted to push southward. During the 15th century the government had organized large tribute-collecting flotillas commanded by [Zheng He](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Zheng-He) to extend China’s influence. Also during the Ming, [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) became more aggressive. In the 15th century Japanese raiders teamed up with Chinese pirates to make coastal raids in Chinese waters, which were of a relatively small scale but were still highly disruptive to Chinese coastal cities. The Ming government eventually tried to stop Japan’s attempt to control Korea, which became a long and costly campaign.

The Ming government was gradually weakened by factionalism between civil officials, interference by palace eunuchs, the burdens of a growing population, and a succession of weak and inattentive emperors. In 1644 a rebel leader, [Li Zicheng](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Li-Zicheng), captured Beijing, and the local Ming military commander requested aid from the [Manchu](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Manchu) tribal peoples who had been [encroaching](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encroaching) on China’s northern borders. The Manchu drove out Li Zicheng and then remained, establishing the Qing dynasty.

## Cultural achievements

[![Ming tombs: Hall of Eminent Favour](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/178646-050-980E8490/Hall-of-Eminent-Favour-Ming-tombs-Beijing.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/178646-050-980E8490/Hall-of-Eminent-Favour-Ming-tombs-Beijing.jpg)

[Ming tombs: Hall of Eminent Favour](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/178646-050-980E8490/Hall-of-Eminent-Favour-Ming-tombs-Beijing.jpg) The Hall of Eminent Favour at the Ming tombs complex, near Beijing.(more)

Despite the many foreign contacts made during the Ming period, cultural developments were characterized by a generally [conservative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conservative) and inward-looking attitude. Ming architecture is largely undistinguished with the [Forbidden City](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Forbidden-City), a palace complex built in Beijing in the 15th century by the Yongle emperor (and subsequently enlarged and rebuilt), its main representative. The best Ming sculpture is found not in large statues but in small ornamental carvings of jade, ivory, wood, and [porcelain](https://www.britannica.com/art/porcelain). Although a high level of workmanship is [manifest](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifest) in Ming decorative arts such as [cloisonné](https://www.britannica.com/art/cloisonne), enamelware, bronze, [lacquerwork](https://www.britannica.com/art/lacquerwork), and furniture, the major achievements in art were in painting and pottery.

[![A Tall Pine and Daoist Immortal](https://cdn.britannica.com/72/72-050-679C6040/Daoist-Immortal-Tall-Pine-ink-colour-self-portrait-1635.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/72/72-050-679C6040/Daoist-Immortal-Tall-Pine-ink-colour-self-portrait-1635.jpg)

[_A Tall Pine and Daoist Immortal_](https://cdn.britannica.com/72/72-050-679C6040/Daoist-Immortal-Tall-Pine-ink-colour-self-portrait-1635.jpg) _A Tall Pine and Daoist Immortal_, ink and colour on silk hanging scroll with self-portrait (bottom centre) by Chen Hongshou, 1635, Ming dynasty; in the National Palace Museum, Taipei, Taiwan.(more)

While there were two main traditions in painting in the Ming period, that of “literati painting” ( [_wenrenhua_](https://www.britannica.com/art/wenrenhua)) of the [Wu school](https://www.britannica.com/art/Wu-school) and that of the “professional academics” ( _huayuanpai_) associated with the [Zhe school](https://www.britannica.com/art/Zhe-school), artists generally stressed independent creation, impressing their work with strong marks of their personal styles.

There were many new developments in ceramics, along with the continuation of established traditions. Three major types of decoration emerged: monochromatic glazes, including celadon, red, green, and yellow; underglaze copper red and cobalt blue; and overglaze, or enamel painting, sometimes [combined](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/combined) with underglaze blue. The latter, often called “blue and white,” was imitated in Vietnam, Japan, and, from the 17th century, in Europe. Much of this porcelain was produced in the huge factory at [Jingdezhen](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jingdezhen) in present-day [Jiangsu](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jiangsu) province. One of the period’s most-influential wares was the [stoneware](https://www.britannica.com/art/stoneware) of Yixing in Jiangsu province, which was exported in the 17th century to the West, where it was known as boccaro ware and imitated by such factories as [Meissen](https://www.britannica.com/place/Meissen-Germany).

The Ming regime restored the former literary examinations for public office, which pleased the literary world, dominated by Southerners. In their own writing the Ming sought a return to classical prose and poetry styles and, as a result, produced writings that were imitative and generally of little consequence. Writers of [vernacular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vernacular) literature, however, made real contributions, especially in novels and drama. Chinese traditional drama originating in the Song dynasty had been banned by the Mongols but survived underground in the South, and in the Ming era it was restored. This was _chuanqi_, a form of [musical theatre](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical) with numerous scenes and contemporary plots. What emerged was [_kunqu_](https://www.britannica.com/art/kunqu) style, less [bombastic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/bombastic) in song and accompaniment than other popular theatre. Under the Ming it enjoyed great popularity, indeed outlasting the dynasty by a century or more. It was adapted into a full-length opera form, which, although still performed today, was gradually replaced in popularity by [_jingxi_](https://www.britannica.com/art/jingxi) (Peking opera) during the Qing dynasty.

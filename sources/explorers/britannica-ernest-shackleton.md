Top Questions


### Who was Ernest Shackleton?

Ernest Henry Shackleton was an Anglo-Irish explorer of [Antarctica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctica) who attempted to reach the [South Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Pole).

### Where did Ernest Shackleton attend school?

Ernest Henry Shackleton attended Dulwich College in London from 1887 until 1890.

### What is Ernest Shackleton best known for?

Ernest Henry Shackleton is best known as a polar explorer who was associated with four expeditions exploring [Antarctica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctica), particularly the Trans-Antarctic ( _Endurance_) Expedition (1914–16) that he led, which, although unsuccessful, became famous as a tale of remarkable perseverance and survival.

### Where was Ernest Shackleton buried?

Ernest Henry Shackleton was buried on the island of [South Georgia](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Georgia) in the South [Atlantic Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean).

[![Ernest Shackleton](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/145278-050-EDFFB8F5/Ernest-Shackleton.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/145278-050-EDFFB8F5/Ernest-Shackleton.jpg)

[Ernest Shackleton](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/145278-050-EDFFB8F5/Ernest-Shackleton.jpg) Anglo-Irish explorer Ernest Shackleton.(more)

**Ernest Shackleton** (born February 15, 1874, Kilkea, County [Kildare](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kildare-county-Ireland), Ireland—died January 5, 1922, Grytviken, South Georgia) was an Anglo-Irish [Antarctic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctica) explorer who attempted to reach the [South Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Pole).

[![Ernest Shackleton](https://cdn.britannica.com/16/9416-004-AAB7B0AC/Ernest-H-Shackleton-1909.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/16/9416-004-AAB7B0AC/Ernest-H-Shackleton-1909.jpg)

[Ernest Shackleton](https://cdn.britannica.com/16/9416-004-AAB7B0AC/Ernest-H-Shackleton-1909.jpg) Ernest Shackleton, 1909.(more)

Educated at Dulwich College (1887–90) in London, Shackleton entered the [mercantile](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/mercantile) marine service in 1890 and became a sublieutenant in the Royal Naval Reserve in 1901. He joined Capt. [Robert Falcon Scott’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Falcon-Scott) British National Antarctic ( _Discovery_) Expedition (1901–04) as third lieutenant and took part, with Scott and Edward Wilson, in the sledge journey over the [Ross Ice Shelf](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ross-Ice-Shelf) when latitude 82°16′33″ S was reached. His health suffered, and he was removed from duty and sent home on the supply ship _Morning_ in March 1903.

## The _Nimrod_ expedition

[![Ernest Shackleton's South Pole expedition](https://cdn.britannica.com/91/18491-004-E4B0E720/Ernest-H-Shackleton-members-planting-expedition-flag.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/91/18491-004-E4B0E720/Ernest-H-Shackleton-members-planting-expedition-flag.jpg)

[Ernest Shackleton's South Pole expedition](https://cdn.britannica.com/91/18491-004-E4B0E720/Ernest-H-Shackleton-members-planting-expedition-flag.jpg) Ernest Shackleton and two members of his expedition after the planting of the British flag within 97 nautical miles (112 statute miles or 180 km) of the South Pole.(more)

[Ernest Shackleton: _My South Polar Expedition_](https://cdn.britannica.com/93/99893-005-D9F3B0C7/South-Polar-Expedition-Ernest-Shackleton-March-30-1910.mp3) _My South Polar Expedition_, an audio recording of Ernest Shackleton, recorded March 30, 1910.(more)

In January 1908 he returned to [Antarctica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctica) as leader of the [British Antarctic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/British-Antarctic-Nimrod-Expedition) ( _Nimrod_) Expedition (1907–09). The expedition, prevented by ice from reaching the intended base site in [Edward VII](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edward-VII) Peninsula, wintered on [Ross Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ross-Island), [McMurdo Sound](https://www.britannica.com/place/McMurdo-Sound). On March 5, 1908, six of Shackleton’s crewmates, including Australian geologist [Douglas Mawson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Douglas-Mawson), left their base camp (a hut) at Cape Royds to become the first people to [summit](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/summit) [Mount Erebus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Erebus), a prominent volcanic peak some 15 miles (24 km) distant. Five of the six would climb to the crater rim by March 10. In October a sledging party of four, which was led by Shackleton, began a trek that came within 97 nautical miles (112 statute miles or 180 km) of the geographic [South Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Pole) by January 1909 before dwindling [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) supplies forced them to turn back. A third party, led by Australian geologist [T.W. Edgeworth David](https://www.britannica.com/biography/T-W-Edgeworth-David), reached the area of the south [magnetic pole](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-pole) on January 16, 1909.

Reflected in Aldrin's faceplate is the Lunar Module and astronaut Neil Armstrong, who took the picture.](https://cdn.britannica.com/24/71324-131-CA22C4BA/Edwin-Aldrin-Moon-July-20-1969.jpg)\\

Shackleton’s sledging party returned to the base camp in late February 1909, but they discovered that the _Nimrod_ had set sail some two days earlier. Shackleton and his party set [fire](https://www.britannica.com/science/fire-combustion) to the camp to signal the ship, which received the signal and returned to the camp a few days later, successfully retrieving them. In addition to the aforementioned achievements, the _Nimrod_ [expedition](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/expedition) allowed the [Victoria Land](https://www.britannica.com/place/Victoria-Land) plateau to be claimed by the British crown. On his return to England, Shackleton was knighted and was made a Commander of the [Royal Victorian Order](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Royal-Victorian-Order).

## The _Endurance_ and the rescue of its crew

[Ernest Shackleton's Antarctic voyage](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/194777-050-06133154/Ernest-Shackleton-voyage-entry-pack-ice-Irish.jpg) The voyage of Anglo-Irish explorer Ernest Shackleton, showing the entry of the _Endurance_ into Antarctic pack ice, its sinking, and Shackleton's journey to South Georgia.(more)

[![Endurance](https://cdn.britannica.com/47/231747-050-692CD98F/discovery-Endurance-shipwreck-stern.jpg?w=300)\\
3 of 3](https://cdn.britannica.com/47/231747-050-692CD98F/discovery-Endurance-shipwreck-stern.jpg)

[_Endurance_](https://cdn.britannica.com/47/231747-050-692CD98F/discovery-Endurance-shipwreck-stern.jpg) The wreck of the _Endurance_ was found in 2022 near the bottom of the Weddell Sea at a depth of 10,000 feet (3,048 meters).(more)

In August 1914 the [British Imperial Trans-Antarctic Expedition](https://www.britannica.com/topic/British-Imperial-Trans-Antarctic-Expedition) (1914–16) left England under Shackleton’s leadership. He planned to cross Antarctica from a base on the [Weddell Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Weddell-Sea) to McMurdo Sound, via the South Pole, but the expedition ship _[Endurance](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Endurance-British-ship)_ was trapped in ice off the Caird coast and drifted for 10 months before being crushed in the [pack ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/pack-ice). The members of the expedition then drifted on ice floes for another five months and finally escaped in boats to Elephant Island in the [South Shetland Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Shetland-Islands), where they subsisted on [seal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/seal-authentication) meat, [penguins](https://www.britannica.com/animal/penguin), and their [dogs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dog).

Shackleton and five others sailed 800 miles (1,300 km) to [South Georgia](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Georgia) in a whale boat, a 16-day journey across a stretch of dangerous ocean, before landing on the southern side of South Georgia. Shackleton and his small crew then made the first crossing of the island to seek aid. Four months later, after leading four separate relief [expeditions](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/expeditions), Shackleton succeeded in rescuing his crew from Elephant Island. Throughout the ordeal, not one of Shackleton’s crew of the _Endurance_ died. A supporting party, the [Ross Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ross-Sea) party led by A.E. Mackintosh, sailed in the _Aurora_ and laid depots as far as latitude 83°30′ S for the use of the Trans-Antarctic party; three of this party died on the return journey.

## Military service and death

[![Ernest Shackleton](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/202008-050-9DBAB862/Ernest-Shackleton.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/202008-050-9DBAB862/Ernest-Shackleton.jpg)

[Ernest Shackleton](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/202008-050-9DBAB862/Ernest-Shackleton.jpg) After the British Imperial Trans-Antarctic Expedition, Shackleton served in the British armed forces starting in 1917.(more)

Shackleton served in the [British army](https://www.britannica.com/topic/British-Army) during [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I) and served as a military adviser in the multinational North Russia Expeditionary Force during the [Russian Civil War](https://www.britannica.com/event/Russian-Civil-War). He attempted a fourth Antarctic expedition, called the Shackleton-Rowett Antarctic Expedition, aboard the _Quest_ in 1921, which had the goal of circumnavigating the continent. Shackleton died at Grytviken, South Georgia, however, at the outset of the journey. His exertions in raising funds to finance his expeditions and the [immense](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/immense) strain of the expeditions themselves were believed to have worn out his strength.

[South Pole](https://cdn.britannica.com/28/241328-050-08BD0C7E/Locator-map-South-Pole.jpg)(more)

**South Pole**, the [southern end of](https://www.britannica.com/science/South-Magnetic-Pole) the Earth’s axis, lying in Antarctica, about 300 miles (480 km) south of the Ross Ice Shelf. This geographic South Pole does not coincide with the magnetic South Pole, from which magnetic compasses point and which lies on the Adélie Coast (at about 66°00′ S, 139°06′ E; the magnetic pole moves about 8 miles \[13 km\] to the northwest each year). Nor does it coincide with the geomagnetic South Pole, the southern end of the Earth’s geomagnetic field (this pole also moves; during the early 1990s it was located about 79°13′ S, 108°44′ E). The geographic pole, at an elevation of some 9,300 feet (2,830 metres; the elevation also changes constantly) above [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level), has six months of complete daylight and six months of total darkness each year. Ice thickness is 8,850 feet (2,700 metres). First reached by the Norwegian explorer [Roald Amundsen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Roald-Amundsen) on December 14, 1911, the pole was reached the following year by the British explorer Robert F. Scott and in 1929 by the American explorer Richard E. Byrd. After reaching the South Pole on January 11, 1986, the British explorer [Robert Swan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Swan) led an expedition to the [North Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Pole), reaching his destination on May 14, 1989 and thereby becoming the first person to walk to both poles. The South Pole is the site of a U.S. station and landing strip (Amundsen-Scott); owing to the movement of the polar ice cap, a new location of the exact rotational pole is marked periodically by station personnel.

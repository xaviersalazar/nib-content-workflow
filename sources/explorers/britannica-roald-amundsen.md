Quick Facts

- **Profession:** polar exploration

- **Nationality:** [Norway](https://www.britannica.com/place/Norway).

- **Achievements/Discoveries:** First person to reach the [South Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Pole) (1911), part of the crew aboard the first vessel to [traverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/traverse) the [Northwest Passage](https://www.britannica.com/place/Northwest-Passage-trade-route) by ship (1905), and one of the first to fly over the [North Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Pole) (1927).

Top Questions


### What is Roald Amundsen remembered for?

Roald Amundsen, a Norwegian explorer, was one of the greatest figures in the field of polar exploration. He was the first explorer to transit the [Northwest Passage](https://www.britannica.com/place/Northwest-Passage-trade-route) (1903–05), the first to reach the [South Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Pole) (1911), and the first to fly over the [North Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Pole) in an [airship](https://www.britannica.com/technology/airship) (1926).

### How did Roald Amundsen become famous?

In 1903 Roald Amundsen and his crew aboard the [sloop](https://www.britannica.com/technology/sloop) _Gjøa_ began a mission to sail through the Northwest Passage and around the northern Canadian coast. They reached Cape Colborne (in present-day [Nunavut](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nunavut)) in August 1905, overwintered at Herschel Island in the [Yukon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yukon-territory), and arrived in [Nome](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nome-Alaska), [Alaska](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alaska), in August 1906 to a heroes’ welcome.

### How did Roald Amundsen die?

In 1928 Roald Amundsen lost his life flying to rescue Italian aeronautical engineer [Umberto Nobile](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Umberto-Nobile), whom he had accompanied in a dirigible flight over the North Pole in 1926, from a dirigible crash at sea near [Spitsbergen](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spitsbergen), [Norway](https://www.britannica.com/place/Norway). The airplane carrying Amundsen, searching for survivors of Nobile’s crash, also crashed, and Amundsen’s remains were never found.

**Roald Amundsen** (born July 16, 1872, Borge, near Oslo, Norway—died June 18, 1928?, Arctic Ocean) was a Norwegian explorer who was the first to reach the [South Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Pole), the first to make a ship voyage through the [Northwest Passage](https://www.britannica.com/place/Northwest-Passage-trade-route), and one of the first to cross the [Arctic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arctic) by air. He was one of the greatest figures in the field of polar exploration.

[![Roald Amundsen](https://cdn.britannica.com/52/96652-050-EF343160/Roald-Amundsen-crew-Gjoa.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/52/96652-050-EF343160/Roald-Amundsen-crew-Gjoa.jpg)

[Roald Amundsen](https://cdn.britannica.com/52/96652-050-EF343160/Roald-Amundsen-crew-Gjoa.jpg) Roald Amundsen and his crew aboard the _Gjøa_.(more)

Amundsen studied [medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/medicine) for a while and then took to sea. In 1897 he sailed as first mate on the _Belgica_ in a Belgian [expedition](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/expedition) that was the first to [winter](https://www.britannica.com/science/winter) in the [Antarctic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctica). In 1903, with a crew of six on his 47-ton sloop _Gjøa_, Amundsen began his mission to sail through the Northwest Passage and around the northern Canadian coast. He reached Cape Colborne (in present-day [Nunavut](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nunavut)) in August 1905, completing his transit of the passage proper, before ice halted his westerly progress for the winter at Herschel Island in the [Yukon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yukon-territory) the following month. Amundsen and his crew resumed the journey in August 1906 and were greeted with a heroes’ welcome when the expedition concluded in [Nome](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nome-Alaska), Alaska, later that month. This achievement whetted his appetite for the spectacular in polar exploration.

![Arctic explorations](https://cdn.britannica.com/82/64882-050-6EF13351/explorers-Arctic-first-Robert-E-Peary-Matthew-1909.jpg)

[Arctic explorations](https://cdn.britannica.com/82/64882-050-6EF13351/explorers-Arctic-first-Robert-E-Peary-Matthew-1909.jpg)(more)

Amundsen’s next plan, to drift across the [North Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Pole) in [Fridtjof Nansen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Fridtjof-Nansen)’s old [ship](https://www.britannica.com/technology/ship), the _[Fram](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Fram-Norwegian-ship),_ was affected by the news that the American explorer [Robert E. Peary](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Edwin-Peary) had reached the North Pole in April 1909, but he continued his preparations. When Amundsen left Norway in June 1910 no one but his brother knew that he was heading for the [South Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Pole) instead of the North. He sailed the _Fram_ directly from the [Madeira Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madeira-Islands) to the [Bay of Whales](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bay-of-Whales), [Antarctica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctica), along the [Ross Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ross-Sea). The base he set up there was 60 miles (100 km) closer to the pole than the Antarctic base of the English explorer [Robert Falcon Scott](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Falcon-Scott), who was heading a [rival](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/rival) expedition with the same goal. An experienced polar traveler, Amundsen prepared carefully for the coming journey, making a preliminary trip to deposit [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) supplies along the first part of his route to the pole and back. To transport his supplies, he used sled [dogs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dog), while Scott depended on Siberian [ponies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/pony).

Amundsen set out with 4 companions, 52 dogs, and 4 sledges on October 19, 1911, and, after encountering good [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather), arrived at the South Pole on December 14. The explorers recorded scientific data at the pole before beginning the return journey on December 17, and they safely reached their base at the Bay of Whales on January 25, 1912. Scott, in the meantime, had reached the South Pole on January 17, but on a difficult return journey he and all his men [perished](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/perished).

Amundsen, Umberto Nobile (the pilot and designer of the airship), and Lincoln Ellsworth flew from Spitsbergen to Alaska, becoming the first people to definitely reach the North Pole.(more)

With funds resulting from his Antarctic adventure, Amundsen established a successful [shipping](https://www.britannica.com/technology/shipping-water-transportation) business. He acquired a new ship, the _Maud_, and tried in 1918 to complete his old plan of drifting across the North Pole, but he was forced to abandon this scheme in favour of trying to reach the North Pole by [airplane](https://www.britannica.com/technology/airplane). In a flight (1925) with the American explorer [Lincoln Ellsworth](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Lincoln-Ellsworth) he arrived to within 150 miles (250 km) of the pole. In 1926, with Ellsworth and the Italian aeronautical engineer [Umberto Nobile](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Umberto-Nobile), he passed over the North Pole in a [dirigible](https://www.britannica.com/technology/airship), crossing from [Spitsbergen](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spitsbergen) (now [Svalbard](https://www.britannica.com/place/Svalbard)), north of [Norway](https://www.britannica.com/place/Norway), to [Alaska](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alaska). [Disputes](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Disputes) over the credit for the flight embittered his final years. In 1928 Amundsen lost his life in flying to rescue Nobile from a dirigible crash near Spitsbergen. Amundsen’s books included _The South Pole_ (1912) and, with Ellsworth, _First Crossing of the Polar Sea_ (1927).

[South Pole](https://cdn.britannica.com/28/241328-050-08BD0C7E/Locator-map-South-Pole.jpg)(more)

**South Pole**, the [southern end of](https://www.britannica.com/science/South-Magnetic-Pole) the Earth’s axis, lying in Antarctica, about 300 miles (480 km) south of the Ross Ice Shelf. This geographic South Pole does not coincide with the magnetic South Pole, from which magnetic compasses point and which lies on the Adélie Coast (at about 66°00′ S, 139°06′ E; the magnetic pole moves about 8 miles \[13 km\] to the northwest each year). Nor does it coincide with the geomagnetic South Pole, the southern end of the Earth’s geomagnetic field (this pole also moves; during the early 1990s it was located about 79°13′ S, 108°44′ E). The geographic pole, at an elevation of some 9,300 feet (2,830 metres; the elevation also changes constantly) above [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level), has six months of complete daylight and six months of total darkness each year. Ice thickness is 8,850 feet (2,700 metres). First reached by the Norwegian explorer [Roald Amundsen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Roald-Amundsen) on December 14, 1911, the pole was reached the following year by the British explorer Robert F. Scott and in 1929 by the American explorer Richard E. Byrd. After reaching the South Pole on January 11, 1986, the British explorer [Robert Swan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Swan) led an expedition to the [North Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Pole), reaching his destination on May 14, 1989 and thereby becoming the first person to walk to both poles. The South Pole is the site of a U.S. station and landing strip (Amundsen-Scott); owing to the movement of the polar ice cap, a new location of the exact rotational pole is marked periodically by station personnel.

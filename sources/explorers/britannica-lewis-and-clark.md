### What was the purpose of the Lewis and Clark Expedition?

Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–06) was a U.S. military expedition, led by Captain Meriwether Lewis and Lieutenant William Clark, to explore the Louisiana Purchase and the Pacific Northwest. The expedition was a major chapter in the history of American exploration.

### Which president sent Lewis and Clark on their expedition?

U.S. President Thomas Jefferson asked his personal secretary, Meriwether Lewis, to lead the expedition to explore the Louisiana Purchase and the Pacific Northwest. As his co-commander, Lewis selected William Clark, who had been his military superior during the government’s battles with the Northwest Indian Federation in the early 1790s.

### Who was Seaman in the Lewis and Clark expedition?

In the Lewis and Clark expedition, Seaman was the name of a Newfoundland dog, which Meriwether Lewis purchased for $20.

### What plants and animals did Lewis discover?

Lewis identified 178 plants new to science, including bitterroot, prairie sagebrush, Douglas fir, and ponderosa pine, as well as 122 animals, such as grizzly bear, prairie dog, and pronghorn antelope.

### Who was the Native American woman who accompanied the expedition?

[Sacagawea](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sacagawea) was a Shoshone woman who, as interpreter, traveled thousands of wilderness miles with the Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–1806), from the Mandan-Hidatsa villages in the Dakotas to the Pacific Northwest.

**Lewis and Clark Expedition**, (1804–06), U.S. military expedition, led by Capt. [Meriwether Lewis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Meriwether-Lewis) and Lieut. [William Clark](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Clark), to explore the [Louisiana Purchase](https://www.britannica.com/event/Louisiana-Purchase) and the [Pacific Northwest](https://www.britannica.com/place/Northwest-region). The expedition was a major chapter in the history of American exploration.

## Commissioning and preparation

On January 18, 1803, U.S. Pres. [Thomas Jefferson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thomas-Jefferson) sent a secret message to [Congress](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Congress-of-the-United-States) asking for $2,500 to send an officer and a dozen soldiers to explore the [Missouri River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Missouri-River), make diplomatic contact with Indians, expand the American fur trade, and locate the [Northwest Passage](https://www.britannica.com/place/Northwest-Passage-trade-route) (the much-sought-after [hypothetical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hypothetical) northwestern water route to the [Pacific Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean)). The proposed trip took on added significance on May 2, when the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) agreed to the Louisiana Purchase— [Napoleon](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Napoleon-I)’s sale of 828,000 square miles (2,100,000 square km) of French territory for $27 million. Jefferson, who had already sponsored several attempts to explore the West, asked his personal secretary, Meriwether Lewis, to lead the expedition. Lewis was dispatched to [Philadelphia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Philadelphia) for instruction in [botany](https://www.britannica.com/science/botany), [celestial navigation](https://www.britannica.com/science/celestial-navigation), [medicine](https://www.britannica.com/science/medicine), and [zoology](https://www.britannica.com/science/zoology). He also purchased supplies and spent $20 on a [Newfoundland dog](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Newfoundland-dog), Seaman.

[![William Clark](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/12858-050-C634509E/William-Clark-portrait-Charles-Willson-Peale-Independence-1810.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/12858-050-C634509E/William-Clark-portrait-Charles-Willson-Peale-Independence-1810.jpg)

[William Clark](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/12858-050-C634509E/William-Clark-portrait-Charles-Willson-Peale-Independence-1810.jpg) William Clark, portrait by Charles Willson Peale, 1810; in Independence National Historical Park, Philadelphia.(more)

Lewis procured weapons at [Harpers Ferry](https://www.britannica.com/place/Harpers-Ferry), Virginia (now in [West Virginia](https://www.britannica.com/place/West-Virginia)), supervised the construction of a 55-foot (17-metre) keelboat, and secured smaller [vessels](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/vessels), in addition to designing an iron-framed boat that could be assembled on the journey. As his co-commander he selected William Clark, who had been his military superior during the government’s battles with the Northwest Indian Federation in the early 1790s. The U.S. secretary of war denied Lewis’s request of a shared command, but Captain Lewis and Lieutenant Clark chose to address one another as “captain” to hide this fact from the other members of the expedition. For his part, Clark recruited men in [Kentucky](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kentucky), oversaw their training that winter at Camp River Dubois in [Illinois](https://www.britannica.com/place/Illinois-state), and served as the expedition’s principal waterman and cartographer.

## Expedition from May 14, 1804, to October 16, 1805

[![Lewis and Clark Expedition](https://cdn.britannica.com/01/64901-050-30A1BD6F/Route-Lewis-and-Clark-Expedition.jpg)\\
1 of 2](https://cdn.britannica.com/01/64901-050-30A1BD6F/Route-Lewis-and-Clark-Expedition.jpg)

[Lewis and Clark Expedition](https://cdn.britannica.com/01/64901-050-30A1BD6F/Route-Lewis-and-Clark-Expedition.jpg) Route of the Lewis and Clark Expedition, 1804–06.(more)

[![Joseph Whitehouse travel journal](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/162798-050-59C8B7FC/Travel-journal-Joseph-Whitehouse-Lewis-and-Clark.jpg?w=300)\\
2 of 2](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/162798-050-59C8B7FC/Travel-journal-Joseph-Whitehouse-Lewis-and-Clark.jpg)

[Joseph Whitehouse travel journal](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/162798-050-59C8B7FC/Travel-journal-Joseph-Whitehouse-Lewis-and-Clark.jpg) Travel journal (1804–05) of Joseph Whitehouse, a member of the Lewis and Clark Expedition.(more)

Over the [duration](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/duration) of the trip, from May 14, 1804, to September 23, 1806, from [St. Louis](https://www.britannica.com/place/Saint-Louis-Missouri), Missouri, to the [Pacific Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean) and back, the [Corps of Discovery](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Corps-of-Discovery), as the expedition company was called, traveled nearly 8,000 miles (13,000 km). The entourage, numbering about four dozen men, covered 10 to 20 miles (16 to 32 km) a day—poling, pushing, and pulling their 10-ton keelboat and two pirogues (dugout boats) up the [Missouri River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Missouri-River). Lewis’s iron-framed boat was later assembled and covered with skins near [Great Falls](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Falls-Montana) (in present-day [Montana](https://www.britannica.com/place/Montana-state)) but had to be abandoned because the seams leaked and there was no pitch to seal them. The captains and at least five others kept journals. President Jefferson had instructed Lewis to make observations of [latitude and longitude](https://www.britannica.com/science/latitude) and to take detailed notes about the [soil](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil), [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology), [animals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal), [plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant), and native peoples. Lewis identified 178 plants new to science, including [bitterroot](https://www.britannica.com/plant/bitterroot), prairie [sagebrush](https://www.britannica.com/plant/sagebrush), [Douglas fir](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Douglas-fir), and ponderosa [pine](https://www.britannica.com/plant/pine), as well as 122 animals, such as [grizzly bear](https://www.britannica.com/animal/grizzly-bear), [prairie dog](https://www.britannica.com/animal/prairie-dog), and [pronghorn](https://www.britannica.com/animal/pronghorn) [antelope](https://www.britannica.com/animal/antelope-mammal). The scientific names _Philadelphus lewisii_ (mock orange), _Lewisia rediva_ (bitterroot), and _Clarkia pucella_ (pink [fairy](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/fairy), or ragged robin) are but three examples of the men’s discoveries. The expedition encountered immense animal herds and ate well, consuming one [buffalo](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bison), two [elk](https://www.britannica.com/animal/elk-mammal), or four [deer](https://www.britannica.com/animal/deer) per day, supplemented by roots, berries, and fish. They named geographic locations after expedition members, peers, loved ones, and even their dog (Seaman’s Creek). They experienced [dysentery](https://www.britannica.com/science/dysentery), [venereal disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/sexually-transmitted-disease), boils, [tick](https://www.britannica.com/animal/tick) bites, and injuries from [prickly pear](https://www.britannica.com/plant/prickly-pear), yet only one man perished over the course of the journey.

Another primary objective involved diplomacy with [Native Americans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indigenous-peoples-of-the-Americas). The expedition held councils with Indians, in which the corps had military parades, handed out peace medals, flags, and gifts, delivered speeches, promised trade, and requested intertribal peace. There also was something of a magic show (magnets, compasses, and Lewis’s air gun) and an invitation for Indian representatives to travel to [Washington, D.C.](https://www.britannica.com/place/Washington-DC) Most tribes welcomed trading opportunities and provided the expedition with food, knowledge, guides, shelter, sex, and entertainment. The [Lakota](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Teton) (encountered in [South Dakota](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Dakota)), however, already had British commercial ties and did not view American competition favourably, especially because it would make their enemies stronger. Their attempt to prevent the expedition from continuing upstream nearly turned violent, but Chief Black Buffalo’s [diplomacy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/diplomacy) defused the situation.

The expedition arrived at the [Mandan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mandan-people) and [Hidatsa](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hidatsa) villages near present-day [Bismarck](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bismarck-North-Dakota), [North Dakota](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Dakota), and constructed [Fort Mandan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Fort-Mandan) in which to spend the winter. The captains prepared maps, [artifacts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/artifacts), mineral samples, plant specimens, and papers to send back in the spring. On April 7, 1805, a small crew departed on a St. Louis-bound keelboat laden with boxes of materials for Jefferson that included live [magpie](https://www.britannica.com/animal/magpie) s and a [prairie dog](https://www.britannica.com/animal/prairie-dog). Meanwhile, the permanent party proceeded up the Missouri in six canoes and two pirogues. It now consisted of 33 people, including soldiers, civilians, Clark’s slave [York](https://www.britannica.com/biography/York-enslaved-man), and two newly hired interpreters—a French Canadian, [Toussaint Charbonneau](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Toussaint-Charbonneau), and his [Shoshone](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shoshone-people) wife, [Sacagawea](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sacagawea), who had given birth to a boy, Jean Baptiste, that February. The departure scene was described by Lewis in his journal:

> This little [fleet](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/fleet) altho’ not quite so rispectable as those of [Columbus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Christopher-Columbus) or [Capt. Cook](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Cook) were still viewed by us with as much pleasure as those deservedly famed adventurers ever beheld theirs…we were now about to penetrate a country at least two thousand miles in width, on which the foot of civillized man had never trodden; the good or evil it had in store for us was for experiment yet to determine, and these little vessells contained every article by which we were to expect to subsist or defend ourselves.

[![Lewis and Clark Expedition](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/96164-004-9B755988/Headwaters-detail-map-Missouri-River-Lewis-and.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/96164-004-9B755988/Headwaters-detail-map-Missouri-River-Lewis-and.jpg)

[Lewis and Clark Expedition](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/96164-004-9B755988/Headwaters-detail-map-Missouri-River-Lewis-and.jpg) Headwaters of the Missouri River, detail from Lewis and Clark Expedition map by William Clark and Meriwether Lewis, 1804–06.(more)

On June 2, 1805, the expedition party arrived at a fork in the river. Not knowing which waterway was the principal stream, they sent out reconnaissance parties up both forks. Although the evidence was not conclusive, the captains believed the south fork to be the major course while everyone else favoured the north. Lewis named the north fork Maria’s River (now [Marias River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Marias-River)) and instructed the party to continue up the south fork. This choice proved correct when the expedition arrived at the [Great Falls](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Falls-Montana) almost two weeks later. An 18-mile (29-km) portage around the falls was made even more difficult by broken terrain, prickly pear cactus, hailstorms, and numerous grizzly bears. On July 4, 1805, the party finished the portage and, to celebrate [Independence Day](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Independence-Day-United-States-holiday), consumed the last of their 120 gallons of alcohol and danced into the night.

Arriving at the Three Forks of the Missouri River (the [confluence](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/confluence) of the Jefferson, Madison, and Gallatin rivers), [Sacagawea](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sacagawea) recognized Beaverhead Rock and informed the others they would soon encounter some [Shoshones](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shoshone-people). Lewis climbed Lemhi Pass, crossing the [Continental Divide](https://www.britannica.com/place/Continental-Divide), only to have his hope for a single mountain portage dashed by the view of endless mountains stretching before him: “I discovered immence ranges of high mountains still to the West of us with their tops partially covered with snow.” Fortunately, in mid-August he met a Shoshone band led by Sacagawea’s brother [Cameahwait](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cameahwait), who provided the expedition with horses. The Shoshone guide Old Toby joined the expedition and led them across the [Bitterroot Range](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bitterroot-Range). On the crossing, Clark [lamented](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/lamented), “I have been wet and as cold in every part as I ever was in my life, indeed I was at one time fearfull my feet would freeze in the thin mockersons \[moccasins\] which I wore.” Cold and hungry, the expedition finally spilled out of the mountains onto the Weippe Prairie, homeland of the [Nez Percé](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nez-Perce-people). Upon the recommendation of a respected elderly woman, Watkuweis, the Nez Percé befriended the expedition. After leaving their horses with Chief Twisted Hair, the explorers hollowed out five cottonwood canoes and floated down the Clearwater and [Snake](https://www.britannica.com/place/Snake-River) rivers, reaching the [Columbia River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Columbia-River) on October 16.

# Pacific Ocean and return

[![Lewis and Clark Expedition](https://cdn.britannica.com/65/96165-004-9C94D29C/Fort-Clatsop-detail-map-Lewis-and-Clark.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/65/96165-004-9C94D29C/Fort-Clatsop-detail-map-Lewis-and-Clark.jpg)

[Lewis and Clark Expedition](https://cdn.britannica.com/65/96165-004-9C94D29C/Fort-Clatsop-detail-map-Lewis-and-Clark.jpg) Fort Clatsop, detail from Lewis and Clark Expedition map by William Clark and Meriwether Lewis, 1804–06.(more)

They finally arrived at the [Pacific Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean) in mid-November, with Clark recording in his journal, “Ocian in view! O! the joy.” Fierce storms [delayed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/delayed) their progress for nearly a month. The members conducted a democratic vote on where to spend the winter, with even York and [Sacagawea](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sacagawea) casting votes. Near present-day [Astoria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Astoria-Oregon), [Oregon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Oregon-state), the corps built [Fort Clatsop](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Fort-Clatsop) and endured a wet, miserable winter by journal writing, drying meat, making [salt](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt-acid-base-reactions), and traveling to see a beached whale. They hoped to encounter vessels along the Pacific that could transport them home, but, finding none, they did an about-face, planning to return along the Columbia and Missouri rivers. After stealing a Clatsop Indian canoe, they headed up the Columbia on March 23, 1806. They arrived at the [Nez Percé](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nez-Perce-people) villages, gathered up their horses, and waited for the snows to melt.

[![Pompey's Pillar](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/101771-050-C2F3693D/Pompey-Pillar-Billings-Mont.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/101771-050-C2F3693D/Pompey-Pillar-Billings-Mont.jpg)

[Pompey's Pillar](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/101771-050-C2F3693D/Pompey-Pillar-Billings-Mont.jpg) Pompey's Pillar, near Billings, Montana, U.S.(more)

On July 3, after recrossing the Bitterroots, the expedition divided into several groups to better explore the region and two major tributaries of the Missouri. Several groups floated down to the [Great Falls](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Falls-Montana), digging up supplies they had [cached](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cached) on their outward journey. Meanwhile, Clark arrived at the [Yellowstone River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yellowstone-River) after crossing Bozeman Pass, the route suggested by Sacagawea. After constructing two canoes, he carved his name and the date in a [sandstone](https://www.britannica.com/science/sandstone) outcropping, Pompey’s Tower (now Pompey’s Pillar), named for Sacagawea’s son, whom Clark called Pomp. In the meantime, Lewis and three men met eight [Blackfeet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Blackfoot-people) on July 26 on a tributary of Maria’s River near present-day Cut Bank, [Montana](https://www.britannica.com/place/Montana-state). A deadly altercation occurred the next morning when the explorers shot two warriors who had stolen their horses and guns. Fleeing on horseback for 24 hours straight, the foursome arrived at the [Missouri River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Missouri-River) to rejoin other members of the [expedition](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/expedition) who were floating downstream. Farther on, this group reunited with Clark, bid farewell to the Charbonneaus, and floated downstream, completing the journey.

The [Corps of Discovery](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Corps-of-Discovery) met with a grand reception at [St. Louis](https://www.britannica.com/place/Saint-Louis-Missouri) on September 23. Congress rewarded them with double pay and public land. The captains each received 1,600 acres (650 hectares), and their men received 320 acres (130 hectares). The final cost for the expedition totaled $38,000. Jefferson appointed Lewis governor of Upper Louisiana Territory and appointed Clark an Indian agent. Some of the expedition stayed in the military, others entered the fur trade, while still others took to farming in the region or returned to the East.

## Legacy

[![Lewis and Clark Expedition: Corps of Discovery annotated member list](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/162864-050-4C069D5F/list-Lewis-and-Clark-William-journal-Corps.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/162864-050-4C069D5F/list-Lewis-and-Clark-William-journal-Corps.jpg)

[Lewis and Clark Expedition: Corps of Discovery annotated member list](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/162864-050-4C069D5F/list-Lewis-and-Clark-William-journal-Corps.jpg) Annotated list of Lewis and Clark's Corps of Discovery from William Clark's journal, 1825–28. Clark notes that Sacagawea (“Se car ja we au”) is dead, among others.(more)

Some insist Lewis and Clark’s [legacy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legacy) is insignificant because they were not the first non-Indians to explore the area, did not find an all-water route across the continent, and failed to publish their journals in a timely fashion. Although the first official account appeared in 1814, the two-volume narrative did not contain any of their scientific achievements. Nevertheless, the expedition contributed significant geographic and scientific knowledge of the West, aided the expansion of the fur trade, and strengthened U.S. claims to the Pacific. Clark’s maps portraying the [geography](https://www.britannica.com/science/geography) of the West, printed in 1810 and 1814, were the best available until the 1840s.

[![Meriwether Lewis, William Clark, and Sacagawea](https://cdn.britannica.com/62/101762-050-26C80BE4/sculpture-Meriwether-Lewis-William-Clark-Sacagawea-Fort.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/62/101762-050-26C80BE4/sculpture-Meriwether-Lewis-William-Clark-Sacagawea-Fort.jpg)

[Meriwether Lewis, William Clark, and Sacagawea](https://cdn.britannica.com/62/101762-050-26C80BE4/sculpture-Meriwether-Lewis-William-Clark-Sacagawea-Fort.jpg) Bronze sculpture of Meriwether Lewis, William Clark, and Sacagawea at Fort Benton, Montana.(more)

No American exploration looms larger in U.S. history. The Lewis and Clark Expedition has been [commemorated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/commemorated) with stamps, monuments, and trails and has had numerous places named after it. St. Louis hosted the 1904 World’s Fair during the expedition’s centennial, and [Portland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Portland-Oregon), Oregon, sponsored the 1905 Lewis and Clark Exposition. In 1978 Congress established the 3,700-mile (6,000-km) Lewis and Clark National Historic Trail. While Lewis and Clark had a great interest in documenting Indian [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures), they represented a government whose policies can now be seen to have fostered dispossession and cultural genocide. This [dichotomy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dichotomy) was on display during the event’s bicentennial, commemorated by two years of special events across the expedition route.

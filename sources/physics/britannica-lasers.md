[Atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) or [molecules](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) in a laser are energized by an external energy source to give off [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light) of certain [wavelengths](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength). That light is amplified as it passes through the laser apparatus until it forms a sharp, concentrated beam. This process of stimulated emission works by imparting [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) to atoms or molecules. These particles do not stay in these higher-energy, or excited, states for long as these states are naturally unstable. Dropping back to a base energy state causes atoms to release the extra energy as light. This emission can occur spontaneously or be stimulated by incoming photons (small packets of light).

In a laser, a passing [photon](https://www.britannica.com/science/photon) with the right energy stimulates an excited atom to emit a photon of the same energy, [phase](https://www.britannica.com/science/phase-mechanics), and direction, creating a coherent light wave. This is possible if more atoms of the laser material are in an excited state than in a lower state, a condition known as [population inversion](https://www.britannica.com/technology/population-inversion)—without it, incoming photons get absorbed instead of multiplying, and a laser cannot be produced. Achieving this requires an external energy source, such as a bright light or [electric current](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-current), to selectively excite the atoms or molecules. Once a population inversion is established, the emitted photons cause further emissions, stimulating more atoms to emit photons that add to the intensity of the resultant light.

Related Topics: [laser](https://www.britannica.com/technology/laser)_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/How-Does-a-Laser-Work)

A laser uses a pair of [mirrors](https://www.britannica.com/technology/mirror-optics) facing each other—an optical resonator—to build up the light energy in the beam. As light bounces back and forth between the mirrors, it passes through the laser material, increasing in intensity with each pass as more excited atoms emit additional photons. One of the mirrors is partially transparent, allowing some light to escape as the laser beam. This setup ensures that the emitted light is [coherent](https://www.britannica.com/science/coherence), monochromatic, and focused in a narrow beam.

**stimulated emission**, in [laser](https://www.britannica.com/technology/laser) action, the release of energy from an excited atom by artificial means. According to [Albert Einstein](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Albert-Einstein), when more atoms occupy a higher [energy state](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy-state) than a lower one under normal temperature equilibrium ( _see_ [population inversion](https://www.britannica.com/technology/population-inversion)), it is possible to force atoms to return to an unexcited state by stimulating them with the same energy as would be emitted naturally.

Related Topics: [laser](https://www.britannica.com/technology/laser)_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/stimulated-emission)

In stimulated emission the emitted light wave will be coherent ( _i.e.,_ in phase; _see_ [coherence](https://www.britannica.com/science/coherence)) with the incoming wave. In laser action the stimulating emission triggers a chain reaction in which the radiation from one atom stimulates another in succession until all the excited atoms in the system have returned to normalcy. In doing so, coherent monochromatic light (light of a single wavelength) is emitted.

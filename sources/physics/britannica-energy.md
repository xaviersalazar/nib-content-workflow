### What is energy?

Energy is the capacity for doing work. It may exist in potential, kinetic, thermal, helectrical, chemical, nuclear, or other forms.

### What is the unit of measurement for energy?

In the International System of Units (SI), energy is measured in [joules](https://www.britannica.com/science/joule). One joule is equal to the work done by a one-newton force acting over a one-meter distance.

### Can energy be created?

Energy can be neither created nor destroyed. It can only be changed from one form to another. This principle is known as the [conservation of energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/conservation-of-energy).

**energy**, in [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science), the capacity for doing [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics). It may exist in [potential](https://www.britannica.com/science/potential-energy), [kinetic](https://www.britannica.com/science/kinetic-energy), [thermal](https://www.britannica.com/science/thermal-energy), electrical, [chemical](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-energy), [nuclear](https://www.britannica.com/science/nuclear-energy), or other various forms. There are, moreover, [heat](https://www.britannica.com/science/heat) and work—i.e., energy in the process of transfer from one body to another. After it has been transferred, energy is always designated according to its nature. Hence, heat transferred may become thermal energy, while work done may [manifest](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifest) itself in the form of [mechanical energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/mechanical-energy).

All forms of energy are associated with [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics). For example, any given body has [kinetic energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/kinetic-energy) if it is in motion. A tensioned device such as a [bow](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bow-and-arrow) or [spring](https://www.britannica.com/technology/spring-machine-component), though at rest, has the potential for creating motion; it contains [potential energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/potential-energy) because of its configuration. Similarly, nuclear [energy](https://www.britannica.com/technology/energy-conversion) is potential energy because it results from the configuration of [subatomic particles](https://www.britannica.com/science/subatomic-particle) in the nucleus of an [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom).

Energy can be neither created nor destroyed but only changed from one form to another. This principle is known as the conservation of energy or the [first law of thermodynamics](https://www.britannica.com/science/thermodynamics/The-first-law-of-thermodynamics#ref258541). For example, when a box slides down a hill, the potential energy that the box has from being located high up on the slope is [converted](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/converted) to kinetic energy, energy of motion. As the box slows to a stop through [friction](https://www.britannica.com/science/friction), the kinetic energy from the box’s motion is converted to thermal energy that heats the box and the slope.

[Energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) and [power](https://www.britannica.com/science/power-physics), while closely related, are distinct concepts in [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science), each with its unique characteristics and applications. Energy is fundamentally the capacity to perform [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics), whereas power is the time rate of doing work or delivering energy. In the [International System of Units (SI)](https://www.britannica.com/science/International-System-of-Units), energy is measured in [joule](https://www.britannica.com/science/joule) s (J). One joule is equal to the work done by a one- [newton](https://www.britannica.com/science/newton-unit-of-measurement) [force](https://www.britannica.com/science/force-physics) acting over a one-meter distance. Energy may exist in [potential](https://www.britannica.com/science/potential-energy), [kinetic](https://www.britannica.com/science/kinetic-energy), [thermal](https://www.britannica.com/science/thermal-energy), electrical, [chemical](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-energy), [nuclear](https://www.britannica.com/science/nuclear-energy), or other forms.

Power is the rate at which work is performed. It measures the amount of energy that is transferred or transformed in a given period of time. In practical terms, in the SI system, the unit of power is [watt](https://www.britannica.com/science/watt-unit-of-measurement) (W) where one watt equals one joule per second. Power tells us how quickly energy is being used or delivered. For example, a high-powered motor can perform the same amount of work as a low-powered motor but in a shorter time span.

Understanding how energy and power differ helps analyze the performance and efficiency of real-world systems. The energy consumption of electrical appliances (usually measured in kilowatt-hours (kWh) indicates total energy used over time. Power ratings specify the rate of energy use per second, which is essential for evaluating efficiency and system performance.

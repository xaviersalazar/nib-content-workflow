**wave**, propagation of disturbances from place to place in a regular and organized way. Most familiar are surface [waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-water) that travel on [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water), but [sound](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics), [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light), and the [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-motion) of [subatomic particles](https://www.britannica.com/science/subatomic-particle) all exhibit wavelike properties. In the simplest waves, the disturbance oscillates periodically ( _see_ [periodic motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/periodic-motion)) with a fixed [frequency](https://www.britannica.com/science/frequency-physics) and [wavelength](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength). Mechanical waves, such as sound, require a medium through which to travel, while electromagnetic waves ( _see_ [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation)) do not require a medium and can be propagated through a vacuum. Propagation of a wave through a medium depends on the medium’s properties. _See also_ [seismic wave](https://www.britannica.com/science/seismic-wave).

## Types and features of waves

Waves come in two kinds, longitudinal and transverse. [Transverse waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/transverse-wave) are like those on water, with the surface going up and down, and [longitudinal waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/longitudinal-wave) are like of those of sound, consisting of alternating compressions and rarefactions in a medium. The high point of a transverse wave is a called the crest, and the low point is called the trough. For longitudinal waves, the [compressions](https://www.britannica.com/science/compression) and [rarefactions](https://www.britannica.com/science/rarefaction) are analogous to the crests and troughs of transverse waves. The distance between successive crests or troughs is called the [wavelength](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength). The height of a wave is the [amplitude](https://www.britannica.com/science/amplitude-physics). How many crests or troughs pass a specific point during a unit of time is called the [frequency](https://www.britannica.com/science/frequency-physics). The [velocity](https://www.britannica.com/science/velocity) of a wave can be expressed as the wavelength multiplied by the frequency.

Waves can travel immense distances even though the oscillation at one point is very small. For example, a thunderclap can be heard kilometres away, yet the sound carried manifests itself at any point only as minute compressions and rarefactions of the air.

## Wave behaviour

Waves display several basic phenomena. In reflection, a wave encounters an obstacle and is reflected back. In refraction, a wave bends when it enters a medium through which it has a different speed. In diffraction, waves bend when they pass around small obstacles and spread out when they pass through small openings. In interference, when two waves meet, they can interfere constructively, creating a wave with larger amplitude than the original waves, or destructively, creating a wave with a smaller (or even zero) amplitude.

## [Reflection](https://www.britannica.com/science/reflection-physics)

When waves hit a boundary and are reflected, the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. The angle of incidence is the angle between the direction of motion of the wave and a line drawn perpendicular to the reflecting boundary.

The speed of a wave depends on the properties of the medium through which it travels. For example, sound travels much faster through water than through air. When a wave enters at an angle a medium through which its speed would be slower, the wave is bent toward the perpendicular. When a wave enters at an angle a medium in which its speed would be increased, the opposite effect happens. With light, this change can be expressed by using [Snell’s law of refraction.](https://www.britannica.com/science/Snells-law)

## [Interference](https://www.britannica.com/science/interference-physics)

![examples of interference](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/96595-050-935D1570/waves-wavelength-phase-wave-amplitude-amplitudes-sum.jpg)

[examples of interference](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/96595-050-935D1570/waves-wavelength-phase-wave-amplitude-amplitudes-sum.jpg) When two waves of identical wavelength (shown in blue and yellow) are in phase, they form a new wave (shown in blue and yellow stripes) with an amplitude equal to the sum of their individual amplitudes (constructive interference). When two waves are of completely opposite phase, they either form a new wave of reduced amplitude (partial destructive interference) or cancel each other out (complete destructive interference). Much more complicated constructive and destructive interference patterns emerge when waves with different wavelengths interact.(more)

The waves from two or more centres of disturbance may reinforce each other in some directions and cancel in others. This phenomenon is called the interference of waves. It is easy to see how this may happen. Consider two sources producing waves of the same wavelength and in phase; that is, at their origin the crests of the waves occur at the same time. If a point P is equidistant from both sources, the crests arrive at P simultaneously and reinforce each other. Similarly, the troughs arrive simultaneously and become deeper. The same situation occurs if the distances to point P are unequal but differ by one or more full wavelengths. If, however, the distances differ by half a wavelength or by an odd number of half wavelengths, then the crests of one wave will coincide with the troughs of the other and the intensity of the resultant wave is decreased. When two such waves are of equal intensity, they will cancel each other completely. Intermediate situations arise in those directions in which the distances traveled by the two waves differ by some other fraction of a wavelength, the waves tending either to reinforce or to cancel each other.

## [Doppler effect](https://www.britannica.com/science/Doppler-effect)

![Doppler shift](https://cdn.britannica.com/49/97249-050-2103161D/Doppler-shift.jpg)

[Doppler shift](https://cdn.britannica.com/49/97249-050-2103161D/Doppler-shift.jpg)(more)

When the source of a wave moves relative to an observer, the observer notices a change in the frequency of the wave. This change is called the [Doppler effect](https://www.britannica.com/science/Doppler-effect), after its discoverer, Austrian physicist [Christian Doppler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Christian-Doppler).

Consider a source emitting a wave such as light or sound of frequency ν moving away from an observer at velocity _v_. The successive crests of the light waves will reach the observer at longer intervals than if the observer were at rest, and calculation shows that the observer will receive them with a frequency ν(1− _v_/ _c_), where _c_ is the velocity of the wave. The frequency of the wave will appear to the observer slightly lower than it would if the source were at rest. If the source is approaching, the frequency will be higher.

In sound this effect is an everyday experience; when a blowing horn is passed on the highway, the observer may notice that the pitch of the note seems to change. The Doppler effect for light waves is evident in [spectroscopy](https://www.britannica.com/science/spectroscopy). A shift to higher frequencies is called a blueshift, and a shift to lower frequencies is called a redshift. The redshifted light from other galaxies is evidence of the expansion of the universe.

## [Standing waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/standing-wave-physics)

If a wave is confined to a closed space, it undergoes both reflection and interference. For example, consider a tube of length l. A disturbance anywhere in the air in the tube will be reflected from both ends and produce in general a series of waves traveling in both directions along the tube. From the geometry of the situation and the finite constant value of acoustic velocity, these must be periodic waves with frequencies fixed by the boundary conditions at the end of the tube. The allowed frequencies of the waves in the tube satisfy sin _k_ _l_ = 0; i.e., the allowed frequencies are ν = _n_ _v_/2 _l_, where _n_ is any integer and _v_ is the acoustic velocity in the tube. These are the frequencies of harmonic waves that can exist in the tube and still satisfy the boundary conditions at the ends. They are called the [characteristic frequencies](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-frequency) or normal modes of vibration of the air column. The [fundamental frequency](https://www.britannica.com/science/fundamental-frequency) ( _n_ = 1) is ν = _v_/2 _l_.

The higher frequencies, called [harmonics](https://www.britannica.com/science/harmonic) or overtones, are multiples of the fundamental. It is customary to refer to the fundamental as the first harmonic; _n_ = 2 gives the second harmonic or first overtone, and so on. Approximately the same set of characteristic frequencies hold for a cylindrical tube open at both ends, though the boundary conditions are different.

There are positions in the tube at which the displacement of the air is zero at all times. This cannot take place in a progressive wave; thus, the wave disturbance corresponding to a normal mode is known as a standing wave. The positions of continuous zero displacement are known as [nodes](https://www.britannica.com/science/node-physics), while the positions for which there is maximum displacement are called [antinodes](https://www.britannica.com/science/antinode). The distance between successive nodes is equal to a half wavelength of the particular mode.

**sound**, a mechanical disturbance from a state of [equilibrium](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/equilibrium) that [propagates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagates) through an elastic material medium. A purely subjective definition of sound is also possible, as that which is perceived by the ear, but such a definition is not particularly [illuminating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/illuminating) and is unduly restrictive, for it is useful to speak of sounds that cannot be heard by the [human ear](https://www.britannica.com/science/ear), such as those that are produced by dog whistles or by [sonar](https://www.britannica.com/technology/sonar) equipment.

The study of sound should begin with the properties of sound [waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-motion). There are two basic types of wave, [transverse](https://www.britannica.com/science/transverse-wave) and longitudinal, [differentiated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/differentiated) by the way in which the wave is [propagated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagated). In a transverse wave, such as the wave generated in a stretched rope when one end is wiggled back and forth, the motion that [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) the wave is perpendicular, or transverse, to the direction (along the rope) in which the wave is moving. An important family of transverse waves is generated by electromagnetic sources such as light or [radio](https://www.britannica.com/topic/radio), in which the electric and magnetic fields [constituting](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituting) the wave oscillate perpendicular to the direction of [propagation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagation).

[![Watch a suspended vibrating spring to learn about the propagation of sound waves](https://cdn.britannica.com/61/73161-138-443D5971/propagation-sound-waves-action.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/propagation-sound-waves-action/-68518)

Sound propagates through air or other mediums as a [longitudinal wave](https://www.britannica.com/science/longitudinal-wave), in which the mechanical [vibration](https://www.britannica.com/science/vibration) constituting the wave occurs along the direction of propagation of the wave. A longitudinal wave can be created in a coiled spring by squeezing several of the turns together to form a [compression](https://www.britannica.com/science/compression) and then releasing them, allowing the compression to travel the length of the spring. Air can be viewed as being composed of layers [analogous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogous) to such coils, with a sound wave [propagating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagating) as layers of air “push” and “pull” at one another much like the compression moving down the spring.

A sound wave thus consists of alternating compressions and [rarefactions](https://www.britannica.com/science/rarefaction), or regions of high [pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/pressure) and [low pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/cyclone-meteorology), moving at a certain speed. Put another way, it consists of a periodic (that is, oscillating or vibrating) variation of pressure occurring around the equilibrium pressure prevailing at a particular time and place. Equilibrium pressure and the sinusoidal variations caused by passage of a pure sound wave (that is, a wave of a single frequency) are represented in Figure 1A and 1B, respectively.

## [Plane waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/plane-wave)

A discussion of sound waves and their propagation can begin with an examination of a plane wave of a single frequency passing through the air. A plane wave is a wave that [propagates](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/propagates) through space as a plane, rather than as a sphere of increasing radius. As such, it is not perfectly representative of sound (see below [Circular and spherical waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics/Circular-and-spherical-waves#ref63975)). A wave of single frequency would be heard as a pure sound such as that generated by a [tuning fork](https://www.britannica.com/technology/tuning-fork) that has been lightly struck. As a theoretical model, it helps to elucidate many of the properties of a sound wave.

## [Wavelength](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength), [period](https://www.britannica.com/science/period-physics), and [frequency](https://www.britannica.com/science/frequency-physics)

Figure 1C is another representation of the sound wave illustrated in Figure 1B. As represented by the sinusoidal curve, the pressure variation in a sound wave repeats itself in space over a specific distance. This distance is known as the wavelength of the sound, usually measured in metres and represented by λ. As the wave propagates through the air, one full wavelength takes a certain time period to pass a specific point in space; this period, represented by _T_, is usually measured in fractions of a second. In addition, during each one-second time interval, a certain number of wavelengths pass a point in space. Known as the frequency of the sound wave, the number of wavelengths passing per second is traditionally measured in [hertz](https://www.britannica.com/science/hertz) or kilohertz and is represented by _f_.

## [Amplitude](https://www.britannica.com/science/amplitude-physics) and [intensity](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-intensity)

## Mathematical values

The equilibrium value of pressure, represented by the evenly spaced lines in Figure 1A and by the axis of the graph in Figure 1C, is equal to the atmospheric pressure that would prevail in the absence of the sound wave. With passage of the compressions and rarefactions that [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) the sound wave, there would occur a fluctuation above and below atmospheric pressure. The magnitude of this fluctuation from equilibrium is known as the amplitude of the sound wave; measured in [pascals](https://www.britannica.com/science/pascal-unit-of-energy-measurement), or [newtons](https://www.britannica.com/science/newton-unit-of-measurement) per square metre, it is represented by the letter _A_. The [amplitude](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/amplitude) of a sound wave determines its intensity, which in turn is perceived by the ear as [loudness](https://www.britannica.com/science/loudness). Acoustic intensity is defined as the average rate of energy transmission per unit area perpendicular to the direction of propagation of the wave. Its relation with amplitude can be written as![Equation.](https://cdn.britannica.com/70/14270-004-703564DD/Equation.jpg)where _ρ_ is the equilibrium [density](https://www.britannica.com/science/density) of the air (measured in kilograms per cubic metre) and _S_ is the [speed of sound](https://www.britannica.com/science/speed-of-sound-physics) (in metres per second). Intensity ( _I_) is measured in [watts](https://www.britannica.com/science/watt-unit-of-measurement) per square metre, the watt being the standard unit of power in electrical or mechanical usage.

The value of [atmospheric pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmospheric-pressure) under “standard atmospheric conditions” is generally given as about 105 pascals, or 105 newtons per square metre. The minimum amplitude of pressure variation that can be sensed by the human ear is about 10-5 [pascal](https://www.britannica.com/science/pascal-unit-of-energy-measurement), and the pressure amplitude at the [threshold](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/threshold) of pain is about 10 pascals, so the pressure variation in sound waves is very small compared with the pressure of the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere). Under these conditions a sound wave propagates in a linear manner—that is, it continues to [propagate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagate) through the air with very little loss, dispersion, or change of shape. However, when the amplitude of the wave reaches about 100 pascals (approximately one one-thousandth the pressure of the atmosphere), significant nonlinearities develop in the propagation of the wave.

Nonlinearity arises from the peculiar effects on air pressure caused by a sinusoidal displacement of air molecules. When the vibratory motion constituting a wave is small, the increase and decrease in pressure are also small and are very nearly equal. But when the motion of the wave is large, each compression generates an excess pressure of greater amplitude than the decrease in pressure caused by each rarefaction. This can be predicted by the [ideal gas law](https://www.britannica.com/science/ideal-gas-law), which states that increasing the volume of a [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter) by one-half decreases its pressure by only one-third, while decreasing its volume by one-half increases the pressure by a factor of two. The result is a net excess in pressure—a phenomenon that is significant only for waves with amplitudes above about 100 pascals.

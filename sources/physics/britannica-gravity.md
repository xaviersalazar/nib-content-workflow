**gravity**, in [mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mechanics), the universal [force](https://www.britannica.com/science/force-physics) of attraction acting between all bodies of matter. It is by far the weakest known force in nature and thus plays no role in determining the internal properties of everyday matter. On the other hand, through its long reach and universal action, it controls the [trajectories](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/trajectories) of bodies in the [solar system](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-system) and elsewhere in the [universe](https://www.britannica.com/science/universe) and the structures and evolution of stars, galaxies, and the whole [cosmos](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cosmos-astronomy). On Earth all bodies have a [weight](https://www.britannica.com/science/weight), or downward force of gravity, proportional to their mass, which Earth’s mass exerts on them. Gravity is measured by the acceleration that it gives to freely falling objects. At [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth)’s surface the acceleration of gravity is about 9.8 meters (32 feet) per second per second. Thus, for every second an object is in free fall, its speed increases by about 9.8 meters per second. At the surface of the [Moon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Moon) the acceleration of a freely falling body is about 1.6 meters per second per second.

The works of [Isaac Newton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isaac-Newton) and [Albert Einstein](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Albert-Einstein) dominate the development of gravitational theory. Newton’s classical theory of gravitational force held sway from his _Principia_, published in 1687, until Einstein’s [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics) in the early 20th century. Newton’s theory is sufficient even today for all but the most precise applications. Einstein’s theory of [general relativity](https://www.britannica.com/science/general-relativity) predicts only minute quantitative differences from the Newtonian theory except in a few special cases. The major significance of Einstein’s theory is its radical [conceptual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conceptual) departure from classical theory and its [implications](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implications) for further growth in physical thought.

The launch of [space](https://www.britannica.com/science/space-physics-and-metaphysics) vehicles and developments of research from them have led to great improvements in measurements of gravity around Earth, other planets, and the Moon and in experiments on the nature of gravitation.

## Development of gravitational theory

## Early concepts

Newton argued that the movements of celestial bodies and the free fall of objects on Earth are determined by the same force. The classical Greek philosophers, on the other hand, did not consider the celestial bodies to be affected by gravity, because the bodies were observed to follow perpetually repeating nondescending trajectories in the sky. Thus, [Aristotle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle) considered that each heavenly body followed a particular “natural” motion, unaffected by external causes or agents. Aristotle also believed that massive earthly objects possess a natural tendency to move toward Earth’s center. Those Aristotelian concepts prevailed for centuries along with two others: that a body moving at constant speed requires a continuous force acting on it and that force must be applied by contact rather than interaction at a distance. These ideas were generally held until the 16th and early 17th centuries, thereby [impeding](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/impeding) an understanding of the true principles of motion and precluding the development of ideas about universal gravitation. This impasse began to change with several scientific contributions to the problem of earthly and celestial motion, which in turn set the stage for Newton’s later gravitational theory.

The 17th-century German astronomer [Johannes Kepler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johannes-Kepler) accepted the argument of [Nicolaus Copernicus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Nicolaus-Copernicus) (which goes back to [Aristarchus of Samos](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristarchus-of-Samos)) that the planets [orbit](https://www.britannica.com/science/orbit-astronomy) the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun), not Earth. Using the improved measurements of planetary movements made by the Danish astronomer [Tycho Brahe](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Tycho-Brahe-Danish-astronomer) during the 16th century, Kepler described the planetary orbits with simple geometric and arithmetic relations. During this same period the Italian astronomer and natural philosopher [Galileo Galilei](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Galileo-Galilei) made progress in understanding “natural” [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics) and simple accelerated motion for earthly objects. He realized that bodies that are uninfluenced by forces continue indefinitely to move and that force is necessary to change motion, not to maintain constant motion. In studying how objects fall toward Earth, Galileo discovered that the motion is one of constant [acceleration](https://www.britannica.com/science/acceleration). He demonstrated that the distance a falling body travels from rest in this way varies as the square of the time. As noted above, the acceleration due to gravity at the surface of Earth is about 9.8 meters per second per second. Galileo was also the first to show by experiment that bodies fall with the same acceleration whatever their [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) (the weak principle of equivalence).

# [Newton’s law of gravity](https://www.britannica.com/science/Newtons-law-of-gravitation)

![effects of gravity on the Moon and Earth](https://cdn.britannica.com/81/94581-050-531CAC1F/Effects-gravity-Earth-Moon.jpg)

[effects of gravity on the Moon and Earth](https://cdn.britannica.com/81/94581-050-531CAC1F/Effects-gravity-Earth-Moon.jpg) Effects of gravity on Earth and the Moon.(more)

Newton discovered the relationship between the [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics) of the [Moon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Moon) and the motion of a body falling freely on [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth). By his [dynamical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynamical) and gravitational theories, he explained Kepler’s laws and established the modern quantitative [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science) of gravitation. Newton assumed the existence of an attractive [force](https://www.britannica.com/science/force-physics) between all massive bodies, one that does not require bodily contact and that acts at a distance. By [invoking](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/invoking) his law of [inertia](https://www.britannica.com/science/inertia) (bodies not acted upon by a force move at constant speed in a straight line), Newton concluded that a force exerted by Earth on the Moon is needed to keep it in a circular motion about Earth rather than moving in a straight line. He realized that this force could be, at long range, the same as the force with which Earth pulls objects on its surface downward. When Newton discovered that the [acceleration](https://www.britannica.com/science/acceleration) of the Moon is 1/3,600 smaller than the acceleration at the surface of Earth, he related the number 3,600 to the square of the radius of Earth. He calculated that the circular orbital motion of radius _R_ and period _T_ requires a constant inward acceleration _A_ equal to the product of 4π2 and the ratio of the radius to the square of the time:![Equation.](https://cdn.britannica.com/60/15560-004-4AFAF363/Equation.jpg)

![gravitational force](https://cdn.britannica.com/90/106990-050-7D3C55E4/Earth-force-distance.jpg)

[gravitational force](https://cdn.britannica.com/90/106990-050-7D3C55E4/Earth-force-distance.jpg) Earth's gravitational force weakens with increasing distance.(more)

The Moon’s [orbit](https://www.britannica.com/science/orbit-astronomy) has a radius of about 384,000 km (239,000 miles; approximately 60 Earth radii), and its period is 27.3 days (its [synodic period](https://www.britannica.com/science/synodic-period), or period measured in terms of lunar phases, is about 29.5 days). Newton found the Moon’s inward acceleration in its orbit to be 0.0027 meter per second per second, the same as (1/60)2 of the acceleration of a falling object at the surface of Earth.

In Newton’s theory every least particle of matter attracts every other particle gravitationally, and on that basis he showed that the attraction of a [finite](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/finite) body with spherical [symmetry](https://www.britannica.com/science/symmetry-physics) is the same as that of the whole mass at the center of the body. More generally, the attraction of any body at a sufficiently great distance is equal to that of the whole mass at the center of mass. He could thus relate the two accelerations, that of the Moon and that of a body falling freely on Earth, to a common interaction, a gravitational force between bodies that diminishes as the inverse square of the distance between them. Thus, if the distance between the bodies is doubled, the force on them is reduced to a fourth of the original.

Newton saw that the gravitational force between bodies must depend on the [masses](https://www.britannica.com/science/mass-physics) of the bodies. Since a body of mass _M_ experiencing a force _F_ [accelerates](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/accelerates) at a rate _F_/ _M_, a force of gravity proportional to _M_ would be consistent with [Galileo’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Galileo-Galilei) observation that all bodies accelerate under gravity toward Earth at the same rate, a fact that Newton also tested experimentally. In Newton’s equation ![Equation.](https://cdn.britannica.com/59/15559-004-42067BB2/Equation.jpg)_F_ 12 is the magnitude of the gravitational force acting between masses _M_ 1 and _M_ 2 separated by distance _r_ 12. The force equals the product of these masses and of _G_, a [universal constant](https://www.britannica.com/science/physical-constant), divided by the square of the distance.

The constant _G_ is a quantity with the physical dimensions (length)3/(mass)(time)2; its numerical value depends on the physical units of length, mass, and time used. ( _G_ is discussed more fully in [subsequent](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/subsequent) sections.)

The force acts in the direction of the line joining the two bodies and so is represented naturally as a [vector](https://www.britannica.com/science/vector-physics), F. ## [Weight](https://www.britannica.com/science/weight) and mass

The weight _W_ of a body can be measured by the equal and opposite force necessary to prevent the downward acceleration; that is _M_ _g_. The same body placed on the surface of the Moon has the same mass, but, as the Moon has a mass of about 1/81 times that of Earth and a radius of just 0.27 that of Earth, the body on the lunar surface has a weight of only 1/6 its Earth weight, as the [Apollo program](https://www.britannica.com/science/Apollo-space-program) astronauts demonstrated. Passengers and instruments in orbiting satellites are in free fall. They experience weightless conditions even though their masses remain the same as on Earth.

Kepler’s very important second law depends only on the fact that the force between two bodies is along the line joining them.

Newton was thus able to show that all three of Kepler’s observationally derived laws follow mathematically from the assumption of his own laws of motion and gravity. In all observations of the motion of a celestial body, only the product of _G_ and the mass can be found.

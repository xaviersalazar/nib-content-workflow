**electricity**, phenomenon associated with stationary or moving [electric charges](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-charge). Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter and is borne by elementary particles. In electricity the particle involved is the [electron](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron), which carries a charge designated, by convention, as negative. Thus, the various [manifestations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifestations) of electricity are the result of the accumulation or [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics) of numbers of electrons.

## [Electrostatics](https://www.britannica.com/science/electrostatics)

Electrostatics is the study of electromagnetic phenomena that occur when there are no moving charges—i.e., after a static [equilibrium](https://www.britannica.com/science/equilibrium-physics) has been established. Charges reach their [equilibrium](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/equilibrium) positions rapidly because the [electric force](https://www.britannica.com/science/Coulomb-force) is extremely strong. The mathematical methods of electrostatics make it possible to calculate the distributions of the [electric field](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-field) and of the [electric potential](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-potential) from a known configuration of charges, [conductors](https://www.britannica.com/art/conductor-music), and [insulators](https://www.britannica.com/science/insulator). Conversely, given a set of conductors with known potentials, it is possible to calculate electric fields in regions between the conductors and to determine the charge distribution on the surface of the conductors. The electric [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) of a set of charges at rest can be viewed from the standpoint of the [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics) required to assemble the charges; alternatively, the energy also can be considered to reside in the electric field produced by this assembly of charges. Finally, energy can be stored in a [capacitor](https://www.britannica.com/technology/capacitor); the energy required to charge such a device is stored in it as electrostatic energy of the electric field.

[Static electricity](https://www.britannica.com/science/static-electricity) is a familiar electric phenomenon in which charged particles are transferred from one body to another. For example, if two objects are rubbed together, especially if the objects are insulators and the surrounding air is dry, the objects [acquire](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/acquire) equal and opposite charges and an attractive [force](https://www.britannica.com/science/force-physics) develops between them. The object that loses [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) becomes positively charged, and the other becomes negatively charged. The force is simply the attraction between charges of opposite sign. The properties of this force were described above; they are incorporated in the mathematical relationship known as [Coulomb’s law](https://www.britannica.com/science/Coulombs-law). The resulting force on _Q_ 1 is in the direction of the total electric field at _Q_ 1, shown in Figure 3. The magnitude of the force, which is obtained as the [square root](https://www.britannica.com/science/square-root) of the sum of the squares of the components of the force given in the above equation, equals 3.22 newtons.

## [Superposition principle](https://www.britannica.com/science/principle-of-superposition-wave-motion)

This calculation demonstrates an important property of the [electromagnetic field](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-field) known as the superposition principle. According to this principle, a field arising from a number of sources is determined by adding the individual fields from each source. The principle is illustrated by Figure 3, in which an electric field arising from several sources is determined by the superposition of the fields from each of the sources. In this case, the electric field at the location of _Q_ 1 is the sum of the fields due to _Q_ 2 and _Q_ 3. Studies of electric fields over an extremely wide range of magnitudes have established the validity of the superposition principle.

The vector nature of an electric field produced by a set of charges introduces a significant complexity. Specifying the field at each point in space requires giving both the magnitude and the direction at each location. In the Cartesian [coordinate system](https://www.britannica.com/science/coordinate-system), this necessitates knowing the magnitude of the _x_, _y_, and _z_ components of the electric field at each point in space. It would be much simpler if the value of the electric field vector at any point in space could be derived from a [scalar](https://www.britannica.com/science/scalar) function with magnitude [and sign](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ampersand).

## [Electric potential](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-potential)

The [electric potential](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-potential) is just such a scalar function. Electric potential is related to the [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics) done by an external force when it transports a [charge](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-charge) slowly from one position to another in an [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) containing other charges at rest. The difference between the potential at point A and the potential at point B is defined by the equation![Equation.](https://cdn.britannica.com/59/15959-004-0B16051A/Equation.jpg)

As noted above, electric potential is measured in [volts](https://www.britannica.com/science/volt-unit-of-measurement). Since work is measured in [joules](https://www.britannica.com/science/joule) in the [Système Internationale d’Unités (SI)](https://www.britannica.com/science/International-System-of-Units), one [volt](https://www.britannica.com/science/volt-unit-of-measurement) is equivalent to one [joule](https://www.britannica.com/science/joule) per [coulomb](https://www.britannica.com/science/coulomb). The charge _q_ is taken as a small test charge; it is assumed that the test charge does not disturb the distribution of the remaining charges during its transport from point B to point A.

[moving a charge](https://cdn.britannica.com/28/228-050-3FF1D6AB/charge-Q-paths-B.jpg) Figure 4: Positive charge + _Q_ and two paths in moving a second charge, _q_, from B to A (see text).(more)

To illustrate the work in equation ( [5](https://www.britannica.com/science/electricity#ref-15959)), Figure 4 shows a positive charge + _Q_. Consider the work involved in moving a second charge _q_ from B to A. Along path 1, work is [done](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/done) to offset the electric repulsion between the two charges. If path 2 is chosen instead, no work is done in moving _q_ from B to C, since the [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics) is perpendicular to the [electric force](https://www.britannica.com/science/Coulomb-force); moving _q_ from C to D, the work is, by [symmetry](https://www.britannica.com/science/symmetry-physics), identical as from B to A, and no work is required from D to A. Thus, the total work done in moving _q_ from B to A is the same for either path. It can be shown easily that the same is true for any path going from B to A. When the initial and final positions of the charge _q_ are located on a sphere centred on the location of the + _Q_ charge, no work is done; the electric potential at the initial position has the same value as at the final position. The sphere in this example is called an [equipotential surface](https://www.britannica.com/science/equipotential-surface). The contribution of a charge to the electric potential at some point in space is thus a scalar quantity directly proportional to the magnitude of the charge and inversely proportional to the distance between the point and the charge. For more than one charge, one simply adds the contributions of the various charges. The result is a [topological](https://www.britannica.com/science/topology) map that gives a value of the electric potential for every point in space.

![potential energy landscape](https://cdn.britannica.com/29/229-050-B73CF63B/energy-landscape-charge.jpg)

[potential energy landscape](https://cdn.britannica.com/29/229-050-B73CF63B/energy-landscape-charge.jpg) Figure 5: Potential energy landscape. (A) Potential energy of a positive charge near a second positive charge. (B) Potential energy of a negative charge near a positive charge (see text).(more)

Figure 5 provides three-dimensional views illustrating the effect of the positive charge + _Q_ located at the origin on either a second positive charge _q_ (Figure 5A) or on a negative charge − _q_ (Figure 5B); the [potential energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/potential-energy) “landscape” is illustrated in each case. The potential energy of a charge _q_ is the product _q_ _V_ of the charge and of the electric potential at the position of the charge. In Figure 5A, the positive charge _q_ would have to be pushed by some external agent in order to get close to the location of + _Q_ because, as _q_ approaches, it is subjected to an increasingly repulsive electric force. For the negative charge − _q_, the potential energy in Figure 5B shows, instead of a steep hill, a deep funnel. The electric potential due to + _Q_ is still positive, but the potential energy is negative, and the negative charge − _q_, in a manner quite [analogous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogous) to a particle under the influence of [gravity](https://www.britannica.com/science/gravity-physics), is attracted toward the origin where charge + _Q_ is located.

The electric field is related to the variation of the electric potential in space. The potential provides a convenient tool for solving a wide variety of problems in electrostatics. In a region of space where the potential varies, a charge is subjected to an electric force. For a positive charge the direction of this force is opposite the [gradient](https://www.britannica.com/science/gradient-mathematics) of the potential—that is to say, in the direction in which the potential decreases the most rapidly. A negative charge would be subjected to a force in the direction of the most rapid increase of the potential. In both instances, the magnitude of the force is proportional to the rate of change of the potential in the indicated directions. If the potential in a region of space is constant, there is no force on either positive or negative charge. In a 12-volt car [battery](https://www.britannica.com/technology/battery-electronics), positive charges would tend to move away from the positive [terminal](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/terminal) and toward the negative terminal, while negative charges would tend to move in the opposite direction—i.e., from the negative to the positive terminal. The latter occurs when a [copper](https://www.britannica.com/science/copper) wire, in which there are [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) that are free to move, is connected between the two terminals of the battery.

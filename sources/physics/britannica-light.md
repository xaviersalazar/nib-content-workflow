### What is light in physics?

Light is electromagnetic radiation that can be detected by the human eye. Electromagnetic radiation occurs over an extremely wide range of wavelengths, from gamma rays with wavelengths less than about 1 × 10−11 metres to radio waves measured in metres.

### What is the speed of light?

The speed of light in a vacuum is a fundamental physical constant, and the currently accepted value is 299,792,458 metres per second, or about 186,282 miles per second.

### What is a rainbow?

A rainbow is formed when sunlight is refracted by spherical water droplets in the atmosphere; two refractions and one reflection, combined with the chromatic dispersion of water, produce the primary arcs of colour.

### Why is light important for life on Earth?

Light is a primary tool for perceiving the world and interacting with it for many organisms. Light from the Sun warms the Earth, drives global weather patterns, and initiates the life-sustaining process of photosynthesis; about 1022 joules of solar radiant energy reach Earth each day. Light’s interactions with matter have also helped shape the structure of the universe.

### What is colour’s relation to light?

In physics [colour](https://www.britannica.com/science/color) is associated specifically with electromagnetic radiation of a certain range of wavelengths visible to the human eye. The radiation of such wavelengths constitutes that portion of the electromagnetic spectrum known as the visible spectrum—i.e., light.

**light**, [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation) that can be detected by the [human eye](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-eye). Electromagnetic [radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiation) occurs over an extremely wide range of [wavelengths](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength), from [gamma rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/gamma-ray) with wavelengths less than about 1 × 10−11 metre to [radio waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/radio-wave) measured in metres. Within that broad [spectrum](https://www.britannica.com/science/spectrum) the wavelengths visible to humans occupy a very narrow band, from about 700 nanometres (nm; billionths of a metre) for [red](https://www.britannica.com/science/red-color) light down to about 400 nm for [violet](https://www.britannica.com/science/violet) light. The spectral regions [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) to the visible band are often referred to as light also, [infrared](https://www.britannica.com/science/infrared-radiation) at the one end and [ultraviolet](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultraviolet-radiation) at the other. The [speed of light](https://www.britannica.com/science/speed-of-light) in a [vacuum](https://www.britannica.com/science/vacuum-physics) is a fundamental [physical constant](https://www.britannica.com/science/physical-constant), the currently accepted value of which is exactly 299,792,458 metres per second, or about 186,282 miles per second.

No single answer to the question “What is light?” satisfies the many [contexts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contexts) in which light is experienced, explored, and exploited. The physicist is interested in the physical properties of light, the artist in an [aesthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aesthetic) appreciation of the visual world. Through the sense of sight, light is a primary tool for perceiving the world and communicating within it. Light from the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun) warms the [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth), drives global [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather) patterns, and initiates the life-sustaining process of [photosynthesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/photosynthesis). On the grandest scale, light’s interactions with [matter](https://www.britannica.com/science/matter) have helped shape the structure of the [universe](https://www.britannica.com/science/universe). Indeed, light provides a window on the universe, from [cosmological](https://www.britannica.com/science/cosmology-astronomy) to atomic scales. Almost all of the information about the rest of the universe reaches Earth in the form of electromagnetic radiation. By interpreting that radiation, [astronomers](https://www.britannica.com/science/astronomy) can glimpse the earliest epochs of the universe, measure the general expansion of the universe, and determine the chemical [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) of [stars](https://www.britannica.com/science/star-astronomy) and the [interstellar medium](https://www.britannica.com/science/interstellar-medium). Just as the invention of the [telescope](https://www.britannica.com/science/optical-telescope) dramatically broadened exploration of the universe, so too the invention of the [microscope](https://www.britannica.com/technology/microscope) opened the intricate world of the [cell](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology). The analysis of the frequencies of light emitted and absorbed by [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) was a principal [impetus](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/impetus) for the development of [quantum mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-mechanics-physics). Atomic and molecular spectroscopies continue to be primary tools for probing the structure of matter, providing ultrasensitive tests of atomic and molecular models and contributing to studies of fundamental [photochemical reactions](https://www.britannica.com/science/photochemical-reaction).

Light transmits spatial and temporal information. This property forms the basis of the fields of [optics](https://www.britannica.com/science/optics) and optical communications and a [myriad](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myriad) of related technologies, both mature and emerging. Technological applications based on the manipulations of light include [lasers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/laser), [holography](https://www.britannica.com/technology/holography), and [fibre-optic](https://www.britannica.com/science/fiber-optics) [telecommunications systems](https://www.britannica.com/technology/telecommunication).

In most everyday circumstances, the properties of light can be derived from the theory of classical [electromagnetism](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetism), in which light is described as coupled [electric](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-field) and [magnetic fields](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-field) [propagating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagating) through space as a traveling [wave](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-physics). However, this wave theory, developed in the mid-19th century, is not sufficient to explain the properties of light at very low intensities. At that level a [quantum](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum) theory is needed to explain the characteristics of light and to explain the interactions of light with atoms and [molecules](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule). In its simplest form, [quantum](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/quantum) theory describes light as consisting of discrete packets of [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy), called [photons](https://www.britannica.com/science/photon). However, neither a classical wave model nor a classical particle model correctly describes light; light has a dual nature that is revealed only in quantum mechanics. This surprising [wave-particle duality](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-particle-duality) is shared by all of the primary [constituents](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituents) of nature (e.g., [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) have both particle-like and wavelike aspects). Since the mid-20th century, a more [comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive) theory of light, known as [quantum electrodynamics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-electrodynamics-physics) (QED), has been regarded by physicists as complete. QED combines the ideas of classical electromagnetism, quantum mechanics, and the [special theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/special-relativity) of [relativity](https://www.britannica.com/science/relativity).

This article focuses on the physical characteristics of light and the theoretical models that describe the nature of light. Its major themes include introductions to the fundamentals of geometrical optics, classical electromagnetic waves and the interference effects associated with those waves, and the foundational ideas of the quantum theory of light. More detailed and technical presentations of these topics can be found in the articles [optics](https://www.britannica.com/science/optics), [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation), [quantum mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-mechanics-physics), and [quantum electrodynamics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-electrodynamics-physics). _See also_ [relativity](https://www.britannica.com/science/relativity) for details of how contemplation of the speed of light as measured in different reference frames was pivotal to the development of [Albert Einstein](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Albert-Einstein)’s theory of special relativity in 1905.

## Theories of light through history

## Ray theories in the ancient world

[![Pythagoras](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/127557-050-3757A37F/Pythagoras-portrait-bust.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/127557-050-3757A37F/Pythagoras-portrait-bust.jpg)

[Pythagoras](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/127557-050-3757A37F/Pythagoras-portrait-bust.jpg) Greek philosopher and mathematician Pythagoras, portrait bust.(more)

While there is clear evidence that simple optical instruments such as plane and curved [mirrors](https://www.britannica.com/technology/mirror-optics) and convex [lenses](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lens-optics) were used by a number of early civilizations, [ancient Greek](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Greece) philosophers are generally credited with the first formal speculations about the nature of light. The [conceptual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conceptual) hurdle of distinguishing the [human perception of visual effects](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-eye) from the physical nature of light hampered the development of theories of light. Contemplation of the [mechanism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mechanism-philosophy) of vision dominated these early studies. [Pythagoras](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pythagoras) ( _c._ 500 bce) proposed that sight is caused by visual rays emanating from the eye and striking objects, whereas [Empedocles](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Empedocles) ( _c._ 450 bce) seems to have developed a model of vision in which light was emitted both by objects and the eye. [Epicurus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Epicurus) ( _c._ 300 bce) believed that light is emitted by sources other than the eye and that vision is produced when light reflects off objects and enters the eye. [Euclid](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Euclid-Greek-mathematician) ( _c._ 300 bce), in his _Optics_, presented a law of [reflection](https://www.britannica.com/science/reflection-physics) and discussed the [propagation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagation) of light rays in straight lines. [Ptolemy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ptolemy) ( _c._ 100 ce) undertook one of the first quantitative studies of the [refraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/refraction) of light as it passes from one transparent medium to another, tabulating pairs of angles of incidence and transmission for combinations of several media.

With the decline of the Greco-Roman realm, scientific progress shifted to the [Islamic world](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-world). In particular, [al-Maʾmūn](https://www.britannica.com/biography/al-Mamun), the seventh [ʿAbbāsid](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Abbasid-caliphate) [caliph](https://www.britannica.com/topic/caliph) of Baghdad, founded the [House of Wisdom](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bayt-al-Hikmah) (Bayt al-Hikma) in 830 ce to translate, study, and improve upon Hellenistic works of [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science) and philosophy. Among the initial scholars were [al-Khwārizmī](https://www.britannica.com/biography/al-Khwarizmi) and [al-Kindī](https://www.britannica.com/biography/al-Kindi). Known as the “philosopher of the Arabs,” al-Kindī extended the [concept](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/concept) of rectilinearly propagating light rays and discussed the mechanism of vision. By 1000, the [Pythagorean](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pythagoreanism) model of light had been abandoned, and a ray model, containing the basic conceptual elements of what is now known as geometrical optics, had emerged. In particular, [Ibn al-Haytham](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ibn-al-Haytham) (Latinized as Alhazen), in _Kitab al-manazir_ ( _c._ 1038; “Optics”), correctly attributed vision to the passive reception of light rays reflected from objects rather than an active emanation of light rays from the eyes. He also studied the mathematical properties of the reflection of light from spherical and parabolic mirrors and drew detailed pictures of the optical components of the human eye. Ibn al-Haytham’s [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics) was translated into Latin in the 13th century and was a motivating influence on the [Franciscan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Franciscans) friar and natural philosopher [Roger Bacon](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Roger-Bacon). Bacon studied the propagation of light through simple lenses and is [credited](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/credited) as one of the first to have described the use of lenses to correct vision.

# Early particle and wave theories

[![Galileo: telescope](https://cdn.britannica.com/60/164560-050-21740197/Two-telescopes-Galileo-Florence-Museo.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/60/164560-050-21740197/Two-telescopes-Galileo-Florence-Museo.jpg)

[Galileo: telescope](https://cdn.britannica.com/60/164560-050-21740197/Two-telescopes-Galileo-Florence-Museo.jpg) Two of Galileo's first telescopes; in the Museo Galileo, Florence.(more)

With the dawn of the 17th century, significant progress was reawakened in Europe. [Compound microscopes](https://www.britannica.com/technology/microscope/The-compound-microscope#ref8853) were first constructed in the Netherlands between 1590 and 1608 (probably by Hans and Zacharias Jansen), and most sources credit another Dutchman, [Hans Lippershey](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hans-Lippershey), with the invention of the telescope in 1608. The Italian astronomer [Galileo](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Galileo-Galilei) quickly improved upon the design of the [refracting telescope](https://www.britannica.com/science/optical-telescope#ref44206) and used it in his discoveries of the moons of [Jupiter](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jupiter-planet) and the rings of [Saturn](https://www.britannica.com/place/Saturn-planet) in 1610. (Refraction refers to the passage of light from one medium into another—in this case, from air into a glass lens.) The German astronomer [Johannes Kepler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johannes-Kepler) presented an approximate mathematical analysis of the focusing properties of lenses in _Dioptrice_ (1611). An [empirical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/empirical) advance was made by the Dutch astronomer [Willebrord Snell](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Willebrord-Snell) in 1621 with his discovery of the mathematical relation ( [Snell’s law](https://www.britannica.com/science/Snells-law)) between the angles of incidence and transmission for a light ray refracting through an interface between two media. In 1657 the French mathematician [Pierre de Fermat](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pierre-de-Fermat) presented an intriguing derivation of Snell’s law based on [his principle of least time](https://www.britannica.com/science/Fermats-principle), which asserted that light follows the path of minimum time in traveling from one point to another. The posthumous publication of the Jesuit mathematician Francesco Grimaldi’s studies in 1665 first described what are now called [diffraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/diffraction) effects, in which light passing an obstacle is seen to penetrate into the geometrical shadow. In 1676 the Danish astronomer [Ole Rømer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ole-Romer) used his measurements of the changes in the apparent orbital periods of the moons of Jupiter over the course of a year to deduce an approximate value for the [speed of light](https://www.britannica.com/science/speed-of-light). The significance of Rømer’s [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics) was the realization that the speed of light is not [infinite](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infinite).

[![René Descartes](https://cdn.britannica.com/62/176962-050-4BC9F588/Rene-Descartes.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/62/176962-050-4BC9F588/Rene-Descartes.jpg)

[René Descartes](https://cdn.britannica.com/62/176962-050-4BC9F588/Rene-Descartes.jpg) Philosopher and mathematician René Descartes.(more)

[Seminal](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Seminal) physical models of the nature of light were developed in parallel with the many empirical discoveries of the 17th century. Two competing models of light, as a collection of fast-moving particles and as a [propagating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagating) [wave](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-physics), were advanced. In _La Dioptrique_ (1637), French philosopher-mathematician [René Descartes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rene-Descartes) described light as a [pressure wave](https://www.britannica.com/science/longitudinal-wave) transmitted at infinite speed through a [pervasive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/pervasive) elastic medium. The prominent English physicist [Robert Hooke](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Hooke) studied diffraction effects and thin-film interference and concluded in _Micrographia_ (1665) that light is a rapid vibration of any medium through which it [propagates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagates). In his _Traité de la Lumière_ (1690; “Treatise on Light”), the Dutch mathematician-astronomer [Christiaan Huygens](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Christiaan-Huygens) formulated the first detailed wave theory of light, in the [context](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context) of which he was also able to derive the laws of [reflection](https://www.britannica.com/science/reflection-physics) and [refraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/refraction).

The most prominent advocate of a particle theory of light was [Isaac Newton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isaac-Newton). [Newton’s](https://www.britannica.com/science/newton-unit-of-measurement) careful investigations into the properties of light in the 1660s led to his discovery that [white](https://www.britannica.com/science/white-color) light consists of a mixture of colours. He struggled with a formulation of the nature of light, ultimately asserting in _[Opticks](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Opticks-by-Newton)_ (1704) that light consists of a stream of corpuscles, or particles. To [reconcile](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reconcile) his particle model with the known law of refraction, Newton speculated that transparent objects (such as glass) exert attractive forces on the particles, with the consequence that the speed of light in a transparent medium is always greater than the speed of light in a [vacuum](https://www.britannica.com/science/vacuum-physics). He also postulated that particles of different colours of light have slightly different masses, leading to different speeds in transparent media and hence different angles of refraction. Newton presented his speculations in _Opticks_ in the form of a series of queries rather than as a set of postulates, possibly conveying an [ambivalence](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ambivalence) regarding the ultimate nature of light. Because of his immense authority in the scientific [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community), there were few challenges to his particle model of light in the century after his death in 1727.

Newton’s corpuscular model survived into the early years of the 19th century, at which time evidence for the wave nature of light became overwhelming. Theoretical and experimental work in the mid to late 19th century convincingly established light as an [electromagnetic wave](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation), and the issue seemed to be resolved by 1900. With the arrival of [quantum mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-mechanics-physics) in the early decades of the 20th century, however, the controversy over the nature of light resurfaced. As will be seen in the following sections, this scientific conflict between particle and wave models of light permeates the history of the subject.

## [Geometrical optics](https://www.britannica.com/science/geometrical-optics): light as rays

A detailed understanding of the nature of light was not needed for the development, beginning in the 1600s, of a practical [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science) of [optics](https://www.britannica.com/science/optics) and optical instrument design. Rather, a set of empirical rules describing the behaviour of light as it [traverses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/traverses) transparent materials and reflects off smooth surfaces was adequate to support practical advances in optics. Known collectively today as geometrical optics, the rules [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) an extremely useful, though very approximate, model of light. Their primary applications are the analysis of optical systems— [cameras](https://www.britannica.com/technology/camera), microscopes, telescopes—and the explanation of simple optical phenomena in nature.

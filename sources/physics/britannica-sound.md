**sound**, a mechanical disturbance from a state of [equilibrium](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/equilibrium) that [propagates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagates) through an elastic material medium. A purely subjective definition of sound is also possible, as that which is perceived by the ear, but such a definition is not particularly [illuminating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/illuminating) and is unduly restrictive, for it is useful to speak of sounds that cannot be heard by the [human ear](https://www.britannica.com/science/ear), such as those that are produced by dog whistles or by [sonar](https://www.britannica.com/technology/sonar) equipment.

The study of sound should begin with the properties of sound [waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-motion). There are two basic types of wave, [transverse](https://www.britannica.com/science/transverse-wave) and longitudinal, [differentiated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/differentiated) by the way in which the wave is [propagated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagated). In a transverse wave, such as the wave generated in a stretched rope when one end is wiggled back and forth, the motion that [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) the wave is perpendicular, or transverse, to the direction (along the rope) in which the wave is moving. An important family of transverse waves is generated by electromagnetic sources such as light or [radio](https://www.britannica.com/topic/radio), in which the electric and magnetic fields [constituting](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituting) the wave oscillate perpendicular to the direction of [propagation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagation).

Sound propagates through air or other mediums as a [longitudinal wave](https://www.britannica.com/science/longitudinal-wave), in which the mechanical [vibration](https://www.britannica.com/science/vibration) constituting the wave occurs along the direction of propagation of the wave. A longitudinal wave can be created in a coiled spring by squeezing several of the turns together to form a [compression](https://www.britannica.com/science/compression) and then releasing them, allowing the compression to travel the length of the spring. Air can be viewed as being composed of layers [analogous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogous) to such coils, with a sound wave [propagating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagating) as layers of air “push” and “pull” at one another much like the compression moving down the spring.

A sound wave thus consists of alternating compressions and [rarefactions](https://www.britannica.com/science/rarefaction), or regions of high [pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/pressure) and [low pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/cyclone-meteorology), moving at a certain speed. Put another way, it consists of a periodic (that is, oscillating or vibrating) variation of pressure occurring around the equilibrium pressure prevailing at a particular time and place. Equilibrium pressure and the sinusoidal variations caused by passage of a pure sound wave (that is, a wave of a single frequency) are represented in Figure 1A and 1B, respectively.

## [Plane waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/plane-wave)

A discussion of sound waves and their propagation can begin with an examination of a plane wave of a single frequency passing through the air. A plane wave is a wave that [propagates](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/propagates) through space as a plane, rather than as a sphere of increasing radius. As such, it is not perfectly representative of sound (see below [Circular and spherical waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics/Circular-and-spherical-waves#ref63975)). A wave of single frequency would be heard as a pure sound such as that generated by a [tuning fork](https://www.britannica.com/technology/tuning-fork) that has been lightly struck. As a theoretical model, it helps to elucidate many of the properties of a sound wave.

## [Wavelength](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength), [period](https://www.britannica.com/science/period-physics), and [frequency](https://www.britannica.com/science/frequency-physics)

Figure 1C is another representation of the sound wave illustrated in Figure 1B. As represented by the sinusoidal curve, the pressure variation in a sound wave repeats itself in space over a specific distance. This distance is known as the wavelength of the sound, usually measured in metres and represented by λ. As the wave propagates through the air, one full wavelength takes a certain time period to pass a specific point in space; this period, represented by _T_, is usually measured in fractions of a second. In addition, during each one-second time interval, a certain number of wavelengths pass a point in space. Known as the frequency of the sound wave, the number of wavelengths passing per second is traditionally measured in [hertz](https://www.britannica.com/science/hertz) or kilohertz and is represented by _f_.

## Mathematical values

The equilibrium value of pressure, represented by the evenly spaced lines in Figure 1A and by the axis of the graph in Figure 1C, is equal to the atmospheric pressure that would prevail in the absence of the sound wave. With passage of the compressions and rarefactions that [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) the sound wave, there would occur a fluctuation above and below atmospheric pressure. The magnitude of this fluctuation from equilibrium is known as the amplitude of the sound wave; measured in [pascals](https://www.britannica.com/science/pascal-unit-of-energy-measurement), or [newtons](https://www.britannica.com/science/newton-unit-of-measurement) per square metre, it is represented by the letter _A_. Acoustic intensity is defined as the average rate of energy transmission per unit area perpendicular to the direction of propagation of the wave. Its relation with amplitude can be written as![Equation.](https://cdn.britannica.com/70/14270-004-703564DD/Equation.jpg)where _ρ_ is the equilibrium [density](https://www.britannica.com/science/density) of the air (measured in kilograms per cubic metre) and _S_ is the [speed of sound](https://www.britannica.com/science/speed-of-sound-physics) (in metres per second). Intensity ( _I_) is measured in [watts](https://www.britannica.com/science/watt-unit-of-measurement) per square metre, the watt being the standard unit of power in electrical or mechanical usage.

The value of [atmospheric pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmospheric-pressure) under “standard atmospheric conditions” is generally given as about 105 pascals, or 105 newtons per square metre. The minimum amplitude of pressure variation that can be sensed by the human ear is about 10-5 [pascal](https://www.britannica.com/science/pascal-unit-of-energy-measurement), and the pressure amplitude at the [threshold](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/threshold) of pain is about 10 pascals, so the pressure variation in sound waves is very small compared with the pressure of the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere). Under these conditions a sound wave propagates in a linear manner—that is, it continues to [propagate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagate) through the air with very little loss, dispersion, or change of shape. However, when the amplitude of the wave reaches about 100 pascals (approximately one one-thousandth the pressure of the atmosphere), significant nonlinearities develop in the propagation of the wave.

Nonlinearity arises from the peculiar effects on air pressure caused by a sinusoidal displacement of air molecules. When the vibratory motion constituting a wave is small, the increase and decrease in pressure are also small and are very nearly equal. But when the motion of the wave is large, each compression generates an excess pressure of greater amplitude than the decrease in pressure caused by each rarefaction. This can be predicted by the [ideal gas law](https://www.britannica.com/science/ideal-gas-law), which states that increasing the volume of a [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter) by one-half decreases its pressure by only one-third, while decreasing its volume by one-half increases the pressure by a factor of two. The result is a net excess in pressure—a phenomenon that is significant only for waves with amplitudes above about 100 pascals.

# The [decibel](https://www.britannica.com/science/decibel) scale

The ear mechanism is able to [respond](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-reception) to both very small and very large [pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/pressure) waves by virtue of being nonlinear; that is, it responds much more efficiently to sounds of very small [amplitude](https://www.britannica.com/science/amplitude-physics) than to sounds of very large amplitude. The reference intensity _I_ 0, corresponding to a level of 0 decibels, is approximately the intensity of a wave of 1,000 [hertz](https://www.britannica.com/science/hertz) [frequency](https://www.britannica.com/science/frequency-physics) at the [threshold](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/threshold) of hearing—about 10-12 watt per square metre. Because the decibel scale mirrors the function of the ear more accurately than a linear scale, it has several advantages in practical use; these are discussed in [Hearing](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics/Noise#ref64015), below.

A fundamental feature of this type of logarithmic scale is that each unit of increase in the decibel scale corresponds to an increase in absolute intensity by a constant multiplicative factor. Thus, an increase in absolute intensity from 10-12 to 10-11 watt per square metre corresponds to an increase of 10 decibels, as does an increase from 10-1 to 1 watt per square metre. The [correlation](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/correlation) between the absolute intensity of a sound wave and its decibel level is shown in Table 1, along with examples of sounds at each level. When the defining level of 0 decibel (10-12 watt per square metre) is taken to be at the threshold of hearing for a sound wave with a frequency of 1,000 hertz, then 130 decibels (10 watts per square metre) corresponds to the threshold of feeling, or the threshold of pain. (Sometimes the threshold of pain is given as 120 decibels, or 1 watt per square metre.)

Sound levels for nonlinear (decibel) and linear (intensity) scales

Although the decibel scale is nonlinear, it is directly measurable, and sound-level meters are available for that purpose. Sound levels for audio systems, [architectural acoustics](https://www.britannica.com/science/architectural-acoustics), and other industrial applications are most often quoted in decibels.

Speed of sound in selected gases

[Equation (10](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics#ref-14264)) states that the speed of sound depends only on absolute temperature and not on pressure, since, if the gas behaves as an [ideal gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/ideal-gas), then its pressure and density, as shown in [equation (9](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics#ref-14265)), will be proportional. This means that the speed of sound does not change between locations at [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level) and high in the mountains and that the [pitch](https://www.britannica.com/art/pitch-music) of [wind](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind) instruments at the same temperature is the same anywhere. In addition, both [equations (9](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics#ref-14265)) and ( [10](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics#ref-14264)) are independent of frequency, indicating that the speed of sound is in fact the same at all frequencies—that is, there is no [dispersion](https://www.britannica.com/science/dispersion-physics) of a sound wave as it [propagates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagates) through air. One assumption here is that the gas behaves as an ideal gas. However, gases at very high pressures no longer behave like an ideal gas, and this results in some [absorption](https://www.britannica.com/science/absorption-physics) and dispersion. In such cases [equations (9](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics#ref-14265)) and ( [10](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics#ref-14264)) must be modified, as they are in advanced books on the subject.

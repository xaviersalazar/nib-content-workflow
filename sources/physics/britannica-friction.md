In (A), a box sits on a table, and there are two forces: the force of gravity, _Fg_, which is the weight of the box, pointing downward, and the normal force, _Fn_, of the table on the box pointing upward. The forces are equal and opposite, and thus the box does not move. In (B), a force, _Fapp_, is applied to the object to push it to the right. However, static friction, _Fs_, acts to the left, with the same magnitude as _Fapp_, so the box remains motionless. In (C), the magnitude of _Fapp_ increases until it reaches the maximum static friction, _Fs max_, which must be overcome for the box to move. In (D), _Fapp_ has become greater still, and the box is moving, but with static friction replaced by kinetic friction, _Fk_.(more)

**friction**, [force](https://www.britannica.com/science/force-physics) that resists the sliding or rolling of one [solid](https://www.britannica.com/science/solid-state-of-matter) object over another. Frictional forces, such as the traction needed to walk without slipping, may be [beneficial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/beneficial), but they also present a great measure of opposition to [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics). About 20 percent of the engine [power](https://www.britannica.com/science/power-physics) of [automobiles](https://www.britannica.com/technology/automobile) is consumed in overcoming frictional forces in the moving parts.

The major cause of friction between [metals](https://www.britannica.com/science/metal-chemistry) appears to be the forces of attraction, known as [adhesion](https://www.britannica.com/science/adhesion-physics), between the contact regions of the surfaces, which are always microscopically irregular. Friction arises from shearing these “welded” junctions and from the action of the irregularities of the harder surface plowing across the softer surface.

Two simple experimental facts characterize the friction of sliding solids. First, the amount of friction is nearly independent of the area of contact. If a brick is pulled along a table, the frictional force is the same whether the brick is lying flat or standing on end. Second, friction is proportional to the load or weight that presses the surfaces together. If a pile of three bricks is pulled along a table, the friction is three times greater than if one brick is pulled. Thus, the ratio of friction _F_ to load _L_ is constant. This constant ratio is called the [coefficient of friction](https://www.britannica.com/science/coefficient-of-friction) and is usually symbolized by the Greek letter mu ( _μ_). Mathematically, _μ_ = _F_/ _L._ Because both friction and load are measured in units of force (such as [pounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/pound-unit-of-weight) or [newtons](https://www.britannica.com/science/newton-unit-of-measurement)), the coefficient of friction is dimensionless. The [value](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/value) of the coefficient of friction for a case of one or more bricks sliding on a clean wooden table is about 0.5, which implies that a force equal to half the weight of the bricks is required just to overcome friction in keeping the bricks moving along at a constant speed. The frictional force itself is directed oppositely to the motion of the object. Because the friction thus far described arises between surfaces in relative motion, it is called [kinetic friction](https://www.britannica.com/science/kinetic-friction).

[Static friction](https://www.britannica.com/science/static-friction), in contrast, acts between surfaces at rest with respect to each other. The value of static friction varies between zero and the smallest force needed to start motion. This smallest force required to start motion, or to overcome static friction, is always greater than the force required to continue the motion, or to overcome kinetic friction.

[Rolling friction](https://www.britannica.com/science/rolling-friction) occurs when a wheel, ball, or cylinder rolls freely over a surface, as in ball and roller bearings. The main source of friction in rolling appears to be [dissipation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dissipation) of [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) involved in deformation of the objects. If a hard ball is rolling on a level surface, the ball is somewhat flattened and the level surface somewhat indented in the regions in contact. The elastic deformation or compression produced at the leading section of the area in contact is a hindrance to motion that is not fully compensated as the substances spring back to normal shape at the trailing section. The internal losses in the two substances are similar to those that keep a ball from bouncing back to the level from which it is dropped. Coefficients of sliding friction are generally 100 to 1,000 times greater than coefficients of rolling friction for corresponding materials. This advantage was realized historically with the [transition](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/transition) from [sledge](https://www.britannica.com/technology/sledge-carrying-device) to [wheel](https://www.britannica.com/technology/wheel). **force**, in [mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mechanics), any [action](https://www.britannica.com/science/action-physics) that tends to maintain or alter the [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics) of a body or to distort it. The [concept](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/concept) of force is commonly explained in terms of [Isaac Newton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isaac-Newton)’s three [laws of motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/Newtons-laws-of-motion) set forth in his _[Principia Mathematica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Principia)_ (1687). According to [Newton’s first principle](https://www.britannica.com/science/law-of-inertia), a body that is at rest or moving at a uniform rate in a straight line will remain in that state until some force is applied to it. The [second law](https://www.britannica.com/science/law-of-force) says that when an external force acts on a body, it produces an [acceleration](https://www.britannica.com/science/acceleration) (change in velocity) of the body in the direction of the force. The magnitude of the acceleration is directly proportional to the magnitude of the external force and inversely proportional to the quantity of matter in the body. [Newton’s third law](https://www.britannica.com/science/law-of-action-and-reaction) states that when one body [exerts](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/exerts) a force on another body, the second body exerts an equal force on the first body. This principle of action and reaction explains why a force tends to deform a body (i.e., change its shape) whether or not it causes the body to move. The deformation of a body can usually be neglected when investigating its motion.

Because force has both magnitude and direction, it is a vector quantity. The representation of forces by vectors implies that they are concentrated either at a single point or along a single line. This is, however, physically impossible. On a loaded component of a structure, for example, the applied force produces an internal force, or [stress](https://www.britannica.com/science/stress-physics), that is distributed over the [cross section](https://www.britannica.com/science/cross-section-physics) of the component. The force of [gravity](https://www.britannica.com/science/gravity-physics) is invariably distributed throughout the volume of a body. Nonetheless, when the [equilibrium](https://www.britannica.com/science/equilibrium-physics) of a body is the primary [consideration](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/consideration), it is generally valid as well as convenient to assume that the forces are concentrated at a single point. In the case of gravitational force, the total [weight](https://www.britannica.com/science/weight) of a body may be assumed to be concentrated at its centre of gravity ( _see_ [gravity, centre of](https://www.britannica.com/science/centre-of-gravity)).

Physicists use the [newton](https://www.britannica.com/science/newton-unit-of-measurement), a unit of the [International System](https://www.britannica.com/science/International-System-of-Units) (SI), for measuring force. A newton is the force needed to accelerate a body weighing one [kilogram](https://www.britannica.com/science/kilogram) by one [metre](https://www.britannica.com/science/metre-measurement) per second per second. The formula _F_ = _ma_ is employed to calculate the number of newtons required to increase or decrease the [velocity](https://www.britannica.com/science/velocity) of a given body. In countries still using the English system of [measurement](https://www.britannica.com/technology/measurement), engineers commonly measure force in pounds. One [pound](https://www.britannica.com/science/pound-unit-of-weight) of force [imparts](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/imparts) to a one-pound object an acceleration of 32.17 [feet](https://www.britannica.com/science/foot-measurement) per second squared.

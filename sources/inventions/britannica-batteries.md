- How does a battery work to provide electricity?







A battery provides electricity through an electrochemical reaction. Typically, a battery consists of two electrodes (anode and cathode) and an electrolyte. The anode is usually a metal that gives up electrons (oxidizes), while the cathode often consists of a metal oxide or sulfide that accepts electrons (is reduced).



When the battery is connected to a device, electrons flow from the anode through an external circuit to the cathode. Simultaneously, ions in the electrolyte move to balance the electron flow, maintaining charge neutrality. This movement of electrons generates an electric current that powers the device. The [oxidation-reduction](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-reaction/Precipitation-reactions#ref277189) (redox) reactions drive this process until the battery is discharged.

- What are the different types of batteries used in electronics?







Batteries for electronics come in primary (disposable) and secondary (rechargeable) types. Primary batteries include zinc-carbon and [alkaline](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alkalinebattery) batteries, commonly used in devices like remote controls and flashlights.



Secondary batteries include [lead-acid](https://www.britannica.com/technology/battery-electronics/Other-primary-battery-systems#ref45854), nickel-cadmium, and nickel-metal hydride batteries. Lead-acid batteries are prevalent in automotive applications, while nickel-cadmium batteries are used in portable appliances. Nickel-metal hydride batteries are found in hybrid-electric vehicles due to their higher capacity and greater tolerance of abuse compared to rechargeable lithium batteries. Silver-zinc batteries are used in portable radar units and communications equipment, as well as in aircraft and space vehicles.

- How do rechargeable and non-rechargeable batteries differ?







Primary, non-rechargeable batteries are designed for single use and are discarded once depleted. In contrast, [storage batteries](https://www.britannica.com/technology/battery-electronics/Alkaline-storage-batteries#ref45855) can be recharged by applying a direct current (DC) of the correct polarity, which restores their energy content and power capability, allowing them to be used repeatedly.



During discharge, a battery's voltage drives electrons through a device. Recharging involves applying a DC voltage, higher than the battery's original voltage, in the opposite direction to force electrons back into the electrodes, restoring the battery's voltage and energy level.

- Why is it important to dispose of batteries properly in electronics?







Improper disposal of batteries in electronics can lead to significant environmental and health hazards. Electronic waste (e-waste) contains hazardous materials that, if not managed correctly, can result in environmental degradation through air and water pollution.



Specifically, heavy metals like lead, mercury, cadmium, and arsenic found in batteries can contaminate soil and water systems, posing risks to human health. Informal recycling processes, such as burning wires to recover metal, release toxic chemicals like dioxins, increasing the risk of cancer and neurological disorders. Proper disposal and recycling of batteries, especially through regulated channels, are essential to mitigate these adverse effects.

**battery**, in [electricity](https://www.britannica.com/science/electricity) and [electrochemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/electrochemistry), any of a class of devices that [convert](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/convert) [chemical energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-energy) directly into electrical energy. Although the term _battery_, in strict usage, designates an assembly of two or more galvanic [cells](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cell-electronics) capable of such [energy conversion](https://www.britannica.com/technology/energy-conversion), it is commonly applied to a single [cell](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cell-electronics) of this kind.

![electrochemical cell: basic components](https://cdn.britannica.com/29/329-004-D81FF9CC/components-cell.jpg)

[electrochemical cell: basic components](https://cdn.britannica.com/29/329-004-D81FF9CC/components-cell.jpg) Basic components of an electrochemical cell.(more)

Every battery (or cell) has a [cathode](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cathode), or positive plate, and an [anode](https://www.britannica.com/technology/anode), or negative plate. These [electrodes](https://www.britannica.com/science/electrode) must be separated by and are often immersed in an [electrolyte](https://www.britannica.com/science/electrolyte) that permits the passage of [ions](https://www.britannica.com/science/ion-physics) between the electrodes. The [electrode](https://www.britannica.com/science/electrode) materials and the electrolyte are chosen and arranged so that sufficient [electromotive force](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromotive-force) (measured in [volts](https://www.britannica.com/science/volt-unit-of-measurement)) and [electric current](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-current) (measured in [amperes](https://www.britannica.com/science/ampere)) can be developed between the [terminals](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/terminals) of a battery to operate lights, machines, or other devices. Since an electrode contains only a limited number of units of chemical [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) convertible to electrical energy, it follows that a battery of a given size has only a certain capacity to operate devices and will eventually become exhausted. The active parts of a battery are usually encased in a box with a cover system (or jacket) that keeps air outside and the electrolyte solvent inside and that provides a structure for the assembly.

Batteries are divided into two general groups: (1) [primary batteries](https://www.britannica.com/technology/primary-cell) and (2) secondary, or storage, batteries. Primary batteries are designed to be used until the voltage is too low to operate a given device and are then discarded. After partial or complete discharge, they can be recharged by the application of [direct current](https://www.britannica.com/science/direct-current) (DC) voltage. While the original state is usually not restored completely, the loss per recharging cycle in commercial [batteries](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/batteries) is only a small fraction of 1 percent even under varied conditions.

## Principles of operation

The [anode](https://www.britannica.com/technology/anode) of an [electrochemical cell](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electrolytic-cell) is usually a [metal](https://www.britannica.com/science/metal-chemistry) that is oxidized (gives up electrons) at a potential between 0.5 [volt](https://www.britannica.com/science/volt-unit-of-measurement) and about 4 volts above that of the [cathode](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cathode). The cathode generally consists of a metal oxide or sulfide that is converted to a less-oxidized state by accepting electrons, along with ions, into its structure. A conductive link via an external circuit (e.g., a lamp or other device) must be provided to carry electrons from the anode to the negative battery contact. Sufficient [electrolyte](https://www.britannica.com/science/electrolyte) must be present as well. The electrolyte consists of a solvent (water, an organic liquid, or even a solid) and one or more chemicals that dissociate into ions in the solvent. These ions serve to deliver [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) and chemical matter through the cell interior to balance the flow of electric current outside the cell during cell operation.

Battery usefulness is limited not only by capacity but also by how fast current can be drawn from it. The salt ions chosen for the electrolyte solution must be able to move fast enough through the solvent to carry chemical matter between the electrodes equal to the rate of electrical demand. Battery performance is thus limited by the [diffusion](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diffusion) rates of internal chemicals as well as by capacity.

The voltage of an individual cell and the diffusion rates inside it are both reduced if the [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature) is lowered from a reference point, such as 21 °C (70 °F). If the temperature falls below the [freezing point](https://www.britannica.com/science/freezing-point) of the electrolyte, the cell will usually produce very little useful current and may actually change internal dimensions, resulting in internal damage and diminished performance even after it has warmed up again. If the temperature is raised deliberately, faster discharge can be sustained, but this is not generally advisable, because the battery chemicals may evaporate or react spontaneously with one another, leading to early failure.

The fundamental relationship of electrochemical cell operation, put forth by the English physicist-chemist [Michael Faraday](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Michael-Faraday) in 1834, is that for every [ampere](https://www.britannica.com/science/ampere) that flows for a period of time, a matching [chemical reaction](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-reaction) or other change must take place. The extent of such changes is dependent on the molecular and [electronic structure](https://www.britannica.com/science/electronic-configuration) of the elements [constituting](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituting) the battery electrodes and electrolyte. Secondary changes may also occur, but a primary pair of theoretically reversible reactions must take place at the electrodes for electricity to be produced. The actual energy generated by a battery is measured by the number of amperes produced × the unit of time × the average voltage over that time. For a cell with electrodes of [zinc](https://www.britannica.com/science/zinc) and [manganese dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/manganese-oxide) (e.g., the common flashlight dry cell), one finds that a chemical equivalent of zinc weighs 32.5 grams (1.4 ounces) and that of manganese dioxide about 87 grams (3.1 ounces). The [discharge](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/discharge) of one equivalent weight of each of these electrodes will cause 32.5 grams of zinc to dissolve and 87 grams of manganese dioxide to change into a different oxide containing more [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) and zinc ions. Some of the electrolyte also will be consumed in the reaction. One chemical equivalent of each electrode produces one [faraday](https://www.britannica.com/science/faraday), or 96,485 [coulombs](https://www.britannica.com/science/coulomb) of current equal to 26.8 [amperes](https://www.britannica.com/science/ampere) per hour. If the cells operate at an average of 1.2 [volts](https://www.britannica.com/science/volt-unit-of-measurement), this would yield 32.2 [watt](https://www.britannica.com/science/watt-unit-of-measurement)-hours of DC energy. There are a large number of elements and [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds) from which to select potentially useful combinations for batteries. The commercial systems in common use represent the survivors of numerous tests where continued use depends on adequate voltage, high current-carrying capacity, low-cost materials, and tolerance for user neglect. Better sealing [technology](https://www.britannica.com/technology/technology) and plastics are making further development of all cell systems possible, particularly those using very active [lithium](https://www.britannica.com/science/lithium-chemical-element) for the anode. This situation has yielded commercial cells with as much as 3.9 volts on load and very high current-carrying capability.

Battery manufacturers have designed many different sizes, voltages, and current loads for different specialized applications. In the case of common household batteries ( _see_ table), standard sizes and electrical properties have been established by the American National Standards Institute (ANSI) and the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC). The most common sizes, given in the form ANSI (IEC), are AAA (R03), AA (R6), C (R14), D (R20), and 9V (6F22).

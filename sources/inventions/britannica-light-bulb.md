- What is an incandescent lamp?







An [incandescent lamp](https://www.britannica.com/technology/incandescent-lamp/Introduction#ref1) produces light by heating a material to a high temperature using electricity. These lamps consist of a carbon or, more commonly, tungsten filament in an evacuated glass bulb.



[Thomas Edison](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lamp/Introduction#ref4042) is credited with inventing the first practical incandescent lamp, and the first commercial installation of his lamp was in 1880. Though initially inefficient, incandescent lamps gained popularity because they were safer than gas lamps. The development of the tungsten filament improved efficiency and is still used today. Later, introducing inert gasses like argon or nitrogen allowed for higher temperatures, whiter light, and longer life.



However, incandescent lamps are less efficient than [fluorescent](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lamp/Electric-discharge-lamps#ref4044) and [LED lamps](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lamp/Electric-discharge-lamps#ref4044), leading to many countries phasing them out to reduce energy consumption.

- How does an incandescent lamp produce light?







An incandescent lamp produces light by heating a material, usually a [tungsten filament](https://www.britannica.com/technology/incandescent-lamp/Introduction#ref284742), to a high temperature through electrical resistance. The filament glows, emitting light. To prevent rapid oxidation, the filament is encased in a [vacuum or inert gas-filled glass bulb](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lightbulb/Introduction#ref1). The amount of light and its color depend on the filament's material and temperature.

- What materials are used to make the filament in an incandescent lamp?







The filament in an incandescent lamp is made of [tungsten](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lamp/Introduction#ref4042). Tungsten replaced carbon, tantalum, and metalized carbon filaments in the early 1900s because it has the [highest melting point](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lamp/Introduction#ref4042) of all suitable materials, allowing lamps to operate at higher temperatures and emit whiter light. Early tungsten lamps faced challenges such as filament molecule migration to the glass bulb, which was later addressed by introducing inert gasses like argon or nitrogen.

- Why does the filament need to be inside a glass bulb?







The filament in an incandescent lamp needs to be enclosed in a glass bulb primarily to prevent rapid oxidation. Without the bulb, the filament, when heated by electric current to produce light, would quickly react with the oxygen in the air and burn out. The glass bulb creates a vacuum or is filled with an inert gas like nitrogen or argon, which inhibits oxidation and allows the filament to glow without being consumed. Additionally, the glass enclosure helps to contain the heat generated by the filament, contributing to the lamp's overall efficiency.

- How do incandescent lamps compare to LED and fluorescent lamps?







Incandescent lamps produce light by heating a filament until it glows, while [LEDs](https://www.britannica.com/technology/LED/Introduction#ref1) (light-emitting diodes) emit light through electroluminescence, and [fluorescent lamps](https://www.britannica.com/technology/fluorescent-lamp/Introduction#ref1) use a phosphor coating to emit light when exposed to ultraviolet radiation.



Incandescent lamps are cheap, reliable and readily available, but they are inefficient in their energy use. [Fluorescent lamps](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lamp/Electric-discharge-lamps#ref4044) produce less heat and consume less electricity, about one-quarter of what incandescent bulbs use. [LED bulbs](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lamp/Electric-discharge-lamps#ref4044) use even less energy and last longer than both incandescent and fluorescent options. Due to the energy inefficiency of incandescent bulbs, many countries have banned or are phasing out their use to reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

- What are some reasons incandescent lamps are being replaced by other types of lighting?







Incandescent lamps are being phased out due to their energy inefficiency. They convert only a small percentage of electricity into light, wasting the rest as heat.



More efficient alternatives, such as [fluorescent lamps](https://www.britannica.com/technology/fluorescent-lamp/Introduction#ref1) and [LEDs](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lamp/Electric-discharge-lamps#ref4044), use significantly less energy to produce the same amount of light. For example, a 60-watt incandescent bulb can be replaced by a 14-watt CFL or a 7-watt LED.



Due to concerns about energy consumption and [global warming](https://www.britannica.com/technology/incandescent-lamp/Introduction#ref284742), many countries have banned or are phasing out incandescent bulbs to reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

## Electric incandescent lamps

[![Who really invented the lightbulb?](https://cdn.britannica.com/79/226779-138-249D3D08/did-you-know-who-invented-the-light-bulb.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/did-you-know-who-invented-the-light-bulb/-259499)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/technology/incandescent-lamp/images-videos)

With the development of [electric power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electric-power) early in the 19th century, the only serious consideration for [lighting](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lighting) by [electricity](https://www.britannica.com/science/electricity) was arc lighting, in which a brilliant light is emitted by an electric spark between two electrodes. The [carbon-arc](https://www.britannica.com/technology/carbon-arc-lamp) electric light was demonstrated as early as 1808, and in 1858 English physicist and chemist [Michael Faraday](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Michael-Faraday) devised the first steam-powered [electric generator](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electric-generator) to operate a large carbon-arc lamp for the South Foreland Lighthouse, but the carbon-arc lamp was so bright and required so much power that it was never widely used; it was limited to large installations such as [lighthouses,](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lighthouse) train stations, and department stores.

[![The role of chemistry in Thomas Edison's inventions](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/187039-138-5204FEFB/Edison-Laboratory-experiments-New-Jersey-West-Orange.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/Edison-Laboratory-experiments-New-Jersey-West-Orange/-207762)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/technology/incandescent-lamp/images-videos)

More-practical lighting could be obtained from an [incandescent](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/incandescent) lamp. In 1801 the English chemist [Sir Humphry Davy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sir-Humphry-Davy-Baronet) had demonstrated the incandescence of platinum strips heated in the open air by electricity, but the strips did not last long. Frederick de Moleyns of England was granted the first patent for an incandescent lamp in 1841; he used powdered [charcoal](https://www.britannica.com/science/charcoal) heated between two platinum wires. Commercial development of an incandescent lamp was delayed until a [filament](https://www.britannica.com/technology/filament-lamp) could be made that would heat to incandescence without melting and until a satisfactory [vacuum tube](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electron-tube) could be built. The mercury pump, invented in 1865, provided an adequate vacuum, and a satisfactory [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element)-filament [bulb](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lightbulb) was developed independently by the English physicist [Sir Joseph Wilson Swan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Wilson-Swan) in 1878 and by the American inventor [Thomas Alva Edison](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thomas-Edison) the following year. By 1880 both had applied for [patents](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/patents) for their incandescent lamps, and the ensuing litigation between the two men was resolved by the formation of a joint company in 1883. However, Edison has always received the major credit for inventing the [lightbulb](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lightbulb), because of his development of the power lines and other equipment needed to establish the incandescent lamp in a practical lighting system.

The [carbon-filament bulb](https://www.britannica.com/technology/carbon-filament-bulb) was actually highly inefficient, but it banished the soot and fire hazards of coal-gas jets and thus soon gained wide acceptance. Indeed, thanks to the incandescent lamp, electric lighting became an accepted part of urban life by 1900. The carbon-filament bulb was eventually succeeded by the more efficient [tungsten](https://www.britannica.com/science/tungsten-chemical-element)-filament incandescent bulb, which was developed by George Coolidge of the [General Electric Company](https://www.britannica.com/money/General-Electric) and first appeared in 1908. In 1911 the drawn tungsten filament was introduced. In 1913 filaments were coiled, and bulbs were filled with inert gas—at first [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen) alone and later nitrogen and [argon](https://www.britannica.com/science/argon-chemical-element) in proportions varied to suit the wattage. These steps increased [efficiency](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/efficiency). Beginning in 1925, bulbs were “frosted” on the inside with hydrofluoric acid to provide a diffused light instead of the glaring brightness of the unconcealed filament. The double-coiled filament used today was introduced about 1930. With these improvements, the filament lamp became the principal form of electric lamp for domestic use until it began to lose favour to the more-efficient [fluorescent lamp](https://www.britannica.com/technology/fluorescent-lamp).

General Electric and the [Westinghouse Electric Company](https://www.britannica.com/money/Westinghouse-Electric-Corporation) produced the first commercial fluorescent discharge lamps in 1938, using [mercury](https://www.britannica.com/science/mercury-chemical-element) vapour and [phosphor](https://www.britannica.com/science/phosphor)-coated tubes to [enhance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhance) visible light output. Fluorescent tubes had roughly double the efficiency of tungsten lamps and were rapidly adopted for commercial and office use. In compact form they found growing use in homes in the early 21st century. Because of concerns about energy use and [global warming](https://www.britannica.com/science/global-warming), governments around the world began to [mandate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/mandate) the phase out of incandescent lamps for domestic use. In 2007 Australia announced plans to phase out incandescent lamps by 2010. In the [European Union](https://www.britannica.com/topic/European-Union) the sale and importation of high-wattage incandescent lightbulbs (100 watts or more, frosted or clear) and of all frosted incandescent bulbs were banned from September 1, 2009, and bulbs of lower wattage were scheduled to be phased out over the succeeding three years, the ban extending to all incandescent bulbs by September 2012. In 2007 the U.S. Congress passed legislation calling for a mandatory phase out of incandescent lamps between 2012 and 2014.

## Nonelectric incandescent lamps

Nonelectric incandescent lamps include the [gas-mantle lamp](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gas-mantle). The mantle is a mesh bag of fabric impregnated with a solution of nitrates of cerium and one or more of the following metals: thorium, beryllium, aluminum, or magnesium. The mantle is fixed over an orifice carrying a flammable gas such as [natural gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-gas), [coal gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/coal-gas), propane, or vaporized benzene or other fuel. When the gas is ignited, the mantle fabric burns away, leaving a brittle residual lattice of metal oxides. Light is produced when this lattice is heated to glowing by the gas combustion, although the mantle itself does not burn. Gas lamps may operate without mantles.

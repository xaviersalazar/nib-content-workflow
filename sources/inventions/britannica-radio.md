### What is radio?

Radio is sound communication by [radio waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/radio-wave), usually through the transmission of music, news, and other types of programs from single [broadcast](https://www.britannica.com/technology/broadcasting) stations to multitudes of individual listeners equipped with radio receivers.

### Was radio used in World War I?

Efforts were made to use radio in [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I), but the technology proved unreliable. Wireless sets were available in battlefield [trenches](https://www.britannica.com/topic/trench-warfare), where they were reserved for emergency communication in the event of cut telephone and telegraph wires.

### Where are radio waves located on the electromagnetic spectrum?

[Radio waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/radio-wave) are located at the low- [frequency](https://www.britannica.com/science/frequency-physics) end of the [electromagnetic spectrum](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-spectrum), beneath [gamma rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/gamma-ray), [X-rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray), [ultraviolet light](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultraviolet-radiation), visible light, [infrared radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/infrared-radiation), [radar](https://www.britannica.com/technology/radar) waves, [microwaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/microwave-radiation), and [television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/television-technology) waves.

### Where was radio invented?

The first practical wireless radio communication system was developed in Italy by [Guglielmo Marconi](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Guglielmo-Marconi). Marconi built on the mathematics of physicist [James Clerk Maxwell](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Clerk-Maxwell) and the experiments of both [Oliver Lodge](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Oliver-Joseph-Lodge) and [Heinrich Hertz](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Heinrich-Hertz) to transmit experimental [broadcasts](https://www.britannica.com/technology/broadcasting) from the lab he built in 1894 at his family’s country villa. He developed his practical wireless communication system in 1895, and he filed a patent for it in England in 1896.

**radio**, a form of [mass media](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mass-media) and sound [communication](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communication) by [radio wave](https://www.britannica.com/science/radio-wave) s, usually through the [transmission](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/transmission) of [music](https://www.britannica.com/art/music), news, and other types of programs from single broadcast stations to multitudes of individual listeners equipped with radio receivers. From its birth early in the 20th century, broadcast radio astonished and delighted the public by providing news and entertainment with an immediacy never before thought possible. From about 1920 to 1945, radio developed into the first electronic mass medium, monopolizing “the airwaves” and defining, along with [newspapers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/newspaper), [magazines](https://www.britannica.com/topic/magazine-publishing), and [motion pictures](https://www.britannica.com/art/film), an entire generation of [mass culture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mass-society). About 1945 the appearance of [television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/television-technology) began to transform radio’s content and role. Broadcast radio remained the most widely available electronic mass medium in the world, though its importance in modern life did not match that of television, and in the early 21st century it faced yet more competitive pressure from digital [satellite](https://www.britannica.com/technology/satellite-communication)\- and [Internet](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Internet)-based audio services.

[Barry Alldis](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/23633-005-935DFDAB/Barry-Alldis-show-Top-20-Radio-Luxembourg.mp3) Barry Alldis introducing his Top 20 show on Radio Luxembourg.(more)

Based on the human voice, radio is a uniquely personal medium, [invoking](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/invoking) a listener’s imagination to fill in mental images around the broadcast sounds. More readily and in a more widespread fashion than any other medium, radio can soothe listeners with comforting [dialogue](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dialogue) or background music, or it can jar them back into reality with polemics and breaking news. Radio also can employ a boundless [plethora](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/plethora) of sound and music effects to entertain and enthrall listeners. Since the birth of this medium, commercial broadcast companies as well as government organs have made conscious use of its unique attributes to create programs that attract and hold listeners’ attention. The history of radio programming and [broadcasting](https://www.britannica.com/technology/broadcasting) around the world is explored in this article.

## Radio’s early years

[![Reginald Fessenden](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/152009-050-0D5CCDC5/coworkers-Reginald-Fessenden-radio-station-Brant-Rock-1906.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/152009-050-0D5CCDC5/coworkers-Reginald-Fessenden-radio-station-Brant-Rock-1906.jpg)

[Reginald Fessenden](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/152009-050-0D5CCDC5/coworkers-Reginald-Fessenden-radio-station-Brant-Rock-1906.jpg) Reginald Fessenden (right) and coworkers in their radio station at Brant Rock, Massachusetts, _c._ 1906.(more)

The first voice and music signals heard over radio waves were transmitted in December 1906 from Brant Rock, Massachusetts (just south of Boston), when Canadian experimenter [Reginald Fessenden](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Reginald-Aubrey-Fessenden) produced about an hour of talk and music for technical observers and any [radio amateurs](https://www.britannica.com/technology/amateur-radio) who might be listening. Many other one-off experiments took place in the next few years, but none led to continuing scheduled services. On the West Coast of the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-United-States), for example, [Charles (“Doc”) Herrold](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Herrold) began operating a wireless transmitter in [conjunction](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conjunction) with his radio school in San Jose, California, about 1908. Herrold was soon providing regularly scheduled voice and music programs to a small local audience of amateur radio operators in what may have been the first such continuing service in the world.

The radio [hobby](https://www.britannica.com/animal/hobby-bird) grew during the decade before [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I), and the ability to “listen in” with earphones (as there were no loudspeakers) and occasionally hear voices and music seemed almost magical. Nevertheless, very few people heard these early broadcasts—most people merely heard _about_ them—in part because the only available receivers were those handmade by radio enthusiasts, the majority of them men and boys. Among these early receivers were crystal sets, which used a tiny piece of galena (lead sulfide) called a “cat’s whisker” to detect radio signals. Although popular, inexpensive, and easy to make, crystal sets were a challenge to tune in to a station. Such experiments were scattered, and so there was little demand for manufactured receivers. (Plug-in radio receivers, which, through the use of loudspeakers, allowed for radio to become a “communal experience,” would not become [widespread](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/widespread) until after 1927.) Early broadcasters in the United States, such as [Herrold](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Herrold), would continue until early 1917, when federal government restrictions forced most radio transmitters off the air for the rest of World War I, stalling the growth of the medium.

After the war, renewed interest in radio broadcasts grew out of experimenters’ efforts, though such broadcasts were neither officially authorized nor licensed by government agencies, as would become the practice in most countries by the late 1920s. Early unauthorized broadcasts sometimes angered government officials, as in England, where concern was raised over interference with official government and military signals. [Amateurs](https://www.britannica.com/technology/amateur-radio) developed the means and simply began to broadcast, sometimes preannounced but often not. As they became more [proficient](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/proficient), they would announce schedules—typically an hour or so for one or two evenings per week.

One of the world’s first scheduled radio broadcast services (known as PCGG) began in Rotterdam, [Netherlands](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-the-Netherlands), on November 6, 1919. Other early Dutch stations were operated by the Amsterdam Stock Exchange (to send information to new members) and by a [news agency](https://www.britannica.com/topic/news-agency) that was seeking a new way to serve newspaper subscribers. Another early station appeared in [Canada](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-Canada) when station XWA (now CFCF) in Montreal began transmitting experimentally in September 1919 and on a regular schedule the next year. (The first commercially [sponsored](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/sponsored) stations in Canada appeared in 1922.) The first British station offered two daily half-hour programs of talk and music from Chelmsford (near London) in 1919–20. Concerns about interference with military wireless transmissions, however, led to a shutdown until 1922, when government-authorized stations appeared, including the first London-based outlet. The first Mexican radio station aired in the capital city in 1921, though many in the country had first heard broadcasts from Cuba or [Puerto Rico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Puerto-Rico). By that point, stations had also appeared in Australia (Melbourne, in 1921), [New Zealand](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Zealand) (from Otago University in Dunedin, also in 1921), and Denmark (from Copenhagen, 1923).

[![Interior of the KDKA “radio shack”](https://cdn.britannica.com/87/151187-050-E9D08372/Interior-radio-shack-building-KDKA-Westinghouse-Pennsylvania-October-1920.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/87/151187-050-E9D08372/Interior-radio-shack-building-KDKA-Westinghouse-Pennsylvania-October-1920.jpg)

[Interior of the KDKA “radio shack”](https://cdn.britannica.com/87/151187-050-E9D08372/Interior-radio-shack-building-KDKA-Westinghouse-Pennsylvania-October-1920.jpg) Interior of the KDKA “radio shack,” constructed atop the Westinghouse building in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, October 1920.(more)

Broadcasting got an important boost in the huge American market when about 30 radio stations took to the air in different cities in 1920–21. Most of these developed out of [amateur operations](https://www.britannica.com/technology/amateur-radio), each dedicated to a different purpose. “Doc” [Herrold](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Herrold) returned to the air in 1921, but he soon had to sell his station for lack of operating funds. The [University of Wisconsin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Wisconsin)’s WHA began as a physics department transmitter, but as early as 1917 it was sending wireless [telegraph](https://www.britannica.com/technology/telegraph) agricultural market reports by [Morse Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Morse-Code) to Wisconsin farmers. WHA, the first American educational outlet, probably began voice broadcasts in early 1921, though several other universities soon initiated stations with similar aims. [KDKA](https://www.britannica.com/topic/KDKA) in Pittsburgh, most often [cited](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/cited) as the first radio outlet in the United States, had begun as the amateur station 8XK in 1916, but it was forced off the air in World War I. It reappeared on November 2, 1920, as a “commercial” voice-and-music service operated by the [Westinghouse](https://www.britannica.com/money/Westinghouse-Electric-Corporation) electrical manufacturer to help sell the company’s radio receivers. Westinghouse added other stations in different cities over the next two years, and [General Electric](https://www.britannica.com/money/General-Electric) and the newly formed [Radio Corporation of America](https://www.britannica.com/money/RCA-Corporation) ( [RCA](https://www.britannica.com/money/RCA-Corporation)) soon entered the radio business as well. Detroit’s amateur operation 8MK (which debuted on August 20, 1920) soon became WWJ, the first station to be owned by a newspaper ( _[The Detroit News](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Detroit-News)_). Initially seen as simply another press-supported [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) service, a radio station became a means of hedging bets in case the new medium proved competitive with newspapers.

Slowly, other American stations took to the air, often as [auxiliaries](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/auxiliaries) to the owner’s primary business, such as a retail store, hotel, or record shop. The [deluge](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/deluge) came in 1922 when more than 550 new stations crowded onto the few available frequencies to build on radio’s appeal across the country. Many quickly disappeared as they could not pay the cost of operations (on-air [advertising](https://www.britannica.com/money/advertising) was rare). Equipment was largely hand-built, and most stations operated with less power than an ordinary reading lamp. Initial studio spaces had walls covered in burlap to deaden sound and, along with a [microphone](https://www.britannica.com/technology/microphone-electroacoustic-device), featured a piano that could be used for filling short bits of air time. A few stations experimented with telephone lines to allow two or more outlets to carry (or “network”) an occasional presidential address or sporting event. Audiences were [enthralled](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/enthralled) as radio became a national craze. Magazines, books, and even movies featured or included references to radio broadcasting.

Most other industrial nations began radio broadcasts by the mid-1920s. France (in Paris) and the [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union) (in Moscow) aired broadcasts in 1922. The first continuing Chinese radio station appeared in Shanghai early in 1923, when stations also appeared in Belgium, Czechoslovakia, Germany, and Spain. The pace quickened when [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-Italy) explored radio in 1924, followed by Japan, Mexico, Norway, and Poland in 1925. All these countries varied in how they authorized and organized radio services, with governments usually playing a far more central role than was the case in the United States.

Stations everywhere faced the same basic problem: what to program in order to attract and hold an audience—and how to support a continuing service financially. Radio quickly became popular anywhere signals could be heard, but how best to [utilize](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/utilize) the medium—what to place on the air, or to “ [program](https://www.britannica.com/topic/program-broadcasting)”—remained to be seen. Most early broadcasts were characterized by haphazardness, though two attractions quickly stood out: the warmth of the human voice (at first nearly always male) and almost any type of music, classical or popular, instrumental or vocal. Virtually everything on the air was live because recordings were of poor quality. Thus, a speaker or a musician could easily fill time until the next segment appeared. Only after the first few years did the notion of “programs” develop, with specific times and lengths, beginnings and endings.

# The [Golden Age](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Golden-Age-of-American-radio) of [American](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-United-States) radio

The Golden Age of American radio as a creative medium lasted, at best, from 1930 to 1955, with the true peak period being the 1940s. Writer-producer-director Norman Corwin, one of radio’s brightest talents, ruefully made the point that radio’s most creative era was “the shortest golden age in history.” During its brief heyday, however, dramatic radio thrived and was a vital part of American [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture). As would become true with [television](https://www.britannica.com/art/television-in-the-United-States) in later decades, frequently used expressions from popular programs became part of the [vernacular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vernacular), and people arranged their personal schedules, as they later did with [television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/television-technology), around their favourite programs.

## A new commercial medium

## The need for regulation

In the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), active [broadcasting](https://www.britannica.com/technology/broadcasting) preceded firm government policy. Indeed, as radio became more and more of a business, station owners banded together to seek stronger government licensing [regulation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/regulation). From 1922 to 1925, [Herbert Hoover](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Herbert-Hoover), then secretary of commerce and in charge of radio policy, [convened](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/convened) four national conferences, each of which petitioned Congress to replace the only existing (and obsolete) laws regarding broadcasting, which had been established in 1912 to regulate ship-to-shore transmissions.

Initially all stations in the United States had to operate on a single [frequency](https://www.britannica.com/technology/frequency-band), 833 kilohertz (kHz), and stations in the same area were forced to share time so their signals did not interfere with each another. The addition of two more frequencies, 619 kHz in December 1921 and 750 kHz in August 1922, helped somewhat, but most larger cities had far more than three stations and thus continued to use shared-time arrangements. At Hoover’s [behest](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/behest), most frequencies between 550 kHz and 1,350 kHz were turned over for broadcast use in May 1923. The Department of Commerce, however, lacked the discretion to reject license applications or to enforce frequency assignments. Considerable interference resulted as operators shifted station frequency (and sometimes the transmitter location, by mounting it in a truck) in an attempt to obtain a clear signal.

This lack of self-regulation and mutual cooperation between station operators resulted in increased pressure on Congress to update radio legislation, which was accomplished with the landmark [Radio Act](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Radio-Act-of-1927) of 1927. This act provided basic assumptions that have continued to underpin broadcasting policy in the United States to this day. [Frequencies](https://www.britannica.com/technology/frequency-band) used for broadcasting were to be held by the government, not owned by licensees. A license would be issued only if “the public interest, convenience or necessity” was served. A new Federal Radio [Commission](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Commission) established by the law would define what “the public interest” meant, though broadcasters would be held responsible for the content they provided.

## The role of [advertising](https://www.britannica.com/money/advertising)

Sale of advertising time was not widely practiced at early radio stations in the United States. Indeed, many objected to the commercialization of radio, among them [Herbert Hoover](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Herbert-Hoover), who said in 1924, “I believe the quickest way to kill broadcasting would be to use it for direct advertising.” Strong arguments were made opposing the “invasion of people’s homes with commerce” (although newspapers and magazines had done so for more than a century) on the grounds that it would lead to entertainment programs pitched to the mass audience, thereby limiting radio’s potential educational and social benefits. Searching for operating funds, stations sought government support, gifts from the wealthy, [voluntary](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/voluntary) contributions, or an annual fee assessed on listeners (the latter an approach already adopted in some countries). A few cities or states operated stations as government services.

The [American Telephone and Telegraph Company](https://www.britannica.com/money/ATandT-Corporation) ( [AT&T](https://www.britannica.com/money/ATandT-Corporation)) brought advertising to American radio when their [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City) radio station, [WEAF](https://www.britannica.com/topic/WEAF), began selling time for “toll broadcasting.” Its first radio commercial, broadcast on August 22, 1922, was a 15-minute real-estate ad offering apartments in Jackson Heights, Queens. But acceptance of radio advertising was slow, as broadcasters did not want to offend listeners. Early ads promoted an institutional image in a style later common to public radio’s “underwriting” announcements.

Nevertheless, by the end of the 1920s, radio was firmly established as an [advertising](https://www.britannica.com/money/advertising) medium, which in turn led to air time’s being sold in set blocks, determined by the length of the program. As radio developed, daytime shows such as soap operas and children’s programs generally ran 15 minutes. Dramatic shows and situation comedies, the bulk of prime-time [programming](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/programming), ran 30 minutes each. Hour-long blocks of time were generally reserved for prestigious big-star shows, such as _Lux Radio Theatre_, or for low-rated but esteemed and experimental shows, such as _The Columbia Workshop_.

Many advertisers made themselves known by eventually adopting the practice of combining their name with the name of the star or the title of the [program](https://www.britannica.com/topic/program-broadcasting), as with _Camel Caravan_, sponsored by the [R.J. Reynolds Tobacco Company](https://www.britannica.com/money/RJ-Reynolds-Tobacco-Company), or _A&P Gypsies_, sponsored by the largest American grocery-store chain at the time. Beginning in the 1930s and continuing for more than two decades, a majority of prime-time network programs were actually created by advertising agencies employed by sponsors. For example, during [Bing Crosby](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bing-Crosby)’s [tenure](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/tenure) as host of _[The Kraft Music Hall](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Kraft-Music-Hall)_, the talent and staff were hired by the [Kraft](https://www.britannica.com/money/Kraft-Foods-Inc) food company’s advertising firm, the [J. Walter Thompson](https://www.britannica.com/money/J-Walter-Thompson-Co) agency. The networks merely provided the airtime and studio facilities. Some of the more creative radio talents functioned as their own producers, receiving a budget from the agency out of which they paid the supporting actors and crew. Even these artists were under strict supervision of the agencies, which usually had representatives present during the rehearsals and broadcast.

## The development of networks and production centres

A fundamental shift in American broadcasting came with the realization by the late 1920s that individual stations could easily share the cost of providing programs as a part of a broader network service with national appeal. The first such network was the [National Broadcasting Company](https://www.britannica.com/money/National-Broadcasting-Co-Inc) ( [NBC](https://www.britannica.com/money/National-Broadcasting-Co-Inc)), primarily organized by the general manager of the [Radio Corporation of America](https://www.britannica.com/money/RCA-Corporation) ( [RCA](https://www.britannica.com/money/RCA-Corporation)), [David Sarnoff](https://www.britannica.com/money/David-Sarnoff), who wanted the company not only to manufacture radios but to broadcast as well. On November 15, 1926, NBC made its [debut](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/debut) over 19 stations extending from the East Coast to Kansas City, Missouri. Over flagship station [WEAF](https://www.britannica.com/topic/WEAF) in New York City, announcer Graham McNamee presided over the inaugural broadcast; guest stars included humourist [Will Rogers](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Will-Rogers), speaking from Independence, Kansas, and opera star [Mary Garden](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mary-Garden), singing from Chicago. A new era in radio dawned with this broadcast. Earlier radio stations had a limited sphere of influence, but these “clear channel” stations, operating at 50,000 watts on a frequency unique to their outlet, could be heard across a significant part of the country, and so some early radio personalities gained a measure of regional or national fame. Nationally known radio stars began to exist after the advent of the networks. By the beginning of 1927, NBC had two networks, the Red and the Blue, which totaled 25 stations; more would join.

[the radio show _Amos 'n' Andy_](https://cdn.britannica.com/10/143310-005-D8B5FC5C/episode-Gosden-radio-show-Amos-n-Andy-1928.mp3) A 1928 episode of the radio show _Amos 'n' Andy_, featuring the comedic duo Gosden and Correll.(more)

The most popular early network series by far was NBC’s _[Amos ’n’ Andy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Amos-n-Andy-American-radio-program)_, a daily 15-minute [situation comedy](https://www.britannica.com/art/situation-comedy) in which two white men ( [Freeman Gosden and Charles Correll](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gosden-and-Correll)) acted the parts of two black operators of a taxicab company in Chicago. The program began as _Sam ’n’ Henry_ on Chicago’s [WGN](https://www.britannica.com/topic/WGN) station in 1926 and quickly became a national phenomenon when it made its network debut under its new name in 1929. Although the characters on the show seem insultingly stereotypical by today’s standards, the show was hugely popular with both white and black radio audiences of the time, with theatres often having to interrupt [movie](https://www.britannica.com/art/film) showings and push a radio on to the stage for the evening broadcast.

Early in 1927, a competing network called United Independent Broadcasters was formed. An early investor in the network was the Columbia Phonograph Company, which insisted that the chain be called the Columbia Phonograph Broadcasting System. However, the record company soon sold its shares to a group of financiers that included Leon [Levy](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Levy), whose father-in-law was cigar magnate Sam Paley; before long, Paley’s son William decided to invest his own million-dollar fortune in the new network. [William S. Paley](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-S-Paley) became president of the [Columbia Broadcasting System](https://www.britannica.com/money/CBS-Broadcasting-Inc) ( [CBS](https://www.britannica.com/money/CBS-Broadcasting-Inc)) on September 25, 1928, two days before his 27th birthday, and he would lead the network for more than 60 years. CBS would soon become a major force in radio, although it would take years before it would challenge NBC’s supremacy.

In the late 1930s the [Federal Communications Commission](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Federal-Communications-Commission) (created by the Communications Act of 1934) investigated the potential for a monopoly on broadcasting, and in 1941 it recommended that no single company own more than one network. As a result, NBC decided to sell its Blue network in 1943. The chain was purchased by [Edward J. Noble](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edward-J-Noble), president of the Life Savers candy company. By 1944 it had been renamed the [American Broadcasting Company](https://www.britannica.com/money/American-Broadcasting-Company) (ABC).

In the earliest years of network radio’s [heyday](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/heyday), most of the evening programs were produced and broadcast from New York City. Chicago also soon developed into a major centre of radio production, transmitting many of the daytime soap operas and afternoon shows for children. Detroit’s [WXYZ](https://www.britannica.com/topic/WXYZ) became a major force in 1933 with popular shows such as _[The Lone Ranger](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Lone-Ranger-American-radio-program)_. In 1934 WXYZ joined with the powerful 50,000-watt stations WLW in Cincinnati, WOR in [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state), and [WGN](https://www.britannica.com/topic/WGN) in Chicago to form the Quality Group, an association that was soon rechristened the [Mutual Broadcasting System](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mutual-Broadcasting-System). The network had 19 stations by the end of 1935; by the mid-1940s Mutual had more than 300 stations, more [affiliates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/affiliates) than either of its rivals. Mutual did not own any of its [affiliated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/affiliated) stations, however, whereas NBC and CBS each owned and operated several stations.

In radio’s earliest days, Hollywood did not provide network programming, with rare exceptions. Networks used telephone lines to transmit their signals to affiliates, and because they were designed to be broadcast from the East Coast to the West, AT&T charged $1,000 an hour to reverse the circuits. Powerful gossip columnist [Louella Parsons](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louella-Parsons)—whose show, _Hollywood Hotel_, debuted on CBS in October 1934—surmounted this fee by inducing top film stars to appear on her program for free. The success of this show established Hollywood as a major centre of radio production.

By the start of the 1940s, most of the best-known radio shows came from Hollywood. New York still had a bustling radio [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community), but the Chicago shows began moving to one coast or the other. Detroit’s [WXYZ](https://www.britannica.com/topic/WXYZ) remained a world unto itself, producing popular adventure shows through the early 1950s. Smaller regionally based networks also existed during the 1930s and ’40s, such as the Boston-based Yankee Network, which ultimately became a pioneer in [FM](https://www.britannica.com/technology/frequency-modulation), or frequency-modulation, broadcasting. (Virtually all broadcasts during radio’s peak years were in AM, or [amplitude modulation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/amplitude-modulation).)

## Ratings systems

As radio grew into a commercial force, it became necessary to determine the popularity of particular shows, as this would affect the price of the program’s advertising time. In 1930 the Association of National Advertisers, along with the Cooperative Analysis of Broadcasting, [devised](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/devised) a ratings system called the Crossley Report, for which several thousand people were polled by telephone and asked to recall the programs to which they had been listening. A refinement of this was created by another company, C.E. Hooper. The firm would make random telephone calls to people who lived in 36 major cities. Those who answered were then asked to name the radio program to which they were currently listening, if any. The tally resulted in an estimate of the number of people listening to a particular show; a rating of 14.2 meant that out of 100 people called, 14.2 were listening to a particular program at the time of the call. Along with this “Hooperating,” as it was then known, the audience share of a given program was listed; this was the rating divided by all the sets then being used. Another firm that measured audience response was the [A.C. Nielsen Co.](https://www.britannica.com/money/A-C-Nielsen), which provided thousands of listeners with a mechanical device called an audiometer. On paper tape, a stylus would scratch a signal showing which station a radio was tuned to during every moment that it was in use.

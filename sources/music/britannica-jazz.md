### What are the origins of jazz?

Jazz music originated from a blend of African and European musical elements, developed by [African Americans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/African-Americans) in the United States. It was influenced by [ragtime](https://www.britannica.com/art/ragtime) and [blues](https://www.britannica.com/art/blues-music), incorporating syncopated [rhythms](https://www.britannica.com/art/rhythm-music), polyphonic ensemble playing, improvisation, and unique [timbres](https://www.britannica.com/science/timbre).

### How did Louis Armstrong contribute to jazz?

Jazz [trumpeter](https://www.britannica.com/art/trumpet) [Louis Armstrong](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-Armstrong) was a major soloist who invented [swing](https://www.britannica.com/art/swing-music) and popularized [scat](https://www.britannica.com/art/scat-music) singing. He was the first superstar of jazz and had a profound effect on its development, influencing numerous musicians and introducing jazz to a global audience.

### What distinguishes jazz from classical music?

Jazz performers are primarily creative, improvising composers, whereas [classical](https://www.britannica.com/art/classical-music) musicians typically interpret someone else’s composition. Jazz is characterized by improvisation, syncopation, and unique instrumental sounds.

### How did Duke Ellington influence jazz?

Pianist [Duke Ellington](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Duke-Ellington) was a master composer who expanded jazz by blending thematic material with compositional frameworks. He explored new instrumental [timbres](https://www.britannica.com/science/timbre) and [harmonies](https://www.britannica.com/art/harmony-music), creating a more personal and emotionally deep expression in jazz.

### What is bebop and how did it change jazz?

[Bebop](https://www.britannica.com/art/bebop) developed in the 1940s. Bebop musicians used chromatically convoluted [melodic](https://www.britannica.com/art/melody) lines and faster [rhythms](https://www.britannica.com/art/rhythm-music). It required greater [chord](https://www.britannica.com/art/chord-music) substitutions and improvisation, moving jazz away from dance music to a more listening-focused art form.

**jazz**, [musical form](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical-form), often improvisational, developed by [African Americans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/African-Americans) and influenced by both European harmonic structure and [African](https://www.britannica.com/art/African-music) rhythms. It was developed partially from [ragtime](https://www.britannica.com/art/ragtime) and [blues](https://www.britannica.com/art/blues-music) and is often characterized by syncopated [rhythms](https://www.britannica.com/art/rhythm-music), polyphonic ensemble playing, varying degrees of improvisation, often deliberate deviations of [pitch](https://www.britannica.com/art/pitch-music), and the use of original [timbres](https://www.britannica.com/science/timbre). Jazz has been called America’s greatest art form, although it quickly became a global [musical expression](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical-expression), finding audiences throughout the world and taking influence from artists of many different [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures).

## Defining jazz: “If you have to ask, you’ll never know”

Any attempt to arrive at a precise, all-encompassing definition of jazz is probably [futile](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/futile). Jazz has been, from its very beginnings at the turn of the 20th century, a constantly evolving, expanding, changing [music](https://www.britannica.com/art/music), passing through several distinctive phases of development. A definition that might apply to one phase—for instance, to [New Orleans style](https://www.britannica.com/art/New-Orleans-style) or [swing](https://www.britannica.com/art/swing-music)—becomes inappropriate when applied to another segment of its history, say, to [free jazz](https://www.britannica.com/art/free-jazz). Early attempts to define jazz as a music whose chief characteristic was [improvisation](https://www.britannica.com/art/improvisation-music), for example, turned out to be too restrictive and largely untrue, since [composition](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical-composition), [arrangement](https://www.britannica.com/art/arrangement), and ensemble have also been essential components of jazz for most of its history. Similarly, [syncopation](https://www.britannica.com/art/syncopation-music) and swing, often considered essential and unique to jazz, are in fact lacking in much authentic jazz, whether of the [1920s](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roaring-Twenties) or of later decades. Again, the long-held notion that swing could not occur without syncopation was roundly disproved when trumpeters [Louis Armstrong](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-Armstrong) and Bunny Berigan (among others) frequently generated enormous swing while playing repeated, unsyncopated quarter [notes](https://www.britannica.com/art/note-staff-notation).

Jazz, in fact, is not—and never has been—an entirely composed, predetermined music, nor is it an entirely extemporized one. For almost all of its history it has employed both creative approaches in varying degrees and endless permutations. And yet, despite these [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) terminological confusions, jazz seems to be instantly recognized and distinguished as something separate from all other forms of musical expression. To repeat Armstrong’s famous reply when asked what _[swing](https://www.britannica.com/art/swing-music)_ meant: “If you have to ask, you’ll never know.” To add to the confusion, there often have been seemingly unbridgeable perceptual differences between the producers of jazz (performers, composers, and arrangers) and its audiences. For example, with the arrival of free jazz and other latter-day avant-garde [manifestations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifestations), many senior musicians maintained that music that didn’t swing was not jazz.

## [West Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/western-Africa) in the American South: Gathering the musical elements of jazz

The elements that make jazz distinctive derive primarily from [West African](https://www.britannica.com/place/western-Africa) musical sources as taken to the North American continent by [enslaved people](https://www.britannica.com/topic/slavery-sociology), who partially preserved them against all odds in the [plantation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/plantation-agriculture) [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) of the American [South](https://www.britannica.com/place/the-South-region). These elements are not precisely identifiable because they were not documented—at least not until the mid- to late 19th century, and then only sparsely. Furthermore, enslaved African Americans came from diverse West African tribal cultures with distinct musical traditions. Thus, a great variety of Black musical sensibilities were assembled on American soil. These in turn rather quickly encountered European musical elements—for example, simple dance and entertainment musics and [shape-note hymn tunes](https://www.britannica.com/art/shape-note-hymnal), such as were prevalent in early 19th-century [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America).

The music that eventually became jazz evolved out of a wide-ranging, gradually [assimilated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/assimilated) mixture of Black and white [folk](https://www.britannica.com/art/folk-music) musics and popular styles, with roots in both West Africa and Europe. It is only a slight oversimplification to assert that the [rhythmic](https://www.britannica.com/art/rhythm-music) and structural elements of jazz, as well as some aspects of its customary instrumentation (e.g., [banjo](https://www.britannica.com/art/banjo-musical-instrument) or [guitar](https://www.britannica.com/art/guitar) and [percussion](https://www.britannica.com/art/percussion-instrument)), derive primarily from West African traditions, whereas the European influences can be heard not only in the harmonic language of jazz but in its use of such conventional instruments as [trumpet](https://www.britannica.com/animal/trumpet-snail), [trombone](https://www.britannica.com/art/trombone), [saxophone](https://www.britannica.com/art/saxophone), [string bass](https://www.britannica.com/art/double-bass), and [piano](https://www.britannica.com/art/piano).

The syncopations of jazz were not entirely new—they had been the central attraction of one of its forerunners, [ragtime](https://www.britannica.com/art/ragtime), and could be heard even earlier in [minstrel music](https://www.britannica.com/art/minstrel-show) and in the work of Creole composer [Louis Moreau Gottschalk](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-Moreau-Gottschalk) ( _Bamboula_, subtitled _Danse des Nègres_, 1844–45, and _Ojos Criollos_, 1859, among others). Nevertheless, jazz syncopation struck non-Black listeners as fascinating and novel, because that particular type of syncopation was not present in European classical music. The syncopations in ragtime and jazz were, in fact, the result of reducing and simplifying (over a period of at least a century) the complex, multilayered, polyrhythmic, and polymetric designs [indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/indigenous) to all kinds of West African ritual dance and ensemble music. In other words, the former accentuations of multiple vertically competing [meters](https://www.britannica.com/art/metre-music) were drastically simplified to syncopated [accents](https://www.britannica.com/art/accent-rhythm).

The [provenance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/provenance) of [melody](https://www.britannica.com/art/melody) (tune, theme, motive, riff) in jazz is more obscure. In all likelihood, jazz melody evolved out of a simplified residue and mixture of African and European vocal materials intuitively developed by people enslaved in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) in the 1700s and 1800s—for example, unaccompanied field hollers and [work songs](https://www.britannica.com/art/work-song-music) associated with the changed social conditions of Black people. The widely prevalent emphasis on [pentatonic](https://www.britannica.com/art/pentatonic-scale) formations came primarily from West Africa, whereas the [diatonic](https://www.britannica.com/art/diatonic) (and later more chromatic) melodic lines of jazz grew from late 19th- and early 20th-century European [antecedents](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/antecedents).

[Harmony](https://www.britannica.com/art/harmony-music) was probably the last aspect of European music to be absorbed by Black Americans. But once acquired, harmony was applied as an additional musical resource to religious texts; one result was the gradual development of [spirituals](https://www.britannica.com/art/spiritual), borrowing from the white religious revival meetings that African Americans in many parts of the South were urged to attend. One crucial outcome of these musical [acculturations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/acculturations) was the development by Black musicians of the so-called [blues scale](https://www.britannica.com/art/scale-music), with its “blue notes”—the flatted third and seventh degrees. This scale is neither particularly African nor particularly European but acquired its peculiar [modality](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/modality) from [pitch](https://www.britannica.com/art/pitch-music) inflections common to any number of West African languages and musical forms. In effect these highly expressive—and in African terms very meaningful—pitch deviations were superimposed on the diatonic scale common to almost all European classical and [vernacular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vernacular) music.

That jazz developed uniquely in the United States, not in the Caribbean or in [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) (or any other realm to which thousands of Black Africans were also transported) is historically fascinating. Many Black people in those other regions were very often emancipated by the early 1800s and thus were free individuals who actively participated in the cultural development of their own countries. In the case of Brazil, the Black population was so geographically and socially isolated from the white establishment that they simply were able to retain their own African musical traditions in a virtually pure form. It is thus [ironic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ironic) that jazz would probably never have evolved had it not been for the [slave trade](https://www.britannica.com/topic/transatlantic-slave-trade) as it was practiced specifically in the United States.

Jazz grew from enslaved African Americans who were prevented from maintaining their ancestral musical traditions and felt the need to substitute some homegrown form of musical expression. Such composers as the Brazilian [mulatto](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mulatto-people) priest José Maurício Nunes Garcia were fully in touch with the musical advances of their time that were developing in Europe and wrote music in those styles and traditions. Enslaved African Americans, by contrast, were restricted not only in their work conditions and religious observances but in leisure activities, including music making. Although enslaved people who played such instruments as the [violin](https://www.britannica.com/art/violin), [horn](https://www.britannica.com/art/horn-musical-instrument-group), and [oboe](https://www.britannica.com/art/oboe-musical-instrument) were exploited for their musical talents in such cities as Charleston, [South Carolina](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Carolina), these were exceptional situations. By and large such musicians were [relegated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/relegated) to picking up whatever little scraps of music were allowed them.

Jazz, as it finally evolved as a distinct [musical](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical) style and language, [comprised](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprised) what Max Harrison calls, in the _New Grove Dictionary of Music and Musicians_, a “composite matrix” made up of a host of [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) [vernacular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vernacular) elements that happened to come together at different times and in different regions. This matrix included the field hollers of the cotton plantations; the work songs on the railroads, rivers, and levees; [hymns](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hymn) and spirituals; [music](https://www.britannica.com/art/music) for [brass](https://www.britannica.com/art/brass-instrument) bands, funeral processions, and parades; popular dance music; the long-standing banjo performing tradition (starting in the 1840s), which culminated half a century later in the banjo’s enormous popularity; wisps of European [opera](https://www.britannica.com/art/opera-music), theater, and [concert](https://www.britannica.com/art/concert) music; and, of course, the [blues](https://www.britannica.com/art/blues-music) and [ragtime](https://www.britannica.com/art/ragtime). These last two forms began to [flourish](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/flourish) in the late 19th century—blues more as an informal music purveyed mostly by itinerant singers, guitarists, and pianists and ragtime becoming (by 1900) America’s popular entertainment and dance music.

_[(What’s the difference between blues and jazz?)](https://www.britannica.com/art/Whats-the-Difference-Between-Blues-and-Jazz)_

Ragtime differs substantially from jazz in that it was (1) a through-composed, fully notated music intended to be played in more or less the same manner each time, much like classical music, and (2) a music written initially and essentially for the [piano](https://www.britannica.com/art/piano). Jazz, by contrast, became a primarily instrumental music, often not notated, and partially or wholly improvised. Ragtime had its own [march](https://www.britannica.com/art/march-music)-derived, four-part form, divided into successive 16-bar sections, whereas jazz, once weaned away from ragtime form, turned to either the 12-bar (or occasionally 8-bar) blues or the 32-bar song forms. What the two music [genres](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/genres) had in common was their syncopated (thus “irregular”) melodies and themes, placed over a constant “regular” 2/4 or 4/4 [accompaniment](https://www.britannica.com/art/accompaniment-music).

[![King of ragtime](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/197046-050-540B4102/Scott-Joplin.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/197046-050-540B4102/Scott-Joplin.jpg)

[King of ragtime](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/197046-050-540B4102/Scott-Joplin.jpg) American composer and pianist Scott Joplin.(more)

The years from 1905 to 1915 were a time of tremendous upheaval for Black musicians. Even the many musicians who had been trained in classical music but had found—as Black performers—no employment in that field were now forced to turn to ragtime, which they could at least play in honky-tonks, bordellos, and clubs; many of these musicians eventually drifted into jazz. Hundreds of other musicians, unable to read and write music, nonetheless had great ability to learn it by ear, as well as superior musical talent. Picking up ragtime and dance music by ear (perhaps not precisely), they began almost out of necessity to [embellish](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/embellish) these syncopated tunes—loosening them up, as it were—until [ornamentation](https://www.britannica.com/art/ornamentation-music) spilled over quite naturally into simple [improvisation](https://www.britannica.com/art/improvisation-music). This process took on a significantly increased momentum once the piano rags of such master composers as [Scott Joplin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Scott-Joplin), Joseph Lamb, and James Scott appeared in arrangements performed regularly by [bands](https://www.britannica.com/art/band-music) and [orchestras](https://www.britannica.com/art/orchestra-music).

[![Jazz's first major composer](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/2789-050-6745C146/piano-Jelly-Roll-Morton-1915.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/2789-050-6745C146/piano-Jelly-Roll-Morton-1915.jpg)

[Jazz's first major composer](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/2789-050-6745C146/piano-Jelly-Roll-Morton-1915.jpg) Jelly Roll Morton at the piano, c. 1915.(more)

That the pianist-composer [Jelly Roll Morton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jelly-Roll-Morton) was a braggart who claimed to be “the inventor of jazz” should not obscure his major role in the development of that music. As early as 1902 Morton played ragtime piano in the vaunted bordellos of [Storyville](https://www.britannica.com/place/Storyville), New Orleans’s famous [red-light district](https://www.britannica.com/topic/red-light-district). Later he began working as an itinerant musician, crisscrossing the South several times and eventually working his way to [Los Angeles](https://www.britannica.com/place/Los-Angeles-California), where he was based for several years. As the first major composer of jazz, Morton seems to have [assimilated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/assimilated) (like a master chef making a great New Orleans [bouillabaisse](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bouillabaisse)) most of the above-mentioned matrix, particularly blues and ragtime, into a single new, distinct, [coherent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/coherent) musical style. Others, such as soprano saxophonist [Sidney Bechet](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sidney-Bechet), trombonist [Kid Ory](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kid-Ory), and cornetists [Bunk Johnson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bunk-Johnson) and Freddie Keppard—four of the most gifted early jazz musicians—arrived at similar conclusions before 1920.

Johnson and others regarded themselves as ragtime musicians. In truth, in the cases of many musicians of that generation—both Black and white—who grew up with ragtime, the listener would be hard put to determine when their playing turned from embellished rags to improvisatory jazz. Musicians confirmed the tenuousness and variety of these early developments in statements such as that of reedman Buster Bailey (speaking of the years before 1920): “I…was [embellishing](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/embellishing) around the melody. At that time \[1917–18\] I wouldn’t have known what they meant by improvisation. But embellishment was a phrase I understood.” And reedman Garvin Bushell said, “We didn’t call the music jazz when I was growing up \[in Springfield, Ohio\].…Ragtime piano was the major influence in that section of the country.…The change to jazz began around 1912 to 1915.”

## Ragtime into jazz: The birth of jazz in [New Orleans](https://www.britannica.com/art/New-Orleans-style)

In spite of the wide dissemination and geographic distribution of these diverse musical traditions, New Orleans was where a distinctive, coherent jazz style evolved. Between 1910 and 1915 a systematization of instrumental functions within an essentially [collective](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/collective) ensemble took shape, as did a regularization of the repertory. Despite the fact that a limited set of instruments was available to Black musicians (at that time, typically, [cornet](https://www.britannica.com/art/cornet), [clarinet](https://www.britannica.com/art/clarinet), [trombone](https://www.britannica.com/art/trombone), [tuba](https://www.britannica.com/art/tuba) or [bass](https://www.britannica.com/art/double-bass), [piano](https://www.britannica.com/art/piano), [banjo](https://www.britannica.com/art/banjo-musical-instrument), and [drums](https://www.britannica.com/art/drum-musical-instrument)—the [saxophone](https://www.britannica.com/art/saxophone) did not become common in jazz for about another decade), they arrived at a brilliant solution emphasizing independent but harmonically linked and simultaneous lines. Each of the seven instruments was assigned a clearly defined individual role in the established polyphonic collective ensemble. Thus, the [cornet](https://www.britannica.com/art/cornet) was responsible for stating and occasionally embellishing the [thematic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/thematic) material—the tune—in the middle range, the [clarinet](https://www.britannica.com/art/clarinet) performed [obbligato](https://www.britannica.com/art/obbligato) or [descant](https://www.britannica.com/art/descant) functions in a high register, the [trombone](https://www.britannica.com/art/trombone) offered [contrapuntal](https://www.britannica.com/art/counterpoint-music) asides in the tenor or baritone range, and the four rhythm instruments provided a unified harmonic foundation.

That this formation, which emphasized independent but harmonically linked simultaneous lines, was not only a brilliant solution but a necessity is confirmed by the inability in those early years of most players to read music. It was not long before musicians began to expand upon these materials and to improvise fresh new melodies and obbligatos of their own making. However, these explorations remained within the collective ensemble concept of New Orleans jazz. Few musicians before 1925 could have created independent, extended, improvised solos. And when the solo as an [integral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integral) element of a jazz performance arrived, the New Orleans format of a tightly [integrated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrated) ensemble improvisation went out of fashion.

By approximately 1915 New Orleans had produced a host of remarkable musicians, mostly cornet and clarinet players, such as the legendary [Buddy Bolden](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Buddy-Bolden) (legendary in part because he never recorded), Buddy Petit, Freddie Keppard, Bunk Johnson, and Sidney Bechet. Most New Orleans musicians, including scores of pianists, found steady employment in the entertainment palaces of [Storyville](https://www.britannica.com/place/Storyville), where, incidentally, the term _jazz_, initially spelled “jass,” was the commonly used [slang](https://www.britannica.com/topic/slang) word for [sexual intercourse](https://www.britannica.com/science/sexual-intercourse). It is [ironic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ironic) that the first jazz recordings were made in [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City) on January 30, 1917, by a second-rate group of white musicians from New Orleans called the [Original Dixieland Jazz Band](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Original-Dixieland-Jazz-Band). Those recordings, with their entertaining but substanceless barnyard sound effects, present a misleading picture of true New Orleans jazz.

New Orleans was not the only place where jazz was being developed. Depending on how narrowly jazz is defined, some early form of it was practiced in places as far-flung as Los Angeles; Kansas City, Missouri; Denver and the Colorado mining towns—not to mention [Baltimore](https://www.britannica.com/place/Baltimore), Maryland; and New York City. The two last-mentioned cities were major centers of ragtime, early pre-stride piano, [vaudeville](https://www.britannica.com/art/vaudeville) entertainment, large-sized dance orchestras, and musical theater, including theater created exclusively by Black performers. Several other at least embryonic jazz groups and musicians were active in [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state) during 1913–19, such as [James Reese Europe](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Reese-Europe) and his various orchestras, Earl Fuller’s Jass Band, Ford Dabney’s band, and the pianists [James P. Johnson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-P-Johnson), Abba Labba, and [Willie “The Lion” Smith](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Willie-Smith-American-jazz-musician).

The closing of Storyville in 1917 was a disaster for New Orleans musicians, many of whom went on to play in Mississippi [riverboat](https://www.britannica.com/art/showboat-theater) orchestras; Fate Marable’s orchestra was the best and most famous of these and included, at times, the young Louis Armstrong. Others headed directly north to [Chicago](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chicago-film-by-Marshall), which rapidly became the jazz capital of the United States. [King Oliver](https://www.britannica.com/biography/King-Oliver), the much-heralded cornet champion of New Orleans, migrated to Chicago in 1918, and in 1922 he sent for his most talented [disciple](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disciple), Armstrong, to join his Creole Jazz Band as second cornetist. The two made history and astounded audiences with their slyly worked out duet breaks, and Armstrong had a chance to cut his musical teeth by freely improvising melodic [counterpoint](https://www.britannica.com/art/counterpoint-music) to Oliver’s lead cornet. More important still, Oliver’s band was able to forge a remarkably unified and [disciplined](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disciplined) style, [integrating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrating) at a very high level the players’ collective and individual instrumental skills, all couched in an irresistible, wonderfully stately, rolling momentum.

## The cornetist breaks away: [Louis Armstrong](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-Armstrong) and the invention of swing

[![Louis Armstrong, 1946](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/190154-050-A19D6219/Louis-Armstrong-1946.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/190154-050-A19D6219/Louis-Armstrong-1946.jpg)

[Louis Armstrong, 1946](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/190154-050-A19D6219/Louis-Armstrong-1946.jpg) Jazz great Louis Armstrong single-handedly invented the style of jazz that came to be called swing.(more)

In late 1924 Armstrong was wooed away by [Fletcher Henderson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Fletcher-Henderson) in New York City. In his year there Armstrong matured into a major soloist and at the same time developed—indeed, single-handedly invented—a compelling, propulsive, rhythmic inflection in his playing that came to be called [swing](https://www.britannica.com/art/swing-music). Early examples of this feeling can be heard in Henderson band recordings and even more clearly on Armstrong’s [Hot Five](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hot-Five) and Hot Seven recordings of 1926–27—e.g., “Potato Head Blues,” “Big Butter and Egg Man,” “S.O.L. Blues,” “Hotter than That,” and “Muggles.” In effect, Armstrong taught the whole Henderson band, including the redoubtable [tenor](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/tenor) saxophonist [Coleman Hawkins](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Coleman-Hawkins), how to swing.

More than that, Armstrong taught the whole world about swing and had a profound effect on the development of jazz that continues to be felt and heard. In that sense alone he can be considered the most influential jazz musician of all time. And beyond his artistic and technical prowess, Armstrong should be remembered as the first superstar of jazz. By the late [1920s](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roaring-Twenties), famous on recordings and in theaters, he more than anyone else carried the message of jazz to America; eventually, as entertainer supreme and jazz ambassador at large, he introduced jazz to the whole world. In this crusade Armstrong’s [unique](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/unique) [singing](https://www.britannica.com/art/singing) style, in essence a vocalization of his improvisatory [trumpet](https://www.britannica.com/art/trumpet) playing, played a crucial role. By often singing without words or texts, he popularized what came to be called [scat](https://www.britannica.com/art/scat-music), a universally comprehensible art form that needed no translation.

After Armstrong’s spectacular breakthrough recordings, such as “West End Blues” (1928), he embarked on a solo career for 10 years, fronting bands whose general mediocrity made him sound by comparison even more brilliant. In the 1940s he formed the Armstrong All-Stars, a group of older New Orleans-style musicians that included trombonist [Jack Teagarden](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jack-Teagarden). Although by then well past his prime, Armstrong, through his physical [vitality](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/vitality) and uncompromisingly high musical standards, was able to preserve his art almost to the end of his life in 1971.

That Armstrong’s playing, both technically and conceptually, was many levels above that of most of his contemporaries can be heard on virtually every recording he made between 1925 and 1940, whether he was paired with other soloists or with orchestras. He exerted a wide-ranging influence on all manner of players—not only trumpeters but trombonists, saxophonists, singers (such as [Billie Holiday](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Billie-Holiday)), and even pianists (such as [Earl Hines](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Earl-Hines) and [Teddy Wilson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Teddy-Wilson)). Armstrong’s influence was also absorbed by white musicians, including some of the better ensembles of the time, such as the [New Orleans Rhythm Kings](https://www.britannica.com/topic/New-Orleans-Rhythm-Kings), Red Nichols and his Five Pennies, and, above all, the outstandingly gifted [Bix Beiderbecke](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bix-Beiderbecke). Inheriting a lyrical, [romantic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/romantic) bent from his German background, Beiderbecke presented another view of the Armstrong revolution, not only in his superb recorded improvisations of “I’m Coming Virginia” and “ [Singin’ the Blues](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Singin-the-Blues)” (both 1927) but also in such pieces as the simply stated, virtually unimprovised “Ol’ Man River” (1928).

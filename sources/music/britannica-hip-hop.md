### What are the four main elements of hip-hop?

While there is some debate over the number of elements of [hip-hop](https://www.britannica.com/art/hip-hop), there are four elements that are considered to be its pillars: [deejaying](https://www.britannica.com/art/disc-jockey), or “turntabling”; [rapping](https://www.britannica.com/art/rap), also known as “MCing” (emceeing) or “rhyming”; [graffiti](https://www.britannica.com/art/graffiti-art) painting, also known as “graf” or “writing”; and [break dancing](https://www.britannica.com/art/break-dance), or “B-boying,” which encompasses hip-hop dance, style, and attitude, along with the sort of virile body language that philosopher [Cornel West](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cornel-West) described as “postural semantics.” Many also cite a fifth essential component: “knowledge of self/consciousness.” Other suggested elements include street [fashion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fashion-society) and language.

### How did hip-hop get its name?

There are various explanations for the source of the term _[hip-hop](https://www.britannica.com/art/hip-hop)_. However, the most popular one involves Keith (“Keef Cowboy”) Wiggins, a member of the seminal [rap](https://www.britannica.com/art/rap) group [Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Grandmaster-Flash-and-the-Furious-Five). The rapper used the words _hip/hop/hip/hop_, imitating the sound of soldiers marching, in reference to a friend who had joined the army. According to some accounts, Kevin (“Lovebug Starski”) Smith was with Wiggins and helped create the phrase. _Hip-hop_ was subsequently popularized in songs, notably [the Sugarhill Gang](https://www.britannica.com/topic/the-Sugarhill-Gang)’s 1979 song “Rapper’s Delight.”

### Who are the founders of hip-hop?

While a number of people were influential in the creation of [hip-hop](https://www.britannica.com/art/hip-hop), much credit is given to [DJ Kool Herc](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kool-Herc) (Clive Campbell), a Jamaican American who was the first major hip-hop [disc jockey](https://www.britannica.com/art/disc-jockey). At a back-to-school party in the [Bronx](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bronx-borough-New-York-City), New York City, on August 11, 1973, he introduced the technique of playing the same album on two [turntables](https://www.britannica.com/technology/turntable) and extending the [drum](https://www.britannica.com/art/drum-musical-instrument) section (which became known as the breakbeat). Many recognize this night as the birth of hip-hop. Other pioneering hip-hop deejays include [Afrika Bambaataa](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Afrika-Bambaataa) and [Grandmaster Flash](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Grandmaster-Flash-and-the-Furious-Five). The three men are often called the “holy trinity” of early hip-hop.

### What was the first major hip-hop song?

Although not the first [hip-hop](https://www.britannica.com/art/hip-hop) song, [the Sugarhill Gang](https://www.britannica.com/topic/the-Sugarhill-Gang)’s “Rapper’s Delight” (1979) is considered the first significant single of the genre. Within weeks of its release, it became a chart-topping phenomenon and gave its name to a new genre of [pop music](https://www.britannica.com/art/popular-music). Part of its crossover appeal was attributed to its lighthearted lyrics and its infectious, danceable beat, which was lifted from Chic’s [disco](https://www.britannica.com/art/disco) hit “Good Times.”

**Hip-hop** is a global cultural movement that attained widespread popularity in the 1980s and ’90s after its birth in [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City) in the 1970s. It is also the backing music for [rap](https://www.britannica.com/art/rap), the musical style incorporating rhythmic and rhyming speech that became the movement’s most lasting and influential art form.

## Origins and the old school

Although widely considered a synonym for rap music, the term _hip-hop_ refers to a complex [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) [comprising](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprising) four elements: [deejaying](https://www.britannica.com/art/disc-jockey), or “turntabling”; rapping, also known as “MCing” or “rhyming”; [graffiti](https://www.britannica.com/art/graffiti-art) painting, also known as “graf” or “writing”; and “B-boying,” which [encompasses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompasses) hip-hop dance, style, and attitude, along with the sort of virile body language that philosopher [Cornel West](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cornel-West) described as “postural semantics.” A fifth element, “knowledge of self/consciousness,” is sometimes added to the list of hip-hop elements, particularly by socially conscious hip-hop artists and scholars.

Hip-hop originated in the predominantly [African American](https://www.britannica.com/topic/African-Americans) and economically depressed South [Bronx](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bronx-borough-New-York-City) section of New York City in the late 1970s. As the hip-hop movement began at society’s margins, its origins are shrouded in [myth](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myth), [enigma](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enigma), and obfuscation. Specifically, it emerged during an era of urban decline marked by the prevalence of [gangs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/gang-crime) and drugs and the wide-scale decimation of homes throughout the borough. The latter phenomenon is remembered as “the burning of the Bronx,” in which [arsonists](https://www.britannica.com/topic/arson) torched apartment buildings so that landlords could collect [insurance](https://www.britannica.com/money/insurance) money, all of which was more or less sanctioned by the city’s leaders. During this time [New York’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state) weakened [infrastructure](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infrastructure) was also hit by power outages, cuts to public services, and riots, as in a massive blackout in the summer of 1977. In the mythology of hip-hop’s development, the Bronx’s Black and Latino youth responded to this discontent by finding a creative outlet in music, art, and dance.

Graffiti and [break dancing](https://www.britannica.com/art/break-dance), the aspects of the culture that first caught public attention, had the least lasting effect. Reputedly, the graffiti movement was started about 1972 by a Greek American teenager who signed, or “tagged,” Taki 183 (his name and street, 183rd Street) on walls throughout the New York City [subway](https://www.britannica.com/technology/subway) system. By 1975 youths in the Bronx, [Queens](https://www.britannica.com/place/Queens-New-York), and [Brooklyn](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brooklyn-borough-New-York-City) were stealing into train yards under cover of darkness to spray-paint colorful mural-size renderings of their names, imagery from underground [comics](https://www.britannica.com/art/comic-book) and [television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/television-technology), and even [Andy Warhol](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Andy-Warhol)-like Campbell’s soup cans onto the sides of subway cars. Soon, influential art dealers in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe), and [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) were displaying graffiti in major galleries. New York City’s Metropolitan Transit Authority responded with dogs, barbed-wire fences, paint-removing acid baths, and undercover [police](https://www.britannica.com/topic/police) squads.

The beginnings of the dancing, rapping, and deejaying components of hip-hop were bound together by the shared [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) in which these art forms evolved. The first major hip-hop deejay was [DJ Kool Herc](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kool-Herc) (Clive Campbell), an 18-year-old immigrant who introduced the huge sound systems of his native [Jamaica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jamaica) to inner-city parties. Using two turntables, he melded percussive fragments from older records with popular dance songs to create a continuous flow of music. Kool Herc and other pioneering hip-hop deejays such as Grand Wizard Theodore, [Afrika Bambaataa](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Afrika-Bambaataa), and [Grandmaster Flash](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Grandmaster-Flash-and-the-Furious-Five) isolated and extended the break beat (the part of a dance record where all sounds but the [drums](https://www.britannica.com/art/drum-musical-instrument) drop out), stimulating improvisational dancing. Contests developed in which the best dancers created break dancing, a style with a [repertoire](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/repertoire) of acrobatic and occasionally airborne moves, including gravity-defying headspins and backspins.

In the meantime, deejays developed new techniques for [turntable](https://www.britannica.com/technology/turntable) manipulation. Needle dropping, created by Grandmaster Flash, prolonged short drum breaks by playing two copies of a record simultaneously and moving the needle on one turntable back to the start of the break while the other played. Sliding the record back and forth underneath the needle created the rhythmic effect called “scratching.”

# Old-School Hip-Hop

1 of 8

Kool Herc was widely credited as the father of modern rapping for his spoken interjections over records, but among the wide variety of oratorical precedents cited for MCing are the epic histories of West African [griots](https://www.britannica.com/art/griot), talking [blues](https://www.britannica.com/art/blues-music) songs, jailhouse toasts (long rhyming poems recounting outlandish deeds and misdeeds), and the dozens (the ritualized word game based on exchanging insults, usually about members of the opponent’s family). Other influences include the hipster-jive announcing styles of 1950s [rhythm-and-blues](https://www.britannica.com/art/rhythm-and-blues) deejays such as [Jocko Henderson](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jocko-Henderson-1688361); the [Black power](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Black-Power-movement) poetry of [Amiri Baraka](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Amiri-Baraka), [Gil Scott-Heron](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gil-Scott-Heron), and [the Last Poets](https://www.britannica.com/topic/the-Last-Poets); rapping sections in recordings by [soul](https://www.britannica.com/art/soul-music) artist [Isaac Hayes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isaac-Hayes) and [funk](https://www.britannica.com/art/funk) [legend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legend) [George Clinton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Clinton-American-musician); and the Jamaican style of rhythmized speech known as toasting.

Rap first came to national prominence in the United States with the release of [the Sugarhill Gang](https://www.britannica.com/topic/the-Sugarhill-Gang)’s song “ [Rapper’s Delight](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rappers-Delight)” (1979) on the independent African American-owned label [Sugar Hill](https://www.britannica.com/money/Sugar-Hill-Records-Rappers-Delight-1688493), helmed by coproducer Sylvia Robinson. Within weeks of its release, it had become a chart-topping phenomenon and given its name to a new [genre](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/genre) of [pop music](https://www.britannica.com/art/popular-music). The major pioneers of rapping were [Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Grandmaster-Flash-and-the-Furious-Five), Kurtis Blow, and the Cold Crush Brothers, whose Grandmaster Caz is controversially considered by some to be the true author of some of the strongest lyrics in “Rapper’s Delight.” These early MCs and deejays [constituted](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituted) rap’s old school.

Old school hip-hop also established the themes that soon epitomized the genre. Hip-hop’s pioneers not only rapped about contemporary social problems but also offered humorous takes on the age-old battle of the sexes in their lyrics. They produced timeless party anthems, bragged about riches and success (or documented “the hustle”), and expressed local pride.

In the mid-1980s the next wave of rappers, the new school, came to prominence. At the forefront was [Run-DMC](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Run-DMC), a trio of middle-class [African Americans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/African-Americans) who fused [rap](https://www.britannica.com/art/rap) with hard [rock](https://www.britannica.com/art/rock-music), defined a new style of hip dress, and brought rap to a mainstream audience. Run-DMC recorded for [Profile](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Profile-Records-Run-This-Way-1688476), one of several new labels that took advantage of the growing market for rap music. [Def Jam](https://www.britannica.com/money/Def-Jam-Records-Hip-Hop-Harbingers-1688344) featured three important innovators: [LL Cool J](https://www.britannica.com/biography/LL-Cool-J), rap’s first [romantic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/romantic) superstar; the [Beastie Boys](https://www.britannica.com/topic/the-Beastie-Boys), a white trio who broadened rap’s audience and popularized digital sampling (composing with music and sounds electronically extracted from other recordings); and [Public Enemy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Public-Enemy), who invested rap with radical Black [political ideology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ideology-society), building on the social [consciousness](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consciousness) of [Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five’s](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Grandmaster-Flash-and-the-Furious-Five) “The Message” (1982). History was made in 1984 when teenage Roxanne Shanté recorded “Roxanne’s Revenge,” a fierce response to UTFO’s “Roxanne, Roxanne” that is considered to be the first rap “diss track.”

Rap’s classical period (1979–93) also included significant contributions from [De La Soul](https://www.britannica.com/topic/De-La-Soul)—whose debut album on [Tommy Boy](https://www.britannica.com/money/Tommy-Boy-Records-Rocking-the-Planet-from-West-85th-Street-1688496), _3 Feet High and Rising_ (1989), pointed in a new and more playful direction—and female rappers such as MC Lyte, [Queen Latifah](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Queen-Latifah), and [Salt-N-Pepa](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Salt-N-Pepa), who offered a distinctly feminist [alternative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alternative) to rap’s predominantly male, often misogynistic viewpoint. Born in Queens and raised in [Brooklyn](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brooklyn-borough-New-York-City), MC Lyte debuted with the self-penned “I Cram to Understand U (Sam)” in 1987 and followed up the next year with _Lyte as a Rock_, the first rap album by a solo female artist. She went on to [collaborate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/collaborate) with non-rap artists such as Irish singer [Sinéad O’Connor](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sinead-OConnor), record the [safe-sex](https://www.britannica.com/topic/safe-sex)-themed duet “I’m Not Havin’ It” (1989) with Positive K, and participate in the Stop the Violence Movement, an anti-gang campaign that conscripted a number of prominent [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state) hip-hop acts.

A Tribe Called Quest also emerged from Queens with an [eclectic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/eclectic) sound that merged a dizzying array of samples with flowing, socially conscious raps from MCs Q-Tip and Phife Dawg. The group’s _The Low End Theory_ (1991) produced the singles “Check the Rhime” and “Scenario” (the latter featuring a breakout performance by Busta Rhymes), and it is widely regarded as one of the greatest hip-hop albums of all time.

Hip-hop artists from places other than [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City) began to make their mark, including DJ Jazzy Jeff and the Fresh Prince ( [Will Smith](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Will-Smith)), from [Philadelphia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Philadelphia); the provocative 2 Live Crew, from [Miami](https://www.britannica.com/place/Miami-Florida); and M.C. Hammer, from [Oakland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Oakland-California), [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state), who experienced short-lived but massive crossover success with a pop audience with such danceable hits as “U Can’t Touch This.”

The most significant response to New York hip-hop, though, came from [Los Angeles](https://www.britannica.com/place/Los-Angeles-California), beginning in 1989 with [N.W.A](https://www.britannica.com/topic/NWA)’s [dynamic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynamic) album _Straight Outta Compton_. N.W.A (Niggaz Wit Attitudes) and former members of that group— [Ice Cube](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ice-Cube), Eazy E, and [Dr. Dre](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Dr-Dre)—led the way as West Coast rap grew in prominence in the early 1990s. Their graphic, frequently violent tales of real life in the inner city, as well as those of Los Angeles rappers such as [Ice-T](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ice-T) (notorious for his 1992 single “Cop Killer”) and [Snoop Dogg](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Snoop-Dogg) and of East Coast counterparts such as Schoolly D, gave rise to the [genre](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/genre) known as [gangsta rap](https://www.britannica.com/art/gangsta-rap). As the Los Angeles-based label [Death Row Records](https://www.britannica.com/money/Death-Row-Records-and-Interscope-Records-1688340) built an empire around Dr. Dre, Snoop Dogg, and the [charismatic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/charismatic), complicated rapper-actor [Tupac Shakur](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Tupac-Shakur), it also entered into a rivalry with New York City’s Bad Boy Records. This developed into a media-fueled hostility between East Coast and West Coast rappers, which culminated in the [still-unsolved murders](https://www.britannica.com/story/who-killed-tupac-shakur) of Shakur and the wildly gifted MC known as [The Notorious B.I.G.](https://www.britannica.com/biography/The-Notorious-B-I-G)

By the late 1990s hip-hop was artistically dominated by the [Wu-Tang Clan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wu-Tang-Clan), from New York City’s [Staten Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Staten-Island), whose combination of street credibility, neo-Islamic mysticism, and [kung fu](https://www.britannica.com/sports/kung-fu) lore made them one of the most complex groups in the history of rap; by [Diddy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sean-Combs) (also known by a variety of other names, including Sean “Puffy” Combs and Puff Daddy), performer, producer, and president of Bad Boy Records, who was responsible for a series of innovative [music videos](https://www.britannica.com/art/music-video); and by the Fugees, who mixed pop music hooks with politics and launched the solo careers of [Wyclef Jean](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wyclef-Jean) and [Lauryn Hill](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Lauryn-Hill). [Missy Elliott](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Missy-Elliott) exploded onto the male-dominated hip-hop scene in 1997 with _Supa Dupa Fly_, an album produced by her childhood friend [Timbaland](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Timbaland). Her success continued with such hits as “Get Ur Freak On” (2001) and the Grammy-winning “Work It” (2002) and “Lose Control” (2005). In 2023 Elliott’s credentials as a significant hip-hop groundbreaker were solidified when she became the first female rapper inducted into the [Rock and Roll Hall of Fame](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rock-and-Roll-Hall-of-Fame-and-Museum).

Although long believed to be popular primarily with urban African American males, hip-hop became the best-selling genre of [popular music](https://www.britannica.com/art/popular-music) in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) in the late 1990s (at least partly by feeding the appetite of some white suburbanites for [vicarious](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vicarious) thrills). Its impact was global, with [formidable](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/formidable) audiences and artist pools in cities such as [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris), [Tokyo](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tokyo), [Sydney](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sydney-New-South-Wales), [Cape Town](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cape-Town), [London](https://www.britannica.com/place/London), and [Bristol](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bristol-England), [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) (where the spin-off [trip-hop](https://www.britannica.com/art/trip-hop) originated). It also generated huge sales of products in the fashion, liquor, electronics, and automobile industries that were popularized by hip-hop artists on [cable television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cable-television) stations such as [MTV](https://www.britannica.com/money/MTV) and The Box and in hip-hop-oriented magazines such as _The Source_ and _Vibe_. A canny blend of entrepreneurship and [aesthetics](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aesthetics), hip-hop was the wellspring of several staple techniques of modern pop music, including digital drumming and [sampling](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sampling-record-production) (which introduced rap listeners to the music of a previous generation of performers, including Chic (with Nile Rodgers), [Parliament-Funkadelic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Parliament-Funkadelic), Rick James, [Roberta Flack](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Roberta-Flack), and [James Brown](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Brown-American-singer), while at the same time creating [copyright](https://www.britannica.com/topic/copyright) controversies).

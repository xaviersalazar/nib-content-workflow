**Western music**, [music](https://www.britannica.com/art/music) produced in Europe as well as those musics derived from the European from ancient times to the present day.

All ancient civilizations entered historical times with a flourishing [musical](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical) [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture). That the earliest writers explained it in terms of [legend](https://www.britannica.com/art/legend-literature) and [myth](https://www.britannica.com/topic/myth) strongly suggests the remote beginnings of the art of [sound](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics). Among the speculations about its origin, the more plausible are that it began as a primitive form of [communication](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communication), that it grew out of a device to expedite communal labour, or that it originated as a powerful adjunct to religious ceremonies. While such theories must necessarily remain speculative, it is clear, despite the prehistoric musical [artifacts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/artifacts) found in central Europe, that the cradle of Western music was the [Fertile Crescent](https://www.britannica.com/place/Fertile-Crescent) cupping the eastern end of the [Mediterranean Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea). There the Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Hebrew nations, among others, evolved political, social [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) that were absorbed by the conquering Greeks and, in turn, by the Romans, who introduced elements of that Mediterranean music to much of western Europe.

In all of these early cultures the social functions of music were essentially the same, since their climate, geographic location, cultural pace, and mutual influences produced many more social similarities than differences. The primary function of music was apparently religious, ranging from heightening the effect of “ [magic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/magic-supernatural-phenomenon)” to ennobling liturgies. The other musical occasions depicted in both pictures and written accounts were equally functional: stirring incitements to military zeal, soothing accompaniments to communal or solitary labour, heightening aids to dramatic spectacles, and enlivening backgrounds to social gatherings that involved either [singing](https://www.britannica.com/art/singing) or [dancing](https://www.britannica.com/art/Western-dance) or both. In every case musical sounds were an adjunct either to bodily movement (dance, march, game, or work) or to [song](https://www.britannica.com/art/song). Many centuries were to pass before pleasure in euphonious sound became an end in itself.

## Roots in antiquity

## Ancient Middle East and Egypt

The inhabitants of the Mesopotamian region around the [Tigris and Euphrates rivers](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tigris-Euphrates-river-system)—the [Sumerians](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sumer), the [Babylonians](https://www.britannica.com/place/Babylonia), and the [Assyrians](https://www.britannica.com/place/Assyria)—flourished from about 3500 to about 500 bce. Their pictures and the few surviving artifacts indicate that they had instruments of every basic type: [idiophones](https://www.britannica.com/art/idiophone), whose sound is made by [resonating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/resonating) as a whole; [aerophones](https://www.britannica.com/art/aerophone), which [resonate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/resonate) a column of blown air; [chordophones](https://www.britannica.com/art/chordophone), with strings to be plucked or struck; and [membranophones](https://www.britannica.com/art/membranophone), made of stretched skins over a resonating body. An undecipherable hymn engraved in stone, dating from about 800 bce, is evidence of a primitive system of [musical notation](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical-notation).

The [Egyptians](https://www.britannica.com/art/Egyptian-art), entering historical times about 500 years later than the Mesopotamians, enjoyed all of the same types of activities and instruments, as may be deduced from numerous written references to music as well as seen on many artifacts, especially the pictures preserved on pottery utensils.

The musical culture of the [Hebrew peoples](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jew-people), recorded from about 2000 bce and documented primarily in the [Hebrew Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-Bible) ( [Old Testament](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Old-Testament)), was more directly influential in the West because of its adoption and [adaptation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adaptation) into the [Christian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity) liturgy. Because of the prohibition of Jewish religious law against the making of “graven images,” there are very few surviving artifacts or pictures. Among the established practices of the temple service still current in the synagogue are the extensive use of the [shofar](https://www.britannica.com/art/shofar) (a ritualistic ram’s-horn trumpet) and the singing of passages from the [Torah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Torah) (Pentateuch; the first five books of the Bible), [prayers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prayer), and songs of praise.

## [Ancient Greece](https://www.britannica.com/art/Greek-music)

Of the eastern Mediterranean cultures, it was undoubtedly that of the Greeks that furnished the most direct link with the musical development of western Europe, by way of the Romans, who defeated them but adopted much of Greek culture intact. Entering historical times relatively late, circa 1000 bce, the Greeks soon dominated their neighbours and absorbed many elements of earlier cultures, which they modified and combined into an [enlightened](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enlightened) and sophisticated civilization. The two basic Greek religious cults—one devoted to [Apollo](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Apollo-Greek-mythology), the other to [Dionysus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dionysus)—became the [prototypes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/prototypes) for the two [aesthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aesthetic) poles, classical and [romantic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/romantic), that have contended throughout Western cultural history. The Apollonians were characterized by objectivity of expression, simplicity, and clarity, and their favoured instrument was the [kithara](https://www.britannica.com/art/kithara), a type of lyre. The Dionysians, on the other hand, preferred the reed-blown [aulos](https://www.britannica.com/art/aulos) and were identified by subjectivity, emotional abandon, and sensuality.

The prevailing doctrine of [ethos](https://www.britannica.com/art/ethos), as explained by ancient Greek philosophers such as [Plato](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Plato) and [Aristotle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle), was based on the belief that music has a direct effect upon the [soul](https://www.britannica.com/topic/soul-religion-and-philosophy) and actions of humankind. As a result, the Greek political and social systems were intertwined with music, which had a primary role in the [dramas](https://www.britannica.com/art/dramatic-literature) of [Aeschylus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aeschylus-Greek-dramatist), [Sophocles](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sophocles), [Euripides](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Euripides), and [Aristophanes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristophanes). And the Grecian educational system was focused upon _musica_ and _gymnastica_, the former referring to all cultural and [intellectual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectual) studies, as distinguished from those related to physical training.

To support its fundamental role in society, an intricate scientific rationale of music evolved, [encompassing](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompassing) [tuning](https://www.britannica.com/art/tuning-and-temperament), [instruments](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical-instrument), [modes](https://www.britannica.com/art/mode-music) (melodic formulas based on certain scales), and [rhythms](https://www.britannica.com/art/rhythm-music). The 6th-century-bce philosopher and mathematician [Pythagoras](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pythagoras) was the first to record the vibratory ratios that established the series of notes still used in Western music. From the total [gamut](https://www.britannica.com/art/gamut) of notes used were derived the various modes bearing the names of Grecian tribes— [Dorian](https://www.britannica.com/art/Dorian-mode), [Phrygian](https://www.britannica.com/art/Phrygian-mode), [Lydian](https://www.britannica.com/art/Lydian-mode), etc. The rhythmic system, deriving from poetry, was based on long–short relationships rather than strong–weak [accentual metre](https://www.britannica.com/art/accentual-verse). After Pythagoras, [Aristoxenus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristoxenus) was the major historian and theoretician of Greek music.

## [Ancient Rome](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Rome)

When the musical culture of the eastern Mediterranean was transplanted into the western Mediterranean by the returning Roman [legions](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/legions), it was inevitably modified by local tastes and traditions. In most cases, the resulting practices were more limited than their models. The [diatonic](https://www.britannica.com/art/diatonic) (seven-note) scale, for example, became the standard, displacing the [chromatic](https://www.britannica.com/art/chromaticism) and [enharmonic](https://www.britannica.com/art/enharmonic) structures of the Grecian system. Of particular consequence was the new concept of [metre](https://www.britannica.com/art/metre-music) as a series of equal durations, with emphasis being determined by [accent](https://www.britannica.com/art/accent-rhythm) (stress) rather than by duration.

An inventory of the musical heritage transplanted from the ancient East (particularly Greece) to Rome reveals the rich treasure inherited: an acoustical theory that accounted for the identification and classification of [tones](https://www.britannica.com/science/tone-sound); a concept of tonal organization resulting in the system of modes; principles of rhythmic organization; basic principles of instrument construction; a system of [notation](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical-notation) that conveyed all necessary indications of [pitch](https://www.britannica.com/art/pitch-music) and duration; and a large repertory of melodies to serve as models for further [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition).

# [The Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages)

## Monophonic [liturgical](https://www.britannica.com/topic/liturgical-music) chant

With the decline of the [Roman Empire](https://www.britannica.com/place/Roman-Empire), the institution destined to perpetuate and expand the [musical](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical) heritage of antiquity was the [Christian church](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity), but it was not a unified process. Many of the cultural centres of the Western church developed distinctive characteristics while sharing the common heritage of the Hebrew liturgy and Greek [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture). In [Milan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Milan-Italy), for example, metrical [hymnody](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hymn), as distinguished from the earlier practice of unmetred [psalmody](https://www.britannica.com/art/psalmody), was [cultivated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultivated), particularly under the influence of the 4th-century bishop [Ambrose](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Ambrose), who first attempted to codify the growing repertory of [chant](https://www.britannica.com/art/vocal-music) s. This body of Milanese church music, therefore, came to be called [Ambrosian chant](https://www.britannica.com/art/Ambrosian-chant). Somewhat later a unique style and repertory known as [Mozarabic chant](https://www.britannica.com/art/Mozarabic-chant) evolved in [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain), and in [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) the [Gallican style](https://www.britannica.com/art/Gallican-chant) prevailed.

But the mainstream of church music was the type of chant practiced in [Rome](https://www.britannica.com/place/Rome). Beginning in the late 6th century, according to tradition, with Pope [Gregory I](https://www.britannica.com/biography/St-Gregory-the-Great), the vast number of traditional melodies that became the foundation for the later development of [Western art music](https://www.britannica.com/art/classical-music) were codified and organized. A [systematic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/systematic) organization of tonal materials also was gradually accomplished, resulting in the eight [church modes](https://www.britannica.com/art/church-mode). Each melody was assigned a specific function in the services of the liturgical year—some for the [mass](https://www.britannica.com/art/mass-music) and some for the [divine offices](https://www.britannica.com/topic/divine-office) such as matins, vespers, and compline. After a period of assimilation, the [Gregorian chant](https://www.britannica.com/art/Gregorian-chant) repertory began a process of expansion in the 9th century, when the practice of troping originated. A [trope](https://www.britannica.com/art/trope-music) is either a text or a melodic section added to a preexisting melody or a combination of text and [music](https://www.britannica.com/art/music) incorporated into existing liturgical music. It is not surprising that church musicians, after years of [singing](https://www.britannica.com/art/singing) traditional chants, should want to express themselves by adding words to vocalized melodies. Perhaps the motive was more functional: the added [syllables](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/syllables) would make the long textless passages easier to remember. Tuotilo (died 915), a monk of [Sankt Gallen](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sankt-Gallen-Switzerland) (in what is now [Switzerland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Switzerland)), is credited with the invention of tropes. [Notker Balbulus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Notker-Balbulus) (died 912) is notable for his association with the [sequence](https://www.britannica.com/art/sequence-musical-composition), a long hymn that originated as a trope added to the final syllable of the [Alleluia](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Alleluia) of the mass.

## Development of [polyphony](https://www.britannica.com/art/polyphony-music)

At the same time that the Gregorian repertory was being expanded by the interpolation of tropes and sequences, it was being further enriched by a revolutionary concept destined to give a new direction to the art of sound for hundreds of years. This concept was [polyphony](https://www.britannica.com/art/polyphony-music), the [simultaneous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/simultaneous) sounding of two or more melodic lines. The practice emerged gradually during the early [Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages), and the lack of definite knowledge regarding its origin has brought forward several plausible theories: it resulted from singers with different natural vocal ranges singing at their most-comfortable [pitch](https://www.britannica.com/art/pitch-music) levels; it was a practice of organists adopted by [singers](https://www.britannica.com/art/singing); or it came about when the repetition of a melody at a different pitch level was sung simultaneously with the original statement of the melody. Whatever motivated this dramatic departure from traditional [monophony](https://www.britannica.com/art/monophony) (music consisting of a single voice part), it was an established practice when it was described in _[Musica enchiriadis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Musica-enchiriadis)_ ( _c._ 900), a manual for singers and one of the major musical documents of the Middle Ages. To a given [plainsong](https://www.britannica.com/art/plainsong-music) (or _[vox principalis](https://www.britannica.com/art/vox-principalis)_), a second voice ( _[vox organalis](https://www.britannica.com/art/vox-organalis)_) could be added at the [interval](https://www.britannica.com/art/interval-music) (distance between notes) of a fourth or fifth (four or five steps) below. Music so performed was known as [organum](https://www.britannica.com/art/organum). While it may be assumed that the first attempts at polyphony involved only parallel motion at a set interval, the _Musica enchiriadis_ describes and gives examples of two-part singing in similar (but not exactly parallel) and contrary movement—evidence that a considerable process of evolution had already taken place.

The next major source of information was the _[Micrologus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Micrologus)_, written in the early 11th century by the Italian monk and musical theorist [Guido d’Arezzo](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Guido-dArezzo-Italian-musician). That work documented principles that were crucial to the further development of polyphony. Rhythmic independence was added to melodic independence, and the added voice might sing two or more tones to one in the original plainsong. During the half century after Guido’s death, developments came more rapidly as the plainsong chant became the lower rather than the upper voice. After the emancipation of the organal part, _vox organalis_, its ultimate freedom was reached in the organums of the monastery of Saint-Martial in [Limoges](https://www.britannica.com/place/Limoges), France, where the plainsong part was reduced to the role of sustaining each [tone](https://www.britannica.com/science/tone-sound) while the organal part indulged in free melismata (groups of notes sung to a single syllable), either improvised or composed. This new style was called _organum purum_.

## The [Notre-Dame school](https://www.britannica.com/art/Notre-Dame-school)

Early in the 12th century the centre of musical activity shifted to the church of [Notre-Dame](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Notre-Dame-de-Paris) in [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris), where the [French](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) composer [Léonin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leonin) recorded in the _Magnus Liber Organi_ (“ [Great Book of Organum](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Great-Book-of-Organa)”) a collection of two-part organums for the entire [church year](https://www.britannica.com/topic/church-year). A generation later his successor, [Pérotin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Perotin), edited and revised the _Magnus Liber_, incorporating the rhythmic patterns already well known in [secular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/secular) music and adding more than one part to the [cantus firmus](https://www.britannica.com/art/cantus-firmus) (the “given” or preexisting plainsong melody). When metre was applied to the original plainsong as well as to the _vox organalis_, the resulting form was called a [clausula](https://www.britannica.com/art/clausula-music). Then, when words were provided for the added part or parts, a clausula became a [motet](https://www.britannica.com/art/motet). At first the words given to the motet were a commentary in Latin on the text of the original plainsong tenor (the voice part “holding” the cantus firmus; from Latin _tenere_, “to hold”). Later in the 13th century the added words were in French and secular in nature. Finally, each added part was given its own text, resulting in the classic Paris motet: a three-part [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) consisting of a portion of plainchant (tenor) overlaid with two faster moving parts, each with its own secular text in French. At the same time another polyphonic form, the [conductus](https://www.britannica.com/art/conductus), was flourishing. It differed from a motet in that its basic part was not plainsong and that all parts sang the same Latin text in note-against-note style. The conductus gradually disappeared with the rise of the motet, which apparently served both liturgical and secular functions.

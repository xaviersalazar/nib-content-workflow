- What is an epic in literature?







An epic is a long narrative poem that recounts heroic deeds and transmits traditions from one generation to another. Epics often focus on the glorious deeds of national heroes and reflect a society's struggle for national identity during a heroic age.



These poems may cover myths, legends, histories, religious tales, or moral theories. Oral poets used verbal formulas to compose and memorize these lengthy poems, and they were further refined over generations to fit the meter. The combination of history and imaginative tales is an essential characteristic of an epic.

- What are some common features of an epic?







An [epic](https://www.britannica.com/art/epic/Introduction#ref1) is a long narrative poem that recounts heroic deeds and transmits traditions from one generation to another. Though the term loosely applies to novels and motion pictures, it encompasses oral and written compositions.



Common features of an epic:


\\* The hero embodies cultural values, faces frailties, and brings cultural renewal through trials or death.


\\* Epics blend tales, myths, heroic poetry, and historical references, fictionalizing history to tie events to a culture's heritage.


\\* They use a technical language refined over generations, employing verbal formulas to describe recurring ideas and situations.


\\* Epics may cover myths, legends, histories, religious teachings, or philosophical theories.


\\* They often focus on major turning points in a culture's history, linking them to tradition and ancient wisdom.


\\* The hero's journey often mirrors a society's rite of passage, metaphorically representing the society's movement through familiar stages.

- Who are typical heroes found in epics?







Typical heroes in epics are kings, warriors, or leaders from a nation's past heroic age. They often possess extraordinary skill, strength, and courage, exceeding that of ordinary people.



These heroes are often of noble birth, sometimes with semidivine origins, and are known for their prowess in battle, resourcefulness, and adherence to a strict code of honor. Though flawed, they are ideal figures who embody mythological or ideological patterns. Some may be slow to develop, while others display unusual beauty and precocity.



In many traditions, heroes are born from a union between a maiden and a divine being. They may be exposed at birth, raised by foster parents, and face a dragon in their first battle. Despite their flaws, epic heroes represent a culture's values and undergo transformations that mirror societal changes.

- How do epics usually start or begin?







Epics often begin by plunging into a crucial situation in a chain of events, a technique known as _in medias res_. This means the story starts in the middle of the action, with earlier events revealed through flashbacks.



Another common convention is the [invocation](https://www.britannica.com/art/invocation/Introduction#ref1), where the poet appeals to a muse or deity for inspiration at or near the beginning of the work. For example, Homer's _Odyssey_ begins with a request to the Muse to tell the story of the man of many ways.

The _Odyssey_ explores themes of homecoming, perseverance, and the struggle against adversity.

- How have epics influenced later literature or storytelling?







Epics have significantly shaped later literature and storytelling by providing a foundation for heroic narratives and cultural values. They often blend historical events with imaginative tales, offering a mix of fact and fantasy that resonates with audiences.



Key influences of epics:


\\* **Character archetypes** Epic heroes embody cultural ideals, and their struggles reflect societal dilemmas.


\\* **Thematic elements** Epics explore universal themes such as love, war, and the hero's journey, which continue to be relevant in modern storytelling.


\\* **Literary devices** Techniques such as _in medias res_ (starting in the middle of the story) and the use of epithets have been adopted by later writers to add depth and grandeur to their works.


\\* **National identity** Epics often transmit a nation's traditions and values, reinforcing a sense of cultural identity.


\\* **Scope and scale** The expansive scope and grand scale of epics have inspired novelists and poets to create ambitious and immersive narratives.

**epic**, long narrative poem recounting heroic deeds, although the term has also been loosely used to describe novels, such as [Leo Tolstoy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leo-Tolstoy)’s [_War and Peace_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/War-and-Peace), and motion pictures, such as [Sergey Eisenstein](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sergei-Eisenstein)’s _Ivan the Terrible_. In literary usage, the term [encompasses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompasses) both oral and written [compositions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compositions). The prime examples of the oral epic are [Homer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Homer-Greek-poet)’s _[Iliad](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Iliad-epic-poem-by-Homer)_ and [_Odyssey_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Odyssey-epic-by-Homer). Outstanding examples of the written epic include [Virgil](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Virgil)’s _Aeneid_ and [Lucan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Lucan-Roman-author)’s _Pharsalia_ in Latin, _[Chanson de Roland](https://www.britannica.com/topic/La-Chanson-de-Roland)_ in [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) French, [Ludovico Ariosto](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ludovico-Ariosto)’s _Orlando furioso_ and [Torquato Tasso](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Torquato-Tasso-Italian-poet)’s _Gerusalemme liberata_ in Italian, _[Cantar de mio Cid](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Cantar-de-Mio-Cid)_ in Spanish, and [John Milton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Milton)’s _Paradise Lost_ and [Edmund Spenser](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edmund-Spenser)’s _Faerie Queene_ in English. There are also seriocomic epics, such as the _Morgante_ of a 15th-century Italian poet, [Luigi Pulci](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Luigi-Pulci), and the pseudo-Homeric _Battle of the Frogs and Mice_. Another distinct group is made up of the so-called [beast epics](https://www.britannica.com/art/beast-epic)—narrative poems written in Latin in the Middle Ages and dealing with the struggle between a cunning fox and a cruel and stupid wolf. Underlying all of the written forms is some trace of an [oral](https://www.britannica.com/art/oral-literature) character, partly because of the monumental persuasiveness of Homer’s example but more largely because the epic was, in fact, born of an [oral tradition](https://www.britannica.com/topic/oral-tradition). It is on the oral tradition of the epic form that this article will focus.

## General characteristics

An epic may deal with such various subjects as [myths](https://www.britannica.com/topic/myth), heroic [legends](https://www.britannica.com/art/legend-literature), histories, [edifying](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/edifying) religious tales, animal stories, or philosophical or [moral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/moral) theories. Epic [poetry](https://www.britannica.com/art/poetry) has been and continues to be used by peoples all over the world to transmit their traditions from one generation to another, without the aid of writing. These traditions frequently consist of legendary narratives about the glorious deeds of their national heroes. Thus, scholars have often identified “epic” with a certain kind of heroic oral poetry, which comes into existence in so-called heroic ages. Such ages have been experienced by many nations, usually at a stage of development in which they have had to struggle for a national identity. This effort, [combined](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/combined) with such other conditions as an adequate [material culture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/material-culture) and a sufficiently productive economy, tend to produce a society dominated by a powerful and warlike nobility, constantly occupied with martial activities, whose individual members seek, above all, everlasting fame for themselves and for their lineages.

## Uses of the epic

The main function of poetry in [heroic-age](https://www.britannica.com/art/heroic-poetry) society appears to be to stir the spirit of the warriors to heroic actions by praising their exploits and those of their illustrious ancestors, by assuring a long and glorious recollection of their fame, and by supplying them with models of ideal heroic behavior. One of the favorite pastimes of the nobility in heroic ages in different times and places has been to gather in [banquet](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/banquet) halls to hear heroic songs, in praise of famous deeds sung by professional singers as well as by the warriors themselves. Heroic songs also were often sung before a battle, and such recitations had tremendous effect on the morale of the combatants. Among the [Fulani](https://www.britannica.com/art/African-literature) (Fulbe) people in the Sudan, for instance, whose epic poetry has been recorded, a nobleman customarily set out in quest of adventures accompanied by a singer ( _mabo_), who also served as his shield bearer. The singer was thus the witness of the heroic deeds of his lord, which he celebrated in an epic poem called _baudi_.

The aristocratic warriors of the heroic ages were thus members of an [illustrious](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/illustrious) family, a link in a long chain of glorious heroes. And the chain could snap if the warrior failed to preserve the honor of the family, whereas, by earning fame through his own heroism, he could give it new luster. Epic traditions were to a large extent the traditions of the aristocratic families: the Old French word _[geste](https://www.britannica.com/art/chanson-de-geste)_, used for a form of epic that flourished in the Middle Ages, means not only a story of famous deeds but also a genealogy.

The passing of a heroic age does not necessarily mean the end of its heroic oral poetry. An oral epic tradition usually continues for as long as the nation remains largely illiterate. Usually it is after the heroic age has passed that the narratives about its legendary heroes are fully elaborated. Even when the nobility that originally created the heroic epic perishes or loses interest, the old songs can persist as entertainments among the people. Court singers, then, are replaced by popular singers, who recite at public gatherings. This popular tradition, however, must be distinguished from a tradition that still forms an [integral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integral) part of the [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) of a nobility. For when a heroic epic loses its contact with the banquet halls of the princes and noblemen, it cannot preserve for long its power of renewal. Soon it enters what has been called the reproductive stage in the life [cycle](https://www.britannica.com/art/cycle-literature) of an oral tradition, in which the bards become noncreative reproducers of songs learned from older singers. Popular oral singers, like the _[guslari](https://www.britannica.com/art/guslar)_ of the Balkans, no doubt vary their songs to a certain extent each time they recite them, but they do so mainly by [transposing](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/transposing) language and minor episodes from one acquired song to another. Such variations must not be confounded with the real enrichment of the tradition by succeeding generations of genuine oral poets of the creative stage. The spread of literacy, which has a disastrous effect on the oral singer, brings about a quick corruption of the tradition. At this degenerate stage, the oral epic soon dies out if it is not written down or recorded.

The [ancient Greek](https://www.britannica.com/art/Ancient-Greek-literature) epic exemplifies the cycle of an oral tradition. Originating in the late [Mycenaean](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mycenaean-civilization) period, the Greek epic outlasted the downfall of the typically heroic-age culture (c. 1100 bce) and maintained itself through the “Dark Age” to reach a [climax](https://www.britannica.com/art/climax-literature) in the Homeric poems by the close of the Geometric period (900–750 bce). After Homer, the activity of the _[aoidoi](https://www.britannica.com/art/aoidos)_, who sang their own epic songs at the courts of the nobility, slowly declined. During the first half of the 7th century, the _aoidoi_ produced such new poems as those of [Hesiod](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hesiod) and some of the earlier poems of what was to become known as the Epic Cycle. Between 625 and 575 bce the _aoidoi_ gave way to oral reciters of a new type, called [rhapsodes](https://www.britannica.com/art/rhapsode) or “stitchers of songs,” who declaimed for large [audiences](https://www.britannica.com/topic/audience-communications) the already famous works of Homer while holding in their hand a staff ( _rhabdos_), which they used to give [emphasis](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/emphasis) to their words. It seems probable that these rhapsodes, who played a crucial role in the transmission of the Homeric epic, were using some sort of written aids to memory before Homeric recitations were adopted in 6th-century Athens as part of the [Panathenaic festivals](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Panathenaea) held each year in honor of the goddess [Athena](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Athena-Greek-mythology).

# Verbal formulas

To compose and to memorize long narrative poems like the _Iliad_ and the _Odyssey_, oral poets used a highly elaborate technical language with a large [store](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mnemonic) of traditional verbal formulas, which could describe recurring ideas and situations in ways that suited the requirements of [meter](https://www.britannica.com/art/metre-prosody). So long as an oral epic tradition remains in its creative period, its language will be continually refined by each generation of poets in opposite directions, refinements that are called scope and economy. Scope is the addition of new phrases to express a larger number of recurrent concepts in varying [metrical](https://www.britannica.com/art/prosody) values fitting the possible positions in a verse. Economy is the elimination of [redundancies](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/redundancies) that arise as gifted poets invent new set phrases that duplicate, both in a general sense and in metrical value, the formulas that already exist in the traditional stock.

Nowhere has this refinement proceeded any nearer to perfection than in the language of the Homeric epic. As has been shown by statistical analysis, it exhibits a remarkable [efficiency](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/efficiency), both in the rareness of unnecessarily duplicative variants and in the coverage of each common concept by the metrical [alternatives](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alternatives) useful in the [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) of the six-foot metric line the Greeks used for epic [poetry](https://www.britannica.com/art/poetry).

Thus, for example, if the idea of a ship has to be expressed at the end of a line of verse, the ship may be described as “well-trimmed” ( _nēos eisēs_), “curved” ( _nēos amphielissa_), or “dark-prowed” ( _nēos kyanoprōiros_), depending entirely on the number of feet that remain to be filled by the phrase in the hexameter; if the phrase has to cover the two final feet of the verse and the words have to be put in the dative case, the formula “of a well-trimmed ship” will be replaced by “to a black ship” ( _nēi melainē_). The sole occurrence of “Zeus who gathers lightning” ( _steropēgereta Zeus_), which is an exact metrical equivalent of the more common “Zeus who delights in thunder” ( _Zeus terpikeraunos_), [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) one of the very few actual duplications of such formulas found in [Homer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Homer-Greek-poet).

Finally, some of the typical scenes in the heroic life, such as the preparation of a meal or sacrifice or the launching or beaching of a ship, contain set descriptions [comprising](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprising) several lines that are used by rote each time the events are narrated.

This highly formalized language was elaborated by generations of oral poets to minimize the conscious effort needed to compose new poems and memorize existing ones. Because of it, an exceptionally gifted _aoidos_, working just prior to the corruption of the [genre](https://www.britannica.com/art/genre-literature), could orally create long and finely structured poems like the _Iliad_ and the _Odyssey_, and those poems could then be transmitted accurately by the following generations of rhapsodes until complete written texts were produced.

## Bases

Oral [heroic](https://www.britannica.com/art/hero-literary-and-cultural-figure) poetry, at its origin, usually deals with outstanding deeds of kings and warriors who lived in the heroic age of the nation. Since the primary function of this poetry is to educate rather than to record, however, the personages are necessarily transformed into ideal heroes and their acts into ideal heroic deeds that conform to mythological or ideological patterns. Some of these patterns are [archetypes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/archetype) found all over the world, while others are peculiar to a specific nation or [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture). Thus, in many epic traditions, heroes are born as a result of the union of a maiden with a divine or supernatural being; because these unions occur outside the usual social norms, the heroes are exposed at birth, fed by an animal, and brought up by humble foster parents in a rustic milieu; they grow up with marvelous speed, fight a dragon—in their first combat—to rescue a maiden whom they marry, and die young in circumstances as fabulous as those that surrounded their birth.

In the traditions of [Indo-European](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indo-European) peoples, a hero is often a twin who [acquires](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/acquires) soon after his supernatural birth an invulnerability that has one defect, generally of his heel or of some other part of his [foot](https://www.britannica.com/art/foot-prosody), which ultimately causes his death. He is educated by a blacksmith, disguises himself as a woman at some time in his youth, and conquers a three-headed dragon, or some other kind of triple opponent, in his first battle. He then begets, by a foreign or supernatural woman, a child who, reared by his mother in her country, becomes a warrior as brave as his father. When this child meets his unknown father, the latter fails to recognize him, so that the father kills his own child after a long and fierce single combat. The hero himself usually dies after committing the third of three sins.

In [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Japanese-mythology), to take another example, renowned members of the warrior [aristocracy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aristocracy) of the past, who have acquired the status of popular heroes, are in many cases supplied in their [legend](https://www.britannica.com/art/legend-literature) with four exceptionally brave and faithful retainers called their _[shi-tennō](https://www.britannica.com/topic/lokapala)_ (“guardian kings”), who guard the four cardinal points; these form the closest entourage of their lord—who is usually depicted as excelling in command but not in physical strength—and defend him from dangers. The retainers reflect a mythological model, taken from [Buddhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism), of four _deva_ kings, who guard the teaching of the Buddha against the attack of the devils.

A striking pattern for a number of epic traditions has been found in a so-called tripartite ideology or “trifunctional system” of the Indo-Europeans. The concept was based on the discovery of the remarkable philosophy of a prehistoric nation that survived as a system of thought in the historic Indo-European civilizations and even in the subconsciousness of the modern speakers of [Indo-European](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indo-European-languages) tongues.

This philosophy sees in the universe three basic principles that are realized by three [categories](https://www.britannica.com/topic/symbolism) of people: priests, warriors, and producers of riches. In conformity with this philosophy, most Indo-European epics have as their central themes interaction among these three principles or functions which are: (1) religion and kingship; (2) physical strength; (3) fecundity, health, riches, beauty, and so forth. In the long [Indian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism) epic the _[Mahabharata](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mahabharata)_, for example, the central figures, the [Pandava brothers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pandavas), together with their father, Pandu, their two uncles, Dhritarashtra and Vidura, and their common wife, Draupadi, correspond to traditional deities presiding over the three functions of the Indo-European [ideology](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ideology).

During the first part of their earthly career, the Pandavas suffer constantly from the persistent [enmity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enmity) and jealousy of their cousins, Duryodhana and his 99 brothers, who, in reality, are incarnations of the demons [Kali](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Kali) and the Paulastya. The demons at first succeeded in snatching the kingdom from the Pandavas and in exiling them. The conflict ends in a devastating war, in which all the renowned heroes of the time take part. The Pandavas survive the massacre, and establish on earth a peaceful and prosperous reign, in which Dhritarashtra and Vidura also participate.

This whole story, it has been shown, is a transposition to the heroic level of an Indo-European [myth](https://www.britannica.com/topic/myth) about the incessant struggle between the gods and the demons since the beginning of the world. Eventually, it results in a bloody eschatological battle, in which the gods and the devils exterminate each other. The destruction of the former world order, however, prepares for a new and better world, exempt from evil influences, over which reign a few divine survivors of the [catastrophe](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/catastrophe).

## Early patterns of development

## In the ancient [Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Middle-East)

[![Epic of Gilgamesh](https://cdn.britannica.com/62/121162-050-25A9F581/Flood-Tablet-epic-series-Gilgamesh-Nineveh-British.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/62/121162-050-25A9F581/Flood-Tablet-epic-series-Gilgamesh-Nineveh-British.jpg)

[_Epic of Gilgamesh_](https://cdn.britannica.com/62/121162-050-25A9F581/Flood-Tablet-epic-series-Gilgamesh-Nineveh-British.jpg) The Flood Tablet, 11th cuneiform tablet in a series relating the Gilgamesh epic, from Nineveh, 7th century bce; in the British Museum, London.(more)

The earliest known epic poetry is that of the [Sumerians](https://www.britannica.com/art/Sumerian-literature). Its origin has been traced to a preliterate heroic age, not later than 3000 bce, when the Sumerians had to fight, under the direction of a warlike aristocracy, for possession of this fertile [Mesopotamian](https://www.britannica.com/art/Mesopotamian-literature) land. Among the [extant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/extant) [literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/literature) of this highly gifted people are fragments of narrative poems recounting the heroic deeds of their early kings: [Enmerkar](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Enmerkar), [Lugalbanda](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lugalbanda), and [Gilgamesh](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gilgamesh). By far the most important in the development of Mesopotamian literature are the five poems of the _[Epic of Gilgamesh](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Epic-of-Gilgamesh)_. This [cycle](https://www.britannica.com/art/cycle-literature) tells the odyssey of a king, Gilgamesh, part human and part divine, who seeks immortality. A god who dislikes his rule, fashions a wild man, Enkidu, to challenge him. Enkidu first lives among wild animals, then goes to the capital and engages in a trial of strength with Gilgamesh, who emerges victorious. The two, now friends, set out on various adventures, in one of which they kill a wild bull that the goddess of love had sent to destroy Gilgamesh because he spurned her marriage proposal. Enkidu dreams the gods have decided he must die for the death of the bull, and, upon awakening, he does fall ill and die. Gilgamesh searches for a survivor of the Babylonian flood to learn how to escape death. The survivor shows him where to find a plant that renews youth, but after Gilgamesh gets the plant it is snatched away by a serpent. Gilgamesh returns, saddened, to his capital.

The [legend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legend) of Gilgamesh was taken over by the [Babylonians](https://www.britannica.com/place/Babylonia), who developed it into a long and beautiful poem, one of the masterpieces of humankind.

Another Babylonian epic, composed about 2000 bce, is called in [Akkadian](https://www.britannica.com/art/Akkadian-literature) _Enuma elish_, after its opening words, meaning “When on high.” Its subject is not heroic but mythological. It recounts events from the beginning of the world to the establishment of the power of [Marduk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Marduk), the great god of Babylon. The outline of a Babylonian poem narrating the adventure of a hero named Adapa (“Man”) can be reconstructed from four fragmentary accounts. It shares with the _Epic of Gilgamesh_ the theme of a human potential for and loss of immortality.

Among clay tablets of the 14th century bce, covered with inscriptions in an old Phoenician [cuneiform](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cuneiform) alphabet, from Ras Shamra (the site of ancient [Ugarit](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ugarit)), in northern [Syria](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Syrian-and-Palestinian-religion), there are important fragments of three narrative poems. One of these is mythological and recounts the career of the god [Baal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Baal-Epic), which seems to coincide with the yearly cycle of vegetation on earth. As was usual with the death of gods in the ancient Mediterranean world, Baal’s end brings about a drought that ceases only with his resurrection. Another fragment, about a hero named [Aqhat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Aqhat-Epic), is perhaps a transposition of this [myth](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myth) of Baal to the human level. Just as the death of Baal is avenged on his slayer by Baal’s sister Anath, so is the murder of Aqhat, which also causes a drought, revenged by his sister Paghat. Since the end of the poem is missing, however, it is not known whether Paghat, like Anath, succeeds in bringing her brother back to life.

The third fragment, the Ugaritic epic of [Keret](https://www.britannica.com/art/Keret-Epic), has been interpreted as a Phoenician version of the Indo-European theme of the [siege](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/siege) of an enemy city for the recovery of an abducted woman. This theme is also the subject of the Greek legend of the [Trojan War](https://www.britannica.com/event/Trojan-War) and of the Indian epic _[Ramayana](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ramayana-Indian-epic)_. The fragmentary text does not reveal, however, whether the expedition of Keret, like that of the Achaean army against Troy, was meant to regain the hero’s wife or to acquire for him a new bride.

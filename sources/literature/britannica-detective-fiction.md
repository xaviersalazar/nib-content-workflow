- What is a detective story?







A detective story is a popular literary genre centered around a crime, its investigation, and the revelation of the culprit. Traditional elements include a seemingly perfect crime, a wrongly accused suspect, bumbling police, a brilliant detective, and a surprising resolution.



The detective uses observation and logic to solve the puzzle, with clues presented to the reader as they are discovered. Edgar Allan Poe's "\\The Murders in the Rue Morgue\\" is considered the first detective story. This genre has produced famous detectives such as Sherlock Holmes and Hercule Poirot.

- How have detective stories changed over time or influenced other genres?







The detective story, a type of popular literature, has evolved since its beginning with Edgar Allan Poe's " [The Murders in the Rue Morgue](https://www.britannica.com/art/detective-story-narrative-genre/Introduction#ref1)" in 1841. Early stories featured a seemingly perfect crime, a wrongly accused suspect, bungling police, a brilliant detective, and a surprising conclusion.



Over time, the character of the detective became as important as the mystery itself, as seen in the works of Dashiell Hammett. The detective story has also influenced other genres, such as the spy novel, with writers like John le Carré adapting the format to explore Cold War mysteries. Though the terms detective story, mystery, and thriller are often used interchangeably, the mystery novel was eventually superseded in popularity by novels of espionage.

**detective story**, type of [popular literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/popular-literature) in which a crime is introduced and investigated and the culprit is revealed.

The traditional elements of the detective story are: (1) the seemingly perfect crime; (2) the wrongly accused suspect at whom [circumstantial evidence](https://www.britannica.com/topic/circumstantial-evidence) points; (3) the bungling of dim-witted police; (4) the greater powers of observation and superior mind of the [detective](https://www.britannica.com/topic/detective); and (5) the startling and unexpected [denouement](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/denouement), in which the detective reveals how the identity of the culprit was [ascertained](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ascertained). Detective stories frequently operate on the principle that superficially convincing evidence is ultimately irrelevant. Usually it is also [axiomatic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/axiomatic) that the clues from which a logical solution to the problem can be reached be fairly presented to the reader at exactly the same time that the [sleuth](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/sleuth) receives them and that the sleuth deduce the solution to the puzzle from a logical interpretation of these clues.

The first detective story was “ [The Murders in the Rue Morgue](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Murders-in-the-Rue-Morgue)” by [Edgar Allan Poe](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edgar-Allan-Poe), published in April 1841. The profession of detective had come into being only a few decades earlier, and Poe is generally thought to have been influenced by the _Mémoires_ (1828–29) of [François-Eugène Vidocq](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francois-Eugene-Vidocq), who in 1817 founded the world’s first detective bureau, in Paris. Poe’s fictional French detective, [C. Auguste Dupin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/C-Auguste-Dupin), appeared in two other stories, “ [The Mystery of Marie Roget](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Mystery-of-Marie-Roget)” (1845) and “ [The Purloined Letter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Purloined-Letter)” (1845). The detective story soon expanded to [novel](https://www.britannica.com/art/novel) length.

The French author [Émile Gaboriau](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Emile-Gaboriau)’s _L’Affaire Lerouge_ (1866) was an enormously successful novel that had several sequels. Wilkie Collins’ _[The Moonstone](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Moonstone)_ (1868) remains one of the finest English detective novels. [Anna Katharine Green](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Anna-Katharine-Green) became one of the first American detective novelists with _The Leavenworth Case_ (1878) _. The Mystery of a Hansom Cab_ (1886) by the Australian Fergus Hume was a phenomenal commercial success.

The greatest of all fictional detectives, [Sherlock Holmes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sherlock-Holmes), along with his loyal, somewhat [obtuse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/obtuse) companion [Dr. Watson](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dr-Watson), made his first appearance in Arthur (later Sir Arthur) [Conan Doyle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Arthur-Conan-Doyle)’s novel _A Study in Scarlet_ (1887) and continued into the 20th century in such collections of stories as _The Memoirs of Sherlock Holmes_ (1894) and the longer _Hound of the Baskervilles_ (1902). So great was the appeal of Sherlock Holmes’s detecting style that the death of Conan Doyle did little to end Holmes’s career; several writers, often expanding upon circumstances mentioned in the original works, have attempted to carry on the Holmesian tradition.

The early years of the 20th century produced a number of distinguished detective novels, among them [Mary Roberts Rinehart’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mary-Roberts-Rinehart) _The Circular Staircase_ (1908) and [G.K. Chesterton’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/G-K-Chesterton) _[The Innocence of Father Brown](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Innocence-of-Father-Brown)_ (1911) and other novels with the [clerical detective](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Father-Brown). From 1920 on, the names of many fictional detectives became household words: Inspector French, introduced in [Freeman Wills Crofts’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Freeman-Wills-Crofts) _[The Cask](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Cask)_ (1920); [Hercule Poirot](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hercule-Poirot), in [Agatha Christie](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Agatha-Christie)’s _[The Mysterious Affair at Styles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Mysterious-Affair-at-Styles)_ (1920), and [Miss Marple](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Miss-Marple), in _Murder at the Vicarage_ (1930); [Lord Peter Wimsey](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lord-Peter-Wimsey), in [Dorothy L. In a sense, the 1930s was the golden age of the detective story, with the detectives named above continuing in new novels. The decade was also marked by the books of [Dashiell Hammett](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Dashiell-Hammett), who drew upon his own experience as a private detective to produce both stories and novels, notably _[The Maltese Falcon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Maltese-Falcon-novel-by-Hammett)_ (1930) featuring [Sam Spade](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sam-Spade). In Hammett’s work, the character of the detective became as important as the “whodunit” aspect of ratiocination was earlier. _[The Thin Man](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Thin-Man-novel-by-Hammett)_ (1934), with Nick and Nora Charles, was more in the conventional vein, with the added fillip of detection by a [witty](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/witty) married couple. Successors to Hammett included [Raymond Chandler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Raymond-Chandler) and [Ross Macdonald](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ross-Macdonald), who also emphasized the characters of their tough but humane detectives [Philip Marlowe](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Philip-Marlowe) and [Lew Archer](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lew-Archer), respectively. At the end of the 1940s, [Mickey Spillane](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mickey-Spillane) preserved the hard-boiled crime fiction approach of Hammett and others, but his emphasis on sex and sadism became a formula that brought him amazing commercial success beginning with _I, the Jury_ (1947).

- What is a mystery story?







A mystery story is a popular narrative genre centered on unraveling the unknown through human or worldly dilemmas. These tales can encompass various elements, such as horror, fantasy, crime-solving, intrigue, secret societies, or any situation involving an enigma.



Mystery stories are broadly divided into supernatural tales and riddle stories. Supernatural tales evoke fear and curiosity, tracing back to the 18th-century Gothic novel. Riddle stories present mysterious facts, withholding explanations until the end, challenging the reader to solve the puzzle.

- How does a mystery story usually build suspense?







Mystery stories build suspense by presenting unknown elements through human or worldly dilemmas. These tales can involve horror, terror, pseudoscientific fantasy, crime-solving, diplomatic intrigue, codes, ciphers, or secret societies, all creating enigma.

- What is the role of clues and red herrings in a mystery story?







In a mystery story, clues guide the reader (and the protagonist) through a complex plot toward the solution of a problem or enigma. They appear as hints, suggestions, or pieces of evidence that help to solve the mystery.



Red herrings are narrative elements meant to distract attention from the real issue or mislead investigators (and readers). They create suspense and can lead to false conclusions, making the mystery more intricate.

- How have mystery stories influenced other types of literature or media?







Mystery stories have significantly influenced other literature and media forms by cultivating curiosity and suspense. The genre's impact extends to the development of character-driven narratives in realistic settings, as seen in works such as Aleksandr Pushkin’s "Queen of Spades".



Thrillers and spy stories, like Ian Fleming's James Bond series and John le Carré’s espionage novels, also owe a debt to the mystery genre, adopting elements such as intrigue and suspenseful plotting. Moreover, the qualities inherent in good mystery writing—economy, sustained suspense, and artful plotting—have been valuable in various narrative forms.

**mystery story**, ages-old popular [genre](https://www.britannica.com/art/genre-literature) of tales dealing with the unknown as revealed through human or worldly dilemmas; it may be a narrative of horror and terror, a pseudoscientific [fantasy](https://www.britannica.com/art/fantasy-narrative-genre), a crime-solving story, an account of diplomatic intrigue, an affair of codes and ciphers and secret societies, or any situation involving an [enigma](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enigma). By and large, mystery stories may be divided into two sorts: tales of the supernatural and riddle stories.

[Supernatural](https://www.britannica.com/topic/supernaturalism) tales are of ancient origin and form a substantial part of the body of [folk literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/folk-literature). But the literary cultivation of fear and curiosity for its own sake began to emerge in the 18th-century pre-Romantic era with the [Gothic novel](https://www.britannica.com/art/Gothic-novel). This [genre](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/genre) was invented by a worldly Englishman, [Horace Walpole](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Horace-Walpole), whose _[Castle of Otranto](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Castle-of-Otranto)_ (1765) may be said to have founded the [horror story](https://www.britannica.com/art/horror-story) as a permanent form. [Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mary-Wollstonecraft-Shelley) introduced the pseudoscientific note in her famous [novel](https://www.britannica.com/art/novel) _[Frankenstein](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Frankenstein-or-The-Modern-Prometheus)_ (1818), about the creation of a monster that ultimately destroys its creator, Dr. Frankenstein.

In the [Romantic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Romantic) era the German storyteller [E.T.A. Hoffmann](https://www.britannica.com/biography/E-T-A-Hoffmann) and the American writer [Edgar Allan Poe](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edgar-Allan-Poe) raised the mystery story to a level far above mere entertainment through their skillful intermingling of reason and madness, eerie atmosphere and everyday reality. They invested their spectres, doubles, and haunted houses with a psychological symbolism that gave their tales a haunting credibility.

The Gothic influence persisted throughout the 19th century in such works as Joseph [Sheridan Le Fanu](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sheridan-Le-Fanu)’s _House by the Churchyard_ and “Green Tea,” Wilkie Collins’ _Moonstone,_ and [Bram Stoker’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bram-Stoker) vampire tale _Dracula._ Later masters of the mystery tale were [Ambrose Bierce](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ambrose-Bierce), [Arthur Machen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Arthur-Machen), Algernon Blackwood, Lord Dunsany, and H.P. Harvey’s “August Heat.” Some of the best-known mystery stories owe their power to their development of full-bodied characters in a realistic social [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) and the very absence of mysterious atmosphere. In this category are [Aleksandr Pushkin’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aleksandr-Sergeyevich-Pushkin) “Queen of Spades” and W.W. Jacob’s “Monkey’s Paw.”

Riddle stories, too, have an ancient heritage. The riddle of Samson, propounded in the Bible (Judges 14:12–18), is the most famous early example, but puzzles were also popular among the ancient Egyptians and the Greeks. The distinguishing feature of the riddling mystery story is that the reader be confronted with a number of mysterious facts and situations, explanation of which is reserved until the end of the story.

Poe’s [short story](https://www.britannica.com/art/short-story) “ [The Gold Bug](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Gold-Bug)” is a classic example of one perennially popular type of mystery, the story of a search for lost treasure. In the more [sinister](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/sinister) field of murder are innumerable tales of roguery involving mystery and crime but without the familiar detective interludes. Two notable riddle stories of modern times offered no solution to the riddle posed and gained wide attention by their novelty: “The Lady or the Tiger” by Frank R. Stockton and “The Mysterious Card” by Cleveland Moffett.

### How does the printing press work?

Printing presses push [paper](https://www.britannica.com/technology/paper) against [inked](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ink-writing-medium) movable type materials to transfer text and images from the type onto the paper. Medieval presses used a handle to turn a wooden [screw](https://www.britannica.com/technology/screw) that moved the platen on which the paper was mounted; the platen squeezed the paper against the type, which was locked in place in a frame, or form. Metal presses, developed late in the 18th century, used steam to drive a cylinder press. [Flatbed presses](https://www.britannica.com/technology/flatbed-press), emerging early in the 19th century, used flat beds to hold the type and either a reciprocating platen or a cylinder to hold paper.

### Why is the printing press important?

The printing press is a useful tool for communicating and disseminating ideas on paper quickly and at a large scale. Before the advent of [radio](https://www.britannica.com/topic/radio), [television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/television-technology), the [Internet](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Internet), and other forms of mass media, printed materials (such as treatises, [books](https://www.britannica.com/topic/book-publication), bulletins, [newspapers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/newspaper), and [magazines](https://www.britannica.com/topic/magazine-publishing)) were used to share ideas quickly and efficiently for the purposes of informing large numbers of people about current events, business opportunities, and cultural and religious practices and for educational purposes.

### When was the printing press invented?

Movable type and paper were invented in China, and printing with movable type was undertaken in Korea by the 14th century, if not earlier. The printing press first became mechanized in Europe. The earliest mention of a printing press in Europe appears in a lawsuit in [Strasbourg](https://www.britannica.com/place/Strasbourg) in 1439. It reveals construction of a press for [Johannes Gutenberg](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johannes-Gutenberg) and his associates. The earliest European printing presses owed much to the medieval paper press, which was in turn modeled after the ancient wine-and-olive press of the Mediterranean area.

**printing press**, machine by which text and images are transferred from movable type to [paper](https://www.britannica.com/technology/paper) or other media by means of [ink](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ink-writing-medium). Movable type and paper were invented in China, and the oldest known [extant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/extant) [book](https://www.britannica.com/topic/book-publication) printed from movable type was created in Korea in the 14th century. [Printing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/printing-publishing) first became mechanized in Europe during the 15th century.

The earliest mention of a mechanized printing press in Europe appears in a lawsuit in Strasbourg in 1439; it reveals construction of a press for [Johannes Gutenberg](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johannes-Gutenberg) and his associates. Gutenberg’s press and others of its era in Europe owed much to the [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) paper press, which was in turn modeled after the ancient wine-and-olive press of the Mediterranean area. A long handle was used to turn a heavy wooden screw, exerting downward pressure against the paper, which was laid over the type mounted on a wooden platen. Gutenberg used his press to [print](https://www.britannica.com/art/printmaking) [an edition of the Bible in 1455](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gutenberg-Bible); [this Bible](https://www.themorgan.org/collections/works/gutenberg/page/1) is the first complete extant book in the West, and it is one of the earliest books printed from movable type. ( _Jikji_, [a book of the teachings of Buddhist priests](https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/btv1b10527116j.r=Jikji?rk=21459;2), was printed by hand from movable type in Korea in 1377.) In its essentials, the wooden press used by Gutenberg reigned supreme for more than 300 years, with a hardly varying rate of 250 sheets per hour printed on one side.

Metal presses began to appear late in the 18th century, at about which time the advantages of the cylinder were first perceived and the application of [steam power](https://www.britannica.com/science/steam-power) was considered. By the mid-19th century [Richard M. Hoe](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Richard-March-Hoe) of [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state) had perfected a power-driven cylinder press in which a large central cylinder carrying the type successively printed on the paper of four impression cylinders, producing 8,000 sheets an hour in 2,000 revolutions. The [rotary press](https://www.britannica.com/technology/rotary-press) came to dominate the high-speed [newspaper](https://www.britannica.com/topic/newspaper) field, but the [flatbed press](https://www.britannica.com/technology/flatbed-press), having a flat bed to hold the type and either a [reciprocating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reciprocating) platen or a cylinder to hold the paper, continued to be used for job printing.

A significant [innovation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/innovation) of the late 19th century was the [offset press](https://www.britannica.com/technology/offset-printing), in which the printing (blanket) cylinder runs continuously in one direction while paper is impressed against it by an impression cylinder. [Offset printing](https://www.britannica.com/technology/offset-printing) is especially valuable for [colour printing](https://www.britannica.com/technology/color-printing), because an offset press can print multiple colours in one run. Offset [lithography](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lithography)—used for books, newspapers, magazines, business forms, and direct mail—continued to be the most widely used printing method at the start of the 21st century, though it was challenged by ink-jet, laser, and other printing methods.

Apart from the introduction of [electric power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electric-power), advances in press design between 1900 and the 1950s consisted of a great number of relatively minor mechanical modifications designed to improve the speed of the operation. Among these changes were better paper feed, improvements in plates and paper, automatic paper reels, and photoelectric control of colour register. The introduction of computers in the 1950s revolutionized printing [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition), with more and more steps in the print process being replaced by digital data. At the end of the 20th century a new electronic printing method, print-on-demand, began to compete with offset printing, though it—and printing generally—came under increasing pressure in developed countries as publishers, newspapers, and others turned to online means of distributing what they had previously printed on paper.

**Gutenberg Bible**, the first complete book [extant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/extant) in the West and one of the earliest printed from movable type, so called after its printer, [Johannes Gutenberg](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johannes-Gutenberg), who completed it about 1455 working at [Mainz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mainz), [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany). The three-volume work, in Latin text, was printed in 42-line columns and, in its later stages of production, was worked on by six compositors simultaneously. It is sometimes referred to as the Mazarin Bible because the first copy described by bibliographers was located in the [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris) library of Cardinal [Mazarin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cardinal-Jules-Mazarin). _The Anthology of Great Buddhist Priests’ Zen Teachings_ (1377), also known as _[Jikji](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jikji)_, was printed in Korea 78 years before the Gutenberg Bible and is recognized as the world’s oldest extant movable metal type book.

[![Gutenberg Bible](https://cdn.britannica.com/25/3625-050-11D707F0/page-Gutenberg-Bible.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/25/3625-050-11D707F0/page-Gutenberg-Bible.jpg)

[Gutenberg Bible](https://cdn.britannica.com/25/3625-050-11D707F0/page-Gutenberg-Bible.jpg) A page from the Gutenberg 42-line Bible, 1456.(more)

Like other contemporary works, the Gutenberg [Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible) had no title page, no page numbers, and no [innovations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/innovations) to distinguish it from the work of a manuscript copyist. This was presumably the desire of both Gutenberg and his customers. Experts are generally agreed that the Bible, though uneconomic in its use of space, displays a technical [efficiency](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/efficiency) not substantially improved upon before the 19th century. The Gothic type is majestic in appearance, [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) in feeling, and slightly less compressed and less pointed than other examples that appeared shortly thereafter.

The original number of copies of this work is unknown; some 40 are still in existence. There are perfect vellum copies in the [U.S. Library of Congress](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Library-of-Congress), the French [Bibliothèque Nationale](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bibliotheque-Nationale-de-France), and the [British Library](https://www.britannica.com/topic/British-Library). In the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) almost complete texts are in the [Huntington](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Huntington-Library-Art-Collections-and-Botanical-Gardens), [Morgan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Morgan-Library-and-Museum), [New York Public](https://www.britannica.com/topic/New-York-Public-Library), [Harvard University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Harvard-University-Library), and [Yale University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yale-University) libraries.

Being tall comes in handy when plucking leaves from trees. But to drink water, a giraffe must spread its long legs outward and lower its head.

Photograph by Richard Du Toit, Minden Pictures

Common Name: Giraffe

Scientific Name: Giraffa camelopardalis

Type: Mammals

Diet: Herbivore

Average Life Span In The Wild: 25 years

Size: 14 to 19 feet

Weight: 1,750 to 2,800 pounds

![](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

Many young giraffes, called calves, die from [lion](https://kids.nationalgeographic.com/animals/mammals/facts/lion) attacks during their first year of life. Once a giraffe reaches adulthood its height is often enough to protect it from lions. Adult [giraffes](https://kids.nationalgeographic.com/pages/article/high-there), however, must still be careful of lions when they are bending down to drink water or rest. Usually giraffes will drink or rest in shifts so that at least one giraffe is always on the lookout for approaching predators.​

​The giraffes' height and excellent vision give them a wide view of the grasslands where they live, making it easy to spot predators from a distance. Some scientists believe that other animals—such as [zebras](https://kids.nationalgeographic.com/animals/mammals/facts/zebra), antelope, and wildebeests—often congregate near giraffes to take advantage of their ability to see danger from a distance. The giraffe could be considered the early warning system of the African [grasslands](https://kids.nationalgeographic.com/nature/habitats/article/grassland).

**Roman road system**, outstanding transportation network of the ancient Mediterranean world, extending from Britain to the Tigris-Euphrates river system and from the Danube River to Spain and northern Africa. In all, the Romans built 50,000 miles (80,000 km) of hard-surfaced highway, primarily for military reasons.

The first of the great Roman roads, the Via Appia ( [Appian Way](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Appian-Way)), begun by the censor Appius Claudius Caecus in 312 bce, originally ran southeast from Rome 162 miles (261 km) to Tarentum (now Taranto) and was later extended to the Adriatic coast at Brundisium (now Brindisi). The long branch running through Calabria to the Straits of Messina was known as the Via Popilia. By the beginning of the 2nd century bce, four other great roads radiated from Rome: the Via Aurelia, extending northwest to Genua (Genoa); the [Via Flaminia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Via-Flaminia), running north to the Adriatic, where it joined the [Via Aemilia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Via-Aemilia), crossed the Rubicon, and led northwest; the Via Valeria, east across the peninsula by way of Lake Fucinus (Conca del Fucino); and the Via Latina, running southeast and joining the Via Appia near Capua. Their numerous feeder roads extending far into the Roman provinces led to the proverb “All roads lead to Rome.”

The Roman roads were notable for their straightness, solid foundations, cambered surfaces facilitating drainage, and use of concrete made from pozzolana (volcanic ash) and lime. Though adapting their technique to materials locally available, the Roman engineers followed basically the same principles in building abroad as they had in Italy. In 145 bce they began the [Via Egnatia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Via-Egnatia), an extension of the Via Appia beyond the Adriatic into Greece and Asia Minor, where it joined the ancient Persian Royal Road.

In northern Africa the Romans followed up their conquest of Carthage by building a road system that spanned the south shore of the Mediterranean. In [Gaul](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gaul-ancient-region-Europe) they developed a system centred on Lyon, whence main roads extended to the Rhine, Bordeaux, and the English Channel. In [Britain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Britain) the purely strategic roads following the conquest were supplemented by a network radiating from London. In [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain), on the contrary, the topography of the country dictated a system of main roads around the periphery of the peninsula, with secondary roads developed into the central plateaus.

The Roman road system made possible Roman conquest and administration and later provided highways for the great migrations into the empire and a means for the diffusion of Christianity. Despite deterioration from neglect, it continued to serve Europe throughout the Middle Ages, and many fragments of the system survive today.

- What is a legion in military terms?







A legion was the largest permanent unit in the ancient Roman army, integral to Rome's military system for conquest and rule. The legion evolved from the Greek phalanx, becoming a flexible formation of well-coordinated units.



Key features of the legion:


\\* **Structure** Initially composed of maniples (small infantry units), it later transitioned to cohorts (larger units) of around 360 men each. A full legion consisted of about 10 cohorts.


\\* **Tactics** The legion utilized both shock and firepower, with soldiers wielding the pilum (javelin) and gladius (short sword).


\\* **Flexibility** Its flexible structure and command system allowed for quick adaptation on the battlefield, as demonstrated by Caesar’s troops.


\\* **Evolution** Over time, the legion incorporated cavalry and artillery, becoming a combined-arms unit.

- Which ancient civilization is most famous for using legions?







The ancient Roman civilization is most famous for its use of [legions](https://www.britannica.com/topic/legion/Introduction#ref1). The Roman legion was a military unit that helped the Roman Republic and Empire conquer and rule the ancient world.



The Roman legion evolved from the Greek phalanx formation because the Romans found it too difficult to use in the hills and valleys of central Italy. They created a new system using small infantry units called maniples. Each maniple had 120 men arranged in 12 files and 10 ranks. Ten cohorts comprised a legion, with a small cavalry force and supporting units, totaling about 10,000 men.



Two weapons that gave the legion its flexibility and force were the pilum, a 2-metre javelin, and the gladius, a 50-centimetre sword.

- How was a Roman legion organized?







A Roman [legion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/legion/Introduction#ref1) was the largest permanent military organization in the Roman army. The Roman tactical system was based on maniples, small infantry units. Each maniple had 120 men in 12 files and 10 ranks. They were arranged in three lines, each with 10 maniples in a checkerboard pattern.



As Roman armies grew, the cohort, with about 360 men, replaced the maniple as the main tactical unit. A legion had 10 cohorts, with 4 in the first line and 3 each in the second and third lines. The 3,600 heavy infantry were supported by cavalry and light infantry, bringing the legion's strength to 6,000 men. Each legion also included a [centurion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/centurion-Roman-military-officer/Introduction#ref1), the commander of a centuria, the smallest unit of a Roman legion.

- What jobs did soldiers have within a legion?







Within a Roman legion, soldiers held various specialized roles. The _centurion_ was a professional officer commanding a _centuria_, the smallest unit, typically of about 100 men. _Legionaries_ were the backbone of the army, responsible for combat and construction tasks. There were also _auxiliaries_, supporting troops with specialized skills like slingers or cavalry. Some soldiers served as archers, slingers, or javelin men. As the empire evolved, the proportion of cavalry troops also increased. These various roles contributed to the legion's effectiveness in both offensive and defensive operations.

- How did legions help Rome build and control its empire?







The Roman [legion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/legion/Introduction#ref1) was a flexible and formidable military unit that played a crucial role in Rome's expansion and control of its empire. The legion's effectiveness stemmed from its tactical system based on small, versatile infantry units called maniples, which were well-coordinated and integrated.



Legionaries were Roman citizens or provincials who became citizens upon enlistment or discharge, which helped Romanize the empire. They were long-service professionals who received pay, bonuses, and retirement benefits, incentivizing their loyalty and service. The army was used to defend the frontiers and enforce the Pax Romana, contributing to a single system and civilization in the Mediterranean world.



Beyond military might, legions constructed fortified camps, roads, and other infrastructure, facilitating trade, communication, and administration throughout the empire. These camps sometimes evolved into great European cities.

**legion**, a [military](https://www.britannica.com/topic/armed-force) organization, originally the largest permanent organization in the armies of [ancient Rome](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Rome). The term [legion](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/legion) also denotes the military system by which imperial Rome conquered and ruled the ancient world.

The expanding early [Roman Republic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Roman-Republic) found the Greek [phalanx](https://www.britannica.com/topic/phalanx-military-formation) formation too unwieldy for fragmented fighting in the hills and valleys of central Italy. Accordingly, the Romans evolved a new tactical system based on small and supple [infantry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/infantry) units called maniples. Each maniple numbered 120 men in 12 files and 10 ranks. Maniples drew up for battle in three lines, each line made up of 10 maniples and the whole arranged in a checkerboard pattern. Separating each unit was an interval equivalent to a maniple’s front of 18 m (60 feet), so that the maniples of the first line could fall back in defense into the intervals of the second line. [Conversely](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Conversely), the second line could merge with the first to form a solid front 10 ranks deep and 360 m (1,200 feet) wide. In the third line, 10 maniples of light infantry were supplemented by smaller units of reserves. The three lines were 75 m (250 feet) apart, and from front to rear one maniple of each line formed a [cohort](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cohort-Roman-military) of 420 men; this was the Roman equivalent of a [battalion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/battalion). Ten cohorts made up the heavy-infantry strength of a legion, but 20 cohorts were usually combined with a small [cavalry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cavalry) force and other supporting units into a little self-supporting army of about 10,000 men.

Two infantry weapons gave the legion its famous flexibility and force; the _[pilum](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pilum),_ a 2-metre (7-foot) javelin used for both throwing and thrusting; and the _[gladius](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gladius),_ a 50-centimetre (20-inch) cut-and-thrust sword with a broad, heavy blade. For protecton each legionary had a metal helmet, cuirass, and convex shield. In battle, the first line of maniples attacked on the double, hurling javelins and then diving in with swords before the enemy had time to recover. Then came the maniples of the second line, and only a resolute foe could rally from the two successive shocks.

As Roman armies of the late Republic and Empire became larger and more professional, the cohort, with an average field strength of 360 men, replaced the maniple as the chief tactical unit within legions. In the military operations of [Lucius Cornelius Sulla](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sulla) and Julius Caesar, a legion was composed of 10 cohorts, with 4 cohorts in the first line and 3 each in the second and third lines. The 3,600 heavy infantry were supported by enough cavalry and light infantry to bring the legion’s strength up to 6,000 men. Seven legions in three lines, [comprising](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprising) about 25,000 heavy infantry, occupied a mile and a half of front.

As Rome evolved from a conquering to a defending power, the cohort was increased to a field strength of 500–600 men. These still depended on the shock tactics of _pilum_ and _gladius,_ but the 5,000–6,000 heavy infantry in a legion were now combined with an equal number of supporting cavalry troops and light infantry made up of archers, slingers, and javelin men. In order to deal with mounted barbarian raiders, the proportion of cavalry rose from one-seventh to one-fourth. By the 4th century ad, with the empire defending its many [fortified](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/fortified) border outposts, as many as 10 catapults and 60 ballistae were assigned to each legion.

In modern times the term legion has been applied to a corps of foreign volunteers or mercenaries, such as the French provincial legions of Francis I and the second-line formations of Napoleon. “Foreign legion” often signifies the irregular corps of foreign volunteers raised by states at [war](https://www.britannica.com/topic/war). The most famous of these is France’s [Foreign Legion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/French-Foreign-Legion) (Légion Étrangère); composed of foreign volunteers and commanded by French officers, it has served in various parts of the French colonial empire since its founding in 1831.

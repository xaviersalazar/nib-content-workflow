### What is a city?

A [city](https://www.britannica.com/topic/city) is a relatively permanent and highly organized center of population, larger than a [town](https://www.britannica.com/topic/town) or [village](https://www.britannica.com/topic/village) although the distinction between a city and a town or village is not always clear. Cities emerged as complex social and political organizations, marked by complex social structures.

Cities are distinguished by the occupational specialization of residents. Inhabitants of cities are typically socially stratified. Cities can project their power into the surrounding countryside, establishing rule over villages and becoming centers of early empires. Modern cities, while no longer sovereign, are vital centers of human activity, facing challenges in governance, technology, culture, and economics. They are hubs of innovation and cultural importance.

### What are some common features found in most cities?

[Cities](https://www.britannica.com/topic/city), as relatively permanent and highly organized centers of population, share several common features. These urban communities have diverse habitations, social arrangements, and supporting activities.

Typical features of modern cities include: \* **Infrastructure** Cities require extensive infrastructure for water supply, waste disposal, and [pollution control](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pollution-control), which are crucial for protecting public health and the environment. \* **Transportation** [Mass transit](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mass-transit) systems like buses and trains facilitate the movement of people within urban areas. \* **Zoning Regulations** [Zoning](https://www.britannica.com/money/zoning-land-use) regulations segregate different urban space uses, such as housing, manufacturing, and retail, into different areas of the city. \* **Architecture** Cities include diverse architecture, including domestic, religious, governmental, recreational, and commercial buildings. \* **Municipal Services** Cities provide essential services such as utilities, police, and fire protection.

### Why do people choose to live in cities?

People choose to live in [cities](https://www.britannica.com/topic/city) for various reasons, including economic opportunities, cultural attractions, and access to services. Cities serve as centers for trade, manufacturing, and innovation, offering a wide array of jobs and career paths. The concentration of industries and businesses in urban areas creates a dynamic environment that attracts ambitious individuals seeking advancement.

Beyond employment, cities provide diverse cultural experiences, including [museums](https://www.britannica.com/topic/museum-cultural-institution), theaters, music venues, and [restaurants](https://www.britannica.com/topic/restaurant). These amenities enrich the lives of city dwellers, offering entertainment and intellectual stimulation. Cities also tend to have better access to education, healthcare, and other essential services. The availability of [mass transportation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mass-transit) makes it easier for residents to navigate the city and access these services. While urban living often presents challenges such as overcrowding and higher living costs, many people find that the benefits outweigh the drawbacks.

**city**, relatively permanent and highly organized center of [population](https://www.britannica.com/science/population-biology-and-anthropology), of greater size or importance than a [town](https://www.britannica.com/topic/town) or [village](https://www.britannica.com/topic/village). The name _city_ is given to certain urban [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) by virtue of some [legal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/American-law) or conventional distinction that can vary between regions or nations. In most cases, however, the concept of city refers to a particular type of [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community), the urban community, and its [culture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/culture), known as “urbanism.”

City [government](https://www.britannica.com/topic/government) is almost everywhere the creation of higher political authority—usually state or national. In most [Western](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Western-World) countries, devolution of powers to the cities occurs through legislative acts that delegate limited self-government to local corporations. Some [European](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) countries adopted general municipal codes that permitted centralized administrative control over subordinate areas through a [hierarchy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hierarchy) of departmental prefects and local [mayors](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mayor). Socialist countries generally employed a hierarchical system of local councils corresponding to, and under the [authority](https://www.britannica.com/topic/authority) of, governing bodies at higher levels of government.

As a type of [community](https://www.britannica.com/topic/community-human), the city may be regarded as a relatively permanent concentration of population, together with its [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) habitations, social arrangements, and supporting activities, occupying a more or less discrete site and having a cultural importance that [differentiates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/differentiates) it from other types of [human](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being) settlement and association. In its elementary functions and [rudimentary](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/rudimentary) characteristics, however, a city is not clearly distinguishable from a town or even a large village. Mere size of population, surface area, or density of settlement are not in themselves sufficient [criteria](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criteria) of distinction, while many of their social correlates (division of labor, nonagricultural activity, central-place functions, and creativity) characterize in varying degree all urban communities from the small country town to the giant [metropolis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/metropolitan-area).

## Ancient world

![Rise of Civilizations](https://cdn.britannica.com/60/273760-050-068065E5/rise-of-civilizations-fertile-crescent-indus-river-valley-china-mesoamerica-andes-8500BCE-1000CE.jpg)

[Rise of Civilizations](https://cdn.britannica.com/60/273760-050-068065E5/rise-of-civilizations-fertile-crescent-indus-river-valley-china-mesoamerica-andes-8500BCE-1000CE.jpg) A timeline of major events in the development of complex society.(more)

During the [Neolithic](https://www.britannica.com/event/Neolithic) (New Stone Age)—a relative chronological period that occurred at different times across the world, generally beginning between 9500 and 3000 bce—humans began living in more or less fixed [settlements](https://www.britannica.com/topic/civilization). Such settlements were largely agricultural [villages](https://www.britannica.com/topic/village). In several places such villages developed into cities supported by [domesticated](https://www.britannica.com/science/domestication) plants and animals. The surplus produced by improved methods of cultivation and stock breeding made it possible to sustain a higher [population density](https://www.britannica.com/science/population-density) while also freeing up some members of the community for craft making and the production of nonessential goods and services.

[![Mohenjo-daro: tower](https://cdn.britannica.com/65/160265-050-3EF9E9B8/Mohenjo-daro-Pakistan.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/65/160265-050-3EF9E9B8/Mohenjo-daro-Pakistan.jpg)

[Mohenjo-daro: tower](https://cdn.britannica.com/65/160265-050-3EF9E9B8/Mohenjo-daro-Pakistan.jpg) Remains of a stupalike stone tower, Mohenjo-daro, Sindh province, southeastern Pakistan.(more)

The earliest recognizable cities had emerged by approximately 3500 bce in the [ancient Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Middle-East). The urban [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) of [Mesopotamia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mesopotamia-historical-region-Asia) and [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt) were distinguished by literacy, technological progress (notably in metals), and increasingly sophisticated forms of social and political organization. The cities of these areas first developed in the [Nile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nile-River) [valley](https://www.britannica.com/science/valley) (in Egypt) and on the Sumerian [coast](https://www.britannica.com/science/coast) in modern [Iraq](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iraq). Cities appeared in the Indus valley at sites such as [Mohenjo-daro](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mohenjo-daro) and [Harappa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Harappa) during the 3rd millennium bce; by 2000 bce cities had also appeared in the [Wei River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wei-River-Gansu-and-Shaanxi-provinces-China) valley in [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China). The overland trade routes brought about the proliferation of cities from [Turkistan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Turkistan) to the [Caspian Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Caspian-Sea) and then to the [Persian Gulf](https://www.britannica.com/place/Persian-Gulf) and eastern [Mediterranean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea). Their economic base in [agriculture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agriculture) (supplemented by trade) and their political-religious institutions gave cities an unprecedented degree of occupational specialization and social stratification. City life was not insular, however, as many cities lent some [coherence](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/coherence) and direction to life and society in their [hinterlands](https://www.britannica.com/money/hinterland).

## Autonomous and dependent cities

It was in the [Greek](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Greece) [city-state](https://www.britannica.com/topic/city-state), or [polis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/polis), that the city idea reached its peak of importance. Originally a devout association of patriarchal [clans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/clan), the polis came to be a small self-governing community of citizens, in contrast to the Asian empires and nomadic groups elsewhere in the world. For citizens, at least, the city and its laws [constituted](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituted) a [moral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/moral) order symbolized in an [acropolis](https://www.britannica.com/technology/acropolis-ancient-Greek-district), magnificent buildings, and public assemblies. It was, in [Aristotle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle)’s phrase, “a common life for a noble end.”

[![Athens: Acropolis](https://cdn.britannica.com/66/102266-050-FBDEFCA1/acropolis-city-state-Greece-Athens.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/66/102266-050-FBDEFCA1/acropolis-city-state-Greece-Athens.jpg)

[Athens: Acropolis](https://cdn.britannica.com/66/102266-050-FBDEFCA1/acropolis-city-state-Greece-Athens.jpg) The Acropolis and surrounding area, Athens.(more)

When the [exclusive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/exclusive) requirements for [citizenship](https://www.britannica.com/topic/citizenship) (citizens originally being landowning men with no history of servitude) were relaxed and as new commercial wealth surpassed that of the older landed citizenry, social strife at home and rivalry abroad gradually weakened the common life of the city-republics. The creativity and variety of the polis gave way before the unifying forces of king worship and [empire](https://www.britannica.com/topic/empire-political-science) epitomized by [Alexander the Great](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alexander-the-Great) and his successors. To be sure, many new cities—often named [Alexandria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alexandria-Egypt) because Alexander had founded them—were planted between the Nile and the [Indus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indus-River), [facilitating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/facilitating) contacts between the major civilizations of Europe and Asia and giving rise to cultural exchanges and commercial trade that left a lasting impact on both East and West. While remaining culturally vibrant, the city itself ceased to be an [autonomous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/autonomous) [body politic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/body-politic) and became a dependent member of a larger political-ideological whole.

The [Romans](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Rome), who fell heir to the Hellenistic world, transplanted the city into the areas beyond the [Alps](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alps) inhabited by pastoral-agricultural Celtic and [Germanic peoples](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Germanic-peoples) and made the city a means to empire (a center for [military](https://www.britannica.com/topic/armed-force) pacification and [bureaucratic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/bureaucratic) control) rather than an end in itself. The enjoyment of the imperial Roman peace entailed the acceptance of the status of [_municipium_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/municipium)—a respectable but subordinate rank within the Roman state. The _municipia_ were supported fiscally by taxes on trade, contributions from members of the community, and income from lands owned by each _municipium_. Over time, however, the idea of public duty gave way to private ambition, especially as Roman citizenship became more universal ( _see_ [_civitas_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/civitas)). Municipal functions atrophied, and the city survived into the [Byzantine](https://www.britannica.com/place/Byzantine-Empire) era principally as a mechanism of fiscal administration, although it often remained a locus of educational development and religious and cultural expression.

## The medieval city, from fortress to emporium

In Latin Europe neither political nor religious reforms could sustain the Roman regime. The breakdown of [public administration](https://www.britannica.com/topic/public-administration) and the [breach](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/breach) of the frontier led to a revival of [parochial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/parochial) outlook and [allegiance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/allegiance), but the focus was not upon the city. Community life centered instead on the [fortress](https://www.britannica.com/technology/castle-architecture) (e.g., walled city), whereas the _civitas_ was attached to the precincts of the episcopal throne, as in [Merovingian Gaul](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Merovingian-dynasty).

Early [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) society was a creation of camp and countryside that fulfilled the local [imperatives](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/imperatives) of sustenance and defense. With Germanic variations on late Roman forms, communities were restructured into functional [estates](https://www.britannica.com/topic/estate-system), each of which owned formal obligations, immunities, and jurisdictions. What remained of the city was comprehended in this [manorial](https://www.britannica.com/topic/manor) order, and the distinction between [town](https://www.britannica.com/topic/town) and country was largely obscured when [secular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/secular) and [ecclesiastical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ecclesiastical) lords ruled over the surrounding counties—often as the vassals of Germanic kings ( _see_ [manorialism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/manorialism)). Social [ethos](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ethos) and organization enforced submission to the [common good](https://www.britannica.com/topic/common-good) of earthly survival and heavenly reward. The attenuation of city life in most of northern and western Europe was accompanied by provincial separatism, economic isolation, and religious otherworldliness. Not before the cessation of attacks by Magyars, [Vikings](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Viking-people), and [Saracens](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Saracen) did urban communities again experience sustained growth.

Recovery after the 10th century was not confined to the city or to any one part of Europe. The [initiatives](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/initiatives) of monastic orders, seigneurs, or lords of the manor, and merchants alike fostered a new era of increased tillage, craftsmanship and manufacturing, a [money](https://www.britannica.com/money/money) economy, scholarship, growth of rural population, and founding of “new towns,” as distinguished from those “Roman” cities that had survived from the period of Germanic and other encroachments. In almost all the “new” medieval towns, the role of the merchant was central in catalyzing the [long-distance trade](https://www.britannica.com/money/international-trade) of commodities and staple goods.

Before the year 1000, contacts with rich [Byzantine](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Byzantine) and Islamic areas in the [Levant](https://www.britannica.com/place/Levant) had revitalized the mercantile power in [Venice](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venice), which grew wealthy from its command of the profitable route to the Holy Land during the [Crusades](https://www.britannica.com/event/Crusades). Meanwhile, merchant communities had attached themselves to the more-accessible castle towns and [dioceses](https://www.britannica.com/topic/diocese) in northern Italy and on the main routes to the Rhineland and Champagne. They later appeared along the rivers of [Flanders](https://www.britannica.com/place/Flanders-medieval-principality-and-historical-region-Europe) and northern France and on the west-east road from [Cologne](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cologne-Germany) to [Magdeburg](https://www.britannica.com/place/Magdeburg-Germany) ( _see_ [Hanseatic League](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hanseatic-League)). In all of these towns, trade was the key to their growth and development.

[![Speyer Cathedral](https://cdn.britannica.com/06/231606-050-3840DA8E/Germany-Speyer-Aerial-view-of-Speyer-Cathedral.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/06/231606-050-3840DA8E/Germany-Speyer-Aerial-view-of-Speyer-Cathedral.jpg)

[Speyer Cathedral](https://cdn.britannica.com/06/231606-050-3840DA8E/Germany-Speyer-Aerial-view-of-Speyer-Cathedral.jpg) Speyer Cathedral in Germany, begun during the 11th century.(more)

It was no coincidence that the 12th and 13th centuries, which saw the founding of more new towns than any time between [the fall of Rome](https://www.britannica.com/place/Roman-Empire/Height-and-decline-of-imperial-Rome#ref348467) and the [Industrial Revolution](https://www.britannica.com/event/Industrial-Revolution), also witnessed a singular upsurge toward civic [autonomy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/autonomy). Throughout western Europe, towns acquired various kinds of municipal institutions loosely grouped under the [designation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/designation) [_commune_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/commune-medieval-Western-Europe). Broadly speaking, the history of the medieval towns is that of the rising merchant classes seeking to free their communities from lordly jurisdiction and to secure their government to themselves. Wherever [monarchical](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monarchy) power was strong, the merchants had to be content with a municipal status, but elsewhere they created city-states. Taking advantage of renewed conflict between popes and emperors, they allied with local nobility to establish communal self-government in the largest cities of [Lombardy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lombardy), [Tuscany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tuscany), and [Liguria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Liguria). In [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany) the city councils sometimes usurped the rights of higher clergy and nobility; [Freiburg im Breisgau](https://www.britannica.com/place/Freiburg-im-Breisgau) obtained its [exemplary](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/exemplary) charter of liberties in 1120. The movement spread to [Lübeck](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lubeck) and later to associated Hanse towns on the Baltic and North seas, touching even the Christian “colonial” towns east of the Elbe and Saale rivers. In the 13th century the great towns of [Bruges](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brugge), [Ghent](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ghent), and [Ypres](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ypres), creditors of the counts of [Flanders](https://www.britannica.com/place/Flanders-medieval-principality-and-historical-region-Europe), virtually governed the entire [province](https://www.britannica.com/topic/province-ancient-Roman-government). In [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), revolutionary uprisings, directed against nobility and clergy, sometimes established free communes, but most communities were content with a franchise from their sovereign—despite their limitations compared with the relative liberty of English boroughs after the [Norman Conquest](https://www.britannica.com/event/Norman-Conquest). Finally the [corporate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/corporate) freedom of the towns brought emancipation to individuals. When bishops in the older German cities treated newcomers as serfs, the emperor [Henry V](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-V-Holy-Roman-emperor) affirmed the principle _Stadtluft macht frei_ (German: “City air brings freedom”) in charters for [Speyer](https://www.britannica.com/place/Speyer) and [Worms](https://www.britannica.com/place/Worms); such new towns, founded on the lands of lay and clerical lords, offered freedom and land to settlers who took up residence for more than “a year and a day.” In France the _villes neuves_(“new towns”) and [_bastides_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bastide) (medieval French towns laid out on a rectangular grid) likewise conferred rights on servile persons.

[![Florence](https://cdn.britannica.com/67/215467-050-0FBF9940/Aerial-view-dome-Cathedral-Santa-Maria-del-Fiore-Florence-Italy.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/67/215467-050-0FBF9940/Aerial-view-dome-Cathedral-Santa-Maria-del-Fiore-Florence-Italy.jpg)

[Florence](https://cdn.britannica.com/67/215467-050-0FBF9940/Aerial-view-dome-Cathedral-Santa-Maria-del-Fiore-Florence-Italy.jpg) View of Florence, Italy, from the campanile of the Duomo.(more)

In the 14th century the growth of urban centers subsided as Europe suffered a series of shocks that included [famine](https://www.britannica.com/science/famine) from 1315 to 1317, the emergence of the [Black Death](https://www.britannica.com/event/Black-Death), which spread across Europe starting in 1347, and a period of political [anarchy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/anarchy) and economic decline that continued through the 15th century. [Turkish](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Turkic-peoples) encroachments on the routes to Asia worsened conditions in town and country alike. Europe turned inward upon itself, and, except for a few large centers, activity in the marketplace was depressed. At a time when local specialization and interregional exchange required more-liberal trade policies, craft protectionism and corporate particularism in the cities tended to hobble the course of [economic growth](https://www.britannica.com/money/economic-growth). The artisan and laboring classes, moreover, grew strong enough to challenge the oligarchical rule of the wealthy burghers and gentry through disruptions such as the [Revolt of the Ciompi](https://www.britannica.com/event/Revolt-of-the-Ciompi) (1378), while social [warfare](https://www.britannica.com/topic/war) peaked in peasant uprisings typified by the [Jacquerie](https://www.britannica.com/event/Jacquerie) (1358), but these tended to be short-lived revolts that failed to bring enduring [social change](https://www.britannica.com/topic/social-change). The era of decline was relieved, some argue, by the slow process of individual emancipation and the cultural efflorescence of the [Renaissance](https://www.britannica.com/event/Renaissance), which effectively grew out of the unique urban [environment](https://www.britannica.com/science/environment) of Italy and was strengthened by a high regard for the Classical heritage. These values laid the [intellectual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectual) basis for the great age of geographic and scientific discovery exemplified in the new technologies of [gunpowder](https://www.britannica.com/technology/gunpowder), [mining](https://www.britannica.com/technology/mining), [printing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/printing-publishing), and [navigation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/navigation-technology). Not before the triumph of princely government, in fact, did political allegiance, economic interests, and spiritual authority again become centered in a viable unit of organization, the absolutist [nation-state](https://www.britannica.com/topic/nation-state).

## The city and the nation-state

The virtue of [absolutism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/absolutism-political-system) in the early modern period lay in its ability to utilize the new technologies on a large scale. Through the centralization of power, economy, and belief, it brought order and progress to Europe and provided a framework in which individual energies could once more be channeled to a common end. While the nation stripped the cities of their remaining [pretensions](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/pretensions) to political and economic independence (heretofore symbolized in their walls and [tariff](https://www.britannica.com/money/tariff) barriers), it created larger systems of interdependence in which territorial [division of labor](https://www.britannica.com/money/division-of-labour) could operate. National wealth also benefited from the new [mercantilist](https://www.britannica.com/money/mercantilism) policies, but all too often the wealth generated by cities was captured by the state in taxes and then dissipated—either in war or by supporting the splendor of court life and the Baroque glory of palaces and churches. Only in colonial areas, notably the Americas, did the age of expansion see the development of many new cities, and it is significant that the capitals and ports of the colonizing nations experienced their most rapid growth during these years. Under absolutist regimes, however, a few large political and commercial centers grew at the expense of smaller outlying communities and the rural hinterlands.

By the 18th century the mercantile classes had grown increasingly disenchanted with [monarchical](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monarchy) rule. Merchants resented their lack of political influence and assured [prestige](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/prestige), and they objected to outmoded regulations that created barriers to commerce—especially those that hindered their efforts to link commercial operations with improved production systems such as factories. Eventually, the merchants would unite with other dissident groups to curb the excesses of absolutism, erase the vestiges of feudalism, and secure a larger voice in the shaping of public policy. In northwestern Europe, where these liberal movements went furthest, the city populations and their influential bourgeois elites played a critical role that was disproportionate to their numbers. Elsewhere, as in Germany, the [bourgeoisie](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bourgeoisie) was more [reconciled](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reconciled) to existing regimes or, as in northern Italy, had assumed a passive if not wholly parasitical role.

With the exceptions of Great Britain and the Netherlands, however, the proportion of national populations resident in urban areas nowhere exceeded 10 percent. # [Industrialization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/industrialization) and the modern world

[![British factory](https://cdn.britannica.com/34/193634-050-BECD7AC4/factories-Industrial-Revolution-workers-house-machines.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/34/193634-050-BECD7AC4/factories-Industrial-Revolution-workers-house-machines.jpg)

[British factory](https://cdn.britannica.com/34/193634-050-BECD7AC4/factories-Industrial-Revolution-workers-house-machines.jpg) British factory interior, late 19th century.(more)

Before 1800, [innovations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/innovations) in agricultural and manufacturing techniques had permitted a singular concentration of productive activity close to the sources of mechanical power—water and coal. A corresponding movement of [population](https://www.britannica.com/science/population-biology-and-anthropology) was accelerated by the perfection of the [steam engine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/steam-engine) and the superiority of the [factory](https://www.britannica.com/technology/factory) over preindustrial [business organization](https://www.britannica.com/money/business-organization). From the standpoint of economy, therefore, the localization of [differentiated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/differentiated) but functionally [integrated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrated) work processes near sources of fuel was the mainspring of industrial urbanism. Under conditions of belt-and-pulley power transmission, urban concentration was a means of (1) minimizing the costs of overcoming frictions in [transport](https://www.britannica.com/technology/history-of-transportation) and communications and (2) maximizing internal [economies of scale](https://www.britannica.com/money/economy-of-scale) and external economies of agglomeration. Although the [intellectual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectual) and social prerequisites for industrialization were not uniquely present in any one nation, an unusual [confluence](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/confluence) of commercial, geographic, and technological factors in [Britain](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-Kingdom) led to far-reaching changes in such strategic activities as textiles, transport, and iron. [Britain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Britain-island-Europe) became “the workshop of the world” and [London](https://www.britannica.com/place/London) its “head office.” Differentiation went so far that the cotton, wool, and iron districts became more specialized and productive, each proceeding within its own cycle of technical and organizational change. By the mid-19th century, similar if less-comprehensive industrial organization was evident in parts of [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), the [Low Countries](https://www.britannica.com/place/Low-Countries), and the northeastern [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States).

[![Toronto](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/76031-050-162E5900/CN-Tower-rest-skyline-Toronto-world-structure.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/76031-050-162E5900/CN-Tower-rest-skyline-Toronto-world-structure.jpg)

[Toronto](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/76031-050-162E5900/CN-Tower-rest-skyline-Toronto-world-structure.jpg) Skyline of Toronto.(more)

The concentration of the manufacturing labor force in mill towns and coke towns gradually undermined traditional social structures and relations. Problems of public order, health, housing, utilities, [education](https://www.britannica.com/topic/education), and [morals](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/morals) were aggravated by the influx of newcomers from the countryside. The combination of high rural birth rates and the industrialization of agriculture raised production levels of foods and fibers but also caused more children to migrate to cities, as fewer were needed to work on the increasingly mechanized farms. Though the lowering of mortality in the 19th century was later offset by declines in fertility, the population of the more-industrialized nations boomed into the 20th century, and the greater part of the increment migrated to the larger towns. The outcome was rural depopulation and the [urbanization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/urbanization) of society. Local political and social institutions, often of [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) origin, were unable to cope with conditions that exaggerated [poverty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/poverty), disrupted [family](https://www.britannica.com/topic/family-kinship) life, and complicated personal adjustment. Piecemeal reforms did little to improve the new [milieu](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/milieu) because, in the last analysis, the “city problem” arose not so much from the lack of public [authority](https://www.britannica.com/topic/authority) as from an unwillingness to pay the costs of social planning, [public health](https://www.britannica.com/topic/public-health), and civic improvement. Generations of urbanites therefore faced long work hours, poor work conditions, overcrowded housing, and inadequate sanitation. The populations of cities, however, adapted to the new urban norms, evidently striking a balance between the [deleterious](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/deleterious) consequences of urbanization and the economic and cultural opportunities uniquely associated with the city.

In the century after 1850, world population doubled, and the proportion of people living in cities of more than 5,000 inhabitants rose from less than 7 percent to almost 30 percent. Between 1900 and 1950 the population living in large cities (100,000 plus) rose by 250 percent, the rate of increase in Asia being three times that in Europe and the United States. Nevertheless, the pattern of industrial urbanization—an overwhelmingly nonagricultural economy organized in a hierarchical system of different-sized cities ranging from one or more [metropolitan centers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/metropolitan-area) at the top to a broad base of smaller-sized cities underneath—was still largely confined to the economically advanced areas: Europe, [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America), Japan, and, to a lesser extent, Australasia.

[![Suburban housing in Las Vegas](https://cdn.britannica.com/48/148448-050-CE622746/Las-Vegas-Nevada.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/48/148448-050-CE622746/Las-Vegas-Nevada.jpg)

[Suburban housing in Las Vegas](https://cdn.britannica.com/48/148448-050-CE622746/Las-Vegas-Nevada.jpg) The suburbs of Las Vegas, Nevada.(more)

Meanwhile, industrial urbanism had entered its [metropolitan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/metropolitan-area) phase. Especially in the United States, the widespread use of cheap [electric power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/electric-power), the advent of [rapid transit](https://www.britannica.com/technology/rapid-transit) and communications, new [building](https://www.britannica.com/technology/building) materials, the automobile, and rising levels of per capita personal income had led to [some relaxation of urban concentration](https://www.britannica.com/topic/urban-sprawl). City dwellers began moving out from older downtown areas to [suburbs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/suburb) and satellite [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) where conditions were thought to be less wearing on nerves and bodies. Rising central-area land values and [property](https://www.britannica.com/money/property-legal-concept) taxes, traffic congestion, decaying [infrastructure](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infrastructure), and street [crime](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crime-law) reinforced the exodus. At the city’s core the [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) of the resident population came to include growing proportions of the aged, minority groups, and the very poor.

[![Rio de Janeiro](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/183454-050-A602E4AE/Night-view-Rio-de-Janeiro.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/183454-050-A602E4AE/Night-view-Rio-de-Janeiro.jpg)

[Rio de Janeiro](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/183454-050-A602E4AE/Night-view-Rio-de-Janeiro.jpg) Night view of Rio de Janeiro.(more)

Just as populations shifted from city centers to suburbs and broader conurbations, manufacturing companies began building their production plants on suburban or rural sites, thereby taking advantage of cheaper land costs, lower labor expenses, less-cumbersome municipal [regulation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/regulation), smaller tax burdens, and, in many cases, more-efficient transportation routes. By the 21st century it was evident that advanced economies would depend more on [human capital](https://www.britannica.com/money/human-capital) than on [physical capital](https://www.britannica.com/money/physical-capital) and on the production of services rather than the manufacture of goods; as a [consequence](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/consequence), cities were shifting from loci of industrial production to centers of knowledge. Thus, while its advantages for manufacturers have diminished, the city remains a fundamental locus for the mass of specialized service activity that forms so large a part of the modern economy.

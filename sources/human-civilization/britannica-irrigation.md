**hydraulic civilization**, according to the theories of the German-American historian Karl A. [Wittfogel](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Karl-Wittfogel), any [culture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/culture) having an agricultural system that is dependent upon large-scale government-managed waterworks—productive (for irrigation) and protective (for [flood](https://www.britannica.com/science/flood) control). Wittfogel advanced the term in his book _[Oriental Despotism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Oriental-Despotism)_ (1957). He believed that such civilizations—although neither all in the Orient nor characteristic of all Oriental societies—were quite different from those of the West.

Wittfogel believed that wherever [irrigation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation) required substantial and centralized control, government representatives monopolized political power and dominated the economy, resulting in an absolutist managerial state. In addition, there was a close identification of these officials with the dominant religion and an atrophy of other centres of power. The [forced labour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/forced-labour) for irrigation projects was directed by the [bureaucratic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/bureaucratic) network. Among these hydraulic civilizations, Wittfogel listed [ancient Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt), Mesopotamia, China, and India and pre-Columbian [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico) and [Peru](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru).

The extreme importance of the role of irrigation in [social development](https://www.britannica.com/science/social-learning) has been disputed by other writers. Not all of the features that Wittfogel linked are necessarily found together, and they also may appear without large-scale irrigation. The static nature of his model has also been criticized. The U.S. anthropologist [Robert McCormick Adams](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-McCormick-Adams) suggested that archaeological evidence fails to support Wittfogel’s [contention](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contention) that irrigation is the primary cause of the formation of coercive political institutions but conceded that, as part of a larger system of subsistence techniques, political structure, and economic relationships, it may help consolidate political control.

- What is irrigation?







[Irrigation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation/Introduction#ref1) is the artificial application of water to land for agriculture. It supplements rainfall to increase crop production, especially in areas with insufficient rainfall. Systems include:



  - **Surface irrigation** Water flows over the field, using flood or furrow systems.
  - **Subirrigation** Water is applied to the soil through tile drains or ditches.
  - **Sprinkler irrigation** Sprinklers distribute water and can control application rates.
  - **Drip irrigation** Water is applied at a very low rate to plant roots, which is considered the most efficient irrigation technique.

However, irrigation can lead to [salinity](https://www.britannica.com/technology/agricultural-technology/Chemicals#ref67844), where salts in the water concentrate in the soil, potentially harming plant growth. Managing this requires a favorable salt balance, where the salt leaving the area equals or exceeds the amount received in the water supply.

- Why do farmers use irrigation in agriculture?







Farmers use [irrigation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation/Introduction#ref1) in agriculture to artificially apply water to land, which is essential for crop growth and increased production. Irrigation is particularly vital in regions with insufficient rainfall or during dry seasons, ensuring a consistent water supply for [vegetable](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vegetable-farming/Care-of-crops-during-growth#ref67914) and [crop](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia/Agriculture#ref48227) cultivation. Efficient irrigation methods, such as [drip irrigation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/water-infrastructure-in-India/Introduction#ref407591), help conserve water and maintain optimal soil moisture, while [sprinkler systems](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation/Introduction#ref67755) can also be used for [frost](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vegetable-farming/Care-of-crops-during-growth#ref67914) protection and application of chemicals.

- What are some common methods of irrigation?







Irrigation involves artificially applying water to land for agricultural production, supplementing rainfall or enabling agriculture in otherwise unusable areas. Common methods include [surface irrigation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation/Introduction#ref67755), [subirrigation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation/Introduction#ref67755), and [sprinkler systems](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation/Introduction#ref67755).



Surface irrigation, used on uniformly sloped land, includes flood and furrow systems. Flood systems apply water across the entire field, while furrow systems run water through plowed furrows between crop rows. Subirrigation, less common, traps water in the root zone using an impermeable layer and tile drains or ditches. Sprinklers, increasingly popular, control application rates and can also provide frost protection and apply chemicals.

- How does irrigation improve crop growth and yield?







Irrigation is the artificial application of water to land for agriculture, supplementing rainfall to boost crop production. It ensures consistent water availability, which is vital for maximizing the benefits of fertilizers, crop rotations, and high-yield crop varieties. Different irrigation methods, such as surface, sprinkler, subirrigation and drip irrigation, can be used based on the type of crop, topography, and water availability. By providing adequate moisture, irrigation expands crop acreage and can transform nonproductive land, especially in arid regions with nutrient-rich soils, into fertile areas.

- What are the advantages and disadvantages of using irrigation?







Irrigation is the artificial application of water to land for agriculture, supplementing rainfall to boost crop production. Different irrigation systems exist, including surface, sprinkler, subirrigation and drip irrigation.



**Advantages:** Irrigation ensures year-round availability of multiple crops, protecting farmers from erratic rainfall and strengthening food security. Sprinkler systems apply controlled water efficiently, minimizing labor. Drip irrigation precisely delivers water to plant roots, maintaining optimal soil moisture.



**Disadvantages:** Irrigation can lead to environmental damage, including soil salinity from concentrated salts. Water quality can be impaired through evapotranspiration, where salts in the irrigation water become more concentrated in the drainage effluent. Additionally, surface irrigation can lead to flooding of cultivated land.

- How can irrigation affect the environment and water resources?







Irrigation can have both positive and negative effects on the environment and water resources. While it is essential for [agriculture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agriculture) in many regions, especially in arid zones, it can also lead to [environmental damage](https://www.britannica.com/technology/agricultural-technology/Chemicals#ref67844).



One major issue is [salinity](https://www.britannica.com/technology/agricultural-technology/Chemicals#ref67844), where salts in the irrigation water accumulate in the soil, potentially harming crops and reducing productivity. To combat this, excess water is used to leach the salts, but this can impair water quality.



Additionally, [poor irrigation practices](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tigris-Euphrates-river-system/Economy#ref48105) and inadequate drainage can cause long-term damage to the soil through salinization. Sustainable agriculture seeks to address the contamination of surface water and groundwater.

**irrigation**, in [agriculture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agriculture). the artificial application of [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) to land. Some land requires irrigation before it is possible to use it for any agricultural production. In other places, irrigation is primarily a means to supplement rainfall and serves to increase production. Some land, of course, does not need any irrigation. Although the practice may be used for nonagricultural purposes to improve the [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment), this article is limited to irrigation in agricultural [contexts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contexts).

Irrigation and [drainage](https://www.britannica.com/topic/drainage) improvements are not necessarily mutually [exclusive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/exclusive). Often both may be required together to assure sustained high-level production of crops. For a discussion of the role of drainage practices in agriculture, _see_ [drainage](https://www.britannica.com/topic/drainage).

## Modern irrigation system planning and construction

## [Water supply](https://www.britannica.com/technology/water-supply-system)

[![Los Angeles Aqueduct](https://cdn.britannica.com/91/141191-050-9603F3A7/Los-Angeles-Aqueduct.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/91/141191-050-9603F3A7/Los-Angeles-Aqueduct.jpg)

[Los Angeles Aqueduct](https://cdn.britannica.com/91/141191-050-9603F3A7/Los-Angeles-Aqueduct.jpg)(more)

The first consideration in planning an irrigation project is developing a [water supply](https://www.britannica.com/science/water-supply). Water supplies may be classified as surface or subsurface. Though both surface and [subsurface water](https://www.britannica.com/science/groundwater) come from [precipitation](https://www.britannica.com/science/precipitation) such as [rain](https://www.britannica.com/science/rain) or snow, it is far more difficult to determine the origin of subsurface water.

In planning a surface water supply, extensive studies must be made of the flow in the stream or [river](https://www.britannica.com/science/river) that will be used. If the streamflow has been measured regularly over a long period, including times of [drought](https://www.britannica.com/science/drought) and [flood](https://www.britannica.com/science/flood), the studies are greatly simplified. From streamflow [data](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/data), determinations can be made of the minimum, maximum, average daily, and average monthly flows; the size of [dams](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dam-engineering), [spillways](https://www.britannica.com/technology/spillway-engineering), and the downstream channel; and the seasonal and carryover storage needed. If adequate streamflow data are not available, the streamflow may be estimated from rain and snow data or from flow data from nearby streams that have similar climatic and physiographic conditions.

The quality, as well as the quantity, of surface water is a factor. The two most important considerations are the amount of [silt](https://www.britannica.com/science/silt) carried and the kind and amount of [salts](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt) dissolved in the water. If the silt content is high, sediment will be deposited in the [reservoir](https://www.britannica.com/technology/reservoir), increasing maintenance costs and decreasing useful life periods. If the salt concentration is high, it may damage crops or [accumulate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/accumulate) in the [soil](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil) and eventually render it unproductive.

Subsurface sources of water must be as carefully investigated as surface sources. In general, less is known about subsurface supplies of water than about surface supplies, so, therefore, subsurface supplies are harder to investigate. Engineers planning a project need to know the extent of the basic geological source of water (the [aquifer](https://www.britannica.com/science/aquifer)), as well as the amount the water level is lowered by pumping and the rate of [recharge](https://www.britannica.com/science/groundwater-recharge) of the aquifer. Often the only way for the engineer to obtain these data reliably is to drill test wells and make on-site measurements. Ideally, a project is planned so as not to use more subsurface water than is recharged. Otherwise, the water is said to be “mined,” meaning that it is being used up as a [natural resource](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-resource) and its use is considered unsustainable.

Two sources of water not often thought of by the layperson are [dew](https://www.britannica.com/science/dew) and [sewage](https://www.britannica.com/technology/sewerage-system) or wastewater. In certain parts of the world, [Israel](https://www.britannica.com/place/Israel) and part of [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia), for example, where atmospheric conditions are right, [sufficient](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/sufficient) dew may be trapped at night to provide water for irrigation. Elsewhere the supply of wastewater from some industries and municipalities is sufficient to irrigate relatively small acreages. Recently, due to greater emphasis on purer water in streams, there has been increased interest in this latter practice.

In some countries ( [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt) for example) sewage is a valuable source of water. In others, such as the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), irrigation is looked upon as a means of disposing of sewer water as a final step in the [wastewater treatment](https://www.britannica.com/technology/wastewater-treatment) process. Unless the water contains unusual chemical salts, such as [sodium](https://www.britannica.com/science/sodium), it is generally of satisfactory quality for agricultural irrigation. Where the practice is used primarily as a means of disposal, large areas are involved and the choice of [crop](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crop-agriculture) is critical. Usually only grass or trees can withstand the year-round applications.

Before a water supply can be [assured](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/assured), the [right](https://www.britannica.com/topic/right) to it must be determined. Countries and states have widely varying laws and customs that determine ownership of water. If the development of a water supply is for a single purpose, then the determination of ownership may be relatively simple; but if the development is multipurpose, as most modern developments are, ownership may be difficult to determine, and agreements must be worked out among countries, states, municipalities, and private owners.

The area that can be irrigated by a water supply depends on the weather, the type of crop grown, and the soil. Numerous methods have been developed to evaluate these factors and predict average annual volume of rainfall needed. Some representative annual amounts of rainfall needed for cropland in the western United States are 305 to 760 mm (12 to 30 inches) for [cereal grains](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cereal) and 610 to 1,525 mm (24 to 60 inches) for [forage](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/forage). In the [Near East](https://www.britannica.com/place/Near-East), [cotton](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cotton-fibre-and-plant) needs about 915 mm (36 inches), whereas [rice](https://www.britannica.com/plant/rice) may require two to three times that amount. In humid regions of the United States, where irrigation supplements rainfall, grain crops may require 150 to 230 mm (6 to 9 inches) of water. In addition to satisfying the needs of the crop, allowances must be made for water lost directly to [evaporation](https://www.britannica.com/science/evaporation) and during transport to the fields.

## Transport systems

[![irrigation canal](https://cdn.britannica.com/70/156670-050-7E37C3D6/Irrigation-canal-farm-field.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/70/156670-050-7E37C3D6/Irrigation-canal-farm-field.jpg)

[irrigation canal](https://cdn.britannica.com/70/156670-050-7E37C3D6/Irrigation-canal-farm-field.jpg) Irrigation canal in a farm field.(more)

The type of transport system used for an irrigation project is often determined by the source of the water supply. If a surface water supply is used, a large [canal](https://www.britannica.com/technology/canal-waterway) or pipeline system is usually required to carry the water to the farms because the reservoir is likely to be distant from the point of use. If subsurface water drawn from wells is used, a much smaller transport system is needed, though canals or [pipelines](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pipeline-technology) may be used. The transport system will depend as far as possible on gravity flow, supplemented if necessary by pumping. From the mains, water flows into branches, or laterals, and finally to distributors that serve groups of farms. Many [auxiliary](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/auxiliary) structures are required, including [weirs](https://www.britannica.com/technology/weir-engineering) (flow-diversion dams), sluices, and other types of dams. Canals are normally lined with concrete to prevent seepage losses, control weed growth, eliminate erosion hazards, and reduce maintenance. The most common type of concrete canal construction is by slip forming. In this type of construction, the canal is excavated to the exact [cross section](https://www.britannica.com/science/cross-section-physics) desired and the concrete placed on the earth sides and bottom.

Pipelines may be constructed of many types of material. The larger lines are usually concrete whereas laterals may be concrete, cement–asbestos, rigid plastic, aluminum, or steel. Although pipelines are more costly than open [conduits](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conduits), they do not require land after construction, suffer little evaporation loss, and are not troubled by [algae](https://www.britannica.com/science/algae) growth.

## Water application

[![irrigation sprinklers](https://cdn.britannica.com/35/157635-050-69B43759/Irrigation-sprinklers.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/35/157635-050-69B43759/Irrigation-sprinklers.jpg)

[irrigation sprinklers](https://cdn.britannica.com/35/157635-050-69B43759/Irrigation-sprinklers.jpg)(more)

After water reaches the farm it may be applied by surface, subsurface, or sprinkler irrigation methods. [Surface irrigation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/surface-irrigation) is normally used only where the land has been graded so that uniform slopes exist. Land grading is not necessary for other methods. Each method includes several variations, only the more common of which are considered here.

Surface irrigation systems are usually classed as either flood or furrow systems. In the flood system, water is applied at the edge of a field and allowed to move over the entire surface to the opposite side of the field. Grain and forage crops are quite often irrigated by flood techniques. The [furrow](https://www.britannica.com/technology/furrow) system is used for row crops such as [corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corn-plant) (maize), [cotton](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cotton-fibre-and-plant), [sugar beets](https://www.britannica.com/plant/sugar-beet), and [potatoes](https://www.britannica.com/plant/potato). Furrows are plowed between crop rows and the water is run in the furrows. In either type of surface irrigation systems, waste-water ditches at the lower edge of the fields permit excess water to be removed for use elsewhere and to prevent waterlogging.

Subirrigation is a less common method. An impermeable layer must be located below, but near, the [root](https://www.britannica.com/science/root-plant) zone of the crop so that water is trapped in the root zone. If this condition exists, water is applied to the soil through tile drains or ditches.

Sprinklers have been used increasingly to irrigate agricultural land. Little or no preparation is needed, application rates can be controlled, and the system may be used for frost protection and the application of chemicals, such as [pesticides](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pesticide), [herbicides](https://www.britannica.com/science/herbicide), and [fertilizers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fertilizer). Sprinklers range from those that apply water in the form of a mist to those that apply an inch or more per hour.

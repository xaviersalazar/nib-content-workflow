**law code**, a more or less systematic and [comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive) written statement of laws. [Law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/law) codes were compiled by the most ancient peoples. The oldest [extant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/extant) evidence for a code is tablets from the ancient archives of the city of Ebla (now at [Tell Mardikh](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ebla), Syria), which date to about 2400 bc. The best known ancient code is the Babylonian [Code of Hammurabi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Code-of-Hammurabi). The Romans began keeping legal records, such as the [Law of the Twelve Tables](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Law-of-the-Twelve-Tables) (451–450 bc), but there was no major codification of [Roman law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-law) until the [Code of Justinian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Code-of-Justinian) (ad 529–565), which was compiled long after the dissolution of the Western Empire. The peoples who overran the Western Empire also made codes of law, such as the [Salic Law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Salic-Law) of the Salian Franks. During the later [Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/topic/medieval-law) in Europe, various collections of maritime customs, drawn up for the use of merchants and lawyers, acquired great authority throughout the continent.

From the 15th through the 18th century, movements in various European countries to organize and compile their numerous laws and customs resulted in local and provincial [compilations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compilations) rather than national ones. The first national codes appeared in the Scandinavian countries in the 17th and 18th centuries. A second generation of codes, exemplified by the [Prussian Civil Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Prussian-Civil-Code) (1794), represented attempts both to bring about legal unity and to provide a synthesis of 18th-century political and philosophical thought. The 19th century brought more widespread movements for national codifications, the first of which was the [Napoleonic Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Napoleonic-Code), which was adopted in France in 1804. Since then, other [civil-law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/civil-law-Romano-Germanic) countries have enacted similar codes, such as the [German Civil Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/German-Civil-Code) (1896), the [Swiss Civil Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Swiss-Civil-Code) (1907), and the [Japanese Civil Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Japanese-Civil-Code) (1896). The Napoleonic Code and the German Civil Code have served as models for the [vast](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/vast) majority of other modern civil codes around the world.

In [common-law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/common-law) countries, such as [Great Britain](https://www.britannica.com/topic/English-law) and the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/topic/American-law), general law codes are the exception rather than the rule, largely because much of the law is based on previous judicial decisions. In the United States these codifications tend to be narrower, covering different types of procedure or penal and probate law. States adopt their own codes, although there have been attempts to establish uniform codes in various areas of law; the most comprehensive of these is the Uniform Commercial Code, which has been adopted by numerous jurisdictions in the country. In Great [Britain](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-Kingdom) some codes have been adopted in narrow areas such as sale and partnership, but there has been considerable [work](https://www.britannica.com/money/work-economics) [done](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/done) in revising and consolidating existing statutes.

- What is common law?







Common law is a body of law based on customs and general principles that has evolved from judicial decisions and case law. It is administered by common-law courts in England since the Middle Ages. The U.S. and Commonwealth member states also use this type of legal system.



English common law originated in the King's Court in the early Middle Ages. Judges and lawyers develop common law through what is now called a theory of adjudication. In this process, judges don't act as lawmakers but rather as discoverers of law. They discern common law from past cases, treatises, and common experience.

- How is common law different from laws written by lawmakers?







Common law is based on custom and general principles embodied in case law, serving as precedent for situations not covered by statutes. It is developed in courts through judicial decisions, which become controlling precedents for future cases. This system, called _stare decisis_, means judges are expected to follow earlier decisions to ensure uniform and predictable justice.



Laws written by lawmakers, or statutes, are rules enacted by a governing authority, especially a legislature. Courts apply and interpret these statutes, often transforming them from generalities to specifics. The goal of statutory interpretation is to discover and give effect to the intention of the legislature. Unlike common law, statutes are formally enacted and provide a written framework for legal conduct.

- How are decisions made in common law systems?







In common-law systems, decisions are made based on statutes and [precedents](https://www.britannica.com/topic/precedent/Introduction#ref1), which are past rulings that guide future cases. The principle of _stare decisis_ dictates that courts should generally adhere to established precedents, ensuring consistency and predictability in justice. Judges interpret laws and apply them to specific situations, and these interpretations become precedents.



However, judges can distinguish cases based on differing factors and may overrule previous decisions if they are deemed erroneous or unsuited for current application. This allows for the evolution of law alongside societal changes, balancing adherence to precedent with the need for fair and just outcomes.

- Why are judges important in common law systems?







In common-law systems, judges play a vital role in interpreting and applying the law. They create precedents, which are legal decisions that guide future cases, ensuring uniform and predictable justice. This principle, known as _stare decisis_, means judges are expected to follow earlier decisions, contributing to a body of "judge-made law".



However, judges do more than just apply existing rules. They interpret statutes, adapt them to specific situations, and fill gaps in the law, effectively shaping legal principles. This lawmaking is crucial because it allows the legal system to evolve and address new issues. While judges are not meant to have unrestricted legislative power, their ability to adapt and modify the law ensures fair resolutions and justice in changing circumstances.

- How does a judge use precedent in common law?







In common law, a judge uses precedent, or _stare decisis_ (Latin for "let the decision stand"), by following earlier court decisions when deciding similar cases. This promotes fairness and predictability in the justice system, as similar situations should have similar outcomes.



However, judges can distinguish cases if the facts differ, and higher courts can overrule precedents if they are deemed erroneous or unsuited for current application. This allows for judicial lawmaking and adaptation to changing circumstances. The accumulation of judicial decisions forms the common law, which governs areas not covered by statutes.

- Which countries mainly use common law, and how does it affect their legal systems?

In these systems, judicial decisions create precedents, which are as authoritative as statutes. Courts interpret and sometimes fill gaps in statutes, developing rules for future cases. This judge-made law is a significant part of the common law.

**common law**, the body of customary [law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/law), based upon judicial decisions and [embodied](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/embodied) in reports of decided cases, that has been administered by the common-law courts of [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) since the [Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages). From it has evolved the type of legal system now found also in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) and in most of the member states of the [Commonwealth](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Commonwealth-association-of-states) (formerly the British Commonwealth of Nations). In this sense common law stands in contrast to the legal system derived from [civil law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/civil-law-Romano-Germanic), now widespread in continental Europe and elsewhere. In another, narrower, sense, common law is contrasted to the rules applied in [English](https://www.britannica.com/topic/English-law) and American courts of [equity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/equity-law) and also to statute law. A standing expository difficulty is that, whereas the United Kingdom is a [unitary state](https://www.britannica.com/topic/unitary-state) in [international law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/international-law), it [comprises](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprises) three major (and other minor) legal systems, those of England and [Wales](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Welsh-law), [Scotland](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Scottish-law), and [Northern Ireland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Northern-Ireland). Historically, the common-law system in England (applied to Wales since 1536) has directly influenced that in Ireland but only partially influenced the distinct legal system in Scotland, which is therefore, except as regards international matters, not covered in this article. Beginning in 1973 the legal systems in the United Kingdom experienced [integration](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integration) into the system of [European Union](https://www.britannica.com/topic/European-Union) (EU) law, which had direct effects upon the domestic law of its [constituent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituent) states—the majority of which had domestic systems that were influenced by the civil-law tradition and that [cultivated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultivated) a more purposive technique of legislative interpretation than was customary in the English common law. However, the United Kingdom exited the EU in 2020. The regime of [human rights](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-rights) represented by the [European Convention on Human Rights](https://www.britannica.com/event/European-Convention-on-Human-Rights-Europe-1950) (1950) has exercised a similar influence in the United Kingdom since the passage by [Parliament](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Parliament) of the [Human Rights Act 1998](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Human-Rights-Act-1998).

## The origin of the common law

The English common law originated in the early Middle Ages in the King’s Court (Curia Regis), a single royal [court](https://www.britannica.com/topic/court-law) set up for most of the country at Westminster, near [London](https://www.britannica.com/place/London). Like many other early legal systems, it did not originally consist of [substantive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/substantive) rights but rather of procedural remedies. The working out of these remedies has, over time, produced the modern system in which rights are seen as primary over procedure. Until the late 19th century, English common law continued to be developed primarily by [judges](https://www.britannica.com/topic/judge-law) rather than legislators.

The common law of England was largely created in the period after the [Norman Conquest](https://www.britannica.com/event/Norman-Conquest) of 1066. The [Anglo-Saxons](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Anglo-Saxon), especially after the accession of [Alfred the Great](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alfred-king-of-Wessex) (871), had developed a body of rules resembling those being used by the [Germanic peoples](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Germanic-peoples) of northern Europe. Local customs governed most matters, while the church played a large part in government. [Crimes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crime-law) were treated as wrongs for which compensation was made to the victim.

The Norman Conquest did not bring an immediate end to [Anglo-Saxon law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Anglo-Saxon-law), but a period of colonial rule by the mainly Norman conquerors produced change. Land was [allocated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/allocated) to feudal vassals of the king, many of whom had joined the conquest with this reward in mind. Serious wrongs were regarded mainly as public crimes rather than as personal matters, and the perpetrators were [punished by death](https://www.britannica.com/topic/capital-punishment) and forfeiture of [property](https://www.britannica.com/money/property-legal-concept). The requirement that, in cases of sudden death, the local [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) should identify the body as English (“presentment of Englishry”)—and, therefore, of little account—or face heavy fines reveals a state of unrest between the Norman conquerors and their English subjects. Government was centralized, a [bureaucracy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bureaucracy) built up, and written records maintained. Controversy exists regarding the extent to which the efficient government of the Anglo-Norman realm was due to the [legacy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legacy) of Anglo-Saxon institutions or to the ruthlessness of the Norman invaders. Elements of the Anglo-Saxon system that survived were the [jury](https://www.britannica.com/topic/jury), [ordeals](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ordeal) ( [trials](https://www.britannica.com/topic/trial-law) by physical test or combat), the practice of [outlawry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/outlawry) (putting a person beyond the protection of the law), and writs (orders requiring a person to appear before a court; _see below_ [The development of a centralized judiciary](https://www.britannica.com/topic/common-law/The-feudal-land-law#ref40226)). Important consolidation occurred during the reign of [Henry II](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-II-king-of-England) (1154–89). Royal officials roamed the country, inquiring about the administration of [justice](https://www.britannica.com/topic/justice-social-concept). [Church and state](https://www.britannica.com/topic/church-and-state) were separate and had their own law and court systems. This led to centuries of rivalry over [jurisdiction](https://www.britannica.com/topic/jurisdiction), especially since appeals from church courts, before the [Reformation](https://www.britannica.com/event/Reformation), could be taken to Rome.

The Normans spoke French and had developed a customary law in Normandy. They had no professional lawyers or judges; instead, literate clergymen acted as administrators. Some of the [clergy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/clergy-Christianity) were familiar with [Roman law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-law) and the [canon law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/canon-law) of the Christian church, which was developed in the universities of the 12th century. [Canon](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Canon) law was applied in the English church courts, but the revived Roman law was less influential in England than elsewhere, despite Norman dominance in government. This was due largely to the early sophistication of the Anglo-Norman system. Norman [custom](https://www.britannica.com/topic/custom-English-law) was not simply transplanted to England; upon its arrival, a new body of rules, based on local conditions, emerged.

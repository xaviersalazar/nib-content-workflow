Agriculture has no single, simple origin. A wide variety of plants and animals have been independently domesticated at different times and in numerous places. The first agriculture appears to have developed at the closing of the last [Pleistocene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pleistocene-Epoch) glacial period, or [Ice Age](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice-age-geology) (about 11,700 years ago). At that time temperatures warmed, glaciers melted, sea levels rose, and ecosystems throughout the world reorganized. The changes were more dramatic in temperate regions than in the tropics.

Although global [climate change](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-change) played a role in the development of agriculture, it does not account for the complex and [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) cultural responses that ensued, the specific timing of the appearance of agricultural [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) in different regions, or the specific regional impact of climate change on local [environments](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environments). By studying populations that did not develop [intensive agriculture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/intensive-agriculture) or certain cultigens, such as wheat and [rice](https://www.britannica.com/plant/rice), archaeologists narrow the search for causes. For instance, [Aboriginal Australians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Australian-Aboriginal) and many of the [Native American](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Native-American) peoples of western [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) developed complex methods to manage diverse sets of plants and animals, often including (but not limited to) [cultivation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cultivation). These practices may be representative of activities common in some parts of the world before 15,000 years ago.

Plant and [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) management was and is a familiar concept within [hunting and gathering cultures](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hunter-gatherer), but it took on new dimensions as [natural selection](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-selection) and mutation produced [phenotypes](https://www.britannica.com/science/phenotype) that were increasingly reliant upon people. Because some [resource management](https://www.britannica.com/money/natural-resource-management) practices, such as intensively tending nondomesticated nut-bearing trees, bridge the boundary between foraging and farming, archaeologists investigating agricultural origins generally frame their work in terms of a [continuum](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/continuum) of subsistence practices.

Notably, agriculture does not appear to have developed in particularly impoverished settings; [domestication](https://www.britannica.com/science/domestication) does not seem to have been a response to [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) scarcity or deprivation. In fact, quite the opposite appears to be the case. It was once thought that human population pressure was a significant factor in the process, but research indicated by the late 20th century that populations rose significantly only after people had established food production. Instead, it is thought that—at least initially—the new animals and plants that were developed through domestication may have helped to maintain ways of life that [emphasized](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/emphasized) hunting and gathering by providing insurance in lean seasons. When considered in terms of food management, dogs may have been initially domesticated as hunting companions, while [meat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/meat) and [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk) could be obtained more reliably from herds of [sheep](https://www.britannica.com/animal/domesticated-sheep), [goats](https://www.britannica.com/animal/goat), [reindeer](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reindeer), or [cattle](https://www.britannica.com/animal/cattle-livestock) than from their wild counterparts or other game animals. Domestication made resource planning a more predictable exercise in regions that combined extreme seasonal variation and rich natural resource abundance.

# Earliest beginnings

[![Barley](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/75931-050-FED41F1F/Barley.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/75931-050-FED41F1F/Barley.jpg)

[Barley](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/75931-050-FED41F1F/Barley.jpg) An early domesticated grain, barley is still eaten today.(more)

The [domestication](https://www.britannica.com/science/domestication) of plants and animals caused changes in their form; the presence or absence of such changes indicates whether a given organism was wild or a domesticate. On the basis of such evidence, one of the oldest transitions from hunting and gathering to agriculture has been identified as dating to between 14,500 and 12,000 bp in [Southwest Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southwest-Asia). It was experienced by groups known as [Epipaleolithic](https://www.britannica.com/event/Mesolithic) peoples, who survived from the end of the [Paleolithic Period](https://www.britannica.com/event/Paleolithic-Period) into early postglacial times and used smaller stone tools (microblades) than their predecessors. The [Natufians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Natufian-culture), an Epipaleolithic [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) located in the [Levant](https://www.britannica.com/place/Levant), possessed stone sickles and intensively collected many plants, such as wild [barley](https://www.britannica.com/plant/barley-cereal) ( _Hordeum spontaneum_). In the eastern [Fertile Crescent](https://www.britannica.com/place/Fertile-Crescent), Epipaleolithic people who had been dependent on hunting [gazelle](https://www.britannica.com/animal/gazelle) s ( _Gazella_ species) and wild goats and sheep began to raise goats and sheep, but not gazelles, as [livestock](https://www.britannica.com/animal/livestock). By 12,000–11,000 bp, and possibly earlier, domesticated forms of some plants had been developed in the region, and by 10,000 bp domesticated animals were appearing. Elsewhere in the Old World the archaeological record for the earliest agriculture is not as well known at this time, but by 8500–8000 bp [millet](https://www.britannica.com/plant/millet-plant) ( _Setaria italica_ and _Panicum miliaceum_) and [rice](https://www.britannica.com/plant/rice) ( _Oryza sativa_) were being domesticated in [East Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/East-Asia).

In the Americas, [squash](https://www.britannica.com/plant/squash) ( _Cucurbita pepo_ and _C. moschata_) existed in domesticated form in southern [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico) and northern [Peru](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru) by about 10,000–9000 bp. By 5000–3000 bp the Native peoples of eastern [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) and what would become the southwestern [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) were turning to agriculture. In sum, [plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant) and [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) domestication, and therefore agriculture, were undertaken in a variety of places, each independent of the others.

( _[Read Britannica’s list of 18 food crops domesticated in the Americas.](https://www.britannica.com/story/18-food-crops-developed-in-the-americas)_)

The [dog](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dog) appears to have been the earliest domesticated animal, as it is found in archaeological sites around the world by the end of the last glacial period. Genetic evidence indicates that a very small number of females—as few as three—were ancestral to 95 percent of all domesticated dogs. The species’ greatest genetic [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity) is in [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China), which indicates that the history of dogs is probably longer there than elsewhere. The earliest dogs found in the Americas are all descendants of the Chinese group, suggesting that they accompanied the first people to reach the New World, an event that occurred at least 13,000 years ago ( _see_ [Native American: Prehistory](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Native-American/Prehistory#ref57816)). People reached Beringia, the temporary [land bridge](https://www.britannica.com/science/land-bridge) between [Siberia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Siberia) and [Alaska](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alaska), as long as 40,000 years ago, suggesting that dogs may have been domesticated even earlier.

[![Best friends](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/188558-050-D21E2D4F/Girl-dog-grass-Pet-canine-child-mammal.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/188558-050-D21E2D4F/Girl-dog-grass-Pet-canine-child-mammal.jpg)

[Best friends](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/188558-050-D21E2D4F/Girl-dog-grass-Pet-canine-child-mammal.jpg) Dogs were the earliest species to be domesticated and likely aided their human companions in hunting and finding food.(more)

Although the exact timing of dog domestication has not been definitively determined, it is clear that the dog was domesticated from the [wolf](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wolf). How and why this happened is not well understood, but the earliest dogs may have assisted humans with hunting and finding [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food). Studies have demonstrated that dogs as young as nine months of age are better at reading human social behavior and communication than wolves or even [chimpanzees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/chimpanzee). This [characteristic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characteristic) appears to be inherited and would have established a very close bond between dogs and humans.

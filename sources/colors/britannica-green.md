**green**, in [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science), [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light) in the [wavelength](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength) range of 495–570 nanometres, which is in the middle of the visible [spectrum](https://www.britannica.com/science/spectrum). In [art](https://www.britannica.com/art/visual-arts), green is a colour on the conventional [colour wheel](https://www.britannica.com/science/color-wheel), located between [yellow](https://www.britannica.com/science/yellow-color) and [blue](https://www.britannica.com/science/blue-color) and opposite [red](https://www.britannica.com/science/red-color), its [complementary colour](https://www.britannica.com/science/complementary-color).

_Green_ is a basic colour term added to languages before or after _yellow_, following [_black_](https://www.britannica.com/science/black-color), [_white_](https://www.britannica.com/science/white-color), and _red_. The word _green_ derives from Proto-Germanic _grōni_ and Old English _grene_. One of the first written records of the term is from the [Caedmon Manuscript](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Caedmon-manuscript) ( _c._ 1000 ce): “Adam stop / On grene græs, gaste geweorthad” (“Adam stepped / On green grass, soul made worthy”).

[RGB color model](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/234589-050-9D460259/color-wheel-additive.jpg) Diagram of the RGB color model, an example of the additive color system, showing how colors mix.(more)

Pigments for green come from [malachite](https://www.britannica.com/science/malachite), [cobalt](https://www.britannica.com/science/cobalt-chemical-element) oxide, [zinc](https://www.britannica.com/science/zinc) oxide, [copper](https://www.britannica.com/science/copper) acetate, and artificial chemical [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds). In the 19th century, green [pigment](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pigment) made with copper arsenite was known for its toxicity, and some supporters of [Napoleon](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Napoleon-I) claimed that the fumes from the green wallpaper at Longwood House on [St. Helena](https://www.britannica.com/place/Saint-Helena-island-South-Atlantic-Ocean) island, his place of exile, contributed to his death.

In addition to the colour wheel, various other colour systems have been used to classify green. Before the invention of colour photography, _[Werner’s Nomenclature of Colour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Werners-Nomenclature-of-Colour)_ (1814) was frequently used by scientists attempting to accurately describe colours observed in nature. In that book the so-called tint “Duck Green” is compared to the “Neck of Mallard,” the “Upper Disk of Yew Leaves,” and “Ceylanite.” In the [Munsell colour system](https://www.britannica.com/science/Munsell-color-system)—adopted in the early 20th century to standardize colour, usually for industry—one of the many variations of green is identified as 2.5G 5/24.

### What is the fashion industry?

The fashion industry is a multibillion-dollar global enterprise devoted to making and selling clothes, including high fashion and mass fashion. It consists of four levels: the production of raw materials, principally [fibers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/fiber-technology) and textiles but also leather and fur; the production of fashion goods by designers, manufacturers, contractors, and others; [retail](https://www.britannica.com/money/retailing) sales; and various forms of advertising and promotion. These levels consist of many separate but interdependent sectors, all of which are devoted to the goal of satisfying consumer demand for apparel under conditions that enable participants in the industry to operate at a profit.

### How did the fashion industry evolve?

Prior to the mid-19th century, most clothing was handmade for individuals, either at home or on order from dressmakers and tailors. By the beginning of the 20th century—with the emergence of new technologies such as the [sewing machine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/sewing-machine), the rise of global [capitalism](https://www.britannica.com/money/capitalism), the development of the factory system of production, and the proliferation of retail outlets such as [department stores](https://www.britannica.com/money/department-store)—clothing had increasingly come to be mass-produced in standard sizes and sold at fixed prices. The Internet has become an important part of the fashion industry. It works as a retail outlet, offering 24-hour shopping and the ability to reach rural customers, and it also provides opportunities for merchandising and marketing, through online magazines, blogs, and social media. The industry developed first in Europe and America and is now highly globalized.

**fashion industry**, multibillion-dollar global enterprise devoted to the business of making and selling clothes. Some observers distinguish between the fashion industry (which makes “high fashion”) and the apparel industry (which makes everyday clothing or “mass fashion”), but by the 1970s the boundaries between the two had blurred. [Fashion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fashion-society) is best defined simply as the style or styles of clothing and accessories worn at any given time by groups of people. There may appear to be differences between the expensive designer fashions shown on the runways of [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris) or [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City) and the mass-produced sportswear and street styles sold in malls and markets around the world. However, the fashion industry encompasses the design, manufacturing, distribution, [marketing](https://www.britannica.com/money/marketing), retailing, [advertising](https://www.britannica.com/money/advertising), and promotion of all types of apparel (men’s, women’s, and children’s) from the most rarefied and expensive haute couture (literally, “high sewing”) and designer fashions to everyday clothing—from couture ball gowns to casual sweatpants. Sometimes the broader term “fashion industries” is used to refer to myriad industries and services that employ millions of people internationally.

The fashion industry is a product of the modern age. Prior to the mid-19th century, most clothing was handmade for individuals, either at home or on order from dressmakers and tailors. By the beginning of the 20th century—with the emergence of new technologies such as the [sewing machine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/sewing-machine), the rise of global [capitalism](https://www.britannica.com/money/capitalism), the development of the factory system of production, and the proliferation of retail outlets such as [department stores](https://www.britannica.com/money/department-store)—clothing had increasingly come to be mass-produced in standard sizes and sold at fixed prices.

Although the fashion industry developed first in [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) and America, today it is an international and highly globalized industry, with clothing often designed in one country, manufactured in another, and sold in a third. For example, an American fashion company might source fabric in [China](https://www.britannica.com/art/china-ceramic-ware) and have the clothes manufactured in [Vietnam](https://www.britannica.com/place/Vietnam), finished in [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy), and shipped to a warehouse in the United States for distribution to retail outlets internationally. The fashion industry has long been one of the largest employers in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), and it remains so in the 21st century. However, employment declined considerably as production increasingly moved overseas, especially to China. Because data on the fashion industry typically are reported for national economies and expressed in terms of the industry’s many separate sectors, aggregate figures for world production of [textiles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/textile) and clothing are difficult to obtain. However, by any measure, the industry unquestionably accounts for a significant share of world economic output.

## Key sectors of the fashion industry

## [Textile](https://www.britannica.com/topic/textile) design and production

[![Textile manufacturing](https://cdn.britannica.com/22/175922-050-D8970FDB/Textile-worker-loom.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/22/175922-050-D8970FDB/Textile-worker-loom.jpg)

[Textile manufacturing](https://cdn.britannica.com/22/175922-050-D8970FDB/Textile-worker-loom.jpg) Textile worker operating a loom.(more)

Most fashions are made from textiles. The partial automation of the spinning and weaving of [wool](https://www.britannica.com/topic/wool), [cotton](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cotton-fibre-and-plant), and other natural fibers was one of the first accomplishments of the [Industrial Revolution](https://www.britannica.com/event/Industrial-Revolution) in the 18th century. In the 21st century those processes are highly automated and carried out by computer-controlled high-speed machinery. A large sector of the textile industry produces [fabrics](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cloth) for use in apparel. Both [natural fibers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/natural-fiber) (such as [wool](https://www.britannica.com/topic/wool), [cotton](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cotton-fibre-and-plant), [silk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/silk), and [linen](https://www.britannica.com/technology/linen)) and [synthetic fibers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/synthetic-fiber) (such as [nylon](https://www.britannica.com/science/nylon), [acrylic](https://www.britannica.com/science/acrylic), and [polyester](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyester)) are used. A growing interest in sustainable fashion (or “eco-fashion”) led to greater use of environmentally friendly fibers, such as [hemp](https://www.britannica.com/plant/hemp). High-tech synthetic fabrics confer such properties as moisture wicking (e.g., Coolmax), stain resistance (e.g., 303 High Tech Fabric Guard), retention or dissipation of body heat, and protection against fire, weapons (e.g., Kevlar), cold (e.g., Thinsulate), [ultraviolet radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultraviolet-radiation) (Solarweave), and other hazards. Fabrics are produced with a wide range of effects through [dyeing](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dye), [weaving](https://www.britannica.com/technology/weaving), [printing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/printing-publishing), and other manufacturing and finishing processes. Together with fashion forecasters, textile manufacturers work well in advance of the apparel production cycle to create fabrics with colors, textures, and other qualities that anticipate consumer demand.

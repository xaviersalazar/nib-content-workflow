**red**, in [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science), the longest [wavelength](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength) of [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light) discernible to the human eye. It falls in the range of 620–750 nanometres in the visible [spectrum](https://www.britannica.com/science/spectrum). In [art](https://www.britannica.com/art/visual-arts), red is a colour on the conventional [colour wheel](https://www.britannica.com/science/color-wheel), located between [violet](https://www.britannica.com/science/violet) and [orange](https://www.britannica.com/science/orange-color) and opposite [green](https://www.britannica.com/science/green-color), its [complementary colour](https://www.britannica.com/science/complementary-color).

_Red_ was the first basic colour term added to languages after [_black_](https://www.britannica.com/science/black-color) and [_white_](https://www.britannica.com/science/white-color). The word _red_ derives from Sanskrit _rudhira_ and Proto-Germanic _rauthaz_. One of the first written records of the term is from an Old English translation (897 ce) of [Pope St. Gregory the Great](https://www.britannica.com/biography/St-Gregory-the-Great)’s _Pastoral Care_: “On thæs sacerdes hrægle sceoldœn hangian bellan & ongemong thæm bellum reade apla” (“On the priest’s robes should hang bells and among the bells red pomegranates”).

Pigments for red come from madder, red lead, red ochre, cinnabar, and artificial chemical compounds. The red pigment used for the figures’ robes in [Titian](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Titian)’s altarpiece _The Assumption_ (1516–18) is vermilion, which is derived from ground cinnabar.

In addition to the colour wheel, various other colour systems have been used to classify red. Before the invention of colour photography, _[Werner’s Nomenclature of Colour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Werners-Nomenclature-of-Colour)_ (1814) was frequently used by scientists attempting to accurately describe colours observed in nature. In that book the so-called tint “Scarlet Red” is compared to the “Scarlet Ibis or Curlew,” the “Large red Oriental Poppy,” and “Light red Cinnaber.” In the [Munsell colour system](https://www.britannica.com/science/Munsell-color-system)—adopted in the early 20th century to standardize colour, usually for industry—one of the many variations of red is identified as 7.5R 4/20.

- What is colour in the context of light?







In the context of optics, color is the aspect of any object that may be described in terms of hue, lightness, and saturation. In physics, color relates to electromagnetic radiation with specific wavelengths that are visible to the human eye. This radiation makes up the visible spectrum, or light.



An object's apparent color is determined by how it interacts with light. The visible spectrum spans from approximately 400 nm at the violet end to 700 nm at the red end. The eye can distinguish millions of colors through two types of light mixture: additive and subtractive. Additive mixture involves combining light beams, while subtractive mixture involves the absorption of parts of the spectrum.

- How does white light split into different colours?







White light, such as sunlight, comprises various wavelengths, each perceived as a different color. When white light passes through a medium like a [prism](https://www.britannica.com/technology/prism-optics/Introduction#ref1) or raindrops, [refraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/refraction/Introduction#ref1) occurs: the change in direction of a wave passing from one medium to another caused by its change in speed.



This [dispersion](https://www.britannica.com/science/dispersion-physics/Introduction#ref1) separates the light into its constituent colors because each wavelength bends at a slightly different angle. Shorter wavelengths (violet/blue) bend more than longer wavelengths (red), creating a spectrum. This is why we see rainbows, where sunlight is spread out into its colors after entering raindrops.

- What is the visible spectrum?







The visible spectrum is the range of [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation) that the human eye can perceive. It is a small portion of the total electromagnetic spectrum, with wavelengths ranging from approximately 400 nm (violet) to 700 nm (red).



Within this range, different wavelengths correspond to different colors. The spectrum obtained by refracting light shows characteristic regions of color, including red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet. Each of these regions represents a range of individual wavelengths. Sunlight is essential for life on Earth, and the transparency of water in the visible light range is vital.

- How do objects get their colour?







Objects get their color through interactions with light. [Color](https://www.britannica.com/science/color/Introduction#ref1) is the aspect of an object described by hue, saturation, and lightness.



An object's color arises from how it interacts with light, a form of electromagnetic radiation with specific wavelengths. When light strikes an object, some wavelengths are absorbed, while others are reflected or transmitted. The colors we see are the wavelengths that are reflected.



For example, a red object absorbs most colors but reflects red wavelengths. Similarly, a blue object reflects blue light while absorbing other colors. Pigments, which are insoluble materials, in the object's surface play a key role in this selective absorption and reflection of light.

- What is the difference between primary colours of light and pigment?







The primary colors of light and pigment differ because light uses additive mixing, while pigment uses subtractive mixing.



### Light



The additive primary colors are red, green, and blue. Combining these colors in varying amounts produces almost any other color. When red, green, and blue are combined in equal amounts, white light is produced.



### Pigment



The subtractive primary colors are cyan, magenta, and yellow. Mixing colorants, like pigments or dyes, results in subtractive color mixing through absorption and selective transmission or reflection of light.

- How do human eyes and the brain perceive different colours?







The human eye distinguishes colors through specialized cells called cones in the [retina](https://www.britannica.com/science/color-vision/Introduction#ref1). These cones contain pigments that absorb different wavelengths of light: red, green, and blue-violet. The brain interprets color based on the varying degrees to which these cones are stimulated.



Visual information undergoes extensive processing in the brain, involving roughly half the cortex. Different parts of the cerebral cortex handle various image features such as color, depth, motion, and object identity. The signals from the eyes travel along the optic nerves to the lateral geniculate nuclei (LGN) in the thalamus and then to the primary visual cortex (V1) in the occipital lobe. Color perception is further processed in area V4 of the visual cortex, where cells respond to color independently of illumination.

In [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science), colour is associated specifically with [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation) of a certain range of [wavelengths](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength) visible to the [human eye](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-eye). [Radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiation#ref28804) of such wavelengths [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) that portion of the [electromagnetic spectrum](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-spectrum) known as the visible spectrum—i.e., [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light).

[Vision](https://www.britannica.com/science/vision-physiology) is obviously involved in the perception of colour. A person can see in dim light, however, without being able to distinguish colours. Only when more light is present do colours appear. Light of some critical intensity, therefore, is also necessary for colour perception. Finally, the manner in which the brain responds to visual stimuli must also be considered. Even under identical conditions, the same object may appear [red](https://www.britannica.com/science/red-color) to one observer and [orange](https://www.britannica.com/science/orange-color) to another. Clearly, the perception of colour depends on vision, light, and individual interpretation, and an understanding of colour involves [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science), [physiology](https://www.britannica.com/science/physiology), and [psychology](https://www.britannica.com/science/psychology).

[Aristotle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle) viewed colour to be the product of a mixture of white and [black](https://www.britannica.com/science/black-color), and this was the prevailing belief until 1666, when [Isaac Newton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isaac-Newton)’s [prism](https://www.britannica.com/technology/prism-optics) experiments provided the scientific basis for the understanding of colour. Newton showed that a prism could break up white light into a range of colours, which he called the [spectrum](https://www.britannica.com/science/spectrum) ( _see_ figure), and that the recombination of these spectral colours re-created the [white light](https://www.britannica.com/science/white-light). Although he recognized that the spectrum was continuous, Newton used the seven colour names red, orange, [yellow](https://www.britannica.com/science/yellow-color), [green](https://www.britannica.com/science/green-color), [blue](https://www.britannica.com/science/blue-color), [indigo](https://www.britannica.com/technology/indigo-dye), and [violet](https://www.britannica.com/science/violet) for segments of the spectrum by [analogy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogy) with the seven notes of the musical scale.

A colour can, however, be precisely specified by its [hue](https://www.britannica.com/science/hue-chromatics), [saturation](https://www.britannica.com/science/saturation-color), and [brightness](https://www.britannica.com/science/brightness-light)—three attributes sufficient to distinguish it from all other possible perceived colours. The [hue](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/hue) is that aspect of colour usually associated with terms such as red, orange, yellow, and so forth. Saturation (also known as [chroma](https://www.britannica.com/science/chromaticity) or tone) refers to relative [purity](https://www.britannica.com/science/purity-color). When a pure, vivid, strong shade of red is mixed with a variable amount of white, weaker or paler reds are produced, each having the same hue but a different saturation. These paler colours are called unsaturated colours. Finally, light of any given combination of hue and saturation can have a variable brightness (also called intensity or value), which depends on the total amount of light energy present.

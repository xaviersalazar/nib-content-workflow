**purple**, a [shade](https://www.britannica.com/science/color) varying between crimson and [violet](https://www.britannica.com/science/violet). Formerly, it was the deep crimson [colour](https://www.britannica.com/science/color) called in Latin _purpura_, from the name of the [shellfish](https://www.britannica.com/animal/shellfish-animal) _Purpura_, which yielded the famous [Tyrian dye](https://www.britannica.com/science/Tyrian-purple). During many ages Tyrian purple was the most celebrated of all [dye](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dye) colours, and it was possibly the first to be permanently fixed on [wool](https://www.britannica.com/topic/wool) or linen. Because the dye was extremely costly, robes coloured with it were worn as a mark of imperial or royal [rank](https://www.britannica.com/topic/social-status), whence the phrase “born in the purple.” In the [Roman Catholic Church](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism), “promotion to the purple” is promotion to the rank of [cardinal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cardinal-Roman-Catholicism).

The ancients derived their purple from the mollusks _Stramonita_ (also called _Purpura_) _haemastoma_ and _[Bolinus](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dye-murex)_ (formerly _Murex_) _brandaris_, the shells of which have been found [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) to ancient dyeworks at [Athens](https://www.britannica.com/place/Athens) and [Pompeii](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pompeii). The colour-producing secretion is contained in a small cyst adjacent to the head of the animal, and this puslike matter, when spread on textile material in the presence of [sunlight](https://www.britannica.com/science/sunlight-solar-radiation), develops a purple-red colour. In 1909 Paul Friedländer showed that the principal component of the dye developed from the mollusks is 6,6’-dibromoindigo.

**violet**, in [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science), [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light) in the [wavelength](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength) range of 380–450 nanometres in the visible [spectrum](https://www.britannica.com/science/spectrum). The shortest wavelength of violet is the shortest of all wavelengths of light discernible to the [human eye](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-eye). In [art](https://www.britannica.com/art/visual-arts), violet is a colour on the conventional [colour wheel](https://www.britannica.com/science/color-wheel), located between [red](https://www.britannica.com/science/red-color) and [blue](https://www.britannica.com/science/blue-color) and opposite [yellow](https://www.britannica.com/science/yellow-color), its [complementary colour](https://www.britannica.com/science/complementary-color). Pigments for violet come from [berries](https://www.britannica.com/science/berry-plant-reproductive-body), [cobalt](https://www.britannica.com/science/cobalt-chemical-element) [phosphate](https://www.britannica.com/science/phosphate) or cobalt [arsenate](https://www.britannica.com/science/arsenate-mineral), carminic acid, kermesic acid, [manganese](https://www.britannica.com/science/manganese), and artificial [chemical compounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound).

Violet is a basic colour term added late to languages. The word _violet_ derives from Old French _violet_ or _violete_. One of the first written records of the term in English is from _The Buke of John Maundeuill_ (mid-14th century): “Men fynd dyamaundz of violet colour” (“Men find diamonds of violet colour”).

In addition to the colour wheel, various other colour systems have been used to classify violet. Before the invention of colour photography, _[Werner’s Nomenclature of Colour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Werners-Nomenclature-of-Colour)_ (1814) was frequently used by scientists attempting to accurately describe colours observed in nature. In that book the so-called tint “Violet Purple” is compared to the “Purple Aster” and the “Amethyst.” In the [Munsell colour system](https://www.britannica.com/science/Munsell-color-system)—adopted in the early 20th century to standardize colour, usually for industry—one of the many variations of violet is identified as 10PB 7/12.

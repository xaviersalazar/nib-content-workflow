**indigo**, an important and valuable vat [dyestuff](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dye), obtained until about 1900 entirely from plants of the genera _Indigofera_ and _Isatis._ Indigo was known to the ancients of Asia, Egypt, Greece, Rome, Britain, and [Peru](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru). It is used in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) mainly for dyeing cotton for work clothes; for a long time it was used to produce heavy (navy blue) shades on wool.

The naturally occurring [precursor](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/precursor) of indigo is indican, a colourless, water-soluble substance that is easily hydrolyzed to [glucose](https://www.britannica.com/science/glucose) and indoxyl; the latter is converted to indigo by mild oxidation, such as exposure to air.

The chemical structure of indigo was announced in 1883 by Adolf von Baeyer; a commercially [feasible](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/feasible) manufacturing process was in use by the late 1890s. The method, still in use throughout the world, consists of a synthesis of indoxyl by fusion of sodium phenylglycinate in a mixture of [caustic soda](https://www.britannica.com/science/sodium-hydroxide) and sodamide.

Indigo can be converted to numerous simpler [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds), but the only [chemical reaction](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-reaction) of practical importance is its reduction to the soluble yellow leucoindigo, in which form it is applied to textile fibres and reoxidized to indigo.

[Tyrian purple](https://www.britannica.com/science/Tyrian-purple), a dyestuff of great importance in antiquity, was obtained from a secretion of a [sea snail](https://www.britannica.com/animal/snailfish) ( _Murex brandaris_) common in the Mediterranean. Its structure is very similar to that of indigo. It has never been produced synthetically on a commercial basis.

**indigo**, (genus _Indigofera_), large genus of more than 750 species of shrubs, trees, and herbs in the pea family ( [Fabaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Fabaceae)). Some species, particularly true indigo ( _Indigofera tinctoria_) and Natal indigo ( _I. arrecta_), were once an important source of [indigo dye](https://www.britannica.com/technology/indigo-dye). The cultivation of indigo plants and the extraction of the [dyestuff](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dye) were an important industry in India up to the beginning of the 20th century. [Synthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Synthetic) indigo, developed about that time, gradually replaced natural indigo as a dyestuff. The plants are native to tropical and subtropical regions worldwide.

Key People: [Elizabeth Pinckney](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Elizabeth-Pinckney)_(Show more)_

Related Topics: [indigo](https://www.britannica.com/technology/indigo-dye)[true indigo](https://www.britannica.com/plant/true-indigo)[Natal indigo](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Natal-indigo)[Faboideae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Papilionoideae)_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/indigo-plant-genus)

Indigo species are highly variable in appearance but are generally silky or hairy with [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) [leaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy). The rose, purple, or white [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) are borne in showy spikes or clusters, and the fruit is a [pod](https://www.britannica.com/science/legume), usually with a thin partition between the seeds.

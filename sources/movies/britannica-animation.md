### What is animation?

Animation is the art of making inanimate objects appear to move. History’s first recorded animator is, arguably, [Pygmalion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pygmalion) of Greek and Roman mythology. The theory of the animated cartoon preceded the invention of the cinema by half a century.

### What place does Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs hold in the history of animation?

[_Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Snow-White-and-the-Seven-Dwarfs-film-1937) was the first film to use up-to-the-minute animation techniques and receive a Hollywood-style release. When it was released in 1937, the film was an immediate box-office sensation and was honoured with a special Academy Award.

### Who made the first film-based animation?

The first film-based animation was made by [J. Stuart Blackton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/J-Stuart-Blackton), whose _Humorous Phases of Funny Faces_ in 1906 launched a successful series of animated films for New York’s pioneering Vitagraph Company.

### Who invented rotoscope animation?

The [Fleischer brothers](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Fleischer-brothers) invented the rotoscope process, in which a strip of live-action footage can be traced and redrawn as a cartoon. They exploited this technique in their pioneering series _Out of the Inkwell_ (1919–29).

**animation**, the art of making inanimate objects appear to move. Animation is an artistic impulse that long predates the movies. History’s first recorded animator is [Pygmalion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pygmalion) of Greek and [Roman mythology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-religion), a sculptor who created a figure of a woman so perfect that he fell in love with her and begged Venus to bring her to life. Some of the same sense of magic, mystery, and transgression still [adheres](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/adheres) to contemporary [film](https://www.britannica.com/art/film) animation, which has made it a primary vehicle for exploring the overwhelming, often bewildering emotions of childhood—feelings once dealt with by [folktales](https://www.britannica.com/art/folk-literature/Folktale#ref237459).

## Early history

![Zoetrope](https://cdn.britannica.com/02/2802-004-B0BC063D/zoetrope-Illustration-image-illusion-rotating-drum-series.jpg)

[Zoetrope](https://cdn.britannica.com/02/2802-004-B0BC063D/zoetrope-Illustration-image-illusion-rotating-drum-series.jpg) Illustration of a zoetrope, which creates the illusion of a moving image within a rotating drum by way of a series of still images.(more)

The theory of the animated cartoon preceded the invention of the cinema by half a century. Early experimenters, working to create conversation pieces for Victorian parlors or new sensations for the touring magic-lantern shows, which were a popular form of entertainment, discovered the principle of [persistence of vision](https://www.britannica.com/science/persistence-of-vision). If drawings of the stages of an action were shown in fast succession, the [human eye](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-eye) would perceive them as a continuous [movement](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics). One of the first commercially successful devices, invented by the Belgian [Joseph Plateau](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Antoine-Ferdinand-Plateau) in 1832, was the [phenakistoscope](https://www.britannica.com/science/phenakistoscope), a spinning cardboard disk that created the [illusion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/illusion) of movement when viewed in a mirror. In 1834 [William George Horner](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-George-Horner) invented the [zoetrope](https://www.britannica.com/technology/zoetrope), a rotating drum lined by a band of pictures that could be changed. The Frenchman [Émile Reynaud](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Emile-Reynaud) in 1876 adapted the principle into a form that could be projected before a theatrical audience. Reynaud became not only animation’s first [entrepreneur](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/entrepreneur) but, with his gorgeously hand-painted ribbons of celluloid conveyed by a system of mirrors to a theater screen, the first artist to give personality and warmth to his animated characters.

With the invention of sprocket-driven film stock, animation was poised for a great leap forward. Although “firsts” of any kind are never easy to establish, the first film-based animator appears to be [J. Stuart Blackton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/J-Stuart-Blackton), whose _[Humorous Phases of Funny Faces](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Humorous-Phases-of-Funny-Faces)_ in 1906 launched a successful series of animated films for [New York’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state) pioneering Vitagraph Company. Later that year, Blackton also experimented with the [stop-motion technique](https://www.britannica.com/art/stop-motion-animation)—in which objects are photographed, then repositioned and photographed again—for his short film _Haunted Hotel_.

In France, Émile Cohl was developing a form of animation similar to Blackton’s, though Cohl used relatively crude stick figures rather than Blackton’s ambitious newspaper-style cartoons. Coinciding with the rise in popularity of the Sunday comic sections of the new tabloid newspapers, the [nascent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nascent) animation industry recruited the talents of many of the best-known artists, including [Rube Goldberg](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rube-Goldberg), [Bud Fisher](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bud-Fisher) (creator of _Mutt and Jeff_) and [George Herriman](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Herriman) (creator of _Krazy Kat_), but most soon tired of the fatiguing animation process and left the actual production work to others.

The one great exception among these early illustrators-turned-animators was [Winsor McCay](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Winsor-McCay), whose elegant, [surreal](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/surreal) _[Little Nemo in Slumberland](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Little-Nemo-in-Slumberland)_ and _Dream of the Rarebit Fiend_ remain [pinnacles](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/pinnacles) of comic-strip art. McCay created a hand-colored short film of Little Nemo for use during his [vaudeville](https://www.britannica.com/art/vaudeville) act in 1911, but it was _[Gertie the Dinosaur](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gertie-the-Dinosaur)_, created for McCay’s 1914 tour, that transformed the art. McCay’s superb draftsmanship, fluid sense of movement, and great feeling for character gave viewers an animated creature who seemed to have a personality, a presence, and a life of her own. The first cartoon star had been born.

McCay made several other extraordinary films, including a re-creation of _The Sinking of the Lusitania_ (1918), but it was left to Pat Sullivan to extend McCay’s discoveries. An Australian-born cartoonist who opened a studio in [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City), Sullivan recognized the great talent of a young animator named [Otto Messmer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Otto-Messmer), one of whose casually invented characters—a [wily](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/wily) black cat named [Felix](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Felix-the-Cat)—was made into the star of a series of immensely popular one-reelers. Designed by Messmer for maximum flexibility and facial expressiveness, the round-headed, big-eyed Felix quickly became the [standard model](https://www.britannica.com/science/standard-model) for cartoon characters: a rubber ball on legs who required a minimum of effort to draw and could be kept in constant motion.

Far more revolutionary was Disney’s decision to create a cartoon with the novelty of synchronized sound. _[Steamboat Willie](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Steamboat-Willie)_ (1928), Mickey’s third film, took the country by storm. A missing element—sound—had been added to animation, making the [illusion](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/illusion) of life that much more complete, that much more magical. Later, Disney would add carefully synchronized music ( _[The Skeleton Dance](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Skeleton-Dance)_, 1929), three-strip [Technicolor](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Technicolor) ( _[Flowers and Trees](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Flowers-and-Trees)_, 1932), and the illusion of depth with his multiplane camera ( _The Old Mill_, 1937). With each step, Disney seemed to come closer to a perfect naturalism, a painterly realism that suggested academic paintings of the 19th century. Disney’s resident technical wizard was [Ub Iwerks](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ub-Iwerks), a childhood friend who followed Disney to [Hollywood](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hollywood-California) and was instrumental in the creation of the multiplane camera and the synchronization techniques that made the Mickey Mouse cartoons and the _Silly Symphonies_ series seem so [robust](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/robust) and fully dimensional.

For Disney, the final step was, of course, _[Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Snow-White-and-the-Seven-Dwarfs-film-1937)_ (1937). Although not the first animated feature, it was the first to use up-to-the-minute techniques and the first to receive a wide, Hollywood-style release. Instead of amusing his audience with talking mice and singing cows, Disney was determined to give them as profound a dramatic experience as the medium would allow; he reached into his own troubled childhood to interpret this rich fable of parental abandonment, [sibling rivalry](https://www.britannica.com/science/sibling-rivalry), and the onrush of adult passion.

# The Fleischer brothers

[Max and Dave Fleischer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Fleischer-brothers) had become successful [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state) animators while Disney was still living in Kansas City. The Fleischers invented the [rotoscoping](https://www.britannica.com/technology/rotoscoping) process, still in use today, in which a strip of live-action footage can be traced and redrawn as a cartoon. The Fleischers exploited this technique in their pioneering series _[Out of the Inkwell](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Out-of-the-Inkwell)_ (1919–29). It was this series, with its lively interaction between human and drawn figures, that Disney struggled to imitate with his early Alice cartoons.

But if Disney was [Mother Goose](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mother-Goose-fictional-character) and [Norman Rockwell](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Norman-Rockwell), the Fleischers (Max produced, Dave directed) were stride piano and red whiskey. Their extremely urban, overcrowded, sexually suggestive, and frequently nightmarish work—featuring the curvaceous torch singer [Betty Boop](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Betty-Boop) and her two oddly infantile colleagues, Bimbo the Dog and Koko the Clown—charts a twisty route through the American [subconscious](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/subconscious) of the 1920s and ’30s, before collapsing into Disneyesque cuteness with the features _Gulliver’s Travels_ (1939) and _Mr. Bug Goes to Town_ (1941; also released as _Hoppity Goes to Town_). The studio’s mainstay remained the relatively impersonal [Popeye](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Popeye) series, based on the [comic strip](https://www.britannica.com/art/comic-strip) created by [Elzie Segar](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Elzie-Crisler-Segar). The spinach-loving sailor was introduced as a supporting player in the Betty Boop cartoon _Popeye the Sailor_ (1933) and quickly ascended to stardom, surviving through 105 episodes until the 1942 short _Baby Wants a Bottleship_, when the Fleischer studio collapsed and rights to the character passed to Famous Studios.

Less edgy than the Fleischers but every bit as anarchic were the animations produced by the [Warner Bros.](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Warner-Brothers) cartoon studio, known to its residents as “Termite Terrace.” The studio was founded by three Disney veterans, Rudolph Ising, Hugh Harmon, and [Friz Freleng](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Friz-Freleng), but didn’t discover its identity until [Tex Avery](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Tex-Avery), fleeing the [Walter Lantz](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Walter-Lantz) studio at Universal, joined the team as a director. Avery was young and irreverent, and he quickly recognized the talent of staff artists such as [Chuck Jones](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Chuck-Jones), [Bob Clampett](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Clampett), and Bob Cannon. Together they brought a new kind of speed and snappiness to the Warners product, beginning with _Gold Diggers of ’49_ (1936). With the addition of director [Frank Tashlin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Frank-Tashlin), musical director Carl W. Stalling, and voice interpreter [Mel Blanc](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mel-Blanc), the team was in place to create a new kind of cartoon character: [cynical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cynical), wisecracking, and often violent, who, refined through a series of cartoons, finally emerged as [Bugs Bunny](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bugs-Bunny) in Tex Avery’s _A Wild Hare_ (1940). Other characters, some invented and some reinterpreted, arrived, including [Daffy Duck](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Daffy-Duck), Porky Pig, Tweety and Sylvester, Pepe LePew, Foghorn Leghorn, [Road Runner](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Road-Runner), and Wile E. Coyote. Avery left Warner Brothers and in 1942 joined [Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer](https://www.britannica.com/money/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer-Inc)’s [moribund](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/moribund) animation unit, where, if anything, his work became even wilder in films such as _Red Hot Riding Hood_ (1943) and _Bad Luck Blackie_ (1949).

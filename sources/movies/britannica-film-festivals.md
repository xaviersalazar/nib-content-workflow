**film festival**, gathering, usually annual, for the purpose of evaluating new or outstanding motion pictures. [Sponsored](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Sponsored) by national or local governments, industry, service organizations, experimental film groups, or individual promoters, the festivals provide an opportunity for filmmakers, distributors, critics, and other interested persons to attend film showings and meet to discuss current artistic developments in film. At the festivals distributors can purchase films that they think can be marketed successfully in their own countries.

The first festival was held in Venice in 1932. Since [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II), film festivals have contributed significantly to the development of the motion-picture industry in many countries. The popularity of Italian films at the Cannes and [Venice film festivals](https://www.britannica.com/art/Venice-Film-Festival) played an important part in the rebirth of the Italian industry and the spread of the postwar Neorealist movement. In 1951 [Kurosawa Akira’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kurosawa-Akira) _[Rashomon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rashomon-film-by-Kurosawa)_ won the Golden Lion at Venice, focusing attention on Japanese films. That same year the first American Art Film Festival at Woodstock, [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state), [stimulated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/stimulated) the art-film movement in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States).

Probably the best-known and most noteworthy of the hundreds of film festivals is held each spring in [Cannes](https://www.britannica.com/art/Cannes-film-festival), France. Since 1947, people interested in films have gathered in that small resort town to attend official and unofficial showings of films. Other important festivals are held in Berlin, [Karlovy Vary](https://www.britannica.com/place/Karlovy-Vary) (Czech Republic), Toronto, Ouagadougou (Burkina Faso), [Park City](https://www.britannica.com/place/Park-City) (Utah, U.S.), [Hong Kong](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hong-Kong), [Belo Horizonte](https://www.britannica.com/place/Belo-Horizonte) (Brazil) and Venice. Short subjects and [documentaries](https://www.britannica.com/art/documentary-film) receive special attention at gatherings in Edinburgh, Mannheim and Oberhausen (both in Germany), and Tours (France). Some festivals feature films of one country, and since the late 1960s there have been special festivals for student filmmakers. Others are highly specialized, such as those that feature only underwater photography or those that deal with specific subjects, such as [mountain climbing](https://www.britannica.com/sports/mountaineering).

### Has a woman ever won an Oscar for best director?

Yes, three women have won the [Oscar for best director](https://www.britannica.com/art/Academy-Award-for-best-director). [Kathryn Bigelow](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kathryn-Bigelow) won the Academy Award for best director in 2010 for her movie _The Hurt Locker_ (2008), making her the first woman director is Oscars history to win the award. Thereafter, [Chloé Zhao](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Chloe-Zhao) won the award for _Nomadland_ (2021) and [Jane Campion](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jane-Campion)  for _The Power of the Dog_ (2022).

Several women have been nominated for the category, including [Sofia Coppola](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sofia-Coppola) ( _Lost in Translation_ \[2003\]), [Greta Gerwig](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Greta-Gerwig) ( _Lady Bird_ \[2017\]), and Justine Triet ( _Anatomy of a Fall_ \[2023\]).

### How has the best director category changed over time, such as recognizing new types of films or directors?

The [Academy Award for best director](https://www.britannica.com/art/Academy-Award-for-best-director) has evolved since its inception in 1929, when it was split into drama and comedy categories. These categories were later combined, and eligibility was set to films released the previous year.

Significant milestones include [Kathryn Bigelow](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kathryn-Bigelow) becoming the first woman to win in 2010, [Ang Lee](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ang-Lee) as the first director of color to win in 2006, and [Chloé Zhao](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Chloe-Zhao) as the first woman of color to win in 2021. These wins reflect a growing recognition of diverse filmmakers and stories, contrasting with earlier eras when the industry was dominated by white men.

### Has any Indian director won the Oscar?

The only Indian director to win an [Oscar](https://www.britannica.com/art/Academy-Award) is [Satyajit Ray](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Satyajit-Ray). He was presented an honorary Academy Award for his lifetime contribution to cinema in 1992. Ray, 70 years old at the time, was unable to travel to [Los Angeles](https://www.britannica.com/place/Los-Angeles-California) to accept the award in person because of his declining health. Instead, a video recording of the legendary Indian filmmaker receiving the award in his hospital bed was played at the Oscars ceremony.

### Who won the Oscar for best directing in 2026?

American filmmaker [Paul Thomas Anderson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Paul-Thomas-Anderson) won the 2026 [Academy Award](https://www.britannica.com/art/Academy-Award) for best director for _[One Battle After Another](https://www.britannica.com/topic/One-Battle-After-Another)_ (2025).

**Academy Award for best director**, award presented annually by the [Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Academy-of-Motion-Picture-Arts-and-Sciences), located in Beverly Hills, California. It honors outstanding achievement by a director in a [movie](https://www.britannica.com/art/history-of-film) from a given year, as determined by the academy’s voting members. At the first [Academy Awards](https://www.britannica.com/art/Academy-Award) ceremony in 1929, the award recognized the work in films from 1927–28, and it was offered in two categories: drama and comedy. However, the two Oscars were subsequently combined into one award that covered all genres. In addition, beginning with the seventh ceremony (1935), only work in movies released the previous year was eligible for consideration. The winning directors are given a gold-plated statuette known as an Oscar.

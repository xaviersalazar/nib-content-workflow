### Where was the hot-air balloon first invented?

The hot-air balloon was first invented in [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France). The [Montgolfier brothers](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Montgolfier-brothers), Joseph-Michel and Jacques-Étienne, are credited with inventing the hot-air balloon in the 18th century after supposedly observing Joseph’s wife’s skirts billowing from the heat of a charcoal burner. The brothers’ first public demonstration of their invention occurred on June 4, 1783, in the small town of Annonay.

### What principle allows hot-air balloons to ascend and descend?

Hot-air balloons rise because hot air is lighter than cold air, following [Archimedes’ principle](https://www.britannica.com/science/Archimedes-principle), which states that an object will rise if its weight is less than that of the displaced [fluid](https://www.britannica.com/) ( [liquid](https://www.britannica.com/science/liquid-state-of-matter) or [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter)). Modern hot-air balloons use a [propane](https://www.britannica.com/science/propane) burner to heat the air inside the balloon to ascend. To descend, the burner is turned off, allowing the air to cool, and a vent is opened to let in cooler air, increasing the air density inside the balloon.

### What are the differences between hot-air balloons and gas balloons?

Hot-air balloons use heated air from a [propane](https://www.britannica.com/science/propane) burner to rise, while [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter) balloons are filled before flight with a gas such as [helium](https://www.britannica.com/science/helium-chemical-element) or [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen). Gas balloons adjust altitude by releasing the gas or by dropping sandbags, whereas hot-air balloons use temperature control to guide a balloon’s altitude.

**hot-air balloon**, large [balloon](https://www.britannica.com/technology/balloon) filled with heated air that floats in the sky with a basket underneath for people to ride in. For a full treatment of all types of manned and unmanned balloon craft, _see also_ [balloon flight](https://www.britannica.com/technology/balloon-flight).

## History

Balloons were used in the first successful human attempts at flying. Experimentation with balloonlike craft may have begun as early as 1709 with the work of Bartolomeu Lourenço de Gusmão. The Portugal-born priest was reportedly inspired upon seeing a soap bubble float above a candle, coming to the conclusion that objects lighter than air could technically fly. He presented a small “flying machine” (a miniature hot-air balloon, in essence) to the Royal House in August 1709, which was received with enthusiasm by King [John V](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-V-king-of-Portugal). The full-size balloon, however, would be invented in France in the late 18th century.

According to one, possibly apocryphal, story, the brothers took inspiration from watching Joseph’s wife’s skirts as they billowed from the heat of a charcoal burner being used to dry laundry.

The Italian-born English scientist Tiberius Cavallo had recently demonstrated that [Archimedes’ principle](https://www.britannica.com/science/Archimedes-principle) is applicable to airborne objects by successfully floating [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen)-filled soap bubbles. When he attempted to duplicate this with animal [bladders](https://www.britannica.com/science/bladder), though, they proved too heavy to ascend. Fortunately, the provincial Montgolfiers were not constrained by laboratory techniques and created their hot-air balloon on a cruder and much larger scale. In 1782 they discovered that heated air, when collected inside a large lightweight paper or fabric bag, caused the bag to rise. On June 4, 1783, the brothers made a public demonstration in Annonay with a 10.5-meter- (35-foot-) diameter unmanned cloth-and-paper balloon, using the heat from a straw fire. The balloon rose into the air about 1,000 meters (3,000 feet), remained there some 10 minutes, and then settled to the ground more than 2.4 km (1.5 miles) from where it rose.

Galvanized by their success, the Montgolfiers traveled to [Versailles](https://www.britannica.com/place/Versailles-France), where they repeated the experiment with a larger balloon. On September 19, 1783, the brothers sent a sheep, a rooster, and a duck aloft as passengers in the same balloon, which floated for about 8 minutes before landing safely about 3.2 km (2 miles) from the launch site. On November 21, 1783, the first crewed untethered flight took place in a Montgolfier balloon with Jean-François Pilâtre de Rozier and François Laurent, marquis d’Arlandes, as passengers. The balloon sailed over [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris) for 9 km (5.5 miles) in about 25 minutes.

## The rip panel and drag rope

Important later additions were the rip panel, first used on April 27, 1839, by the American aeronaut John Wise, and the drag rope, invented about 1830 by the English aeronaut [Charles Green](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Green-British-balloonist). A rip panel is an elongated section of the balloon that is lightly fixed in place and can be quickly ripped or pulled open at the moment of landing. It adds greatly to the safety of ballooning by making quick deflation possible. The drag rope, or compensator, is a long, heavy rope that trails the balloon in order to slow down the balloon’s vertical and horizontal speed in landing operations before the basket touches down.

## Elements of a hot-air balloon

![Hot-air balloon](https://cdn.britannica.com/30/114530-050-1D3E5BB7/components-balloon.jpg)

[Hot-air balloon](https://cdn.britannica.com/30/114530-050-1D3E5BB7/components-balloon.jpg) Basic components of a hot-air balloon.(more)

Early hot-air balloons burned straw and alcohol spirits for fuel, though by 1900 these fuels had been replaced with [petroleum](https://www.britannica.com/science/petroleum). Compressed liquefied [propane](https://www.britannica.com/science/propane) is used almost exclusively today. Hot-air balloon burners use vaporizing coils to preheat the fuel for efficient combustion. Most of these coils are made of [stainless steel](https://www.britannica.com/technology/stainless-steel), but [copper](https://www.britannica.com/science/copper) coils also work adequately. The burners are mounted, often on gimbals, on the suspension concentration ring between the basket and the mouth of the balloon. Most systems have redundant burners to compensate for problems with defective controls or contaminated fuel. A particularly notorious early problem was leaking seals on control valves.

A variety of materials has been used for the actual balloon, or envelope. [Cotton](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cotton-fibre-and-plant), [nylon](https://www.britannica.com/science/nylon), and [polyester](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyester) are common for the envelopes of hot-air balloons. Cotton, having a poor weight-to-strength ratio, is only favored for carnival “smoke” balloons. In addition to the choice of envelopes, an even greater variety of systems for carrying the load or passengers has been used. For sport ballooning, the traditional wicker basket, albeit with a stainless steel frame, is popular. Criteria for evaluation of a basket design should include toughness, energy absorption, and electrical resistance, but style and marketability are more often the governing factors.

## Mechanics

[![Hot-air balloon](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/236664-050-6B7AFB5E/Close-up-image-of-flames-from-a-hot-air-balloon-burner.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/236664-050-6B7AFB5E/Close-up-image-of-flames-from-a-hot-air-balloon-burner.jpg)

[Hot-air balloon](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/236664-050-6B7AFB5E/Close-up-image-of-flames-from-a-hot-air-balloon-burner.jpg) A hot-air balloon is prepared by heating the air inside the balloon.(more)

As the Montgolfier brothers had referred to earlier, Archimedes’ principle states that if the weight of an object is less than that of the displaced fluid ( [liquid](https://www.britannica.com/science/liquid-state-of-matter) or, in this case, [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter)), the object rises. This net result, [buoyancy](https://www.britannica.com/science/buoyancy), is the main principle by which hot-air balloons operate.

Modern hot-air balloons operate on the same principles as the hot-air balloons of the 18th and 19th centuries. In essence, because hot air is lighter than cold air, a pilot can adjust a [propane](https://www.britannica.com/science/propane) burner to either increase or decrease the heat in the balloon’s envelope, allowing it to ascend or descend.

Heating the air inside the balloon creates hotter air that is less [dense](https://www.britannica.com/science/density) than the surrounding air, pushing the hot-air balloon upward. To come back down, the gas heaters are turned off and the air inside the balloon starts to cool. A vent at the top of the balloon is also opened to allow more surrounding cool air to move into the balloon as the hot air cools, increasing the density of the air inside the balloon as the balloon slowly descends.

## Doing the math

A balloon can carry the difference between its weight and the weight of the air that it displaces. Consider this example: 28 cubic meters (1,000 cubic feet) of hot air, at normal hot-air balloon operating temperatures, weighs about 22.5 kg (50 pounds). Thus, the lifting force of the hot air at low altitudes can be obtained by subtracting its weight from the typical weight of the same volume of air (about 34 kg, or 75 pounds, in this example).

Because the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere) is compressed by its own weight, it is less dense at higher altitudes. At 3,600 meters (about 12,000 feet) the atmosphere is approximately two-thirds as dense and so will provide two-thirds the buoyancy. This effect continues progressively, so that at 15,000 meters (50,000 feet) it is only one-tenth as dense, at 30,000 meters (100,000 feet) one-hundredth, and at 45,000 meters (150,000 feet) one-thousandth. In order to carry the same load at an altitude of 50 km (30 miles) as at sea level, a balloon would have to be 1,000 times as big and yet weigh the same (that is, keeping everything constant except the balloon’s volume).

## Controlling buoyancy and altitude

The buoyancy of a hot-air balloon is controlled by heating the air in the balloon or by changing the amount of ballast (extra weight). Tiny changes in one of these components can force dramatic changes in a balloon’s flight. Just a one- or two-degree change in temperature in a hot-air balloon or a few grams of ballast dropped will make a balloon ascend or descend accordingly. On the other hand, in violent maneuvers (such as during a storm), whole bags of sand or great blasts of heat from a balloon’s burner may be required for a proper correction. To help in rapid atmospheric cooling, modern hot-air balloons also have very large hot-air release vents in the form of a [parachute](https://www.britannica.com/technology/parachute) that can seal and unseal an opening in the top of the balloon.

Changing the altitude of a balloon will permit it to follow different air currents. A 20- or 30-degree difference in wind direction usually occurs in the first few thousand meters of altitude, but a full circle of wind directions (“box winds”) can also occur. [Albuquerque](https://www.britannica.com/place/Albuquerque), New Mexico, is famous for its box winds, which can be used to climb and descend back to the original launch site. If there is only a simple and stable wind pattern, no additional control is possible. With superior weather monitoring, use of the Global Positioning System ( [GPS](https://www.britannica.com/technology/GPS)), and radio or satellite communication, remarkable flight control is possible.

## Hot-air balloons versus gas balloons

Although they are often treated the same way in popular culture, hot-air balloons differ significantly from gas balloons in how they operate. Gas balloons use either [helium](https://www.britannica.com/science/helium-chemical-element) or [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) gas to lift the balloon. The gases do not require heat; rather, they are stored in the balloon from the start of a balloon flight. The amount of gas (and thus, the level at which the balloon floats) can be adjusted by releasing gas (to lower the balloon) or by dropping sandbags (to lift the balloon higher).

## Recreation and sport

In addition to quiet morning or afternoon flights drifting cross-country to enjoy the view, many balloonists enjoy competitive sporting events and attempting to set new records. A balloonist may fly alone in the basket or carry several passengers. Often, many balloons meet to launch together without any competitive goals. Individual flights generally last from one to three hours and may go several kilometers, though they often land very close to the takeoff point.

Balloon rallies may consist of just a few balloons for a one-day outing or up to several hundred balloons for a weeklong festival. Competitive events include distance within a time limit, spot landing, and “hare and hound” races. Hare and hound races are easy to organize and judge since they only require one (hare) balloon to launch first and fly a reasonable distance. The competitors attempt to land as close as possible to the hare’s landing position. In crowded conditions, markers are often dropped to simulate the landings, and the balloons fly on to more open locations.

Commercial ride operators are in business almost everywhere in the world. Some ride balloons carry 10 to 20 passengers at a time in gigantic partitioned baskets. In California and France, wine-country flights are popular tourist attractions. African safari flights, at low altitude over vast game preserves, are perhaps the pinnacle of ride ballooning.

## In media and popular culture

## Media

Hot-air balloons have been a fixture in popular culture ever since their invention. [Jules Verne](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jules-Verne)’s classic novel _[Around the World in Eighty Days](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Around-the-World-in-Eighty-Days-by-Verne)_ (published serially in 1872) does not actually include a hot-air balloon, but film adaptations released in 1956 and 2004 feature hot-air balloon as a method by which the protagonist travels the world.

In the film _[The Wizard of Oz](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Wizard-of-Oz)_ (1939), it is revealed that the Wizard had arrived in Oz by way of a hot-air balloon, which he then leaves in. After Dorothy exits the balloon’s basket to retrieve her dog, the balloon takes off before she can climb back in, unfortunately leaving her behind. Many of the citizens of Oz had believed in the Wizard’s powers because of his unconventional arrival in the giant balloon.

## Festivals

[![Balloons at the 2010 Albuquerque International Balloon Fiesta](https://cdn.britannica.com/74/163974-138-6DEF1D90/Balloons-Albuquerque-International-Balloon-Fiesta-New-Mexico-2010.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/Balloons-Albuquerque-International-Balloon-Fiesta-New-Mexico-2010/-345938)

Every year colorful festivals celebrating hot-air balloon flight are held worldwide, the largest of which takes place in [Albuquerque](https://www.britannica.com/place/Albuquerque), New Mexico. Originally featuring just 13 balloons in 1972, the Albuquerque International Balloon Fiesta has grown to include more than 500 balloons per year and has been called “the world’s most photographed event.” Major festivals in the United States are also held in Readington, New Jersey, and [Charlotte](https://www.britannica.com/place/Charlotte-North-Carolina), North Carolina. Other annual hot-air balloon festivals occur in many cities around the world, including [Cappadocia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cappadocia), Turkey; Saint-Jean-sur-Richelieu, Quebec, Canada; [Saga](https://www.britannica.com/place/Saga-Japan), Japan; [Canberra](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canberra), Australian Capital Territory; Taitung, Taiwan; and [Bristol](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bristol-England), England.

**balloon**, large airtight bag filled with hot [air](https://www.britannica.com/science/air) or a lighter-than-air [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter), such as [helium](https://www.britannica.com/science/helium-chemical-element) or [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen), to provide [buoyancy](https://www.britannica.com/science/buoyancy) so that it will rise and float in the atmosphere. Transport balloons have a basket or container hung below for passengers or [cargo](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/cargo). A self-propelled steerable balloon is called an [airship](https://www.britannica.com/technology/airship) or a dirigible.

Balloons were used in the first successful human attempts at flying. Experimentation with balloonlike craft may have begun as early as 1709 with the work of Bartolomeu Lourenço de Gusmão, a Brazilian priest and inventor. In 1783 [Joseph](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Michel-Montgolfier) and [Étienne Montgolfier](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jacques-Etienne-Montgolfier) at Annonay, France, confirmed that a fabric bag filled with hot air would rise. On June 4 of that year they launched an unmanned balloon that traveled more than 1.5 miles (2.4 km). At Versailles they repeated the experiment with a larger balloon on September 19, 1783, sending a sheep, rooster, and duck aloft.

On November 21, 1783, the first manned flight took place when [Jean-François Pilâtre de Rozier](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Francois-Pilatre-de-Rozier) and [François Laurent, marquis d’Arlandes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francois-Laurent-Marquis-dArlandes), sailed over Paris in a Montgolfier balloon. They burned wool and straw to keep the air in the balloon hot; their flight covered 5.5 miles (almost 9 km) in about 23 minutes. In December of that year the physicist [Jacques Charles](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jacques-Charles), accompanied by [Nicolas-Louis Robert](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Nicolas-Louis-Robert), flew a balloon filled with hydrogen on a two-hour flight.

The growth of army sizes, and the introduction of new weapons like long-range heavy artillery and chemical gas, turned combat into mechanized carnage on an unprecedented scale. It also made it more essential than ever for armies to gather information about enemy troops and weapons. Stationary balloons were used for observation and artillery spotting as early as the American Civil War but found widespread use in World War I. Military uses for balloons were soon developed. Anchored observation balloons were used by [Napoleon](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Napoleon-I) in some of his battles and by both sides in the [American Civil War](https://www.britannica.com/event/American-Civil-War) and in [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I). The powered airship developed from balloons, but, while the airship was eventually [supplanted](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/supplanted) by the [airplane](https://www.britannica.com/technology/airplane), balloons have continued to find useful applications. During [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II), balloons were anchored over many parts of Britain to defend against low-level bombing or dive-bombing.

Balloons have also proved enormously valuable to science. As early as 1911–12, [Victor Francis Hess](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Victor-Francis-Hess), an Austrian physicist, made a daring series of balloon ascents as high as 5,000 metres (about 3 miles) to prove the existence of cosmic rays. Advances in weather science since 1900 have resulted in great part from intensive exploration of the upper air by instrumented free balloons, which have risen to an altitude of 30 km (19 miles). [Auguste Piccard](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Auguste-Piccard-Swiss-Belgian-physicist), Swiss physicist and educator, set a world’s [altitude](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/altitude) record in May 1931 in a balloon of his own design, which featured the first pressurized cabin used in flight. [Jean-Felix Piccard](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Felix-Piccard), twin brother of Auguste, experimented with plastic balloons and helped to design the polyethylene Skyhook series of high-altitude balloons with which the U.S. Air Force sent manned flights to more than 100,000 feet (30,000 metres) to collect data on the upper atmosphere. Sport [ballooning](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ballooning-aviation) has gained in popularity over the years.

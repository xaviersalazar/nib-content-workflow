also called:dot-com boom and bust Internet bubble

Date:1995 - 2000

The dot-com boom of 1995–2000 (and ultimate bust in 2001–2002) was a period of large, rapid, and ultimately unsustainable increases in the [stock market](https://www.britannica.com/money/stock-market-basics)—specifically in the valuation of shares in [Internet](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Internet) service and [technology companies](https://www.britannica.com/money/information-technology-stocks), then commonly referred to as “dot-com” companies, including fledgling businesses, or “ [start-ups](https://www.britannica.com/money/start-up-company),” with little or no record of profitability or with unrealistic business models.

During the dot-com bubble, the technology-dominated [Nasdaq](https://www.britannica.com/money/NASDAQ) Composite index (a representation of the total value of the outstanding shares of companies listed on the Nasdaq stock exchange) rose nearly sevenfold from 743 to 5,048, reflecting the early enthusiasm of investors in dot-com enterprises and the willingness of [venture capitalists](https://www.britannica.com/money/venture-capital) to finance the [initial public offerings](https://www.britannica.com/money/ipo-initial-public-offering) (IPOs) of Internet start-ups, many of whose share prices then skyrocketed.

Indeed, many start-up employees who were initially [compensated with stock options](https://www.britannica.com/money/what-are-incentive-stock-options) quickly became millionaires once their companies went public.

As the valuations of shares in new and existing dot-com companies continued to rise, many investors became convinced that the U.S. economy had been fundamentally transformed and that several factors that had [traditionally figured in the valuation of a company’s shares](https://www.britannica.com/money/financial-ratios)—such as the [assets and liabilities on its balance sheet](https://www.britannica.com/money/company-balance-sheet), the [revenue and profit on its income statement](https://www.britannica.com/money/how-to-analyze-an-income-statement), as well as market share and cash flow—were [not directly relevant to assessing the future performance of dot-com companies, particularly start-ups](https://www.britannica.com/money/tam-sam-som-explained). Accordingly, investors continued to pour money even into debt-ridden companies that had no realistic hope of ever turning a profit. Such [investor overconfidence](https://www.britannica.com/money/behavioral-biases-in-finance#ref360573) (often referred to as “irrational exuberance,” a phrase attributed to then– [Federal Reserve](https://www.britannica.com/money/Federal-Reserve-System) chair [Alan Greenspan](https://www.britannica.com/money/Alan-Greenspan)) led the shares of dot-com companies to be priced far in excess of the values that [traditional assessment factors](https://www.britannica.com/money/what-is-fundamental-analysis) would have justified.

As do all financial bubbles, the dot-com bubble finally burst. In early 2000, after the U.S. [Federal Reserve](https://www.britannica.com/money/Federal-Reserve-System) announced a modest [increase in interest rates to stave off inflationary pressures](https://www.britannica.com/money/fiscal-vs-monetary-policy#ref354039)—a move that aimed to reduce investment capital by making borrowing more expensive—investors in dot-com companies began a panicked sell-off of their holdings. Between March 2000 and October 2002, the Nasdaq fell from 5,048 to 1,139, erasing nearly all of its gains during the dot-com bubble. By the time the index bottomed out in October 2002, most publicly traded dot-com companies had failed.

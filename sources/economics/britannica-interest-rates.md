Interest is the price paid for the use of [credit](https://www.britannica.com/money/credit) or [money](https://www.britannica.com/money/money). The interest rate is the price paid, expressed as a percentage—typically on an annualized basis—of the underlying credit amount.

When you borrow money, perhaps to [buy a house](https://www.britannica.com/money/first-time-home-buyer) or [a car](https://www.britannica.com/money/financing-a-car) or [to attend college](https://www.britannica.com/money/federal-vs-private-student-loans), you _pay_ interest. When you deposit money in a bank checking account, purchase a [certificate of deposit](https://www.britannica.com/money/cd-vs-savings-account) (CD), or buy a [bond or other fixed-income security](https://www.britannica.com/money/fixed-income-investment-types), you _earn_ interest.

## How interest is calculated

Interest rates are calculated in two ways. Simple interest is tallied as a percentage of the [principal](https://www.britannica.com/money/principal) over time; [compound interest (also called compounding interest)](https://www.britannica.com/money/what-is-compounding) includes accrued interest along with the principal. Most loans and savings deposits use compound interest. An interest rate may be fixed or variable:

- **A fixed rate** will stay the same over the life of the loan. [Conventional mortgages](https://www.britannica.com/money/how-does-a-mortgage-work), [auto loans](https://www.britannica.com/money/financing-a-car), and many [student loans](https://www.britannica.com/money/subsidized-vs-unsubsidized-student-loans) are fixed.
- **A variable-rate loan** is tied to a benchmark, such as a bank’s prime lending rate. Any changes to that prime rate will change the loan’s interest rate. Loans tied to variable rates include [adjustable-rate mortgages](https://www.britannica.com/money/fixed-vs-variable-rate-mortgage) (ARMs), [home equity lines of credit](https://www.britannica.com/money/home-equity-loan-heloc-second-mortgage), and [credit card debt](https://www.britannica.com/money/understanding-credit-cards).

## Rates are tied to risk

When you lend money, the rate you earn depends on several factors, including the [time to maturity](https://www.britannica.com/money/bond-duration) and the [riskiness of the entity to whom you’re lending money](https://www.britannica.com/money/bond-ratings-credit-risk). For example, money on deposit in a checking account that’s backed by the FDIC typically pays very little interest. But you can withdraw it, penalty-free, at any time. On the other end of the spectrum are longer-dated, [high-yield (“junk”) bonds](https://www.britannica.com/money/junk-bonds-high-yield), which pay high interest rates, but have a higher risk of [default](https://www.britannica.com/money/default) (i.e., you could lose your money).

- What is a mantis?







A mantis is any of about 2,000 species of large, slow-moving insects of the order Mantodea. Mantises are characterized by their front legs, which have enlarged femurs (upper portion) with a groove lined with spines into which the tibia (lower portion) presses. They use these spined front legs to seize prey in a viselike grip, feeding exclusively on living insects.



As insects, mantises have a body formed of a head, thorax, and abdomen, and six legs. Mantises are typically found among vegetation, where their camouflage helps them to stalk or await their victims. They are considered beneficial insects because of their voracious consumption of garden pests.

- How does a mantis catch its food?







Mantises are effective hunters due to their ability to rotate their heads and look over their shoulders. While searching for food, they maintain a posture that looks deceptively humble, as if their front legs are folded in prayer. Mantises are ambush predators that do not actively pursue their prey. They grab their victims using their raptorial front legs, which shoot out quickly from their bodies. They eat their catch while it is alive, often starting with the neck to quickly end the struggle.



Their triangular heads can easily turn, and their vision is well-developed, which is critical for their diet of insects. They may be camouflaged to resemble foliage, dried leaves, twigs, lichens, flowers, or ants to hide from predators and to inconspicuously stalk or await victims.

**mantis**, (order Mantodea), any of approximately 2,000 [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) of large, typically slow-moving [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect) that are characterized by front legs with enlarged femurs (upper portion) that have a groove lined with spines into which the tibia (lower portion) presses. Using their spined front legs, mantises, which feed exclusively on living insects, seize prey in a viselike grip. Mantises are considered [beneficial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/beneficial) insects for their voracious [consumption](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consumption) of garden pests.

As insects, mantises have a body formed of a head, thorax, and abdomen, and six legs. The triangular head is easily turned to face in different directions and features a pair of large, [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) eyes and three supplemental ocelli eyes. Since the mantis diet consists almost entirely of insects, vision is critically important and is unusually well developed. Their specialized front legs have hinged principal leg segments for seizing and holding prey. Their elongated bodies may or may not have wings.

## Natural history

[![Moss mantis (Haania confusa) insect camouflaged with green and brown markings stands on a green leaf.](https://cdn.britannica.com/69/196169-050-DEA31A47/Moss-stick-insect.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/69/196169-050-DEA31A47/Moss-stick-insect.jpg)

[Moss mantis](https://cdn.britannica.com/69/196169-050-DEA31A47/Moss-stick-insect.jpg) A camouflaged moss mantis ( _Haania confusa_).(more)

Usually found among vegetation, a mantis may be [disguised](https://www.britannica.com/science/concealing-coloration) to resemble green or brown foliage, a dried leaf, a slender twig, a lichen, a brightly colored flower, or an ant. This camouflage hides it from predators and also makes it inconspicuous as it stalks or awaits victims. When alarmed the mantis assumes a “threatening” attitude by raising and rustling its wings (if it is a winged species) and often displays bright warning coloration. Most species can run swiftly—both to escape predators and to catch prey—and running is common in mantises that live on the ground, in deserts, or on tree trunks in the tropics. However, the majority of mantises are [ambush](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/ambush) predators and do not actively pursue their prey.

The female, which often eats the male during mating, lays eggs in clusters of less than 10 to more than 300. Usually they are laid in regular layers surrounded by a viscous quick-drying liquid that provides a light but tough protective covering. The cocoonlike capsules (oothecae) of most mantises have a distinctive shape and size. Although most mantis species attach oothecae to vegetation, these egg cases may be attached to rocks in some environments or placed in grooves in the sand and covered over in the desert. The nymphs, which lack wings but otherwise closely resemble adults, all emerge at the same time. The nymphs are often cannibalistic.

## Major genera and species

The [classification](https://www.britannica.com/science/classification-biology) of this group has varied greatly among entomologists. It was formerly placed as a of the order [Orthoptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/orthopteran) or sometimes with the cockroaches, which also produce an ootheca, in the [defunct](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/defunct) order Dictyoptera. Many modern treatments have elevated the group to its own order, Mantodea; the family Mantidae is the order’s largest family.

[![Mantis nymph](https://cdn.britannica.com/34/253834-050-59688F02/Little-mantis-ready-to-jump.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/34/253834-050-59688F02/Little-mantis-ready-to-jump.jpg)

[Mantis nymph](https://cdn.britannica.com/34/253834-050-59688F02/Little-mantis-ready-to-jump.jpg) A tiny mantis nymph crossing between two ferns. (more)

The majority of mantis species are tropical or subtropical. Representative European genera are _Mantis_ ( _M. religiosa_ is the most widespread), _Ameles_, _Iris_, and _Empusa_. North American genera include _Stagmomantis_ (the Carolina mantis \[ _S. carolina_\] is widely distributed), _Litaneutria_ (the agile ground mantis \[ _L. minor_\], a small western species, is the sole mantis native to Canada), and _Thesprotia_ and _Oligonicella_ (both very slender forms). The European mantis ( _M. religiosa_), Mediterranean mantis ( _Iris oratoria_), narrow-winged mantis ( _Tenodera angustipennis_), and Chinese mantis ( _T. aridifolia sinensis_) have been introduced into [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America). The latter species, native to many parts of eastern Asia, is the largest mantis found in North America, ranging from 7 to 10 cm in length.

## Cultural significance

The name mantis derives from the ancient Greek μάντις (‘diviner’), a term later applied to the insect in post-Classical and folk tradition. Numerous [myths](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myths) and [legends](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legends) are associated with the mantis because it can remain motionless or sway gently back and forth, with head raised and front legs outstretched in an apparent attitude of supplication or incantation. According to superstition, the brown saliva of a mantis can cause blindness in a human, and a mantis, if eaten, can kill a horse or mule. The common name praying mantis and the scientific name _Mantis religiosa_, together with many other names such as _Gottesanbeterin_ (German), _prie-Dieu_ (French), _prega-Diou_ (Provençal), and the West Indian “god-horse,” suggest piety. The names devil’s horse and mule killer also are used. Since all mantises are ferocious carnivores, “preying” rather than “praying” may better describe them.

- What is mimicry in biology?







In biology, mimicry is when two or more unrelated organisms share a superficial resemblance. This resemblance gives one or both organisms an advantage, like protection from predators, by deceiving the agent of natural selection.



There are different types of mimicry, including:


\\* **Batesian mimicry** A harmless organism (the mimic) resembles a dangerous one (the model). The mimic benefits because predators mistake it for the model and leave it alone.


\\* **Müllerian mimicry** Two or more dangerous organisms look similar, sharing the same warning systems, such as bright colors. Predators learn to avoid one organism and then avoid all similar organisms.

- Why do some animals or plants use mimicry?







Mimicry is when one organism evolves to resemble another unrelated species, which gives it an advantage. Often, this resemblance protects the mimic from predators. For example, leaf insects use mimicry as camouflage, resembling leaves to avoid detection.



There are different types of mimicry. In Batesian mimicry, a harmless species evolves to look like a dangerous one. In Müllerian mimicry, multiple dangerous species evolve to resemble each other, reinforcing the warning signal to predators. Automimicry is when some members of a species mimic other members of the same species.

- What is the difference between mimicry and camouflage?







[Mimicry](https://www.britannica.com/science/mimicry/Introduction#ref1) and [camouflage](https://www.britannica.com/science/coloration-biology/The-adaptive-value-of-biological-coloration#ref25417) are both types of deceptive coloration, but they work in different ways. Camouflage involves blending into the surrounding environment to avoid detection, whereas mimicry involves resembling another species to gain an advantage, such as protection from predators. In mimicry, the signals have special significance for both the receiver and the sender, who has evolved the signals to be perceived by the receiver. In camouflage, the sender seeks to avoid detection by imitating what is a neutral background to the receiver. Leaf mimicry is a form of camouflage where animals resemble leaves for protection or to ambush prey.

- What are some common examples of mimicry in nature?







Mimicry in nature involves the close resemblance of one organism (the mimic) to another (the model), or even to parts of its own body, deceiving a third party.



  - **Batesian mimicry** is when a harmless species evolves to resemble a dangerous one. For example, edible pierid butterflies mimic distasteful heliconiid butterflies to avoid predators.
  - **Müllerian mimicry** occurs when multiple unpalatable species share similar warning coloration, benefiting from a shared signal that predators learn to avoid. Mimicry rings of various insects, such as beetles, butterflies, and wasps, are an example of this.
  - **Aggressive mimicry** is when a predator mimics a non-threatening species to lure prey. An example is the American zone-tailed hawk, which resembles non-aggressive vultures to surprise small animals.
  - **Automimicry** is mimicry within the same species, where one part of an animal's body resembles another. Some animals may shape parts of their bodies to look like teeth or horns.

- How does Batesian mimicry differ from Müllerian mimicry?







**Batesian mimicry** and **Müllerian mimicry** are two distinct forms of biological resemblance, both named after their 19th-century discoverers.



  - **Batesian mimicry** occurs when a harmless species evolves to resemble a dangerous one. The mimic benefits because predators avoid it, mistaking it for the dangerous model.

  - **Müllerian mimicry** involves multiple dangerous species that come to resemble one another. This convergence benefits all involved because predators learn to avoid one common warning signal, like a specific color pattern, and all species with that signal benefit.

- How does mimicry help species survive and evolve over time?







Mimicry helps species survive and evolve via natural selection, where resemblances to other organisms or objects increase survival rates.



**Batesian mimicry**, named after H.W. Bates, involves a harmless species (the mimic) resembling a dangerous one (the model). This protects the mimic, as predators avoid it, mistaking it for the harmful model.



**Müllerian mimicry** occurs when multiple dangerous species resemble each other, reinforcing the warning signal to predators.



**Aggressive mimicry** is when predators or parasites mimic a third party to gain an advantage.



Mimicry's success depends on the signal receiver's behavior; the receiver must be deceived for the mimicry to work.

**mimicry**, in [biology](https://www.britannica.com/science/biology), phenomenon [characterized](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characterized) by the superficial resemblance of two or more organisms that are not closely related taxonomically. This resemblance confers an advantage—such as protection from predation—upon one or both organisms by which the organisms deceive the animate agent of [natural selection](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-selection). The agent of selection (which may be, for example, a predator, a [symbiont](https://www.britannica.com/science/symbiosis), or the host of a [parasite](https://www.britannica.com/science/parasitism), depending on the type of mimicry encountered) interacts directly with the similar organisms and is deceived by their similarity. This type of [natural selection](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-selection) distinguishes mimicry from other types of convergent resemblance that result from the action of other forces of natural selection (e.g., [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature), [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) habits) on unrelated organisms.

In the most-studied mimetic relationships, the advantage is one-sided, one species (the mimic) gaining advantage from a resemblance to the other (the model). Since the discovery of mimicry in [butterflies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/butterfly-insect) in the mid-19th century, a great many [plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant) and [animals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) have been found to be mimetic. In many cases the organisms involved belong to the same class, order, or even family, but numerous instances are known of plants mimicking animals and vice versa. Although the best-known examples of mimicry involve similarity of appearance, investigations have disclosed fascinating cases in which the resemblance involves [sound](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics), [smell](https://www.britannica.com/science/smell), [behavior](https://www.britannica.com/science/animal-behavior), and even [biochemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/biochemistry).

A key element in virtually every mimetic situation is deception by the mimic, perpetrated upon a third party, which mistakes the mimic for the model. This third party may be the [collective](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/collective) potential predators upon the mimic, potential [prey](https://www.britannica.com/science/predation) of a predacious mimic, or even one [sex](https://www.britannica.com/science/sex) of the mimic’s own species. In some cases, such as host mimicry by parasites, the organism deceived is the model.

Because of the variety of situations in which mimicry occurs, a formal definition must rest upon the effect of certain key communicative signals upon the appropriate receiver and the resultant evolutionary effect upon the emitters of the signals. Mimicry may be defined as a situation in which virtually identical signals, emitted by two different organisms, have in common at least one receiver that reacts in the same manner to both signals because it is advantageous to react in that manner to one of them (that of the model), although it may be disadvantageous to react thus to the counterfeit signal.

The distinction between [camouflage](https://www.britannica.com/topic/camouflage-military-tactic) and mimicry is not always clear when only the model and the mimic are at hand. When the receiver is known and its reactions understood, however, the distinction is quite clear: in mimicry the signals have a special significance for the receiver and for the sender, which has evolved the signals in order to be perceived by the receiver; in camouflage the sender seeks to avoid detection by the receiver through imitation of what is neutral background to the receiver. ## Batesian mimicry

In 1862 the English naturalist [Henry W. Bates](https://www.britannica.com/biography/H-W-Bates) published an explanation for unexpected similarities in appearance between certain Brazilian forest butterflies of two distinct families. Members of one family, the Heliconiidae, are unpalatable to birds and are conspicuously colored; members of the other family, the [Pieridae](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Pieridae), are edible to [predators](https://www.britannica.com/science/predation). Bates concluded that the [conspicuous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conspicuous) [coloration](https://www.britannica.com/science/coloration-biology) of the inedible [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) must serve as a warning for predators that had learned of their inedibility through experience. The deceptively similar [color](https://www.britannica.com/science/color) patterns of the edible species would provide protection from the same predators. This form of mimicry, in which a defenseless organism bears a close resemblance to a noxious and conspicuous one, is called [Batesian](https://www.britannica.com/science/Batesian-mimicry), in honor of its discoverer.

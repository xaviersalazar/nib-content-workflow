### What is a bee?

A bee (superfamily Apoidea) is any of more than 20,000 species of insects in the suborder [Apocrita](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Apocrita) (order [Hymenoptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/hymenopteran)), which includes the familiar [honeybee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honeybee) ( _Apis_) and [bumblebee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bumblebee) ( _Bombus_ and _Psithyrus_) as well as thousands of more wasplike and flylike bees.

### What are the differences between bees and wasps?

The principal biological difference between [bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bee) and [wasps](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wasp) is that bees (except for parasitic bees) provide their young with a mixture of pollen and honey, but wasps feed their young insects or spiders. Also, whereas wasps are covered with unbranched hairs, bees have at least a few branched or feathered hairs to which pollen often clings.

Bees and wasps, however, have a great deal in common. [Learn how they’re related.](https://www.britannica.com/explore/hymenoptera-ants-bees-wasps)

### Do male bees collect pollen?

Male bees are usually short-lived and never collect [pollen](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollen), nor do they have other responsibilities providing for the young. Female bees do all the work of [nest](https://www.britannica.com/science/nest) making and provisioning and usually have special anatomical structures that assist them in carrying pollen.

### Do all bees live in colonies?

Most bees are solitary, or nonsocial, in habit, and do not live in colonies. Among such bees, there are no castes. Some solitary bees make chimneys or turrets at the nest entrance, and others nest in wood or the pith of twigs or canes.

### Why are bees important?

The ecological value of bees as [pollinators](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollination) of crops is enormously greater than the value of their honey and wax production. There is no doubt that bees and the [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) that they pollinate evolved simultaneously.

**bee**, (superfamily Apoidea), any of more than 20,000 species of [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect) in the suborder [Apocrita](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Apocrita) (order [Hymenoptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/hymenopteran)), including the familiar [honeybee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honeybee) ( _Apis_) and [bumblebee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bumblebee) ( _Bombus_) as well as thousands more wasplike and flylike bees. Adults range in size from about 2 mm to 4 cm (about 0.08–1.6 inches).

Bees are closely related to certain types of [wasps](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wasp), the principal biological difference between them being that bees (except for parasitic [cuckoo bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/cuckoo-bee)) provide their young with [pollen](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollen) and sometimes [honey](https://www.britannica.com/topic/honey), whereas wasps feed their young [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) food or [provision](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/provision) their nests with insects or spiders. Associated with this difference in food preference are certain structural differences, the most essential being that wasps are covered with unbranched hairs, whereas bees have at least a few branched or feathered hairs to which pollen often clings.

Bees are entirely dependent on [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) for food, which consists of [pollen](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollen) and [nectar](https://www.britannica.com/science/nectar), the latter sometimes modified and stored as honey. There is no doubt that bees and the flowers that they pollinate [evolved simultaneously](https://www.britannica.com/science/coevolution). As bees go from flower to flower gathering pollen, a small amount is rubbed from their bodies and deposited on the flowers they visit. This loss of pollen is significant, for it often results in [cross-pollination](https://www.britannica.com/science/cross-pollination) of plants. The practical [value](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/value) of bees as [pollinators](https://www.britannica.com/science/major-types-of-pollinators) is enormously greater than the value of the honey and wax of [honeybees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honeybee) and [stingless bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/stingless-bee).

Male bees are usually short-lived and never collect pollen, nor do they have other responsibilities in connection with providing for the young. Female bees do all the work of nest making and provisioning and usually have special anatomical structures that assist them in carrying pollen. Most bees are polylectic, meaning that they gather pollen from a wide variety of flowers. However, some bees collect pollen only from flowers of certain families, others from flowers of certain colors. Oligolectic bees gather pollen from only a few related kinds of flowers. The mouth parts of bees, like the pollen-collecting and pollen-carrying devices, seem to be adapted to different flowers.

Most bees are [solitary](https://www.britannica.com/animal/solitary-bee), or nonsocial, in habit and do not live in colonies. In these species each female makes her own nest (usually a burrow in the ground) and [provisions](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/provisions) it. Among such bees there are no castes. Some solitary bees make chimneys or turrets at the nest entrance, others nest in [wood](https://www.britannica.com/science/wood-plant-tissue) or in the pith of twigs or canes. Most solitary bees are short-lived as adults. Some species may be in flight only a few weeks of the year, having spent the rest of the year in their cells as eggs, [larvae](https://www.britannica.com/science/larva), [pupae](https://www.britannica.com/science/pupa), and young adults.

Solitary bees provide all of the food the larvae require to complete development when the cells are sealed. Social bees, such as the bumblebee and the honeybee, feed their young progressively. For the [life cycle](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/life%20cycle) of social bees, _see_ [bumblebee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bumblebee); [honeybee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honeybee); [stingless bee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/stingless-bee).

The Apoidea includes seven families: [Colletidae](https://www.britannica.com/animal/plasterer-bee-family), which are [plasterer bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/plasterer-bee-family) consisting of five or six subfamilies, about 45 genera, and some 2,500 species; [Andrenidae](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mining-bee), which are medium- and large-sized solitary [mining bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mining-bee), including some parasitic species; [Halictidae](https://www.britannica.com/animal/sweat-bee), about 4,500 species of [sweat bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/sweat-bee), which are attracted to perspiration; Melittidae, bees that mark a transitional form between the lower and the higher bees; [Megachilidae](https://www.britannica.com/animal/leaf-cutter-bee) ( [leaf-cutting](https://www.britannica.com/animal/leaf-cutter-bee) and [mason bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mason-bee)), noted for their elaborate nest structures; Stenotritidae, a small family of Australian bees; and [Apidae](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Apidae) with some 5,700 species of [bumblebees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bumblebee), [honeybees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honeybee), [stingless bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/stingless-bee), [carpenter bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/carpenter-bee), and digger bees.

The answer: it depends on the bee. There are more than 20,000 species of [bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bee), with quite a bit of diversity when it comes to stinging. The most familiar bee is the [western honeybee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/western-honeybee) ( _Apis mellifera_), the females of which do indeed die after stinging. Stingers are modified ovipositors (egg-laying organs), meaning male bees cannot sting. The stinger of a [honeybee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honeybee) is barbed, which means once it pierces human [skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skin), it gets stuck. As the bee tries to fly away, the stinger is ripped from its body, along with part of its abdomen, digestive tract, muscles, and nerves. This catastrophic injury is fatal to the bee, who has given her life in service to the colony. Interestingly, another group of [honey](https://www.britannica.com/topic/honey)-making bees, the [stingless bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/stingless-bee), do not have stingers at all.

Few, if any, solitary bees (which are the vast majority of the world’s bees), die after stinging. Some, such as most [mining bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mining-bee) and certain [leaf-cutting bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/leaf-cutter-bee), lack the ability to sting or cannot pierce human skin. Others, like [mason bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mason-bee), do not have barbed stingers and are thus capable of stinging without dying. However, they are not considered aggressive and rarely sting unless directly provoked.

- What is a dragonfly?







A dragonfly is an insect belonging to the suborder Anisoptera within the order [Odonata](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Odonata/Introduction#ref1). Dragonflies are aerial predators, recognized for their agility and speed, often found near freshwater habitats. They have long bodies, two pairs of narrow, intricately veined wings, and large, bulging eyes that provide a field of vision approaching 360 degrees.



Dragonfly young, called larvae or nymphs, are aquatic predators. They consume mosquito larvae, worms, crustaceans, tadpoles, and small fish. Adult dragonflies are also voracious predators, feeding on flying insects like mosquitoes and flies. Dragonflies have been held in high regard in folklore and art, especially in Asian cultures. However, 16% of dragonfly and damselfly species are at risk of extinction due to habitat loss.

- What do dragonflies look like?







Adult dragonflies have long bodies and two pairs of narrow, intricately veined, membranous wings that may have colored markings. Unlike [damselflies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/damselfly), the front and rear wing pairs are shaped differently. Dragonflies rest with their wings spread horizontally. They have a powerful build and are generally much stronger fliers than damselflies. Dragonflies also have huge bulging eyes that occupy most of the head, giving some a field of vision approaching 360 degrees.



The large wings are strengthened by a complex network of veins. Each wing also has a thickened patch (the pterostigma) on the leading edge of the wing tip. The forewings of dragonflies are narrower than the hind wings. The sloping thorax accommodates very large wing muscles and sets the legs forward, where they can best grasp prey.

- Where do dragonflies live?







Dragonflies are insects of the order Odonata, found throughout the world, with about 3,000 species inhabiting mostly freshwater habitats. They are most numerous and varied in the tropics but can be found as far north as the boreal forests of Siberia and North America, and as far south as the Southern Hemisphere (except Antarctica).



Dragonfly larvae, also known as nymphs or naiads, are aquatic and live in various shallow freshwater habitats like tree holes, ponds, marshes, and streams. They are often bottom-dwelling, with mottled or drab colors that camouflage them among sediments or water plants. Adult dragonflies are strong fliers and are usually found flying near water.

- How do dragonflies catch their food?







Dragonflies are among the fastest insects and most effective aerial predators, eating mosquitoes, flies, and sometimes prey that is 60% of their own weight. Their agility and speed help them capture small flying insects.



Dragonfly larvae, also dedicated predators, possess a unique structure called a "mask," which is a fusion of the larva’s third pair of mouthparts. At the end of the mask is a set of fang-like pincers used to seize prey such as mosquito larvae, worms, crustaceans, tadpoles, and small fish.



Adult dragonflies have exceptional vision, with huge, bulging eyes occupying most of their head, giving them a field of vision approaching 360 degrees. Some dragonfly species have regions of increased resolution on their eyes, which they use to spot flying insects.

- What is the life cycle of a dragonfly?







The dragonfly life cycle begins with eggs laid in or near water, either inside plant tissue or dropped onto the water's surface. The eggs hatch into aquatic larvae, also known as nymphs or naiads, which are dedicated predators.



These functionally wingless larvae possess a unique anatomical structure called the "mask," which is a fusion of mouthparts used to seize prey. They absorb oxygen from the water through gills inside their rectum and can use jet propulsion to escape danger.



As the larva grows, it molts, with wing sheaths becoming apparent about halfway through development. Eventually, the larva crawls out of the water and molts one last time, emerging as a soft, pale, reproductively immature adult, leaving behind a cast skin. The adult dragonfly then flies away from the water to feed and mature. Adult life consists of prereproductive and reproductive periods. Dragonflies exhibit a unique mating posture, with the male grasping the female's head to form a "wheel" position for sperm transfer. After mating, the female lays eggs, either alone or with the male still attached. Some dragonfly species are migratory.

- How are dragonflies important to their ecosystems?







Dragonflies are important to their ecosystems as both larvae and adults. Dragonfly larvae are aquatic predators that feed on various small water animals, including [mosquito](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mosquito) larvae, worms, crustaceans, tadpoles, and small fish. Adult dragonflies are aerial predators that feed on small flying insects, such as mosquitoes and flies. Because they are nonmigratory and stay in small areas throughout their lives, macroinvertebrates such as dragonfly larvae are good indicators of the environmental health of streams and waterways.

**dragonfly**, (suborder Anisoptera), any of a group of roughly 3,000 species of aerial predatory [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect) most commonly found near freshwater habitats throughout most of the world. [Damselflies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/damselfly) (suborder Zygoptera) are sometimes also called dragonflies in that both are odonates (order [Odonata](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Odonata)). Extremely strong and agile fliers, dragonflies are among the fastest insects known and are voracious predators of flying insects such as [mosquitoes](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mosquito-insect) and [flies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fly-insect).

In 2021 the [International Union for Conservation of Nature](https://www.britannica.com/topic/International-Union-for-Conservation-of-Nature) (IUCN) released the first global [assessment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/assessment) of dragonflies as an update to their [Red List of Threatened Species](https://www.britannica.com/topic/IUCN-Red-List-of-Threatened-Species). The study found that widespread [habitat loss](https://www.britannica.com/science/habitat-loss) in [wetlands](https://www.britannica.com/science/wetland) and along rivers is contributing to the decline of dragonfly species around the world, with 16 percent of dragonflies and damselflies now at risk of [extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology).

## Distinguishing characteristics and flight behavior

[![Long-winged skimmer](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/100792-050-2743DD21/skimmer.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/100792-050-2743DD21/skimmer.jpg)

[Long-winged skimmer](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/100792-050-2743DD21/skimmer.jpg) Swift long-winged skimmer ( _Pachydiplax longipennis_).(more)

Adult dragonflies are characterized by long bodies with two narrow pairs of intricately veined, membranous wings that, while generally transparent, may have colored markings. Unlike damselflies, the front and rear wing pairs are shaped differently. In addition, dragonflies rest with their wings spread horizontally, rather than held vertically against each other (with the exception of one very small family, Epiophlebiidae). Dragonflies have a more powerful build and are generally much stronger fliers than damselflies. Dragonflies also have huge bulging eyes that occupy most of the head, giving some a field of vision approaching 360 degrees.

The winged adults are diversely colored in a variety of shades ranging from metallic to pastel. Compared with other insects, they are large, with some having wingspans of up to 16 cm (about 6 inches). Even the smallest species are about 20 mm (0.8 inch) across. Dragonfly wing muscles must be warm to function optimally, and so, if cool, the insect often engages in wing-whirring and basking in the sun to generate heat before taking flight. The dragonfly’s speed and agility contribute to its being one of the most effective aerial predators. Small flying insects, such as [mosquitoes](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mosquito-insect), are the usual fare, but some dragonflies regularly consume [prey](https://www.britannica.com/science/predation) that is 60 percent of their own weight.

## Life cycle and reproduction

![Dragonfly larva](https://cdn.britannica.com/18/24018-004-C8846E21/dragonfly-larva-mask-fish.jpg)

[Dragonfly larva](https://cdn.britannica.com/18/24018-004-C8846E21/dragonfly-larva-mask-fish.jpg) A dragonfly larva captures a fish by extending its labial mask.(more)

Young dragonflies, called [larvae](https://www.britannica.com/science/larva) or sometimes [nymphs](https://www.britannica.com/science/nymph-entomology) or naiads, are aquatic and are as dedicated predators under water as the adults are in the air. The functionally wingless larvae are usually mottled or dull in color, matching the sediments or water plants among which they live. They have bulging eyes somewhat similar to the adults, but possess a [formidable](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/formidable) anatomical structure not present in the adult. Called the “mask,” it is a fusion of the larva’s third pair of mouthparts. Disproportionately large, the mask folds beneath both the head and thorax when it is not in use. At the end of the mask is a set of fanglike pincers used to seize prey such as [mosquito](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mosquito-insect) larvae, [worms](https://www.britannica.com/animal/worm), [crustaceans](https://www.britannica.com/animal/crustacean), [tadpoles](https://www.britannica.com/science/tadpole), and small [fish](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fish). Different species of dragonfly larvae can be described as sprawlers, burrowers, hiders, or claspers. Their shape, metabolism, and respiration differ concordantly with the microhabitat they occupy.

[![Egg-laying](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/243595-050-93C75B2E/Emperor-dragonfly-Anax-imperator.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/243595-050-93C75B2E/Emperor-dragonfly-Anax-imperator.jpg)

[Egg-laying](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/243595-050-93C75B2E/Emperor-dragonfly-Anax-imperator.jpg) An emperor dragonfly ( _Anax imperator_) laying her eggs in the water. (more)

Larvae crawl from eggs laid in or near water. Some species lay their eggs inside plant tissue, others attach their eggs to substrates at or above the water’s surface, and some may drop or wash their eggs from their abdomen onto water. Larvae absorb oxygen from the water using [gills](https://www.britannica.com/science/gill-respiratory-system) inside the [rectum](https://www.britannica.com/science/rectum). The abdomen draws water in and pumps it out again through the anus. Water can be forcibly expelled in this way, resulting in jet propulsion as a means of escape. Solid waste is also expelled in this manner. As the larva grows, it molts, its future wings first becoming apparent about halfway through the larva’s development. These wing sheaths then enlarge rapidly with each successive molt. Eventually, the larva crawls out of the water (often at night) and molts one last time, [emerging](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/emerging) as an adult and leaving behind a cast skin ( [exuvia](https://www.britannica.com/science/exuvia)).

Dragonflies, like damselflies, exhibit a [mating](https://www.britannica.com/science/mating) posture unique to the Odonata. The male and female contort themselves into the “wheel” position before sperm is transferred. Before and after mating, dragonflies often fly in tandem, with the male towing the female in flight using claspers at the tip of his abdomen to grip the back of her head. Pairs of some species may remain in tandem while the female lays her eggs.

Many dragonfly families have descriptive common names associated with their scientific names. Examples include the hawkers (Aeshnidae), petaltails (Petaluridae), and clubtails (Gomphidae). Numerous other names related to neither [taxonomy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/taxonomy) nor fact have traditionally been applied to dragonflies, such as _horse stinger_. Dragonflies have also been known as “snake doctors” in the American South, owing to the superstition that they nurse ill snakes back to health. The term _devil’s darning needle_ is derived from a superstition that dragonflies may sew up the eyes, ears, or mouth of a sleeping child, especially one who has misbehaved. In reality, dragonflies present no danger to humans.

- What is an insect?







Insects, belonging to the class Insecta (or Hexapoda) within the phylum Arthropoda, represent the largest group of animals. Key features that distinguish insects from other arthropods include a body divided into three main sections: the head, thorax, and abdomen. The head has mouthparts, eyes, and a pair of antennae. The three-segmented thorax typically features three pairs of legs (hence, Hexapoda) and, in most adults, one or two pairs of wings. Lastly, the many-segmented abdomen houses the digestive, excretory, and reproductive organs.



Insects play crucial roles in nature, aiding in the decomposition of organic matter and soil formation. They are essential for pollination and serve as predators of other insects. Many plants rely on insects for pollination. They grow through metamorphosis, either complete or gradual, from egg to larva or nymph to adult.

- What does it mean for an animal to be an arthropod?







Arthropods are members of the phylum Arthropoda, the animal kingdom's largest phylum. About 84% of the 1.8 million animal species described by science are arthropods.



Key traits of arthropods:


\\* **Exoskeleton** Arthropods have a jointed skeletal covering, or exoskeleton, made of chitin bound to protein.


\\* **Segmentation** Their bodies are usually segmented.


\\* **Appendages** They have paired, jointed appendages, which gives them their name, meaning "jointed feet".


\\* **No locomotory cilia** Arthropods lack locomotory cilia, even in the larval stages.



The phylum Arthropoda is divided into four subphyla: Chelicerata (arachnids), Crustacea (crustaceans), Hexapoda (insects and springtails), and Myriapoda (millipedes and centipedes). They are found in every habitat on Earth and have a great variety of adaptations.

- What are the main body parts of an insect?







Insects, belonging to the class Insecta, are arthropods characterized by a body divided into three main parts: the head, thorax, and abdomen.



  - **Head** The insect head features antennae, eyes, and mouthparts. Mouthparts are adapted for feeding and may consist of a labrum, mandibles, maxillae, and a labium.
  - **Thorax** The thorax is segmented and typically has three pairs of legs (six legs total). It also usually has one or two pairs of wings.
  - **Abdomen** The abdomen contains the digestive, excretory, and reproductive organs. It consists of multiple segments and may feature spiracles (breathing holes).

- How many different types of insects are there?







Insects, belonging to the arthropod class Insecta, are the most diverse group of animals. Entomologists have identified approximately one million insect species, which represents about three-fourths of all known animal species. Experts estimate that the actual number of living insect species could be as high as 5 million to 10 million.



The order Coleoptera (beetles) contains the greatest number of species. Other orders with large numbers of species are Lepidoptera (butterflies and moths), Hymenoptera (ants, bees, wasps), and Diptera (true flies). Insects have adapted to nearly every terrestrial and freshwater habitat, including deserts, jungles, glacial fields, and hot springs.

- How are insects adapted to live in so many different environments?







Insects, as arthropods, exhibit remarkable adaptability to diverse environments through several key features. Their ability to thrive in varied habitats is attributed to their capacity to feed on all sorts of organic matter, and their feeding and digestion methods have become modified accordingly. They possess a waxy layer on the exoskeleton that minimizes water loss, crucial for terrestrial survival.



Many insects have wings, formed from folds of integument, which allow them to move to new habitats and avoid unfavorable conditions. They can also adjust their body positioning to regulate temperature, seeking shade in hot conditions or sun to warm themselves in cooler environments. Some species have evolved antifreeze mechanisms like glycerol accumulation to endure freezing temperatures. Additionally, some camouflage themselves using materials from their surroundings for protection.

- Why are insects important to humans and the environment?







Insects play crucial roles in both human life and the environment. They assist in [decomposition](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect/Distribution-and-abundance#ref41253) and [soil formation](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect/Distribution-and-abundance#ref41253), helping to recycle nutrients. Many plants rely on insects for [pollination](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect/Distribution-and-abundance#ref41253), and some insects act as predators, controlling populations of other insects.



Certain insects produce commercially valuable products like [honey](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect/Insects-as-a-source-of-raw-materials#ref41255), [silk](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect/Insects-as-a-source-of-raw-materials#ref41255), [wax](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect/Insects-as-a-source-of-raw-materials#ref41255), and dyes. Conversely, some insects can be pests, causing agricultural damage by devouring crops and transmitting diseases. For some cultures around the world, insects are a significant [food source](https://www.britannica.com/topic/entomophagy/Introduction#ref1), providing protein and other nutrients.

[horse fly](https://cdn.britannica.com/01/30701-004-4884AF17/Horse-fly.jpg) Horse fly ( _Tabanus trimaculatus_).(more)

In a popular sense, “insect” usually refers to familiar pests or disease carriers, such as [bedbugs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bedbug), [houseflies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/housefly), clothes moths, [Japanese beetles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Japanese-beetle), [aphids](https://www.britannica.com/animal/aphid), [mosquitoes](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mosquito-insect), [fleas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/flea), [horseflies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/horse-fly), and hornets, or to [conspicuous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conspicuous) groups, such as [butterflies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/butterfly-insect), [moths](https://www.britannica.com/animal/moth-insect), and beetles. Many insects, however, are [beneficial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/beneficial) from a [human](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being) viewpoint; they pollinate plants, produce useful substances, control [pest](https://www.britannica.com/science/pest-vermin) insects, act as scavengers, and serve as food for other animals ( _see below_ [Importance](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect/Distribution-and-abundance#ref41252)). Furthermore, insects are valuable objects of study in elucidating many aspects of [biology](https://www.britannica.com/science/biology) and [ecology](https://www.britannica.com/science/ecology). Much of the scientific knowledge of [genetics](https://www.britannica.com/science/genetics) has been gained from [fruit fly](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fruit-fly) experiments and of population biology from flour beetle studies. Insects are often used in investigations of hormonal action, nerve and sense organ function, and many other physiological processes. Insects are also used as environmental quality indicators to assess water quality and soil contamination and are the basis of many studies of [biodiversity](https://www.britannica.com/science/biodiversity).

In numbers of [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) and individuals and in adaptability and wide distribution, insects are perhaps the most eminently successful group of all animals. They dominate the present-day land fauna with about 1 million described species. This represents about three-fourths of all described animal species. Entomologists estimate the actual number of living insect species could be as high as 5 million to 10 million. The orders that contain the greatest numbers of species are [Coleoptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Coleoptera) ( [beetles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bee)), [Lepidoptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/lepidopteran) (butterflies and [moths](https://www.britannica.com/animal/moth-insect)), [Hymenoptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/hymenopteran) ( [ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ant), bees, [wasps](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wasp)), and [Diptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dipteran) (true flies).

## Appearance and habits

The majority of insects are small, usually less than 6 mm (0.2 inch) long, although the range in size is wide. Some of the [feather-winged beetles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/feather-winged-beetle) and parasitic wasps are almost microscopic, while some tropical forms such as the hercules beetles, African goliath beetles, certain Australian stick insects, and the wingspan of the hercules [moth](https://www.britannica.com/animal/moth-insect) can be as large as 27 cm (10.6 inches).

[![Green drake mayfly](https://cdn.britannica.com/55/37555-050-F704B9F1/mayfly-life-span-adults.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/55/37555-050-F704B9F1/mayfly-life-span-adults.jpg)

[Green drake mayfly](https://cdn.britannica.com/55/37555-050-F704B9F1/mayfly-life-span-adults.jpg) A female green drake mayfly ( _Ephemera danica_).(more)

In many species the difference in body structure between the sexes is pronounced, and knowledge of one sex may give few clues to the appearance of the other sex. In some, such as the twisted-wing insects ( [Strepsiptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/strepsipteran)), the female is a mere inactive bag of eggs, and the winged male is one of the most active insects known. Modes of reproduction are quite [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse), and reproductive capacity is generally high. Some insects, such as the [mayflies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mayfly), feed only in the immature or larval stage and go without food during an extremely short [adult](https://www.britannica.com/science/adulthood) life. Among social insects, queen termites may live for up to 50 years, whereas some adult mayflies live less than two hours.

Some insects advertise their presence to the other sex by flashing lights, and many imitate other insects in colour and form and thus avoid or minimize attack by predators that feed by day and find their [prey](https://www.britannica.com/science/predation) visually, as do birds, lizards, and other insects.

Behaviour is diverse, from the almost [inert](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/inert) parasitic forms, whose [larvae](https://www.britannica.com/science/larva) lie in the nutrient bloodstreams of their hosts and feed by absorption, to [dragonflies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dragonfly) that pursue victims in the air, [tiger beetles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/tiger-beetle) that outrun prey on land, and predaceous water beetles that outswim prey in water.

**firefly**, (family Lampyridae), family of some 2,000 species of beetles (insect order [Coleoptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Coleoptera)) found in most tropical and [temperate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/temperate) regions that have special light-producing organs on the underside of the [abdomen](https://www.britannica.com/science/abdomen). Most fireflies are nocturnal, although some species are diurnal. They are soft-bodied beetles that range from 5 to 25 mm (up to 1 inch) in length. The flattened, dark brown or [black body](https://www.britannica.com/science/blackbody) is often marked with [orange](https://www.britannica.com/science/orange-color) or [yellow](https://www.britannica.com/science/yellow-color).

Taxonomy

- Kingdom: [Animalia](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal)

- Phylum: [Arthropoda](https://www.britannica.com/animal/arthropod)

- Class: [Insecta](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect)

- Order: [Coleoptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Coleoptera)

- Family: Lampyridae


_See also_ [list of insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/list-of-insects-2073946).

Some adult fireflies do not eat, whereas many feed on [pollen](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollen) and [nectar](https://www.britannica.com/science/nectar). In a few species females are predatory on males of other firefly species. Both sexes are usually winged and [luminous](https://www.britannica.com/science/bioluminescence), although in some species only one sex has the light-producing organ. Females lacking wings and resembling the long, flat [larvae](https://www.britannica.com/science/larva) are commonly referred to as glowworms. The larvae are sometimes luminescent before they [hatch](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/hatch). Larvae live on the ground and feed on snails and slugs by injecting a fluid into their prey and then withdrawing the partly digested matter through hollow mouthparts. The common glowworm ( _[Lampyris noctiluca](https://www.britannica.com/animal/European-glowworm)_) is a member of this family ( _see_ [glowworm](https://www.britannica.com/animal/glowworm)).

Most fireflies produce short, rhythmic flashes in a pattern characteristic of the species. The rhythmic flash pattern is part of a signal system that brings the sexes together. Both the rate of flashing and the amount of time before the female’s response to the male are important. Some authorities feel that the flashing is also a protective mechanism, reminding predators of the firefly’s bitter taste. However, some frogs eat such large numbers of fireflies that they themselves glow.

Firefly light is produced under nervous control within special cells (photocytes) richly supplied with air tubes (tracheae). Firefly light is a [cold light](https://www.britannica.com/science/luminescence) with approximately 100 percent of the energy given off as light and only a minute amount of heat. Only light in the [visible spectrum](https://www.britannica.com/science/light) is [emitted](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/emitted). Some tropical members of the coleopteran family Elateridae are also called fireflies ( _see_ [click beetle](https://www.britannica.com/animal/click-beetle)).

Also called: lightning bug_(Show more)_

The emitted light (due to a chemical process known as [bioluminescence](https://www.britannica.com/science/bioluminescence)) of such organisms as fireflies, various marine organisms ( _see_ [marine bioluminescence](https://www.britannica.com/science/marine-bioluminescence)), and a number of [algae](https://www.britannica.com/science/algae), [fungi](https://www.britannica.com/science/fungus), and [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria) is based on the oxidation of any of several organic molecules known as [luciferins](https://www.britannica.com/science/luciferin). The luciferins are broken down by the [enzyme](https://www.britannica.com/science/enzyme) [luciferase](https://www.britannica.com/science/luciferase) and react with the energy molecule [ATP](https://www.britannica.com/science/adenosine-triphosphate) and oxygen, resulting in a glowing light.

[Fireflies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/firefly-insect) light up summer nights using a chemical process known as [bioluminescence](https://www.britannica.com/science/bioluminescence), the key chemical players of which are the [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light)-emitting compound [luciferin](https://www.britannica.com/science/luciferin) and the enzyme [luciferase](https://www.britannica.com/science/luciferase). Luciferin, in the presence of luciferase, undergoes oxidation, resulting in the production of oxyluciferin, an unstable excited-state intermediate compound. As oxyluciferin quickly returns to a lower energy state, it gives off light. Because the reaction generates very little heat, and nearly all the energy is released as light, firefly light is often referred to as “cold light.”

In the [chemical reaction](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-reaction) that produces firefly light, luciferase plays a crucial role as a [catalyst](https://www.britannica.com/science/catalyst), speeding up the reaction between luciferin and [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen). This enzyme-substrate reaction is specific to each organism, meaning fireflies have their own unique luciferase and luciferin compounds.

Related Topics: [bioluminescence](https://www.britannica.com/science/bioluminescence)[firefly](https://www.britannica.com/animal/firefly-insect)[luciferase](https://www.britannica.com/science/luciferase)[luciferin](https://www.britannica.com/science/luciferin)_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/How-Do-Fireflies-Light-Up)

Fireflies use their light for various purposes, including communication, mating, and potentially as a means of defense against predators. The rhythmic flashing patterns are species-specific signals that help males and females find each other in the dark. The timing and pattern of these flashes are crucial for successful mating, as they allow fireflies to recognize and respond to potential mates.

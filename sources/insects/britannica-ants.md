- What is an ant?







Ants are social insects that belong to the family Formicidae and the order Hymenoptera. They live in organized colonies and are found worldwide, especially in tropical and subtropical regions.



Within an ant colony, there are three castes: queens, males, and workers. The colony is a family community, and each ant is an integral unit. The essential work in the ant society is performed by the workers, all of whom are female. They build nests, feed and tend to the young, and defend the colony.



Ants eat both plant and animal substances. Many species are opportunistic omnivores, consuming nectar, resins, saps, live prey, and scavenged carcasses. Some ants, like harvester ants, gather seeds and fruits, while leafcutter ants cultivate a fungus for food.

- Where do ants live?

The nests they build can be in the ground, under rocks, or above ground using materials like twigs, gravel, or sand. Subterranean nests can be extensive networks of tunnels and chambers.

- What do ants eat?







Ants' diets vary widely across the 10,000 species on Earth. Many are opportunistic omnivores, consuming both plant-based foods like nectar and resins, and animal-based foods, such as live prey and carcasses. Some species are carnivorous hunters, like bullet ants and driver ants, which eat insects, spiders, frogs, and other small animals.



Other ants, known as herder ants, collect honeydew from aphids, while honey ants have specialized workers that store honeydew in their abdomens for times of scarcity. Harvester ants gather seeds and fruits, and leafcutter ants cultivate a fungus for food. Most ants live in nests, and worker ants gather food daily for the larvae and mature ants, using scent marks to guide them.

- How do ants communicate with each other?







Ants primarily communicate through [pheromones](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemoreception/Pheromones#ref279489), which are chemical signals that can transmit various messages. These pheromones are secreted from glands and perceived through olfactory receptors.



Different pheromones serve different purposes, such as marking foraging trails, recruiting nestmates for defense, attracting mates, and distinguishing colony members from non-members. Alarm pheromones, for instance, are highly volatile and quickly dissipate to warn other ants of danger. Trail pheromones, on the other hand, are more persistent, guiding ants to food sources.



In addition to pheromones, ants also use [touch and vibrations](https://www.britannica.com/animal/termite/Nutrition#ref39600) to communicate, especially within the dark confines of their nests. For example, alarmed ants may tap their heads against the ground, creating vibrations that alert others.

- What roles do different ants have in a colony?







Within an ant colony, ants have different roles divided among castes, each with specific functions and morphological features. Generally, a colony contains queens, males, and workers.



  - **Queens** The fertile females are responsible for laying eggs. After mating, a queen can either start a new nest or take over an existing one.

  - **Males** Their primary function is to mate with the queen. They don't participate in nest activities and depend on the colony for food.

  - **Workers** All workers are female, and they perform essential tasks such as building the nest, tending to the brood, and defending the colony. Workers may be further differentiated into subcastes, such as:
    - **Soldiers** defend the colony.
    - **Outside workers** forage for food.
    - **Inside workers** maintain the nest.
    - **Nest builders** construct and repair the nest.
    - **Repletes** are a specialized worker caste of honey ants that store food.
    - **Guards** protect the nest entrance.

**ant**, (family Formicidae), any of approximately 10,000 species of [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect) that are social in habit and live together in organized colonies. Ants occur worldwide but are most numerous, both in numbers and in species, in tropical and subtropical regions. Ants are essential members of the [ecosystems](https://www.britannica.com/science/ecosystem) they inhabit, and some even serve as [keystone species](https://www.britannica.com/science/keystone-species) that have a disproportionately large effect on their ecological [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities). Some ants are considered pests to humans, and a number are [invasive species](https://www.britannica.com/science/invasive-species) in areas outside their native ranges.

## Physical description

![Black carpenter ant](https://cdn.britannica.com/55/88055-050-95880081/Black-carpenter-ant.jpg)

[Black carpenter ant](https://cdn.britannica.com/55/88055-050-95880081/Black-carpenter-ant.jpg) Illustration of a black carpenter ant ( _Camponotus pennsylvanicus_).(more)

Ants range in size from about 2 to 25 mm (about 0.08 to 1 inch). Their color is usually yellow, brown, red, or black. A few genera (e.g., _Pheidole_ of [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America)) have a metallic luster.

Typically, an ant has a large head and a slender, oval [abdomen](https://www.britannica.com/science/abdomen) joined to the [thorax](https://www.britannica.com/science/thorax), or midsection, by a small waist. In all ants there are either one or two finlike extensions running across the thin waist [region](https://www.britannica.com/science/region-geography). The antennae are always elbowed. There are two sets of jaws: the outer pair is used for carrying objects such as food and for digging, and the inner pair is used for chewing. Some species, such as the debilitating [bullet ant](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bullet-insect) ( _Paraponera clavata_), have a powerful sting at the tip of the abdomen.

## Natural history

[![Hear E.O. Wilson talk about his research on how ants determine the death of another](https://cdn.britannica.com/28/193428-138-19AC479D/Edward-O-Wilson-ants-research-ant.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/Edward-O-Wilson-ants-research-ant/-218154)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ant/images-videos)

Ants are [social insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/social-insect), and the [colony](https://www.britannica.com/science/colony-animal-society) is a family [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) of which every ant is an [integral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integral) unit. Apart from the community, any one individual cannot properly function or survive, and the [larvae](https://www.britannica.com/science/larva) are completely dependent upon the continuous care of the adults. There are generally three [castes](https://www.britannica.com/science/caste-zoology), or classes, within a colony: [queens](https://www.britannica.com/science/queen-insect-caste), males, and [workers](https://www.britannica.com/science/worker). Male ants play no part in everyday nest activities. They live only for a short time, occur in limited numbers, and are virtual parasites of the colony, which must feed them. The fertile female, the queen, performs only one task: egg laying. The life cycle of the ant has four stages—egg, larva, [pupa](https://www.britannica.com/science/pupa), and adult—and typically spans a period of 8 to 10 weeks for worker ants.

[![Queen ant](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/225308-050-D105E599/Red-ant-queen-with-a-cluster-of-ant-eggs.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/225308-050-D105E599/Red-ant-queen-with-a-cluster-of-ant-eggs.jpg)

[Queen ant](https://cdn.britannica.com/08/225308-050-D105E599/Red-ant-queen-with-a-cluster-of-ant-eggs.jpg) A red ant queen with a cluster of her eggs.(more)

At certain times of the year, the winged males and virgin queens [fly](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fly-insect) into the air, where the queen mates with a single male. During the flight he transfers to her [seminal](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/seminal) receptacle all the [sperm](https://www.britannica.com/science/sperm) she will require for the rest of her life, which may be as long as 15 years. The males die soon afterward, and the fertilized queen establishes a new nest or takes over the current nest. Her wings then drop off, and the bulky wing muscles degenerate, providing nutritive materials from the breakdown of the [muscle](https://www.britannica.com/science/muscle) tissue. As soon as the wings have fallen, her ovaries become functional, and egg laying begins. In primitive species, the queen leaves the nest and forages for food for the larvae. In more advanced forms, the queen rarely leaves the nest. She feeds so-called nutrition eggs or other food stores within her own body to the first brood. The larvae that survive in the nest develop into dwarf workers, which [forage](https://www.britannica.com/plant/forage) outside the nest for food to nourish additional larvae.

[![Honey ant repletes](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/124713-050-F07C91FD/Honey-ant-repletes.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/124713-050-F07C91FD/Honey-ant-repletes.jpg)

[Honey ant repletes](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/124713-050-F07C91FD/Honey-ant-repletes.jpg) A type of highly specialized worker, a honey ant repletes ( _Myrmecocystus_ species) store honeydew in their distended abdomens for the colony to use.(more)

The essential work in the ant society—such as building the nest, feeding and tending the brood, and defending the nest—is performed by the workers, all of whom are female. The workers can be [differentiated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/differentiated) morphologically and physiologically as soldiers, outside workers, inside workers, and nest builders. In the division of labor among some ant forms, highly specialized types of [polymorphism](https://www.britannica.com/science/polymorphism-biology) have developed. The _Cryptocercus_ ants, for example, make nests in hollow stems of plants, then bore a circular entrance that remains under constant surveillance by special guards whose heads are modified into pluglike structures that fit the entrance. Each guard is relieved after several hours, and another guard takes its place; entrance guards are useless for other tasks. [Honey ant](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honey-ant) repletes are a special type of worker that are fed so much that the size of their abdomens is greatly increased. Unable to walk, they hang as living honey jugs from the ceiling of the nest, to be used as a food source when fresh food is [scarce](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/scarce).

There are approximately 10,000 species of ants. Ants occur nearly worldwide but are most numerous—both in numbers and in species—in tropical and subtropical regions.

Most ants live in nests, which may be located in the ground or under a rock or built aboveground and made of twigs, sand, or gravel. Many subterranean nests are quite extensive, with a multitude of tunnels and specialized chambers. [Carpenter ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/carpenter-ant) ( _Camponotus_) are large black ants common in North America that live in old logs and timbers. Some species live in trees or in the hollow stems of weeds. [Weaver ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/weaver-ant) (genus _Oecophylla_), found in the tropics of Africa and [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia), make nests of leaves and similar materials held together with silk secreted by the larvae. _Dolichoderus_, a [genus](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/genus) of ants that are found worldwide, glues together bits of animal feces for its nest. The widely distributed pharaoh ant ( _Monomarium pharaonis_), a small yellowish insect, builds its nest either in houses, when found in cool climates, or outdoors, when it occurs in warm climates.

## Food

The food of ants consists of both plant and animal substances. Many ants are generalists and utilize a wide range of organic substances for food. Worker ants forage daily, and collected food and water is brought to the larvae and mature ants in the nest. They frequently use scent marks, which they place on their pathways, to find their way back to the nest and direct other colony members to a food source.

[![Worker ants](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/189292-050-004B3614/swarm-ants-insect.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/189292-050-004B3614/swarm-ants-insect.jpg)

[Worker ants](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/189292-050-004B3614/swarm-ants-insect.jpg) A swarm of ants cooperating to collectively move a dead insect on the forest floor.(more)

Some ant species are hunters and scavengers. Bullet ants, for example, [forage](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/forage) for live [spiders](https://www.britannica.com/animal/spider-arachnid), [frogs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/frog), and [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect), including [grasshoppers](https://www.britannica.com/animal/grasshopper-insect), beetles, and [katydids](https://www.britannica.com/animal/long-horned-grasshopper), or their carcasses. Certain species, including those of the genus _Formica_, often eat the eggs and larvae of other ants or those of their own species in other nests. [Sahara desert ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Sahara-desert-ant) (genus _Cataglyphis_) scavenge for dead insects on the scorching sand and salt-pan terrain of the [Sahara](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sahara-desert-Africa); they can tolerate surface temperatures of 60 °C (140 °F) or higher for short periods, making them one of the most heat-tolerant groups of insects known.

Some species eat the liquid secretions of plants, either directly or indirectly from the bodies of other insects. Many ants collect [nectar](https://www.britannica.com/science/nectar) from floral or extrafloral [nectaries](https://www.britannica.com/science/nectary), and some [utilize](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/utilize) [resins](https://www.britannica.com/science/resin) and [saps](https://www.britannica.com/science/sap-plant-physiology). A number of ants, known as herder ants ( _Lasius niger_ and others), protect and carefully tend to herds of [aphids](https://www.britannica.com/animal/aphid), from which the ants collect [honeydew](https://www.britannica.com/science/honeydew) (a by-product of digestion secreted by certain aphids). The [honey ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honey-ant) ( _Myrmecocystus_, _Camponotus_, and others) store honeydew in the distended abdomens of specialized workers. Some genera ( _Leptothorax_) eat the honeydew that has fallen onto the surface of a leaf. The so-called Argentine ant ( _Iridomyrmex humilis_) and many [fire ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fire-ant) ( _Solenopsis_) also eat honeydew.

[Harvester ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/harvester-ant) ( _Messor_, _Pogonomyrmex_) store grass, seeds, or berries in the nest, whereas ants of the genus _Trachymyrmex_ of [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) eat only [fungi](https://www.britannica.com/science/fungus), which they [cultivate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultivate) in their nests. The Texas [leafcutter ant](https://www.britannica.com/animal/leafcutter-ant) ( _Atta texana_) is a [pest](https://www.britannica.com/science/pest-vermin) that often strips the leaves from plants to provide nourishment for its fungus gardens.

## Notable ant behaviors

The social behavior of ants, along with that of [honeybees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honeybee), is the most complex in the insect world. The group is also extremely [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse), with any number of foraging, nesting, and social behaviors.

[![Acacia ants mutualism](https://cdn.britannica.com/17/176917-050-8A99BB7D/acacia-ants-Mutualism-bullhorn-thorn-plant-shelter.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/17/176917-050-8A99BB7D/acacia-ants-Mutualism-bullhorn-thorn-plant-shelter.jpg)

[Acacia ants mutualism](https://cdn.britannica.com/17/176917-050-8A99BB7D/acacia-ants-Mutualism-bullhorn-thorn-plant-shelter.jpg) Mutualism between acacia ants ( _Pseudomyrmex ferruginea_) and the bullhorn acacia ( _Vachellia cornigera_), or swollen thorn acacia. The plant provides food and shelter to the ants, and the ants defend the plant against browsing animals.(more)

[Acacia ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/acacia-ant) ( _Pseudomyrmex ferruginea_) inhabit the [bullhorn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Vachellia-cornigera) acacia (or bullhorn wattle; _Vachellia cornigera_). The ants obtain food and shelter, and the acacia depends on the ants for protection from browsing animals, which the ants drive away. Neither member can survive successfully without the other, exemplifying obligative [mutualism](https://www.britannica.com/science/mutualism-biology).

Slave-making ants, of which there are many species, have a variety of methods for “enslaving” the ants of other species. The queen of _Bothriomyrmex decapitans_ of Africa, for example, allows herself to be dragged by _Tapinoma_ ants into their nest. She then bites off the head of the _Tapinoma_ queen and begins laying her own eggs, which are cared for by the “enslaved” _Tapinoma_ workers. Workers of the slave-making ant _Protomognathus americanus_ raid nests of _Temnothorax_ ants, stealing the latter’s pupae. The pupae are raised by _P. americanus_ to serve as slaves, and, because the _Temnothorax_ pupae become imprinted on the chemical odor of the slave-making ants, as adults the captive ants forage and routinely return to the slave-making ant nest.

Some species live in the nests of other species as [parasites](https://www.britannica.com/science/parasitism). In these species the [parasite](https://www.britannica.com/science/parasitism) larvae are given food and nourishment by the host workers. _Wheeleriella santschii_ is a parasite in the nests of _Monomorium salomonis_, the most common ant of northern Africa.

[Army ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/army-ant), of the subfamily Dorylinae, are nomadic and [notorious](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/notorious) for the destruction of plant and animal life in their path. The army ants of tropical America ( _[Eciton](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Eciton-army-ant)_), for example, travel in columns, eating insects and other invertebrates along the way. Periodically, the colony rests for several days while the queen lays her eggs. As the colony travels, the growing larvae are carried along by the workers. Habits of the [African driver ant](https://www.britannica.com/animal/driver-ant) ( _Dorylus_) are similar.

Did you know?

Scientists think the first ants began farming inside their colonies in South American rainforests in the early [Tertiary Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Tertiary-Period), some 55–60 million years ago. In contrast, humans began farming only about 15,000–10,000 years ago.

All of the nearly 300 members of the subtribe Attina, including certain species of the [genera](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/genera) _Atta_, _Acromyrmex_, _Cyphomyrmex_, _Sericomyrmex_, and _Trachymyrmex_, are farmers that intentionally cultivate [fungi](https://www.britannica.com/science/fungus) in their colonies to consume as food. One of the most well-known of these [mutualisms](https://www.britannica.com/science/mutualism-biology) is that of the leafcutter ant ( _Atta cephalotes_), which has evolved a highly developed and intricate farming system for a specific fungus, _Leucoagaricus gongylophorus_. The ants meticulously cut pieces of leaves from a variety of rainforest plants to bring back to special chambers in their underground nests. The leaves are then chewed into a pulp and serve as a substrate for the fungus. As the fungal crop grows, the worker ants weed out any competing fungi to protect their harvest and utilize antimicrobial substances to [inhibit](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibit) the growth of harmful pathogens. In return for this care, the fungus converts the pulped vegetation into food that sustains the entire ant colony, producing specialized cells (called gongylidia) that are consumed by the ant farmers. Given that the fungus completely relies on ants for its perpetuation and is not found on its own in the wild, some scientists describe it as an ant- [domesticated](https://www.britannica.com/science/domestication) species.

[Ant fungiculture](https://cdn.britannica.com/44/255944-050-434DDCE8/Leafcutter-ants-Atta-cephalotes-tend-their-fungus-garden.jpg) Leafcutter ants ( _Atta cephalotes_) tending their fungus garden at an exhibit at the Como Park Zoo and Conservatory in St. Paul, Minnesota. Adept fungus farmers, the ants physically and chemically shape their subterranean growth chambers to foster the growth of a specific fungus that serves as their primary food source. The gray- to tan-colored fungus grows on the chewed leaves the ants foraged for this purpose.(more)

Ask Anything

There are approximately 10,000 species of [ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ant) on Earth, with a wide range of diets and behaviors among them. Many ant species are opportunistic [omnivores](https://www.britannica.com/science/omnivore) and consume plant-based foods, such as [nectar](https://www.britannica.com/science/nectar), [resins](https://www.britannica.com/science/resin), and [saps](https://www.britannica.com/science/sap-plant-physiology), as well as animal-based foods, including live prey and scavenged carcasses, as they encounter them. The stilt-legged [Sahara desert ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Sahara-desert-ant), for example, commonly scavenge dead insects in the extreme heat of their harsh habitat. Others are more dominantly [carnivorous](https://www.britannica.com/science/carnivore-consumer) hunters. These include [bullet ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bullet-insect), known for their extremely painful bites, and [driver ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/driver-ant), which move en masse to overwhelm unfortunate living prey such as [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect), [spiders](https://www.britannica.com/animal/spider-arachnid), [frogs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/frog), and other small animals. Some ants even steal and eat the eggs and [larvae](https://www.britannica.com/science/larva) of other ants.

Other ants utilize animals more peaceably. Herder ants carefully manage and protect herds of [aphids](https://www.britannica.com/animal/aphid) to collect honeydew, a sugary secretion produced by the sap-sucking insects. Several ant species, known as [honey ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honey-ant), have specialized workers that store honeydew in their distended abdomens until needed by the colony in times of food scarcity.

Many ants are largely [herbivorous](https://www.britannica.com/science/herbivore). [Harvester ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/harvester-ant), for example, gather [seeds](https://www.britannica.com/science/seed-plant-reproductive-part) and fruits, storing them in their nests for future consumption. Another famous [mutualism](https://www.britannica.com/science/mutualism-biology) is seen in the relationship between acacia ants and bullhorn acacia trees. In exchange for food and shelter, the ants protect their host tree from other animal herbivores.

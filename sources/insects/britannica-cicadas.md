Top Questions


### How many species of cicadas are there and where are they found?

Cicadas form a large family of [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect), with over 3,000 species. Cicadas are found in tropical and temperate areas worldwide, including [deserts](https://www.britannica.com/science/desert), [grasslands](https://www.britannica.com/science/grassland), and [forests](https://www.britannica.com/science/forest).

### What is the loudest cicada species?

The shrill thorntree cicada ( _Brevisana brevis_) is the loudest cicada species and is thought to be the loudest of all [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect). The noisy insect is native to [Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Africa) and its songs can exceed 106 decibels.

### Do cicadas bite or sting?

Equipped only with sap sucking mouthparts, cicadas lack mandibles and thus do not bite. They also do not have the modified ovipositor (egg-laying organ) required for stinging. Indeed, stinging in insects is a phenomenon largely limited to certain [ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ant), [bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bee), and [wasps](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wasp), to which cicadas are unrelated.

### Why do some cicadas wait 17 years?

[Periodical cicadas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/periodical-cicada) (genus _Magicicada_) emerge as adults every 13 or 17 years in large numbers. Their long life cycle may have evolved, at least in part, to avoid highly specialized predators such as parasitic [wasps](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wasp), which tightly regulate the populations of many other insect species.

### Are cicadas harmful to anything?

Cicadas are largely harmless to humans and animals, though their [exoskeletons](https://www.britannica.com/science/exoskeleton-anatomy) can be a stomach irritant to pets that consume them. Although not ordinarily considered a pest, the females may damage young saplings during their egg laying if they are present in high numbers. Similarly, the underground [nymphs](https://www.britannica.com/science/nymph-entomology) feed on tree [roots](https://www.britannica.com/science/root-plant), and enormous numbers may harm young or vulnerable trees. However, the sap is taken from roots very slowly over months and years, and most trees survive.

**cicada**, (family Cicadidae), family of more than 3,000 species of sound-producing [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect). Cicadas are found in tropical and temperate areas worldwide and occur in [deserts](https://www.britannica.com/science/desert), [grasslands](https://www.britannica.com/science/grassland), and [forests](https://www.britannica.com/science/forest). Cicadas have long been used in folk medicines, as religious and [monetary](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/monetary) symbols, and as an important source of food for humans and many other organisms. The cicada appears in the mythology, literature, and music of many [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures), including those of some [Indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Indigenous) peoples in the Americas, and the males of certain Asian species have even been kept in cages for their melodious songs.

Cicadas are medium to large insects, ranging in size from 2 to 5 cm (0.8 to 2 inches). They characteristically have short antennae, two pairs of transparent membranous wings, prominent [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) eyes, and three simple eyes (ocelli) that form a triangle between the compound eyes. Equipped only with [sap](https://www.britannica.com/science/sap-plant-physiology) sucking mouthparts, cicadas lack mandibles and thus do not bite. The insects also do not have the modified ovipositor (egg-laying organ) required for stinging. Indeed, stinging in insects is a phenomenon largely limited to certain [ants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ant), [bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bee), and [wasps](https://www.britannica.com/animal/wasp), to which cicadas are unrelated. Major cicada species are generally easily recognized by differences in songs, behavior, and [morphology](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/morphology).

The shrill thorntree cicada ( _Brevisana brevis_), found in Africa, is thought to be the loudest insect in the world. Exceeding 106 decibels, the males’ songs are nearly as loud as a chainsaw (110 decibels).

Male cicadas produce loud noises by vibrating membranes ( [tymbals](https://www.britannica.com/science/timbal-organ)) near the base of the [abdomen](https://www.britannica.com/science/abdomen). Males of each species typically have three distinct [sound responses](https://www.britannica.com/science/animal-communication): a congregational song that is regulated by daily weather fluctuations and by songs produced by other males; a courtship song, usually produced prior to copulation; and a disturbance squawk, produced by individuals captured, held, or disturbed into flight. Most North American cicadas produce rhythmical ticks, buzzes, [whines](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/whines), or screams, although in some species the song is musical. Females lack tymbals, though some species use their wings to produce clicking or snapping sounds, often in response to the songs of the males.

## Life cycle

All cicada species have a life cycle that consists of three stages: egg, [nymph](https://www.britannica.com/science/nymph-entomology), and adult. Adult female cicadas usually lay their eggs in woody plant tissues. Newly hatched [nymphs](https://www.britannica.com/science/nymph-entomology) burrow into the ground where they suck juices from roots of [perennial](https://www.britannica.com/science/perennial) plants. Nymphs usually undergo five molts during the year or more required to reach maturity. When prompted to emerge—the biology of which is not well understood for long-lived species—mature nymphs often appear aboveground en masse and undergo their final molt on nearby trees. The dorsal line of their exoskeleton splits, and the winged adults emerge slowly through this opening, leaving behind hollow casts known as exuviae. The winged adults live for only a few weeks, just long enough to find a mate and lay eggs. Although not ordinarily considered a pest, the females, if numerous, may damage young saplings during their egg laying.

A large and [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) group, the cicada family [comprises](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprises) more than 500 genera worldwide. The [periodical cicadas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/periodical-cicada) ( _Magicicada_) are among the most fascinating and best-known cicadas. The genus contains seven North American species of [17-year cicadas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/17-year-cicada) (often erroneously called 17-year locusts) and [13-year cicadas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/13-year-cicada). In what are among the longest life cycles of any insects, periodical cicadas emerge in spring only every 13 or 17 years, with millions of insects materializing from the soil in a matter of weeks and dying shortly thereafter. These species occur in chronologically and geographically isolated broods.

To distinguish them from the periodical cicadas, a number of North American species are known as annual cicadas. These are seen every summer, though their [life cycles](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/life%20cycles) typically span 2–5 years. Annual cicadas include the dog-day cicadas (genus _Neotibicen_), scrub cicadas (genus _Diceroprocta_), several species of the genus _Neocicada_, and members of the genus _Okanagana_.

The nearly 60 species of the genus _Cicada_ are found in Europe, the [Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/Middle-East), and parts of Africa. The common, or [ash](https://www.britannica.com/plant/ash-tree), cicada ( _Cicada orni_) is one of the most familiar species in Europe. The [New Forest](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Forest-district-England) cicada ( _Cicadetta montana_) is the only cicada native to the United Kingdom, where it is considered an [endangered species](https://www.britannica.com/science/endangered-species).

The largest cicadas in the world are found in Asia. Members of the genus _Megapomponia_ are large-bodied insects native to [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia). Included is the empress cicada ( _Megapomponia imperatoria_), the most massive of all known cicada species, with a superlative body length of about 7 cm (2.8 inches). The only member of its genus, _Tacua speciosa_ can reach more than 5.5 cm (2 inches) in length. The insect is unusual in that its wings are [opaque](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/opaque) instead of transparent like those of other cicada species.

- What kinds of creatures do entomologists study?







Entomologists are zoologists who scientifically study insects, a group of arthropods characterized by a segmented body plan. Insects have three pairs of legs, exoskeletons, and bodies divided into three parts: head, thorax, and abdomen.



Entomologists study insect genetics, taxonomy, morphology, physiology, behavior, and ecology, including how insects benefit or harm humans. They also examine insect roles in ecosystems, such as pest control by dragonflies and organic matter decomposition by scavenger insects. Mayflies and stoneflies can be used to assess water quality. Some entomologists work in forensics, applying their knowledge in civil and criminal cases.

- How do insects differ from other animals in zoology?







Entomology is a branch of [zoology](https://www.britannica.com/science/entomology/Introduction#ref1) focused on the scientific study of insects. Insects are distinguished from other arthropods and animals by having a body divided into three major regions: the head, thorax, and abdomen. The [thorax](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect/Thorax#ref41286) has three pairs of legs in adults and usually one or two pairs of wings. The head bears the mouthparts, eyes, and a pair of antennae. The abdomen contains the digestive, excretory, and reproductive organs. Insects dominate the present-day land fauna with about one million described species, representing about three-fourths of all described animal species.

- Why are insects important for ecosystems?







Insects, a branch of zoology studied in [entomology](https://www.britannica.com/science/entomology/Introduction#ref1), play crucial roles in ecosystems. [Pollination](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect/Distribution-and-abundance#ref41253) is another vital function, with many plants relying on insects like [bees](https://www.britannica.com/science/Why-Are-Bees-Important/Introduction#ref1) for reproduction, which maintains plant biodiversity and supports various wildlife by providing food and shelter. Additionally, some insects act as [predators](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect/Distribution-and-abundance#ref41253) that control populations of other insects.

- How has the study of entomology helped humans in agriculture or medicine?







Entomology, the study of insects, significantly aids humans in both agriculture and medicine. In agriculture, it helps develop methods for [pest management](https://www.britannica.com/science/entomology/Introduction#ref1) that integrate chemical, biological, cultural, and sanitation strategies to control damage to agricultural products. Entomological studies also elucidate the roles insects play in ecosystems, such as pest control by predatory insects and the decomposition of organic matter by scavenger insects.



[Forensic entomology](https://www.britannica.com/science/forensic-science/Forensic-pathology#ref337283) assists police in determining the time of death by examining insect populations on corpses, as insects infest bodies at predictable rates. Additionally, insects can be analyzed to determine the presence of poisons in a body after decomposition.

**entomology**, branch of [zoology](https://www.britannica.com/science/zoology) dealing with the scientific study of [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect). The Greek word _entomon_, meaning “notched,” refers to the segmented body plan of the [insect](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect). The zoological categories of [genetics](https://www.britannica.com/science/genetics), [taxonomy](https://www.britannica.com/science/taxonomy), [morphology](https://www.britannica.com/science/morphology-biology), [physiology](https://www.britannica.com/science/physiology), behaviour, and [ecology](https://www.britannica.com/science/ecology) are included in this field of study. Also included are the applied aspects of economic entomology, which [encompasses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompasses) the harmful and [beneficial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/beneficial) impact of insects on humans and their activities. Entomology also plays an important role in studies of biodiversity and [assessment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/assessment) of environmental quality.

Throughout history the study of insects has intrigued great scientific minds. In the 4th century bce, the Greek philosopher and scientist [Aristotle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle) provided descriptions of insect anatomy, establishing the groundwork for modern entomology. [Pliny the Elder](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pliny-the-Elder) added to Aristotle’s list of [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon). The Italian naturalist [Ulisse Aldrovandi](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ulisse-Aldrovandi) published a major [treatise](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/treatise), _De Animalibus Insectis_ (“Of Insect Animals”) in 1602. With the aid of the newly developed [microscope](https://www.britannica.com/technology/microscope), the Dutch naturalist [Jan Swammerdam](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jan-Swammerdam) was able to observe the minute structures of many insect species. Modern insect classification began in the 18th century. The French biologist [René-Antoine Ferchault de Réaumur](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rene-Antoine-Ferchault-de-Reaumur) published the first of six volumes of _Mémoires pour servir à l’histoire des insectes_ (“Memoirs Serving as a History of Insects”) in 1734. [Carolus Linnaeus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Carolus-Linnaeus), in _Systema Naturae_ (10th ed., 1758), applied his system of binomial [nomenclature](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nomenclature) to organize the classification of insect species. Entomology emerged as a distinct field of study in the early 19th century, with the publication of such works as the eight-volume _British Entomology_ (1824–39), by [John Curtis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Curtis-British-entomologist-and-illustrator), and the founding of entomological societies in [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris) and London.

The body of knowledge gleaned from the study of insects has enabled modern economic entomologists to develop a wide range of methods for controlling insect [pests](https://www.britannica.com/science/pest-control). Some insects are perceived as threats to humans, both as agents of crop destruction and as disseminators of disease. Methods of [integrating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrating) pest management, which combine chemical, biological, cultural, and sanitation strategies, have been devised to control the damage done by insects to agricultural products. Benefits from insect studies include improvements in pest-management practices and advances in genetics research. Studies using the [vinegar fly](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Drosophila-melanogaster) ( _Drosophila melanogaster_) have established the foundation and techniques used in virtually all aspects of genetics research conducted today. Insects also have been used in biochemical, developmental, behavioral, environmental, and ecological studies. The many functions that insects perform in the ecosystem, such as the pest control that dragonflies and mantises provide as predators of other insects or the decomposition of organic matter that scavenger insects accelerate, have been elucidated by entomological study. Insects that inhabit streams and other freshwater habitats such as mayflies, caddisflies, and stoneflies are used as biotic indicators of water quality. Insects are also used by [forensic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/forensic) entomologists in a wide variety of legal situations that include both civil and criminal cases.

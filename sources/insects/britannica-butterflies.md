**butterfly**, (superfamily Papilionoidea), any of numerous species of [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect) belonging to multiple families. Butterflies, along with the [moths](https://www.britannica.com/animal/moth-insect) and the [skippers](https://www.britannica.com/animal/skipper-insect-Lepidoptera-order), make up the [insect](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect) order [Lepidoptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/lepidopteran). Butterflies are nearly [worldwide](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/worldwide) in their distribution. Although their [larvae](https://www.britannica.com/science/larva) may be destructive to certain plants, butterflies are major [pollinators](https://www.britannica.com/science/major-types-of-pollinators) of the world’s wild and domesticated [flowering plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/angiosperm), and all stages of their life cycle are important to local [food webs](https://www.britannica.com/science/food-web). For more detailed coverage, _see also_ [Lepidoptera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/lepidopteran) and [list of butterflies and moths](https://www.britannica.com/animal/list-of-butterflies-and-moths-2073110).

The wings, bodies, and legs, like those of moths, are covered with dustlike [scales](https://www.britannica.com/science/scale-zoology) that come off when the [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) is handled. Unlike [moths](https://www.britannica.com/animal/moth-insect), butterflies are active during the day and are usually brightly colored or strikingly patterned. Perhaps the most distinctive physical features of the butterfly are its club-tipped antennae and its habit of holding the wings vertically over the back when at rest. The lepidopteran [life cycle](https://www.britannica.com/science/life-cycle) has four stages: [egg](https://www.britannica.com/science/egg-biology), larva ( [caterpillar](https://www.britannica.com/science/caterpillar)), [pupa](https://www.britannica.com/science/pupa) (chrysalis), and adult (imago). The larvae and adults of most butterflies feed on [plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant), often only specific parts of specific types of plants.

## Butterfly declines and conservation

A [major study published in 2025](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adp4671) found that populations of butterflies across the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) are in serious decline, with total butterfly abundance having decreased by 22 percent from 2000 to 2020. The study found dramatic population drops for many individual species across all North American butterfly families: more than 100 species had declined by more than 50 percent, and more than 20 species had declined by more than 90 percent. Numerous other studies have documented regional declines throughout the world, indicating a widespread and serious problem. [Pervasive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Pervasive) [pesticide](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pesticide) use, including [insecticides](https://www.britannica.com/technology/insecticide) that directly kill or weaken butterflies and [herbicides](https://www.britannica.com/science/herbicide) that kill the plants they rely on, [habitat loss](https://www.britannica.com/science/habitat-loss), and [anthropogenic climate change](https://www.britannica.com/science/global-warming) are the major drivers of these losses. Access to pesticide-free [habitat](https://www.britannica.com/science/habitat-biology)—wild areas as well as native plant gardens at homes, schools, and businesses—with abundant host plants for caterpillars and [nectar](https://www.britannica.com/science/nectar) plants for adults can help stabilize and support struggling butterfly populations.

- What is pollination?







Pollination is the process of transferring pollen grains from a flower's stamen to its ovules, facilitating fertilization and seed production. This is essential for the reproduction of most wild plants and the production of many fruits and seed crops.



Flowering plants rely on external agents for pollen transport, including insects, wind, birds, mammals, and water. Animal pollinators, especially insects and bees, play a crucial role in ecosystems and agriculture by pollinating various fruit and vegetable crops. Declines in pollinator populations, such as bees, can have significant ecological and economic consequences.

- How do plants attract pollinators?







Plants attract pollinators through a variety of adaptations, including advertising and sexual deception.



  - **Coloration** The coloration and patterns of flowers attract pollinators and signal plant species. Flowers pollinated by insects often display yellow, blue, and ultraviolet patterns, which elicit a strong response in the insect eye. Nectar guides, which are darkly colored patterns near the flower's center, orient the insect toward the proper pollinating location.
  - **Scent** Many flowers produce odors from volatile secondary compounds, including aromatic phenolic compounds, that attract pollinators. Some plants, like bee orchids, mimic the scent, appearance, and texture of a female bee to lure male bees into "pseudocopulation." During this process, the flower releases pollen on the bee, which then pollinates the next flower the bee visits.
  - **Nectar** Nectar in flowers attracts pollinators such as insects, birds, and bats. Nectaries are located at the base of the flower stamens, which brings animal visitors into contact with the pollen to be transferred.

**pollination**, transfer of [pollen](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollen) grains from the [stamens](https://www.britannica.com/science/stamen) (the [flower](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) parts that produce them) to the [ovule-bearing](https://www.britannica.com/science/ovule) organs or to the [ovules](https://www.britannica.com/science/ovule) (seed precursors) themselves. In [gymnosperm plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/gymnosperm) such as [conifers](https://www.britannica.com/plant/conifer) and [cycads](https://www.britannica.com/plant/cycad), in which the ovules are exposed, the pollen is simply caught in a drop of fluid [secreted](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/secreted) by the [ovule](https://www.britannica.com/science/ovule). In [flowering plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/angiosperm), however, the ovules are contained within a hollow organ called the [pistil](https://www.britannica.com/science/pistil), and the pollen is deposited on the pistil’s receptive surface, the [stigma](https://www.britannica.com/science/stigma). There the pollen germinates and gives rise to a [pollen tube](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollen-tube), which grows down through the pistil toward one of the ovules in its base. In an act of [double fertilization](https://www.britannica.com/science/double-fertilization), one of the two sperm cells within the pollen tube fuses with the egg cell of the ovule, making possible the [development](https://www.britannica.com/science/biological-development) of an [embryo](https://www.britannica.com/science/embryo-human-and-animal), and the other cell combines with the two subsidiary sexual nuclei of the ovule, which initiates formation of a reserve food [tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/tissue), the [endosperm](https://www.britannica.com/science/endosperm). The growing ovule then transforms itself into a [seed](https://www.britannica.com/science/seed-plant-reproductive-part).

As a [prerequisite](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/prerequisite) for [fertilization](https://www.britannica.com/science/fertilization-reproduction), pollination is essential to the perpetuation of the vast majority of the world’s wild plants as well as to the [production](https://www.britannica.com/technology/agricultural-technology) of most [fruit](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body) and seed crops. It also plays an important part in programs designed to improve plants by [breeding](https://www.britannica.com/science/plant-breeding). Furthermore, studies of pollination are invaluable for understanding the evolution of flowering plants and their distribution in the world today. As sedentary organisms, plants usually must enlist the services of external agents for pollen transport. In flowering plants, these are (roughly in order of diminishing importance) insects, [wind](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind), birds, mammals, and water. _See also_ [major types of pollinators](https://www.britannica.com/science/major-types-of-pollinators).

## Types: [self-pollination](https://www.britannica.com/science/self-pollination) and [cross-pollination](https://www.britannica.com/science/cross-pollination)

![Cross-pollination](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/95388-050-3C841D57/process-cross-pollination-animal-pollinator.jpg)

[Cross-pollination](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/95388-050-3C841D57/process-cross-pollination-animal-pollinator.jpg) The process of cross-pollination using an animal pollinator.(more)

An egg cell in an ovule of a flower may be fertilized by a sperm cell derived from a pollen grain produced by that same flower or by another flower on the same [plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant), in either of which two cases fertilization is said to be due to self-pollination (autogamy); or, the sperm may be derived from pollen originating on a different plant individual, in which case the process is called cross-pollination (heterogamy). Both processes are common, but cross-pollination clearly has certain evolutionary advantages for the species: the seeds formed may [combine](https://www.britannica.com/technology/combine) the [hereditary](https://www.britannica.com/science/heredity-genetics) traits of both parents, and the resulting offspring generally are more varied than would be the case after self-pollination. In a changing [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment), some of the individuals resulting from cross-pollination still may be found capable of [coping](https://www.britannica.com/science/adaptation-biology-and-physiology) with their new situation, ensuring survival of the [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon), whereas the individuals resulting from self-pollination might all be unable to adjust. Self-pollination, or [selfing](https://www.britannica.com/science/self-fertilization), although foolproof in a stable environment, thus is an evolutionary cul-de-sac. There also is a more direct, visible difference between selfing and outbreeding (cross-pollination): in those species where both methods work, cross-pollination usually produces more, and better quality, seeds. A dramatic demonstration of this effect is found with hybrid corn (maize), a superior product that results from cross-breeding of several especially bred lines.

- What is an atmosphere?







The atmosphere is a gaseous envelope extending from a planet's surface into space, decreasing in density with distance due to the planet's gravitational pull. Some planetary bodies, like Mercury, have almost nonexistent atmospheres, while others, such as Venus, Earth, and Mars, retain theirs.



Earth's atmosphere is composed of 78.08% diatomic nitrogen, 20.95% diatomic oxygen, 0.93% argon, 0 to 4% water, and 0.04% carbon dioxide, with smaller amounts of inert gases and other compounds. It is essential for life, as it maintains water in its three phases. The atmosphere also acts like a greenhouse, trapping heat and maintaining Earth's temperature. Furthermore, the [ozone layer](https://www.britannica.com/list/16-questions-about-whats-in-the-sky-answered) protects life from harmful solar radiation.

- Why is the Earth's atmosphere called a 'gaseous envelope'?







The Earth's atmosphere is called a 'gaseous envelope' because it is a layer of gas and [aerosols](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere/Introduction#ref1) that surrounds the planet. This [mixture of gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/air/Introduction#ref1) is held in place by gravity and extends from the surface of the Earth outward into space. The density of the atmosphere decreases as you move further away from the Earth's surface.



The atmosphere is essential for life on Earth because it contains gases like oxygen and carbon dioxide that living organisms need to survive. It also contains water in its three states, solid, liquid, and gas, which is also essential for life. Additionally, the [ozone layer](https://www.britannica.com/list/16-questions-about-whats-in-the-sky-answered) in the atmosphere protects living things from the harmful effects of the sun's ultraviolet radiation.

- What gases make up Earth's atmosphere?







Earth's atmosphere is a mixture of gases held in place by gravity. The gases with steady concentrations include nitrogen (78.084%), oxygen (20.946%), argon (0.934%), neon (0.0018%), helium (0.000524%), methane (0.0002%), krypton (0.000114%), hydrogen (5e-05), nitrous oxide (5e-05), and xenon (8.7e-06).



Gases with variable concentrations include water vapor (0 to 7%), carbon dioxide (0.01 to 0.1%), ozone (0 to 0.01%), sulfur dioxide (0 to 0.0001%), and nitrogen dioxide (0 to 0.000002%). The atmosphere is divided into the [homosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere/Stratosphere-and-mesosphere#ref261450), where the composition is consistent, and the heterosphere, where gases separate based on molecular mass.

- How does the atmosphere protect life on Earth?







The atmosphere sustains life on Earth by acting as a protective blanket of gases and liquids.



The atmosphere contains:


\\* Steady gases such as [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen) and [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen)


\\* Variable gases such as [water vapor](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) and [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide)

The atmosphere also traps heat through the greenhouse effect, which is caused by gases such as carbon dioxide and [water vapor](https://www.britannica.com/science/water). This keeps Earth warm enough to support life.

- What are the main layers of Earth's atmosphere?







The Earth's atmosphere consists of distinct layers characterized by changes in temperature and altitude.



The main layers of the atmosphere, from the lowest to the highest, are:


\\* **Troposphere:** The lowest region, extending 6–11 miles (10–18 km) above the Earth's surface, contains most weather systems and clouds.


\\* **Stratosphere:** Located above the troposphere and up to about 30 miles (50 km), the temperature in this layer increases with height. The increase in temperature results in strong thermodynamic stability.


\\* **Mesosphere:** This region is above the stratosphere, reaching approximately 30 to 50 miles (50 to 80 km) above the Earth. In the mesosphere, temperatures decrease with increasing altitude.


\\* **Thermosphere:** In the thermosphere, which is above the mesosphere, temperatures increase with height and can range from 440 °F (227 °C) to 3,137 °F (1,725 °C). The ionosphere, a region with abundant ions, is part of the thermosphere.


\\* **Exosphere:** The outermost region of Earth's atmosphere, starting at about 300 miles (500 km). In this layer, molecular densities are low, making collisions between molecules very rare.

- How do humans affect the atmosphere or its composition?







Humans impact the atmosphere and its composition significantly. Activities like burning fossil fuels increase [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/www.britannica.com/science/air-pollution/The-global-reach-of-air-pollution#ref286164) levels, contributing to [global warming](https://www.britannica.com/science/www.britannica.com/science/air-pollution/The-global-reach-of-air-pollution#ref286164) and climate change. Deforestation also plays a role, as burning forests releases stored carbon back into the atmosphere.



Additionally, industrial processes emit trace gases like [nitrous oxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/www.britannica.com/list/5-notorious-greenhouse-gases) and fluorinated gases that trap heat and exacerbate the greenhouse effect. Certain air pollutants can also lead to [ozone depletion](https://www.britannica.com/science/www.britannica.com/science/ozone-layer/Introduction#ref298214) in the stratosphere, increasing the amount of harmful UV radiation reaching the Earth's surface. These alterations can have far-reaching consequences for ecosystems and human health.

**atmosphere**, the [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter) and [aerosol](https://www.britannica.com/science/aerosol) envelope that extends from the [ocean](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean), land, and [ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice)-covered surface of a [planet](https://www.britannica.com/science/planet) outward into space. The density of the atmosphere decreases outward, because the [gravitational attraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/gravity-physics) of the [planet](https://www.britannica.com/science/planet), which pulls the gases and aerosols (microscopic suspended particles of dust, soot, smoke, or chemicals) inward, is greatest close to the surface. Atmospheres of some planetary bodies, such as [Mercury](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mercury-planet), are almost nonexistent, as the primordial atmosphere has escaped the relatively low gravitational attraction of the planet and has been released into space. Other planets, such as [Venus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venus-planet), [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth), [Mars](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mars-planet), and the giant outer planets of the [solar system](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-system), have retained an atmosphere. In addition, Earth’s atmosphere has been able to contain [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) in each of its three phases (solid, liquid, and gas), which has been essential for the development of [life](https://www.britannica.com/science/life) on the planet.

The [evolution of Earth’s current atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/evolution-of-the-atmosphere-1703862) is not completely understood. It is thought that the current atmosphere resulted from a gradual release of gases both from the planet’s interior and from the metabolic activities of life-forms—as opposed to the primordial atmosphere, which developed by outgassing (venting) during the original formation of the planet. Current [volcanic](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcanism) gaseous emissions include water vapour (H2O), [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide) (CO2), sulfur dioxide (SO2), [hydrogen sulfide](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen-sulfide) (H2S), [carbon monoxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-monoxide) (CO), [chlorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/chlorine) (Cl), [fluorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/fluorine) (F), and diatomic [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen) (N2; consisting of two atoms in a single molecule), as well as traces of other substances. Approximately 85 percent of volcanic emissions are in the form of water vapour. In contrast, carbon dioxide is about 10 percent of the effluent.

During the early evolution of the atmosphere on Earth, water must have been able to exist as a liquid, since the oceans have been present for at least three billion years. Given that solar output four billion years ago was only about 60 percent of what it is today, enhanced levels of carbon dioxide and perhaps [ammonia](https://www.britannica.com/science/ammonia) (NH3) must have been present in order to retard the loss of [infrared radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/infrared-radiation) into space. The initial life-forms that evolved in this environment must have been anaerobic (i.e., surviving in the absence of oxygen). In addition, they must have been able to resist the biologically destructive [ultraviolet radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultraviolet-radiation) in sunlight, which was not absorbed by a layer of [ozone](https://www.britannica.com/science/ozone-layer) as it is now.

# [Radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiant-energy)

The temperature of the atmosphere and surface is influenced by [electromagnetic radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetic-radiation), and this radiation is traditionally divided into two types: [insolation](https://www.britannica.com/science/insolation) from the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun) and emittance from the surface and the atmosphere. Insolation is frequently referred to as shortwave radiation; it falls primarily within the ultraviolet and visible portions of the electromagnetic spectrum and consists predominantly of [wavelengths](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength) of 0.39 to 0.76 micrometres (0.00002 to 0.00003 inch). Radiation emitted from Earth is called long-wave radiation; it falls within the infrared portion of the spectrum and has typical wavelengths of 4 to 30 micrometres (0.0002 to 0.001 inch). Wavelengths of radiation emitted by a body depend on the temperature of the body, as specified by [Planck’s radiation law](https://www.britannica.com/science/Plancks-radiation-law). The Sun, with its surface temperature of about 6,000 kelvins (K; about 5,725 °C, or 10,337 °F), emits at a much shorter wavelength than does Earth, which has lower surface and atmospheric temperatures of about 250 to 300 K (−23 to 27 °C, or −9.4 to 80.6 °F).

A fraction of the incoming shortwave radiation is absorbed by atmospheric gases, including water vapour, and warms the air directly, but in the absence of [clouds](https://www.britannica.com/science/cloud-meteorology) most of this energy reaches the surface. The [scattering](https://www.britannica.com/science/scattering) of a fraction of the shortwave radiation—particularly of the shortest wavelengths by air molecules in a process called [Rayleigh scattering](https://www.britannica.com/science/Rayleigh-scattering)—produces Earth’s blue skies.

When tall thick clouds are present, a large percentage (up to about 80 percent) of the insolation is reflected back into space. (The fraction of reflected shortwave radiation is called the cloud [albedo](https://www.britannica.com/science/albedo).) Of the solar radiation reaching Earth’s surface, some is reflected back into the atmosphere. Values of the surface albedo range as high as 0.95 for fresh snow to 0.10 for dark, organic soils. On land, this reflection occurs entirely at the surface. In water, however, albedo depends on the angle of the Sun’s rays and the depth of the water column. If the Sun’s rays strike the water surface at an oblique angle, albedo may be higher than 0.85; if these rays are more direct, only a small portion, perhaps as low as 0.02, is reflected, while the rest of the insolation is scattered within the water column and absorbed. Shortwave radiation penetrates a volume of water to significant depths (up to several hundred metres) before the insolation is completely attenuated. The heating by solar radiation in water is distributed through a depth, which results in smaller temperature changes at the surface of the water than would occur with the same insolation over an equal area of land.

![Earth's axis and sunlight](https://cdn.britannica.com/35/166935-050-EEE8D66A/change-Earth-seasons-sunlight-amount-surface-latitudes.jpg)

[Earth's axis and sunlight](https://cdn.britannica.com/35/166935-050-EEE8D66A/change-Earth-seasons-sunlight-amount-surface-latitudes.jpg) The primary cause of Earth's seasons is the change in the amount of sunlight reaching the surface at various latitudes over the course of a year. Because Earth is tilted on its axis with respect to the plane of its orbit around the Sun, different parts of its surface are in direct (overhead) sunlight at different times of the year. (more)

The amount of [solar radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-radiation) reaching the surface depends on latitude, time of year, time of day, and orientation of the land surface with respect to the Sun. In the Northern Hemisphere north of 23°30′, for example, solar insolation at local noon is less on slopes facing the north than on land oriented toward the south.

Solar radiation is made up of direct and diffuse radiation. Direct shortwave radiation reaches the surface without being absorbed or scattered from its line of propagation by the intervening atmosphere. The image of the Sun’s disk as a sharp and distinct object represents that portion of the solar radiation that reaches the viewer directly. Diffuse radiation, in contrast, reaches the surface after first being scattered from its line of propagation. On an overcast day, for example, the Sun’s disk is not visible, and all of the shortwave radiation is diffuse.

Long-wave radiation is emitted by the atmosphere and propagates both upward and downward. According to the [Stefan-Boltzmann law](https://www.britannica.com/science/Stefan-Boltzmann-law), the total amount of long-wave energy emitted is proportional to the fourth power of the temperature of the emitting material (e.g., the ground surface or the atmospheric layer). The magnitude of this radiation reaching the surface depends on the temperature at the height of emission and the amount of [absorption](https://www.britannica.com/science/absorption-physics) that takes place between the height of emission and the surface. A larger fraction of the long-wave radiation is absorbed when the intervening atmosphere holds large amounts of water vapour and carbon dioxide. Clouds with liquid water concentrations near 2.5 grams per cubic metre absorb almost 100 percent of the long-wave radiation within a depth of 12 metres (40 feet) into the cloud. Clouds with lower liquid water concentrations require greater depths before complete absorption is attained (e.g., a cloud with a water content of 0.05 gram per cubic metre requires about 600 metres \[about 2,000 feet\] for complete absorption). Clouds that are at least this thick emit long-wave radiation from their bases downward to Earth’s surface. The amount of long-wave radiation emitted corresponds to the temperature of the lowest levels of the cloud. (Clouds with warmer bases emit more long-wave radiation downward than colder clouds.)

## [Conduction](https://www.britannica.com/science/thermal-conduction)

The magnitude of [heat](https://www.britannica.com/science/heat) flux by conduction below a surface depends on the thermal conductivity and the vertical gradient of temperature in the material beneath the surface. Soils such as dry [peat](https://www.britannica.com/technology/peat), which has very low thermal conductivity (i.e., 0.06 watt per metre per K), permit little heat flux. In contrast, [concrete](https://www.britannica.com/technology/concrete-building-material) has a thermal conductivity about 75 times as large (i.e., 4.60 watts per metre per K) and allows substantial heat flux. In water, the thermal conductivity is relatively unimportant, since, in contrast to land surfaces, insolation extends to substantial depths in the water; in addition, water can be mixed vertically.

## [Convection](https://www.britannica.com/science/convection)

This process is referred to as free convection.(more)

2 of 2](https://cdn.britannica.com/12/113712-004-8E0090CE/air-conditions-atmosphere-weather-climate.jpg)

[rising air in a stable atmosphere](https://cdn.britannica.com/12/113712-004-8E0090CE/air-conditions-atmosphere-weather-climate.jpg) A rising parcel of air cools faster than the environment that surrounds it, causing that parcel to descend over time.(more)

Vertical mixing (convection) occurs in the atmosphere as well as in bodies of water. This process of mixing is also referred to as [turbulence](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmospheric-turbulence). It is a mechanism of heat flux that occurs in the atmosphere in two forms. When the surface is substantially warmer than the overlying air, mixing will spontaneously occur in order to redistribute the heat. This process, referred to as [free convection](https://www.britannica.com/science/free-convection), occurs when the [environmental lapse rate](https://www.britannica.com/science/normal-lapse-rate) (the rate of change of an atmospheric variable, such as temperature or density, with increasing altitude) of temperature decreases at a rate greater than 1 °C per 100 metres (approximately 1 °F per 150 feet). This rate is called the [adiabatic lapse rate](https://www.britannica.com/science/adiabatic-process) (the rate of temperature change occurring within a rising or descending air parcel). In the ocean, the temperature increase with depth that results in free convection is dependent on the temperature, salinity, and depth of the water. For example, if the surface has a temperature of 20 °C (68 °F) and a salinity of 34.85 parts per thousand, an increase in temperature with depth of greater than about 0.19 °C per km (0.55 °F per mile) just below in the upper layers of the ocean will result in free convection. In the atmosphere, the temperature profile with height determines whether free convection occurs or not. In the ocean, free convection depends on the temperature and salinity profile with depth. Colder and more saline conditions in a surface parcel of water, for example, make it more likely for that parcel to sink spontaneously and thus become part of the process of free convection.

Mixing can also occur because of the [shear stress](https://www.britannica.com/science/shear-stress) of the wind on the surface. Shear stress is the pulling force of a fluid moving in one direction as it passes close to a fluid or object moving in another. As a result of surface [friction](https://www.britannica.com/science/friction), the average wind velocity at Earth’s surface must be zero unless that surface is itself moving, such as in rivers or ocean currents. [Winds](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind) above the surface decelerate when the vertical wind shear (the change in wind velocity at differing altitudes) becomes large enough to result in vertical mixing. The process by which heat and other atmospheric properties are mixed as a result of [wind shear](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind-shear) is called [forced convection](https://www.britannica.com/science/forced-convection). Free and forced convection are also called convective and mechanical turbulence, respectively. This convection occurs as either sensible turbulent heat flux (heat directly transported to or from a surface) or [latent turbulent heat flux](https://www.britannica.com/science/latent-heat) (heat used to evaporate water from a surface). When this mixing does not occur, wind speeds are weak and change little with time; plumes from power-plant stacks within this layer, for example, spread very little in the vertical and remain in close proximity to the stacks.

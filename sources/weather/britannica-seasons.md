**season**, any of four divisions of the [year](https://www.britannica.com/science/year) according to consistent annual changes in the [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather). The seasons—winter, spring, [summer](https://www.britannica.com/science/summer-season), and autumn—are commonly regarded in the Northern Hemisphere as beginning respectively on the [winter](https://www.britannica.com/science/winter) [solstice](https://www.britannica.com/science/solstice), December 21 or 22; on the vernal [equinox](https://www.britannica.com/science/equinox-astronomy), March 20 or 21; on the [summer solstice](https://www.britannica.com/science/summer-solstice-astronomy), June 21 or 22; and on the [autumnal equinox](https://www.britannica.com/science/autumnal-equinox), September 22 or 23 (at the [equinoxes](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/equinoxes), the [days](https://www.britannica.com/science/day) and nights are equal in length; at the [winter solstice](https://www.britannica.com/science/winter-solstice) the [day](https://www.britannica.com/science/day) is the year’s shortest, and at the summer solstice it is the year’s longest). In the Southern Hemisphere, summer and winter are reversed, as are spring and [fall](https://www.britannica.com/science/autumn-season).

Outside the tropics and the polar regions, the essential characteristic of the annual cycle is a temperature oscillation between a single maximum and a single minimum. This oscillation results from the annual variation in the angle at which the [Sun’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun) rays reach [Earth’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth) surface and from the annual variation in the duration of [sunlight](https://www.britannica.com/science/sunlight-solar-radiation) on Earth’s surface each day. As Earth moves in its orbit around the Sun, its [axis](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/axis) maintains a nearly constant orientation in space, inclined about 66°33′ to the orbital plane. During the six-month half of each orbit when the [North Pole](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Pole) is inclined toward the Sun, a point in the Northern Hemisphere receives the Sun’s rays at an angle closer to 90° than does a point in the Southern Hemisphere; this causes greater heating and more hours of daylight in the Northern Hemisphere than in the Southern Hemisphere. During the other six months, these conditions are reversed.

In polar [latitudes](https://www.britannica.com/science/latitude) the seasons consist of a short summer and a long winter; this division is based primarily on sunlight, as there is [continuous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/continuous) darkness all winter and continuous daylight or twilight all summer. In low latitudes, where the range of the annual insolation (receipt of solar radiation) and temperature cycle is very small, seasonal weather variations are based largely on rainy and dry periods. These moisture variations result from the movements of the [intertropical convergence zone](https://www.britannica.com/science/intertropical-convergence-zone), a narrow belt of abundant [precipitation](https://www.britannica.com/science/precipitation) that encircles Earth near the Equator. It shifts north and south seasonally with the Sun and causes the areas it crosses to have alternating wet and dry seasons; those regions very near the Equator that are crossed twice each year by this belt have two wet and two dry seasons.

Seasons occur due to the tilt of Earth’s axis, which is approximately 23.5°. Because of this tilt, each hemisphere receives different amounts of sunlight through the year. The Earth’s distance from the Sun, even though it changes slightly over a year, is not a factor in the change of seasons.

When a hemisphere leans toward the Sun, the Sun’s rays hit it more directly, leading to longer days and increased absorption of solar energy, producing the warmer temperatures of summer. The hemisphere facing away from the Sun receives less direct sunlight, resulting in winter.

Latitude also influences how seasons are experienced. The Equator is lit more consistently throughout the year because the Earth bulges outward somewhat there. Since sunlight strikes equatorial regions more steadily year-round, the variation in temperature and, therefore, in seasons in these regions is less pronounced. In contrast, higher latitudes experience more pronounced seasonal changes due to the greater difference in solar angle between summer and winter. This is why tropical regions have less temperature fluctuation, while temperate regions have more distinct seasons.

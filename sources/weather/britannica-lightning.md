### What causes lightning?

Lightning is a visible electrical discharge from a [cloud](https://www.britannica.com/science/cloud-meteorology). This happens when there is an imbalance of charges between a region of the cloud and another surface (usually the ground, a building, another region of the same cloud, or another cloud) that is significant enough to break through [air](https://www.britannica.com/science/air) resistance.

### How dangerous is lightning?

Lightning is very dangerous. Lightning strikes on people, though comparatively rare, can cause conditions ranging from [amnesia](https://www.britannica.com/science/amnesia) to [cardiac arrest](https://www.britannica.com/science/cardiac-arrest). Lightning can also cause the surrounding air to heat up to 27,700 degrees Celsius (almost 50,000 degrees Fahrenheit), often setting nearby objects on fire. Lightning causes thousands of fires in the U.S. every year.

### What is ball lightning?

[Ball lightning](https://www.britannica.com/science/ball-lightning) is a mysterious phenomenon that observers describe as a levitating sphere of [electricity](https://www.britannica.com/science/electricity) or [plasma](https://www.britannica.com/science/plasma-state-of-matter), usually seen during [thunderstorms](https://www.britannica.com/science/thunderstorm). One theory proposes that ball lightning is caused by a reaction between [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) and lightning-stricken soil, creating plasma. Some scientists are skeptical about the existence of ball lightning.

### What are the chances of being struck by lightning?

The odds that one will be struck by lightning in the U.S. during one’s lifetime are about [1 in 15,300](https://www.weather.gov/safety/lightning-odds). On average, 270 people in the U.S. are struck per year, and only about 10 percent of that number die from the event. Around the world, approximately 2,000 people are struck by lightning every year.

### Can lightning strike the same place twice?

There is nothing preventing lightning from striking the same place twice. While the saying “Lightning never strikes the same place twice” refers to the rationale that rare events are unlikely to happen again, lightning striking the same location multiple times can be quite common, especially at [lightning rods](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lightning-rod) on top of skyscrapers.

[electrical charge distribution in a thunderstorm](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/7578-050-1A948331/charges-regions-thundercloud-charge-lightning-discharge-others.jpg) When the electrical charges become sufficiently separated in a thundercloud, with some regions acquiring a negative charge and others a positive, a discharge of lightning becomes likely. About one-third of lightning flashes travel from the cloud to the ground; most of these originate in negatively charged regions of the cloud.(more)

Lightning occurs when regions of excess positive and negative charge develop within the cloud. Typically, there is a large volume of positive charge in the upper regions of the cloud, a large negative charge in the centre, and a small positive charge in the lower regions. These charges reside on [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) drops, [ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice) particles, or both.

[![triggered lightning](https://cdn.britannica.com/99/7599-050-76BF8A49/lightning-discharge-presence-tower-Lugano-Switzerland-Mount.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/99/7599-050-76BF8A49/lightning-discharge-presence-tower-Lugano-Switzerland-Mount.jpg)

[triggered lightning](https://cdn.britannica.com/99/7599-050-76BF8A49/lightning-discharge-presence-tower-Lugano-Switzerland-Mount.jpg) Triggered lightning. This discharge is triggered by the presence of the tall tower atop Mount San Salvatore, near Lugano, Switzerland.(more)

Cloud-to-ground lightning is initiated by a preliminary breakdown process within the cloud, typically between the centre region of negative charge and the small positive charge below it. This process creates a channel of partially [ionized](https://www.britannica.com/science/ionization) air—air in which neutral [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) and [molecules](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) have been converted to electrically charged ones. Next, a stepped leader (initial lightning stroke) forms and propagates downward, following channels created by the preliminary breakdown process. The leader is highly branched in the direction of its propagation. Most leader channels are negatively charged. When the stepped leader nears the ground, an upward, connecting discharge of opposite polarity rises and meets it at a point typically about 30 metres (100 feet) above the ground. When the junction is complete, the cloud is effectively connected to the ground, and a very bright return stroke propagates back to the cloud at a speed about one-third the speed of light, following the leader channel. A typical lightning flash to the ground contains three or four leader-return stroke sequences in rapid succession. Occasionally, when there is a strike to a mountain or tall building, the first leader will start at the ground and propagate upward.

The [potential](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-potential) difference between cloud and ground is of the order of 10 to 100 million [volts](https://www.britannica.com/science/volt-unit-of-measurement), and the peak currents in return strokes to negative leaders are typically about 30,000 [amperes](https://www.britannica.com/science/ampere). The peak temperatures in the return-stroke channel are on the order of 30,000 °C (50,000 °F). The entire process is very rapid; the leader stroke reaches the ground in about 30 milliseconds, and the return stroke reaches the centre of the cloud in about 100 microseconds. During this stage, approximately 105 [joules](https://www.britannica.com/science/joule) of energy per metre are dissipated within the lightning channel. This sudden dissipation splits air molecules in the channel—principally those of [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen), [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen), and water—into their respective atoms, and, on average, one [electron](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) is removed from each atom. The conversion from neutral air molecules to a completely [ionized](https://www.britannica.com/science/ion-physics) [plasma](https://www.britannica.com/science/plasma-state-of-matter) occurs in a few microseconds.

[lightning and thunder](https://cdn.britannica.com/34/24034-050-07C1B2DC/chart-lightning-flash-thunder-kilometre-mile-distance.jpg)(Top) As shown in the chart, the elapsed time between seeing a flash of lightning and hearing the thunder is roughly three seconds for each kilometre, or five seconds for each mile. (Bottom) An observer's relative distance from the main lightning channel and its secondary branches determines whether thunder is heard to start with a sudden clap or a softer rumbling.(more)

[Thunder](https://www.britannica.com/science/thunder) is produced by rapid heating of the air in the lightning channel and a consequent increase in [air pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmospheric-pressure). The pressure produced from the stroke plasma, which is much greater than the pressure of the surrounding [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere), causes the channel to expand at supersonic speeds, which ultimately produces a sound wave heard as thunder. The claps, rolls, and rumbles that characterize the sound of thunder are produced by the complex geometry and tortuosity of the lightning channel as well as the effects of the atmosphere and local topography on [sound](https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics) propagation.

Lightning is a significant [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather) hazard and occurs at an average rate of 50 to 100 discharges per second worldwide. [Lightning rods](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lightning-rod) and metallic conductors can be used to protect a structure by intercepting and diverting the lightning current into the ground as harmlessly as possible. When lightning is likely to occur, people are advised to stay indoors or in a car, away from open [doors](https://www.britannica.com/technology/door) and [windows](https://www.britannica.com/technology/window), and to avoid contact with any electrical appliances or [plumbing](https://www.britannica.com/technology/plumbing) that might be exposed to the outside [environment](https://www.britannica.com/science/environment).

The visible flash of a [lightning](https://www.britannica.com/science/lightning-meteorology) strike travels at the [speed of light](https://www.britannica.com/science/speed-of-light) (670 million miles per hour), while the electrical discharge itself travels more slowly, roughly around 200 million miles per hour (about 322 million km per hour). However, the actual speed of the discharge varies, depending on the stage.

The initial stage of a lightning strike, known as the stepped leader, moves toward the ground at an average speed of about 1.5 x 105 meters per second, which is roughly one two-thousandth the speed of light. This is the phase where the lightning channel is being established. Once the stepped leader connects with an upward discharge from the ground, the return stroke occurs. This is the bright flash that typically is associated with lightning. The return stroke travels up the established channel at a speed of approximately 1 x 108 meters per second, or about one-third the speed of light.

The [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) involved in a lightning strike is immense. During the return-stroke stage, approximately 105 [joules](https://www.britannica.com/science/joule) of energy per meter are dissipated within the lightning channel. Temperatures in the channel can reach up to 30,000 °C (50,000 °F), which is about five times hotter than the surface of the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun). The air near the heat of the channel expands quickly, producing the sound wave heard as [thunder](https://www.britannica.com/science/thunder).

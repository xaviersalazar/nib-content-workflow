- What is an aurora?







[Auroras](https://www.britannica.com/science/aurora-atmospheric-phenomenon) are luminous atmospheric phenomena primarily in the high latitudes of both hemispheres. In the Northern Hemisphere, they are called aurora borealis (northern lights), and in the Southern Hemisphere, aurora australis (southern lights).



Auroras are caused by the interaction of energetic particles of the [solar wind](https://www.britannica.com/story/what-causes-the-northern-and-southern-lights) with atoms in the upper atmosphere. The energetic particles, released by coronal mass ejections and solar flares, are driven outward by the solar wind. Some electrons are captured by Earth’s magnetic field and conducted along magnetic field lines towards the magnetic poles. These electrons collide with oxygen and nitrogen atoms, causing them to emit radiation at various wavelengths, creating the colors of the aurora.

- Why are auroras often seen near the North and South Poles?







Auroras, known as aurora borealis in the Northern Hemisphere and aurora australis in the Southern Hemisphere, are frequently observed near the North and South Poles due to the Earth's magnetic field. High-energy particles from the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/story/what-causes-the-northern-and-southern-lights) travel via solar wind and strike Earth's magnetic field, following the field lines down into the atmosphere at the magnetic poles.



These solar particles collide with [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/story/what-causes-the-northern-and-southern-lights) and [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/story/what-causes-the-northern-and-southern-lights) atoms, causing them to emit radiation at various wavelengths, producing the auroral colors. The auroras are confined to high latitudes in oval-shaped zones around Earth's magnetic poles.

- What causes the colorful lights of an aurora?







The colorful lights of an [aurora](https://www.britannica.com/science/aurora-atmospheric-phenomenon/Introduction#ref1) are caused by the interaction of energetic particles from the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun/Solar-terrestrial-effects#ref54151) with atoms in the Earth's upper atmosphere.



Here's a breakdown:


\\* The sun's corona sends a [solar wind](https://www.britannica.com/story/what-causes-the-northern-and-southern-lights) of high-energy particles (electrons and protons) towards Earth.


\\* These particles interact with Earth's magnetic field and are directed towards the North and South magnetic poles.


\\* The energetic particles collide with oxygen and nitrogen atoms, stripping away electrons and creating ions in excited states.


\\* When these ions return to their normal state, they emit radiation at various wavelengths, producing the colors of the aurora. Oxygen produces red or green light, while nitrogen produces green and purple light.

- What is the difference between the Northern Lights and the Southern Lights?







The [aurora borealis](https://www.merriam-webster.com/wordplay/history-of-words-on-natural-lights/aurora) (Northern Lights) and [aurora australis](https://www.merriam-webster.com/wordplay/history-of-words-on-natural-lights/aurora) (Southern Lights) are dazzling light displays in the night sky, especially at high latitudes, and are atmospheric phenomena.



Both occur when high-energy particles from the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/story/what-causes-the-northern-and-southern-lights) strike Earth’s magnetic field and collide with oxygen and nitrogen atoms in the atmosphere. These collisions cause the atoms to emit light of various colors, like green, purple, and red. The auroras are similar light shows that appear as vibrant, rippling curtains, waving arcs, shimmering bands, and shooting rays.

- How do solar storms affect the appearance of auroras?







Solar storms, such as [coronal mass ejections](https://www.britannica.com/science/coronal-mass-ejection) (CMEs), significantly influence auroras. CMEs are large eruptions from the Sun's corona that send plasma (protons and electrons) toward Earth.



When these energetic particles reach Earth, they interact with the [geomagnetic field](https://www.britannica.com/science/geomagnetic-field), causing disturbances. This interaction leads to geomagnetic storms, which can intensify auroral displays, making them visible at lower latitudes than normal. The collision of charged particles with oxygen and nitrogen atoms in Earth's atmosphere results in the emission of light at various wavelengths, producing the characteristic colors of the aurora.



During periods of intense solar activity, auroras can extend to middle latitudes, with the aurora borealis seen as far south as 40° latitude in the United States. The most intense auroral displays have been recorded during significant geomagnetic storms, with auroras visible as far south as the tropics.

- What scientific tools do researchers use to study auroras?







Researchers use a variety of scientific tools to study auroras, which are luminous displays in the sky caused by interactions between solar wind particles and the Earth's atmosphere. These tools help in observing and analyzing different aspects of auroras:



  - **Telescopes:** Telescopes gather radiant energy and focus it on detectors to study celestial phenomena. They are calibrated for sensitivity and spectral response, which allows for the identification of radiation emission processes.

  - **Spectrometers:** Spectrometers analyze the radiation from auroras to identify the elements and processes responsible for their emission. X-ray spectrometers are also used to determine crystal structure and composition.

  - **Rockets and Spacecraft:** Rockets and spacecraft carry scientific instruments above Earth's atmosphere to detect and analyze electromagnetic radiation, which is mostly blocked by the atmosphere. Satellites equipped with ultraviolet detector systems have provided a means for studying solar activity.

**aurora**, [luminous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/luminous) phenomenon of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth)’s upper [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere) that occurs primarily in high latitudes of both hemispheres; in the Northern Hemisphere auroras are called aurora borealis, aurora polaris, or [northern lights](https://www.britannica.com/science/northern-lights-atmospheric-phenomenon), and in the Southern Hemisphere they are called aurora australis or [southern lights](https://www.britannica.com/science/southern-lights).

A brief treatment of auroras follows. For full treatment, _see_ [ionosphere and magnetosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/ionosphere-and-magnetosphere).

[auroral oval](https://cdn.britannica.com/10/76810-050-17353B61/image-oval-Earth-North-Polar-ultraviolet-light-February-1996.jpg) Earth's full North Polar auroral oval, in an image taken in ultraviolet light by the U.S. Polar spacecraft over northern Canada, April 6, 1996. Polar, launched in February 1996, was designed to further scientists' understanding of how plasma energy contained in the solar wind interacts with Earth's magnetosphere.(more)

Auroras are caused by the interaction of energetic particles ( [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) and [protons](https://www.britannica.com/science/proton-subatomic-particle)) of the [solar wind](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-wind) with [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) of the upper [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere). Such interaction is confined for the most part to high latitudes in [oval](https://www.britannica.com/science/auroral-oval)-shaped zones that surround [Earth’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth) [magnetic poles](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-pole) and maintain a more or less fixed orientation with respect to the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun). During periods of low solar activity, the auroral zones shift poleward. During periods of intense solar activity, auroras occasionally extend to the middle latitudes; for example, the aurora borealis has been seen as far south as 40° latitude in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States). Auroral emissions typically occur at [altitudes](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/altitudes) of about 100 km (60 miles); however, they may occur anywhere between 80 and 250 km (about 50 to 155 miles) above Earth’s surface.

Auroras take many forms, including luminous curtains, arcs, bands, and patches. The uniform arc is the most stable form of aurora, sometimes persisting for hours without noticeable variation. However, in a great display, other forms appear, commonly undergoing dramatic variation. The lower edges of the arcs and folds are usually much more sharply defined than the upper parts. Greenish rays may cover most of the sky poleward of the magnetic [zenith](https://www.britannica.com/science/zenith-astronomy), ending in an arc that is usually folded and sometimes edged with a lower red border that may ripple like drapery. The display ends with a poleward retreat of the auroral forms, the rays gradually [degenerating](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/degenerating) into diffuse areas of white [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light).

Auroras receive their [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) from charged particles traveling between the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun) and [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth) along bundled ropelike magnetic fields. [Electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) and other charged particles, which are released by [coronal mass ejections](https://www.britannica.com/science/coronal-mass-ejection), [solar flares](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-flare), and other emanations from the Sun, are driven outward by the [solar wind](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-wind). Some electrons are captured by Earth’s magnetic field ( _see_ [geomagnetic field](https://www.britannica.com/science/geomagnetic-field#ref9347)) and conducted along magnetic [field lines](https://www.britannica.com/science/line-of-force) downward toward the magnetic poles. [Alfvén waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/Alfven-wave)—which are generated in the dayside and nightside regions of the [magnetosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetosphere) and in the region of the magnetosphere called the magnetotail—push these electrons along and accelerate them up to 72.4 million km (45 million miles) per hour. They collide with [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) and [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen) atoms, knocking away electrons from these atoms to leave [ions](https://www.britannica.com/science/ion-physics) in excited states. These [ions](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/ions) emit [radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiation) at various [wavelengths](https://www.britannica.com/science/wavelength), creating the characteristic colours (red or greenish blue) of the aurora.

The auroras are produced by the interaction of the planet's powerful magnetic field and particles in its upper atmosphere.(more)

In addition to Earth, other [planets](https://www.britannica.com/science/planet) in the [solar system](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-system) that have atmospheres and substantial magnetic fields—i.e., [Jupiter](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jupiter-planet), [Saturn](https://www.britannica.com/place/Saturn-planet), [Uranus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Uranus-planet), and [Neptune](https://www.britannica.com/place/Neptune-planet)—display auroral activity on a large scale. Auroras also have been observed on Jupiter’s moon [Io](https://www.britannica.com/place/Io-satellite-of-Jupiter), where they are produced by the interaction of Io’s atmosphere with Jupiter’s powerful [magnetic field](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-field).

**magnetosphere**, region in the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere) where magnetic phenomena and the high atmospheric conductivity caused by [ionization](https://www.britannica.com/science/ionization) are important in determining the behaviour of charged particles.

The [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth), in contrast to Mars and Venus, has a significant surface [magnetic field](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-field) (approximately 0.5 gauss), which, like its gravitational [field](https://www.britannica.com/science/field-physics), becomes weaker as the distance from the centre of the Earth increases. In the direction of the Sun, at approximately 10 Earth radii (almost 65,000 km, or 40,000 miles), the magnetic field is so weak that the pressure associated with particles escaping from the Earth’s gravity is comparable to the opposing pressure associated with the [solar wind](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-wind)—the flux mainly of protons and electrons escaping from the Sun’s gravitational field. This [equilibrium](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/equilibrium) region, with a characteristic thickness of 100 km (60 miles), is called the [magnetopause](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetopause) and marks the outer boundary of the magnetosphere. The lower boundary of the magnetosphere is several hundred kilometres above the Earth’s surface.

On the night side, or the side away from the Sun, the forces associated with the magnetic field and the solar wind are parallel, and thus the magnetosphere extends a considerable distance, possibly even several astronomical units (one [astronomical unit](https://www.britannica.com/science/astronomical-unit) is the average distance between the Earth and the Sun, about 1.5 × 108 km).

In the direction perpendicular to the solar wind, the random motion of the solar-wind particles [exerts](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/exerts) a small pressure on the magnetic field, constricting the magnetosphere slightly. The net result is that the shape of the magnetosphere in gross terms is similar to that of a comet, with the Earth located near the nucleus, or head, of the “comet” and the magnetospheric “tail” trailing out well beyond the Earth, away from the Sun.

Between 10 and 13 Earth radii toward the Sun, there exists the [magnetosheath](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetosheath), a region of magnetic turbulence in which both the magnitude and direction of [Earth’s magnetic field](https://www.britannica.com/science/geomagnetic-field) vary erratically. This disturbed region is thought to be caused by the production of magnetohydrodynamic shock waves, which in turn are caused by high-velocity solar-wind particles. Ahead of this [bow shock](https://www.britannica.com/science/bow-wave) boundary, toward the Sun, is the undisturbed solar wind.

- What is climate?







Climate in meteorology refers to the long-term atmospheric conditions at a specific location, summarizing atmospheric elements like [solar radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology/Introduction#ref1), temperature, humidity, precipitation, pressure, and wind over an extended period. It is more than just average weather; it includes the range, variability, and frequency of weather events over decades or centuries. Climate differs across regions due to factors like latitude, land versus water distribution, and [ocean currents](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology/Temperature#ref53251). Climate is time-dependent, so historical context is needed when describing it.

- How is climate different from weather?







Climate and weather both refer to atmospheric conditions, but they differ in duration and scale.



Weather is the state of the atmosphere at a specific location over a short period, involving phenomena like temperature, humidity, and wind. It can change rapidly, even from moment to moment.



Climate, however, represents the average atmospheric conditions over a long period (30 years or more) for a larger area. It includes not only average values but also extreme ranges, variability, and the frequency of occurrences. Climate is time-dependent, so values should specify the years they represent.

- What is meteorology and what do meteorologists study?







[Meteorology](https://www.britannica.com/science/meteorology/Introduction#ref1) is the scientific study of the atmosphere, particularly the troposphere and lower stratosphere. It systematically examines weather and its causes, providing the basis for weather forecasting.



Meteorologists study atmospheric phenomena, weather patterns, and long-term atmospheric changes. They use mathematical tools and computer systems to evaluate the physical and chemical interactions within the atmosphere. They also apply models to predict atmospheric conditions.

- Which factors influence Earth's climate?







Many factors affect Earth's climate. Solar radiation is the most important, but latitude, land versus water, [ocean currents](https://www.britannica.com/story/how-do-ocean-currents-affect-the-biosphere/Introduction#ref1), and [prevailing winds](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology/Temperature#ref53251) also play significant roles.



The atmosphere also influences climate. Water vapor, clouds, and ozone absorb solar radiation, and the atmosphere acts as a radiative blanket, trapping heat. [Greenhouse gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/greenhouse-gas/Introduction#ref1) such as carbon dioxide and methane also trap infrared radiation, leading to warmer surface temperatures.



[Volcanic activity](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcano/Secondary-damage#ref253598) can affect climate by injecting dust and aerosol droplets into the stratosphere, which can lower average world temperatures. Finally, the Earth's orbital geometry influences the distribution of solar radiation, causing climate variations.

- How do meteorologists measure and predict climate patterns?







Meteorologists use various methods to measure and predict climate patterns. They analyze historical data from the atmosphere, oceans, and land, as well as paleoclimatic data from sources like [ice cores](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-change/Evidence-for-climate-change#ref275766) and [tree rings](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-change/Evidence-for-climate-change#ref275766).



They employ [in situ measurements](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere/Cloud-research#ref261466) with instruments on towers and balloons, and [remote sensing](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere/Cloud-research#ref261466) using radar and satellites. [Climate models](https://www.britannica.com/science/global-warming/Theoretical-climate-models#ref274844) of varying complexity simulate the climate's response to forcings and internal variability, with more-detailed models solving radiative balance and atmospheric motion. These models are tested against observed changes and are used in detection and attribution studies to understand anthropogenic influences.

- Why is understanding climate important for people and the environment?







Understanding [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology/Introduction#ref1) is crucial because it shapes weather patterns, influencing ecosystems and human activities. Climate knowledge aids agriculture by informing decisions on crop management and land use, promoting [food security](https://www.britannica.com/topic/global-food-security/Introduction#ref360647) through sustainable practices that withstand climatic fluctuations. Awareness of climate change helps societies adapt, mitigating risks from extreme weather, sea-level rise, and disrupted resources. This understanding supports environmental conservation, protecting species and habitats from climate-related threats, and helps manage infectious diseases influenced by changing climatic conditions.

**climate**, conditions of the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere) at a particular location over a long period of time; it is the long-term summation of the atmospheric elements (and their variations) that, over short time periods, [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather). These elements are [solar radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-radiation), [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature), [humidity](https://www.britannica.com/science/humidity), [precipitation](https://www.britannica.com/science/precipitation) (type, frequency, and amount), [atmospheric pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmospheric-pressure), and [wind](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind) (speed and direction).

From the ancient Greek origins of the word ( _klíma_, “an inclination or slope”—e.g., of the [Sun’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun) rays; a [latitude](https://www.britannica.com/science/latitude) zone of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth); a clime) and from its earliest usage in English, climate has been understood to mean the atmospheric conditions that prevail in a given [region](https://www.britannica.com/science/region-geography) or zone. In the older form, _clime_, it was sometimes taken to include all aspects of the [environment](https://www.britannica.com/science/environment), including the natural vegetation. The best modern definitions of climate regard it as [constituting](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituting) the total experience of weather and atmospheric behaviour over a number of years in a given region. Climate is not just the “average weather” (an obsolete, and always inadequate, definition). It should include not only the average values of the climatic elements that prevail at different times but also their extreme ranges and variability and the frequency of various occurrences. Just as one year differs from another, decades and centuries are found to differ from one another by a smaller, but sometimes significant, amount. Climate is therefore time-dependent, and climatic values or indexes should not be quoted without specifying what years they refer to.

# [Solar radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-radiation) and temperature

[![See how differing amounts of solar radiation at the poles and Equator affect Earth's climate and atmosphere](https://cdn.britannica.com/50/81850-138-C471B234/Differences-amount-solar-radiation-poles-processes-Equator.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/Differences-amount-solar-radiation-poles-processes-Equator/-100532)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology/images-videos)

[Air](https://www.britannica.com/science/air) temperatures have their origin in the [absorption](https://www.britannica.com/science/absorption-physics) of [radiant energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiant-energy) from the Sun. They are subject to many influences, including those of the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere), [ocean](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean), and land, and are modified by them. As variation of solar radiation is the single most important factor affecting climate, it is considered here first.

## Solar radiation

## Distribution of radiant [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) from the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun)

[Nuclear fusion](https://www.britannica.com/science/nuclear-fusion) deep within the Sun releases a tremendous amount of [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) that is slowly transferred to the solar surface, from which it is radiated into [space](https://www.britannica.com/science/space-physics-and-metaphysics). The planets intercept minute fractions of this energy, the amount depending on their size and distance from the Sun. A 1-square-metre (11-square-foot) area perpendicular (90°) to the rays of the Sun at the top of [Earth’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth) atmosphere, for example, receives about 1,365 watts of [solar power](https://www.britannica.com/technology/solar-power). (This amount is comparable to the power [consumption](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consumption) of a typical electric heater.) Because of the slight ellipticity of Earth’s orbit around the Sun, the amount of [solar energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-energy) intercepted by Earth steadily rises and falls by ±3.4 percent throughout the year, peaking on January 3, when Earth is closest to the Sun. Although about 31 percent of this energy is not used as it is scattered back to space, the remaining amount is sufficient to power the movement of atmospheric winds and oceanic currents and to sustain nearly all biospheric activity.

![season](https://cdn.britannica.com/68/91868-050-731234C2/Diagram-relation-position-Earth-Sun-season-Northern.jpg)

[season](https://cdn.britannica.com/68/91868-050-731234C2/Diagram-relation-position-Earth-Sun-season-Northern.jpg) A diagram shows the position of Earth at the beginning of each season in the Northern Hemisphere.(more)

Most surfaces are not perpendicular to the Sun, and the energy they receive depends on their solar elevation angle. (The maximum solar elevation is 90° for the overhead Sun.) This angle changes systematically with [latitude](https://www.britannica.com/science/latitude), the time of year, and the time of day. The noontime elevation angle reaches a maximum at all latitudes north of the Tropic of Cancer (23.5° N) around June 22 and a minimum around December 22. South of the [Tropic of Capricorn](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tropic-of-Capricorn) (23.5° S), the opposite holds true, and between the two tropics, the maximum elevation angle (90°) occurs twice a year. When the Sun has a lower elevation angle, the solar energy is less intense because it is spread out over a larger area. Variation of solar elevation is thus one of the main factors that accounts for the dependence of climatic [regime](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/regime) on latitude. The other main factor is the length of daylight. For latitudes poleward of 66.5° N and S, the length of day ranges from zero (winter [solstice](https://www.britannica.com/science/solstice)) to 24 hours (summer solstice), whereas the [Equator](https://www.britannica.com/place/Equator) has a constant 12-hour day throughout the year. The seasonal range of [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature) consequently decreases from high latitudes to the tropics, where it becomes less than the diurnal range of temperature.

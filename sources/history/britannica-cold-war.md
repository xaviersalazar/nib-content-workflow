### What was the Cold War?

The Cold War was an ongoing political rivalry between the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) and the [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union) and their respective allies that developed after [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II). This hostility between the two superpowers was first given its name by [George Orwell](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Orwell) in [an article](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/you-and-the-atom-bomb/) published in 1945. Orwell understood it as a nuclear stalemate between “super-states”: each possessed weapons of mass destruction and was capable of annihilating the other.

The Cold War began after the surrender of Nazi Germany in 1945, when the uneasy alliance between the United States and Great Britain on the one hand and the Soviet Union on the other started to fall apart. The Soviet Union began to establish left-wing governments in the countries of eastern Europe, determined to safeguard against a possible renewed threat from Germany. The Americans and the British worried that Soviet domination in eastern Europe might be permanent. The Cold War was solidified by 1947–48, when U.S. aid had brought certain Western countries under American influence and the Soviets had established openly [communist](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communism) regimes. Nevertheless, there was very little use of weapons on battlefields during the Cold War. It was waged mainly on political, economic, and propaganda fronts and lasted until 1991.

### How did the Cold War end?

The Cold War came to a close gradually. The unity in the [communist](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communism) bloc was unraveling throughout the 1960s and ’70s as a split occurred between [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) and the [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union). Meanwhile, [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) and certain Western countries were becoming more economically independent. Increasingly complex international relationships developed as a result, and smaller countries became more resistant to superpower cajoling.

The Cold War truly began to break down during the administration of [Mikhail Gorbachev](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mikhail-Gorbachev), who changed the more totalitarian aspects of the Soviet government and tried to democratize its political system. Communist regimes began to collapse in eastern Europe, and democratic governments rose in [East Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/East-Germany), [Poland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Poland), [Hungary](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hungary), and [Czechoslovakia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Czechoslovakia), followed by the reunification of West and East Germany under [NATO](https://www.britannica.com/topic/North-Atlantic-Treaty-Organization) auspices. Gorbachev’s reforms meanwhile weakened his own communist party and allowed power to shift to the constituent governments of the Soviet bloc. The Soviet Union collapsed in late 1991, giving rise to 15 newly independent nations, including a [Russia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Russia) with an anticommunist leader.

### Why was the Cuban missile crisis such an important event in the Cold War?

In the late 1950s, both the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) and the [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union) were developing intercontinental ballistic [missiles](https://www.britannica.com/technology/nuclear-weapon). In 1962 the Soviet Union began to secretly install missiles in Cuba to launch attacks on U.S. cities. The confrontation that followed, known as the [Cuban missile crisis](https://www.britannica.com/event/Cuban-missile-crisis), brought the two superpowers to the brink of war before an agreement was reached to withdraw the missiles.

The conflict showed that both superpowers were wary of using their nuclear weapons against each other for fear of mutual atomic annihilation. The signing of the [Nuclear Test-Ban Treaty](https://www.britannica.com/event/Nuclear-Test-Ban-Treaty) followed in 1963, which banned aboveground nuclear weapons testing. Still, after the crisis, the Soviets were determined not to be humiliated by their military inferiority again, and they began a buildup of conventional and strategic forces that the United States was forced to match for the next 25 years.

**Cold War**, the open [yet](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/yet) restricted rivalry that developed after [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II) between the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) and the [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union) and their respective allies. The Cold War was waged on political, economic, and [propaganda](https://www.britannica.com/topic/propaganda) fronts and had only limited recourse to weapons. The term was first used by the English writer [George Orwell](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Orwell) in an article published in 1945 to refer to what he predicted would be a [nuclear](https://www.britannica.com/technology/nuclear-power) stalemate between “two or three monstrous super-states, each possessed of a [weapon](https://www.britannica.com/technology/nuclear-weapon) by which millions of people can be wiped out in a few seconds.” It was first used in the United States by the American financier and presidential adviser [Bernard Baruch](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bernard-Baruch) in a speech at the State House in Columbia, [South Carolina](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Carolina), in 1947.

## Origins of the Cold War

Following the surrender of [Nazi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nazi-Party) [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-Germany) in May 1945 near the close of [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II), the uneasy wartime alliance between the United States and Great [Britain](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-United-Kingdom) on the one hand and the Soviet Union on the other began to unravel. By 1948 the Soviets had installed left-wing governments in the countries of eastern Europe that had been liberated by the [Red Army](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Red-Army). The Americans and the British feared the permanent Soviet domination of eastern Europe and the threat of Soviet-influenced communist parties coming to power in the [democracies](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/democracies) of western Europe. The Soviets, on the other hand, were determined to maintain control of eastern Europe in order to safeguard against any possible renewed threat from Germany, and they were intent on spreading [communism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communism) worldwide, largely for ideological reasons. The Cold War had solidified by 1947–48, when U.S. aid provided under the [Marshall Plan](https://www.britannica.com/event/Marshall-Plan) to western Europe had brought those countries under American influence and the Soviets had installed openly communist regimes in eastern Europe.

## The struggle between superpowers

[![North Atlantic Treaty Organization](https://cdn.britannica.com/15/76015-050-39E107FE/Dean-Acheson-meeting-North-Atlantic-Treaty-Organization-September-15-1950.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/15/76015-050-39E107FE/Dean-Acheson-meeting-North-Atlantic-Treaty-Organization-September-15-1950.jpg)

[North Atlantic Treaty Organization](https://cdn.britannica.com/15/76015-050-39E107FE/Dean-Acheson-meeting-North-Atlantic-Treaty-Organization-September-15-1950.jpg) U.S. Secretary of State Dean Acheson (center) calling to order a meeting of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) on September 15, 1950.(more)

The Cold War reached its peak in 1948–53. In this period the Soviets unsuccessfully [blockaded](https://www.britannica.com/event/Berlin-blockade) the Western-held sectors of West [Berlin](https://www.britannica.com/place/Berlin) (1948–49); the United States and its European allies formed the [North Atlantic Treaty Organization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/North-Atlantic-Treaty-Organization) (NATO), a unified military command to resist the Soviet presence in Europe (1949); the Soviets exploded their first atomic warhead (1949), thus ending the American monopoly on the atomic bomb; the Chinese communists came to power in mainland China (1949); and the Soviet-supported communist government of [North Korea](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Korea) invaded U.S.-supported [South Korea](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Korea) in 1950, setting off an indecisive [Korean War](https://www.britannica.com/event/Korean-War) that lasted until 1953.

From 1953 to 1957 Cold War tensions relaxed somewhat, largely owing to the death of the longtime Soviet dictator [Joseph Stalin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Stalin) in 1953; nevertheless, the standoff remained. A unified military organization among the [Soviet-bloc](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eastern-bloc) countries, the [Warsaw Pact](https://www.britannica.com/event/Warsaw-Pact), was formed in 1955; and [West Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/West-Germany) was admitted into NATO that same year. Another intense stage of the Cold War was in 1958–62. The United States and the Soviet Union began developing intercontinental [ballistic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ballistic) [missiles](https://www.britannica.com/technology/nuclear-weapon), and in 1962 the Soviets began secretly installing missiles in Cuba that could be used to launch nuclear attacks on U.S. cities. This sparked the [Cuban missile crisis](https://www.britannica.com/event/Cuban-missile-crisis) (1962), a confrontation that brought the two superpowers to the brink of war before an agreement was reached to withdraw the missiles.

[![Nuclear Test-Ban Treaty](https://cdn.britannica.com/45/112545-050-3B3D6CF0/John-F-Kennedy-Nuclear-Test-Ban-Treaty-October-7-1963.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/45/112545-050-3B3D6CF0/John-F-Kennedy-Nuclear-Test-Ban-Treaty-October-7-1963.jpg)

[Nuclear Test-Ban Treaty](https://cdn.britannica.com/45/112545-050-3B3D6CF0/John-F-Kennedy-Nuclear-Test-Ban-Treaty-October-7-1963.jpg) U.S. Pres. John F. Kennedy signing the Nuclear Test-Ban Treaty, October 7, 1963.(more)

The Cuban missile crisis showed that neither the United States nor the Soviet Union were ready to use nuclear weapons for fear of the other’s retaliation (and thus of mutual atomic annihilation). The two superpowers soon signed the [Nuclear Test-Ban Treaty of 1963](https://www.britannica.com/event/Nuclear-Test-Ban-Treaty), which banned aboveground nuclear weapons testing. But the crisis also hardened the Soviets’ determination never again to be [humiliated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/humiliated) by their military inferiority, and they began a buildup of both conventional and strategic forces that the United States was forced to match for the next 25 years.

Throughout the Cold War the United States and the Soviet Union avoided direct military confrontation in Europe and engaged in actual combat operations only to keep allies from defecting to the other side or to overthrow them after they had done so. Thus, the Soviet Union sent troops to preserve communist rule in [East Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/East-Germany) (1953), [Hungary (1956)](https://www.britannica.com/event/Hungarian-Revolution-1956), [Czechoslovakia (1968)](https://www.britannica.com/event/Prague-Spring), and [Afghanistan (1979)](https://www.britannica.com/event/Soviet-invasion-of-Afghanistan). For its part, the United States [helped overthrow a left-wing government in Guatemala (1954)](https://www.britannica.com/place/Guatemala/The-postcolonial-period#ref40949), supported an [unsuccessful invasion of Cuba](https://www.britannica.com/event/Bay-of-Pigs-invasion) (1961), [invaded the Dominican Republic (1965)](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dominican-Republic/Bosch-Balaguer-and-their-successors#ref217439) and [Grenada (1983)](https://www.britannica.com/place/Grenada/Independence#ref54646), and undertook a long (1954–75) and unsuccessful effort to prevent communist North [Vietnam](https://www.britannica.com/place/Vietnam) from bringing South Vietnam under its rule ( _see_ [Vietnam War](https://www.britannica.com/event/Vietnam-War)).

# Toward a new world order

[![What if the Cold War turned hot?](https://cdn.britannica.com/12/218812-138-ABCDE048/cold-war.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/cold-war/-248743)

What if the Cold War turned hot?Learn what a war between the U.S. and the Soviet Union—both in possession of nuclear weapons—could have looked like.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/event/Cold-War/images-videos)

In the course of the 1960s and ’70s, however, the bipolar struggle between the Soviet and American blocs gave way to a more-complicated pattern of international relationships in which the world was no longer split into two clearly opposed blocs. A major split had occurred between the Soviet Union and China in 1960 and widened over the years, shattering the unity of the communist bloc. In the meantime, western Europe and [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) achieved [dynamic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynamic) [economic growth](https://www.britannica.com/money/economic-growth) in the 1950s and ’60s, reducing their relative inferiority to the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States). Less-powerful countries had more room to assert their independence and often showed themselves resistant to superpower coercion or [cajoling](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cajoling).

[![Large crowd celebrates on and around the Berlin Wall in front of the Brandenburg Gate](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/92851-050-78D396E4/People-East-wall-gathering-West-Berlin-Wall-November-10-1989.jpg?w=1000)](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/92851-050-78D396E4/People-East-wall-gathering-West-Berlin-Wall-November-10-1989.jpg)

[Berlin Wall opening](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/92851-050-78D396E4/People-East-wall-gathering-West-Berlin-Wall-November-10-1989.jpg) People from East and West Berlin gathering at the Berlin Wall on November 10, 1989, one day after the wall opened.(more)

The 1970s saw an easing of Cold War tensions as evinced in the [Strategic Arms Limitation Talks](https://www.britannica.com/event/Strategic-Arms-Limitation-Talks) (SALT) that led to the SALT I and II agreements of 1972 and 1979, respectively, in which the two superpowers set limits on their antiballistic missiles and on their strategic missiles capable of carrying nuclear weapons. That was followed by a period of renewed Cold War tensions in the early 1980s as the two superpowers continued their massive arms buildup and competed for influence in the [Third World](https://www.britannica.com/money/Third-World). But the Cold War began to break down in the late 1980s during the administration of Soviet leader [Mikhail S. Gorbachev](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mikhail-Gorbachev). He dismantled the totalitarian aspects of the Soviet system and began efforts to democratize the Soviet [political system](https://www.britannica.com/topic/political-system). When communist regimes in the Soviet-bloc countries of eastern Europe collapsed in 1989–90, Gorbachev [acquiesced](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/acquiesced) in their fall. The rise to power of democratic governments in East Germany, [Poland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Poland), Hungary, and [Czechoslovakia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Czechoslovakia) was quickly followed by the [unification of West and East Germany](https://www.britannica.com/topic/German-reunification) under [NATO](https://www.britannica.com/topic/North-Atlantic-Treaty-Organization) [auspices](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/auspices), again with Soviet approval.

[![Ronald Reagan and Mikhail Gorbachev](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/160731-050-AF981674/Pres-Mikhail-Gorbachev-Ronald-Reagan-Soviet-Moscow-1988.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/160731-050-AF981674/Pres-Mikhail-Gorbachev-Ronald-Reagan-Soviet-Moscow-1988.jpg)

[Ronald Reagan and Mikhail Gorbachev](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/160731-050-AF981674/Pres-Mikhail-Gorbachev-Ronald-Reagan-Soviet-Moscow-1988.jpg) U.S. Pres. Ronald Reagan (left) talking with Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev in Moscow, 1988.(more)

Gorbachev’s internal reforms had meanwhile weakened [his own Communist Party](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Communist-Party-of-the-Soviet-Union) and allowed power to shift to Russia and the other [constituent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituent) republics of the Soviet Union. On December 31, 1991, the [Soviet Union formally ceased to exist](https://www.britannica.com/event/the-collapse-of-the-Soviet-Union), and 15 Soviet republics became independent countries: Armenia, Azerbaijan, Belarus, [Estonia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Estonia), Georgia, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, [Latvia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Latvia), [Lithuania](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lithuania), Moldova, Russia, [Tajikistan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tajikistan), Turkmenistan, Ukraine, and Uzbekistan. The Cold War had come to an end.

### What does samurai mean?

The term _samurai_ was originally used to denote [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan)’s aristocratic warriors ( _bushi_), but it came to apply to all the members of the country’s warrior class who rose to power in the 12th century and dominated the Japanese government until the [Meiji Restoration](https://www.britannica.com/event/Meiji-Restoration) in 1868.

### When did samurai exist?

Emerging from provincial warrior bands, the samurai of the [Kamakura period](https://www.britannica.com/event/Kamakura-period) (1192–1333), with their military skills and deep pride in their [stoicism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Stoicism), developed a disciplined culture distinct from the earlier quiet refinement of the imperial court. Samurai lived an austere lifestyle, and the samurai culture produced many uniquely [Japanese arts](https://www.britannica.com/art/Japanese-art), such as the [tea ceremony](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tea-ceremony) and flower arranging ( [ikebana](https://www.britannica.com/art/ikebana)), that continue today.

### Why did samurai commit seppuku?

The ideal samurai was supposed to follow an unwritten code of conduct, later formalized as [Bushidō](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bushido), which held bravery, [honour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/honour), and personal loyalty above [life](https://www.britannica.com/science/life) itself; ritual [suicide](https://www.britannica.com/topic/suicide) by disembowelment ( [seppuku](https://www.britannica.com/topic/seppuku)) was institutionalized as a respected alternative to dishonour or defeat. The proper method for committing the act was to plunge a short [sword](https://www.britannica.com/technology/sword) into the left side of the [abdomen](https://www.britannica.com/science/abdomen), draw the blade laterally across to the right, and then turn it upward.

**samurai**, member of the [Japanese](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) warrior caste. The term _samurai_ was originally used to denote the [aristocratic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/aristocracy) warriors ( _bushi_), but it came to apply to all the members of the warrior class that rose to power in the 12th century and dominated the Japanese government until the [Meiji Restoration](https://www.britannica.com/event/Meiji-Restoration) in 1868.

[![Kusakabe Kimbei: Samurai in Armour](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/196871-050-B8665B11/Samurai-Armour-Kusakabe-Kimbei.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/196871-050-B8665B11/Samurai-Armour-Kusakabe-Kimbei.jpg)

[Kusakabe Kimbei: _Samurai in Armour_](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/196871-050-B8665B11/Samurai-Armour-Kusakabe-Kimbei.jpg) _Samurai in Armour_, hand-colored albumen silver print by Kusakabe Kimbei, c. 1870s–90s; in the J. Paul Getty Museum, Los Angeles.(more)

Emerging from provincial warrior bands, the samurai of the [Kamakura period](https://www.britannica.com/event/Kamakura-period) (1192–1333), with their military skills and deep pride in their stoicism, developed a disciplined culture distinct from the earlier, quiet refinement of the imperial court. During the [Muromachi period](https://www.britannica.com/event/Muromachi-period) (1338–1573) under the growing influence of [Zen](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zen) Buddhism, the samurai culture produced many such uniquely Japanese arts as the tea ceremony and flower arranging that continue today. The ideal samurai was supposed to be a stoic warrior who followed an unwritten code of conduct, later formalized as [Bushidō](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bushido), which held bravery, honour, and personal loyalty above life itself; ritual [suicide](https://www.britannica.com/topic/suicide) by disembowelment ( [seppuku](https://www.britannica.com/topic/seppuku), also called _hara-kiri_) was institutionalized as a respected alternative to dishonour or defeat.

[![samurai in full armour](https://cdn.britannica.com/79/152479-050-6657870B/samurai-armour-plate-Japanese-Victoria-and-Albert.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/79/152479-050-6657870B/samurai-armour-plate-Japanese-Victoria-and-Albert.jpg)

[samurai in full armour](https://cdn.britannica.com/79/152479-050-6657870B/samurai-armour-plate-Japanese-Victoria-and-Albert.jpg) A samurai in full armour depicted on a Japanese plate, 1850–75; in the Victoria and Albert Museum, London.(more)

In the early part of the [Tokugawa period](https://www.britannica.com/event/Tokugawa-period) (1603–1867), the samurai, who accounted for less than 10 percent of the population, were made a closed caste as part of a larger effort to freeze the social order and stabilize society. Although still allowed to wear the two swords emblematic of their social position, most samurai were forced to become civil bureaucrats or take up some trade during the 250 years of peace that prevailed under the Tokugawa shogunate (military dictatorship). Moreover, the rise of the cities and the expansion of a merchant economy during early 18th-century Japan led to the flowering of a vibrant urban culture, which eventually superseded the austere life-style of the samurai. At the same time, the economic position of the samurai, who lived primarily on fixed stipends, was being eroded. In spite of their high social rank, a growing number of samurai families suffered impoverishment by the end of the Tokugawa period.

Lower-ranking samurai, eager for advancement and realizing a new sense of national purpose in the face of encroachment by the Western powers during the mid-19th century, took part in the movement against the Tokugawa regime that resulted in the [Meiji Restoration](https://www.britannica.com/event/Meiji-Restoration) of 1868. The samurai class lost its privileged position when [feudalism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/feudalism) was officially abolished in 1871. Discontented former samurai rose in rebellion several times during the 1870s, but these revolts were quickly suppressed by the newly established national army.

**Bushidō**, the code of conduct of the [samurai](https://www.britannica.com/topic/samurai), or _bushi_ (warrior), [class](https://www.britannica.com/topic/social-class) of premodern [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan). In the mid-19th century, however, the precepts of Bushidō were made the basis of [ethical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ethical) training for the whole society, with the emperor replacing the feudal [lord](https://www.britannica.com/topic/lord), or [daimyo](https://www.britannica.com/topic/daimyo), as the focus of loyalty and sacrifice. As such it contributed to the rise of Japanese [nationalism](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nationalism) following the [Meiji Restoration](https://www.britannica.com/event/Meiji-Restoration) (1868) and to the strengthening of civilian morale during the [Sino-Japanese War](https://www.britannica.com/event/Second-Sino-Japanese-War) (1937–45) and [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II). Instruction in the code was officially abandoned with Japan’s defeat in 1945. Elements of the code remain, however, in the practice of Japanese martial arts and in the sport of [sumo](https://www.britannica.com/sports/sumo-sport) wrestling.

[![Samurai](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/147163-050-A15A408D/Samurai-drawing.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/147163-050-A15A408D/Samurai-drawing.jpg)

[Samurai](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/147163-050-A15A408D/Samurai-drawing.jpg) Samurai on horseback, drawing, late 19th century.(more)

The name Bushidō was not used until the 16th century, but the idea of the code developed during the [Kamakura period](https://www.britannica.com/event/Kamakura-period) (1192–1333), as did the practice of [seppuku](https://www.britannica.com/topic/seppuku) (ritual disembowelment). At that time the Minamoto family established Japan’s first [military government](https://www.britannica.com/topic/military-government) ( _bakufu_), headed by a hereditary leader called the [shogun](https://www.britannica.com/topic/shogun). The precise content of the Bushidō code varied historically as the samurai class came under the influence of [Zen](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zen) Buddhist and [Confucian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Confucianism) thought, but its one unchanging ideal was martial spirit, including athletic and [military](https://www.britannica.com/topic/armed-force) skills as well as fearlessness toward the enemy in battle. [Frugal](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Frugal) living, kindness, honesty, and personal [honour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/honour) were also highly regarded, as was filial piety. However, the supreme obligation of the samurai was to his lord, even if this might cause suffering to his parents.

During the [Edo (Tokugawa) period](https://www.britannica.com/event/Tokugawa-period) (1603–1867) Bushidō thought was infused with Confucian [ethics](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ethics) and made into a [comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive) system that stressed obligation or duty. The samurai was equated with the Confucian “perfect gentleman” and was taught that his essential function was to exemplify [virtue](https://www.britannica.com/topic/virtue-in-Christianity) to the lower classes. Obedience to authority was stressed, but duty came first even if it entailed violation of statute law. The extent to which duty superseded all else is perhaps best exemplified in the story of the [47](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chushingura) [_rōnin_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ronin) from the early 18th century. The samurai, who had become masterless ( _rōnin_) after their lord had been treacherously murdered, [avenged](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/avenged) their lord’s death and afterward were all ordered to commit seppuku.

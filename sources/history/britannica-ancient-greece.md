Top Questions


### Is ancient Greece a country?

No, ancient Greece was a civilization. The Greeks had cultural traits, a [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-religion), and a [language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-language) in common, though they spoke many dialects. The basic political unit was the [city-state](https://www.britannica.com/topic/city-state). Conflict between city-states was common, but they were capable of banding together against a common enemy, as they did during the Persian Wars (492–449 BCE). Powerful city-states such as [Athens](https://www.britannica.com/place/Athens) and [Sparta](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sparta) exerted influence beyond their borders but never controlled the entire Greek-speaking world.

### Where was ancient Greece located?

Ancient Greek civilization was concentrated in what is today [Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece) and along the western coast of Turkey. However, ancient Greek colonists established cities all around the [Mediterranean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea) and along the coast of the [Black Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Black-Sea).

### Was ancient Greece a democracy?

Each ancient Greek city-state had its own government. Common forms of government included [tyranny](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tyranny) and [oligarchy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/oligarchy). In 507 BCE, under the leadership of [Cleisthenes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cleisthenes-of-Athens), the citizens of Athens began to develop a system of popular rule that they called [democracy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/democracy), which would last nearly two centuries. In their governing body, the Assembly ( [Ecclesia](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ecclesia-ancient-Greek-assembly)), all adult male citizens, perhaps 10 to 15 percent of the total population, were eligible to vote.

### When did ancient Greece start and end?

Ancient Greek civilization flourished from the period following [Mycenaean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mycenae) civilization, which ended about 1200 BCE, to the death of [Alexander the Great](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alexander-the-Great), in 323 BCE. By that time, Greek cultural influence had spread around the Mediterranean and, through Alexander the Great’s campaign of conquest, as far afield as India.

### Why is ancient Greece important?

The political, philosophical, artistic, and scientific achievements of ancient Greek civilization formed a legacy with unparalleled influence on Western civilization. Greek political ideas have influenced modern forms of government, [Greek pottery](https://www.britannica.com/art/Greek-pottery) and sculpture have inspired artists for millennia, and [Greek epic, lyric, and dramatic poetry](https://www.britannica.com/art/Greek-literature) is still read around the world.

**ancient Greek civilization**, the period following [Mycenaean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mycenae) civilization, which ended about 1200 bce, to the death of [Alexander the Great](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alexander-the-Great), in 323 bce. It was a period of political, philosophical, artistic, and scientific achievements that formed a [legacy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legacy) with unparalleled influence. ## The post-Mycenaean period and Lefkandi

![ancient Greece](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/1033-050-895AB1EE/Ancient-Greece.jpg)

[ancient Greece](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/1033-050-895AB1EE/Ancient-Greece.jpg)(more)

The period between the catastrophic end of the Mycenaean civilization and about 900 bce is often called a [Dark Age](https://www.britannica.com/event/Dark-Ages). It was a time about which Greeks of the Classical age had confused and actually false notions. [Thucydides](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thucydides-Greek-historian), the great ancient historian of the 5th century bce, wrote a sketch of Greek [history](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history) from the [Trojan War](https://www.britannica.com/event/Trojan-War) to his own day, in which he notoriously fails, in the appropriate chapter, to signal any kind of dramatic rupture. (He does, however, speak of [Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece) “settling down gradually” and colonizing [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy/The-people#ref26974), [Sicily](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sicily), and what is now western [Turkey](https://www.britannica.com/place/Turkey). This surely implies that Greece was settling down after something.) Thucydides does indeed display sound knowledge of the series of [migrations](https://www.britannica.com/science/migration-animal) by which Greece was resettled in the post-Mycenaean period. The most famous of these was the “ [Dorian invasion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dorian-invasion),” which the Greeks called, or connected with, the legendary “return of the [descendants](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/descendants) of [Heracles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Heracles).” Although much about that invasion is problematic—it left little or no archaeological trace at the point in time where tradition puts it—the problems are of no concern here. Important for the understanding of the [Archaic](https://www.britannica.com/art/Archaic-period) and Classical periods, however, is the powerful belief in Dorianism as a linguistic and religious concept. Thucydides casually but significantly mentions soldiers speaking the “Doric dialect” in a narrative about ordinary military matters in the year 426. That is a surprisingly abstract way of looking at the subdivisions of the Greeks, because it would have been more natural for a 5th-century Greek to identify soldiers by home cities. Equally important to the understanding of this period is the hostility to [Dorians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dorian), usually on the part of [Ionians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ionian), another linguistic and religious subgroup, whose most-famous city was [Athens](https://www.britannica.com/place/Athens). So extreme was this hostility that Dorians were prohibited from entering Ionian sanctuaries; [extant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/extant) today is a 5th-century example of such a prohibition, an inscription from the island of [Paros](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paros).

Phenomena such as the tension between Dorians and Ionians that have their origins in the Dark Age are a reminder that Greek civilization did not emerge either unannounced or uncontaminated by what had gone before. The Dark Age itself is beyond the scope of this article. One is bound to notice, however, that archaeological finds tend to call into question the whole concept of a Dark Age by showing that certain features of Greek civilization once thought not to antedate about 800 bce can actually be pushed back by as much as two centuries. One example, chosen for its relevance to the emergence of the Greek [city-state](https://www.britannica.com/topic/city-state), or [polis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/polis), will [suffice](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/suffice). In 1981 [archaeology](https://www.britannica.com/science/archaeology) pulled back the curtain on the “darkest” phase of all, the Protogeometric Period ( _c._ 1075–900 bce), which takes its name from the [geometric shapes](https://www.britannica.com/art/Proto-Geometric-style) painted on [pottery](https://www.britannica.com/art/Greek-pottery). A grave, rich by the standards of any period, was uncovered at a site called Lefkandi on [Euboea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Euboea-island-Greece), the island along the eastern flank of [Attica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Attica-ancient-district-Greece) (the territory controlled by Athens). The grave, which dates to about 1000 bce, contains the (probably cremated) remains of a man and a woman. The large [bronze](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bronze-alloy) vessel in which the man’s ashes were deposited came from [Cyprus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cyprus), and the [gold](https://www.britannica.com/science/gold-chemical-element) items buried with the woman are splendid and sophisticated in their workmanship. Remains of [horses](https://www.britannica.com/animal/horse) were found as well; the animals had been buried with their snaffle bits. The grave was within a large collapsed house, whose form anticipates that of the Greek [temples](https://www.britannica.com/topic/temple-building) two centuries later. Previously it had been thought that those temples were one of the first [manifestations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifestations) of the “monumentalizing” associated with the beginnings of the city-state. Thus, that find and those made in a set of nearby cemeteries in the years before 1980 attesting further contacts between [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt) and [Cyprus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cyprus) between 1000 and 800 bce are important evidence. They show that one corner of one island of Greece, at least, was neither impoverished nor isolated in a period usually thought to have been both. The difficulty is to know just how exceptional Lefkandi was, but in any view it has revised former ideas about what was and what was not possible at the beginning of the 1st millennium bce.

The first “date” in Greek [history](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history) is 776 bce, the year of the first [Olympic Games](https://www.britannica.com/sports/Olympic-Games). It was computed by a 5th-century-bce researcher called [Hippias](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hippias-of-Elis). He was originally from [Elis](https://www.britannica.com/place/Elis), a place in the western [Peloponnese](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peloponnese) in whose territory [Olympia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Olympia-ancient-site-Greece) itself is situated. This date and the list of early victors, transmitted by another literary tradition, are likely to be reliable, if only because the list is so unassuming in its early reaches. That is to say, local victors predominate, including some [Messenians](https://www.britannica.com/place/Messenia). [Messene](https://www.britannica.com/place/Messene) lost its independence to neighbouring [Sparta](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sparta) during the course of the 8th century, and this fact is an additional guarantee of the reliability of the early Olympic victor list: Messenian victors would hardly have been invented at a time when Messene as a political entity had ceased to exist. Clearly, then, record keeping and organized activity involving more than one [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) and centring on a [sanctuary](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sanctuary-religion), such as Olympia, go back to the early 8th century. (Such competitive activity is an example of what has been called “peer-polity interaction.”) Records imply a degree of [literacy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/literacy), and there too the tradition about the 8th century has been confirmed by late 20th-century finds. A cup, bearing the inscription in Greek in the Euboean script “I am the cup of Nestor,” can be securely dated to before 700 bce. It was found at an island site called Pithekoussai ( [Ischia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Island-of-Ischia)) on the [Bay of Naples](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bay-of-Naples).

## Overseas projects

The early overseas activity of the [Euboeans](https://www.britannica.com/place/Euboea-island-Greece) has already been remarked upon in connection with the discoveries at Lefkandi. They were the prime movers in the more or less organized—or, at any rate, remembered and recorded—phase of Greek overseas settlement, a process known as colonization. (Euboean priority can be taken as absolutely certain because archaeology supports the literary tradition of the Roman historian [Livy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Livy) and others: Euboean pottery has been found both at Pithekoussai to the west and at the Turkish site of Al-Mina to the east.) This more-organized phase began in [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy) about 750 and in [Sicily](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sicily) in 734 bce; its episodes were remembered, perhaps in writing, by the colonies themselves. The word _organized_ needs to be stressed, because various [considerations](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/considerations) make it necessary to push back beyond that date the beginning of Greek colonization. First, it is clear from archaeological finds, such as the Lefkandi material, and from other new evidence that the Greeks had already, before 750 or 734, confronted and exchanged goods with the inhabitants of [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy) and [Sicily](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sicily). Second, Thucydides says that Dark Age [Athens](https://www.britannica.com/place/Athens) sent colonies to [Ionia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ionia), and [archaeology](https://www.britannica.com/science/archaeology) bears this out—however much one discounts for propagandist exaggeration by the imperial Athens of Thucydides’ own time of its prehistoric colonizing role. However, after the founding of [Cumae](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cumae) (a mainland Italian offshoot of the island settlement of Pithekoussai) about 750 bce and of Sicilian [Naxos](https://www.britannica.com/place/Naxos-ancient-Greek-colony-Sicily) and [Syracuse](https://www.britannica.com/place/Syracuse-Italy) in 734 and 733, respectively, there was an explosion of colonies to all points of the compass. The only exceptions were those areas, such as pharaonic [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt) or inner [Anatolia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Anatolia), where the inhabitants were too militarily and politically advanced to be easily overrun.

One may ask why the Greeks suddenly began to launch these overseas projects. It seems that commercial interests, greed, and sheer curiosity were the motivating forces. An older view, according to which [Archaic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Archaic) [Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece) exported its surplus population because of an uncontrollable rise in population, must be regarded as largely discredited. In the first place, the earliest well-documented colonial operations were small-scale affairs, too small to make much difference to the situation of the sending community (the “metropolis,” or mother city). That is certainly true of the colonization of [Cyrene](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cyrene-ancient-Greek-colony-Libya), in [North Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Africa), from the island of [Thera](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thera) (Santorin); on this point, an inscription has confirmed the classic account by the 5th-century Greek historian [Herodotus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Herodotus-Greek-historian). In the second place, population was not uncontrollable in principle: artificial means such as [infanticide](https://www.britannica.com/topic/infanticide) were available, not to mention more-modern techniques like [contraception](https://www.britannica.com/topic/contraception). Considerations of this kind much reduce the evidential value of discoveries establishing, for example, that the number of graves in [Attica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Attica-ancient-district-Greece) and the Argolid (the area round [Argos](https://www.britannica.com/place/Argos)) increased dramatically in the later Dark Age or that there was a serious drought in 8th-century Attica (that is the admitted [implication](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implication) of a number of dried-up wells in the Athenian [agora](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agora), or civic centre). In fact, no single explanation for the colonizing activity is plausible. Political difficulties at home might sometimes be a factor, as, for instance, at [Sparta](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sparta), which in the 8th century sent out a colony to Taras (Tarentum) in Italy as a way of getting rid of an unwanted half-caste group. Nor can one rule out simple craving for excitement and a desire to see the world. The [lyric poetry](https://www.britannica.com/art/lyric) of the energetic and high-strung poet [Archilochus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Archilochus-Greek-author), a 7th-century Parian involved in the colonization of [Thasos](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thasos), shows the kind of lively minded individual who might be involved in the colonizing movement.

So far, the vague term _community_ has been used for places that sent out colonies. Such vagueness is historically appropriate, because those places themselves were scarcely [constituted](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituted) as united entities, such as a [city](https://www.britannica.com/topic/city), or [polis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/polis). For example, it is a curious fact that [Corinth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Corinth-Greece), which in 733 colonized Syracuse in Sicily, was itself scarcely a properly constituted polis in 733. (The formation of Corinth as a united entity is to be put in the second half of the 8th century, with precisely the colonization of Syracuse as its first [collective](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/collective) act.)

## The beginnings of the polis

The name given to [polis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/polis) formation by the Greeks themselves was _synoikismos_, literally a “gathering together.” _Synoikismos_ could take one or both of two forms—it could be a physical concentration of the population in a single city or an act of purely political unification that allowed the population to continue living in a dispersed way. The classic discussion is by [Thucydides](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thucydides-Greek-historian), who distinguishes between the two kinds of _synoikismos_ more carefully than do some of his modern critics. He makes the correct point that Attica was politically synoecized at an early date but not physically synoecized until 431 bce when [Pericles](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pericles-Athenian-statesman) as part of his war policy brought the large rural population behind the city walls of Athens. A more extreme instance of a polis that was never fully synoecized in the physical sense was Sparta, which, as Thucydides elsewhere says, remained “settled by villages in the old Greek way.” It was an act of conscious [arrogance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/arrogance), a way of claiming to be invulnerable from attack and not to need the walls that Thucydides again and again treats as the sign and guarantee of civilized polis life. The urban history of Sparta makes an interesting case history showing that [Mycenaean](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mycenaean) Sparta was not so physically or psychologically secure as its Greek and Roman successors. The administrative centre of Mycenaean Sparta was probably in the Párnon Mountains at the excavated site of the Menelaion. Then Archaic and Classical Sparta moved down to the plain. [Byzantine](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Byzantine) Sparta, more insecure, moved out of the plain again to perch on the site of Mistra on the opposite western mountain, Taygetos. Finally, modern Sparta is situated, once again peacefully and confidently, on its old site on the plain of the river Eurotas.

The enabling factors behind the beginnings of the Greek polis have been the subject of intense discussion. One approach connects the beginnings of the polis with the first monumental buildings, usually [temples](https://www.britannica.com/topic/temple-building) like the great early 8th-century temple of [Hera](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hera) on the island of [Samos](https://www.britannica.com/place/Samos-island-Greece). The concentration of resources and effort required for such constructions presupposes the formation of self-conscious polis units and may actually have [accelerated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/accelerated) it. As stated above, however, the evidence from Lefkandi makes it hard to see the construction of such monumental buildings as a sufficient cause for the emergence of the polis, a process or event nobody has yet tried to date as early as 1000 bce.

Another related theory argues that the birth of the Greek city was signaled by the placing of rural sanctuaries at the margins of the territory that a community sought to define as its own. That theory fits admirably a number of [Peloponnesian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peloponnese) [sanctuaries](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sanctuary-religion); for instance, the temple complex of Hera staked out a claim, on the part of relatively distant Argos, to the plain stretching between city and [sanctuary](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/sanctuary), and the Corinthian sanctuary on the promontory of Perachora, also dedicated to Hera, performed the same function. Yet there are difficulties. It seems that the Isthmia sanctuary, which at first sight seems a good candidate for another Corinthian rural sanctuary, was already operational as early as 900 bce, in the Protogeometric Period, and that date is surely too early for polis formation. Nor does the theory easily account for the rural temple of the goddess Aphaea in the middle of [Aegina](https://www.britannica.com/place/Aegina-island-Greece). The sanctuary is admittedly a long way from the town of Aegina, but Aegina is an island, and there is no obvious neighbour against whom territorial claims could plausibly have been [asserted](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/asserted). Finally, a theory that has to treat the best-known polis—namely, Athens—and Attica as in every respect exceptional is not satisfactory: there is no Athenian equivalent of the Argive [Heraeum](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Heraeum).

A third theory attacks the problem of the beginnings of the polis through [burial](https://www.britannica.com/topic/burial-death-rite) practice. In the 8th century (it is said) formal burial became more generally available, and that “democratization” of burial is evidence for a fundamentally new attitude toward society. The theory seeks to associate the new attitude with the growth of the polis. There is, however, insufficient archaeological and historical evidence for that view (which involves an implausible [hypothesis](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hypothesis) that the process postulated was discontinuous and actually reversed for a brief period at a date later than the 8th century). Moreover, it is [vulnerable](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vulnerable) to the converse objection as that raised against the second theory: the evidence for the third theory is almost exclusively Attic, and so, even if it were true, it would explain Athens and only Athens.

Fourth, one may consider a theory whose unspoken [premise](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/premise) is a kind of “geographic determinism.” Perhaps the Greek landscape itself, with its small alluvial plains often surrounded by defensible mountain systems, somehow prompted the formation of small and [acrimonious](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/acrimonious) poleis, endlessly going to war over boundaries. That view has its attractions, but the obvious objection is that, when Greeks went to more-open areas such as Italy, Sicily, and North Africa, they seem to have taken their [animosities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/animosities) with them. That in turn invites speculations of a psychologically determinist sort; one has to ask, without hope of an answer, whether the Greeks were naturally particularist.

A fifth enabling factor that should be borne in mind is the influence of the colonizing movement itself. The political structure of the metropolis, or sending city, may sometimes have been inchoate. The new colony, however, threatened by hostile native neighbours, rapidly had to “get its act together” if it was to be a viable cell of Hellenism on foreign soil. That effort in turn affected the situation in the metropolis, because Greek colonies often kept close religious and social links with it. A 4th-century inscription, for instance, attests close ties between [Miletus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Miletus) and its daughter city [Olbia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Olbia) in the [Black Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Black-Sea) region. Here, however, as so often in Greek history, generalization is dangerous; some mother-daughter relationships, like that between [Corinth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Corinth-Greece) and Corcyra ( [Corfu](https://www.britannica.com/place/Corfu)), were bad virtually from the start.

A related factor is [Phoenician](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Phoenician) influence (related, because the early Phoenicians were great colonizers, who must often have met trading Greeks). The Phoenician coast was settled by [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) similar in many respects to the early Greek poleis. It is arguable that Phoenician influence, and [Semitic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Semite) influence generally, on early Greece has been seriously underrated.

Theories such as these are stimulating and may each contain a particle of truth. The better position, however, is that generalization itself is as yet premature; in particular, archaeologically based theoretical reconstructions need much more refining. All one can say in summary is that in roughly the same period—namely, the 8th century—a number of areas, such as Corinth and [Megara](https://www.britannica.com/place/Megara-Greece), began to define their borders, deny [autonomy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/autonomy) to their [constituent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituent) villages, and generally act as separate states. Attica’s political synoecism, which occurred a little earlier, was complete perhaps about 900. Tempting though it is to seek a single explanatory model for those very roughly [contemporaneous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contemporaneous) processes, one should perhaps allow that different paths of development were followed in different areas, even in areas next door to each other. After all, the Archaic and Classical histories of mighty democratic imperial Athens, of the miserable polis of Megara which nevertheless colonized [Byzantium](https://www.britannica.com/place/Istanbul), of wealthy, oligarchic Corinth, and of federal [Boeotia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Boeotia) were all very different even though Athens, [Megara](https://www.britannica.com/place/Megara-Greece), Corinth, and [Boeotia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Boeotia) were close neighbours.

One is perhaps on firmer ground when one examines the evidence for prepolis aggregations of larger units, often religious in character. There are a number of such associations whose origins lie in the Dark Age and whose existence surely promoted some feeling of local and particularist identity among the participants. The Ionians in Anatolia formed themselves into a confederation of 12 communities, the Ionian Dodecapolis, with a common meeting place; and there were [comparable](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/comparable) groupings among the Dorian Greeks of Anatolia and even among the [Carians](https://www.britannica.com/place/Caria) (partially Hellenized non-Greeks) in the same part of the world. The central location for such organizations was characteristically small and insignificant. One poorly attested but intriguing early Archaic league was the “ [Calaurian Amphictyony](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Calaurian-Amphictyony)” (an [amphictyony](https://www.britannica.com/topic/amphictyony) was a religious league of “dwellers round about”). Calauria, the small island now called [Póros](https://www.britannica.com/place/Poros), was not a place of any consequence in itself, but the league’s seven members included Athens and [Aegina](https://www.britannica.com/place/Aegina-island-Greece), two major Greek poleis. The most famous and enduring such [amphictyony](https://www.britannica.com/topic/amphictyony), however, was the one that, originally from a distance, administered the affairs of the sanctuary of [Delphi](https://www.britannica.com/place/Delphi-ancient-city-Greece) in central Greece. That sanctuary contained the most-famous, though not the oldest, Greek [oracle](https://www.britannica.com/topic/oracle-religion) (the oldest was at [Dodona](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dodona)); oracles were a mechanism by which divinely inspired utterances were produced in answer to specific questions. Finally, it is worth noting an adventurous suggestion that Lefkandi itself might have been the centre of some kind of religious amphictyony, but, if so, this would be an exception to the principle that religious centres tended themselves to be insignificant, however mighty their participating members.

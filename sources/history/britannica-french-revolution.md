### What was the French Revolution?

The French Revolution was a period of major social upheaval that began in 1787 and ended in 1799. It sought to completely change the relationship between the rulers and those they governed and to redefine the nature of political power. It proceeded in a back-and-forth process between revolutionary and reactionary forces.

### Why did the French Revolution happen?

There were many reasons. The [bourgeoisie](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bourgeoisie)—merchants, manufacturers, professionals—had gained financial power but were excluded from political power. Those who were socially beneath them had very few rights, and most were also increasingly impoverished. The [monarchy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monarchy) was no longer viewed as divinely ordained. When the king sought to increase the tax burden on the poor and expand it to classes that had previously been exempt, revolution became all but inevitable.

### Why did the French Revolution lead to war with other nations?

[King Louis XVI](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-XVI) of France yielded to the idea of a new [constitution](https://www.britannica.com/topic/constitution-politics-and-law) and to the sovereignty of the people but at the same time sent emissaries to the rulers of neighboring countries seeking their help in restoring his power. Many revolutionaries, especially the [Girondins](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Girondin), believed that the revolution needed to spread throughout Europe to succeed. An Austro-Prussian army invaded France, and French revolutionary forces pushed outward.

### How did the French Revolution succeed?

In some respects, the French Revolution did not succeed. But the ideas of [representational](https://www.britannica.com/topic/representation-government) democracy and basic property rights took hold, and it sowed the seeds of the later revolutions of [1830](https://www.britannica.com/event/Revolutions-of-1830) and [1848](https://www.britannica.com/event/Revolutions-of-1848).

**French Revolution**, [revolutionary](https://www.britannica.com/topic/revolution-politics) movement that shook [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) between 1787 and 1799. It reached its first climax in 1789 when the [ancien régime](https://www.britannica.com/event/ancien-regime) in France was overthrown. The [revolution](https://www.britannica.com/topic/revolution-politics) was a complex series of events in its causes and later in its effects. Its ideas and values—which were associated with such modern [trends](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/trends) as the growth of [liberal democracy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/liberal-democracy), the development of [nationalism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/nationalism), and the rise of socialism—have had worldwide influence.

## Origins of the Revolution

The French Revolution had general causes common to all the revolutions of the West at the end of the 18th century and particular causes that explain why it was by far the most violent and the most universally significant of these revolutions. The first of the general causes was the social structure of the West. The [feudal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/feudalism) regime had been in steady decline since the Middle Ages and had already disappeared in parts of [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe). The increasingly numerous and prosperous elite of wealthy commoners—merchants, manufacturers, and professionals, often called the [bourgeoisie](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bourgeoisie)—aspired to political power in those countries where it did not already possess it. Some of the [peasants](https://www.britannica.com/topic/peasantry), many of whom owned land, had attained an improved standard of living and [education](https://www.britannica.com/topic/education) and wanted to get rid of the last vestiges of feudalism so as to acquire the full rights of landowners and to be free to increase their holdings. About half of the peasants, however, remained poor or landless. Furthermore, from about 1730, higher [standards of living](https://www.britannica.com/money/standard-of-living) had reduced the [mortality](https://www.britannica.com/science/mortality-demography) rate among adults considerably. This, together with other factors, had led to an increase in the population of Europe unprecedented for several centuries: it doubled between 1715 and 1800. For France, which, with 26 million inhabitants in 1789, was the most populated country of Europe, the problem was most [acute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/acute).

A larger population created a greater demand for food and consumer goods. The discovery of new gold mines in [Brazil](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brazil) had led to a general rise in prices throughout the West from about 1730, indicating a prosperous economic situation. From about 1770 this trend slackened, and economic crises, provoking alarm and even revolt, became frequent. Arguments for social reform began to be advanced. The [philosophes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/philosophe)—intellectuals whose writings inspired these arguments—were certainly influenced by 17th-century theorists such as [René Descartes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rene-Descartes), [Benedict de Spinoza](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Benedict-de-Spinoza), and [John Locke](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Locke), but they came to very different conclusions about political, social, and economic matters. A revolution seemed necessary to apply the ideas of [Montesquieu](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Montesquieu), [Voltaire](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Voltaire), or [Jean-Jacques Rousseau](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Jacques-Rousseau). This [Enlightenment](https://www.britannica.com/event/Enlightenment-European-history) was spread among the educated classes by the many “societies of thought” that were founded at that time: [Masonic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Freemasonry) lodges, agricultural societies, and reading rooms.

It is uncertain, however, whether revolution would have come without the added presence of a political crisis. Faced with the heavy expenditure that the wars of the 18th century entailed, the rulers of Europe sought to raise money by taxing the nobles and clergy, who in most countries had hitherto been exempt. To justify this, the rulers likewise [invoked](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/invoked) the arguments of advanced thinkers by adopting the role of “ [enlightened despots](https://www.britannica.com/topic/enlightened-despotism).” This provoked reaction throughout Europe from the privileged bodies, diets, and estates. In [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) this backlash contributed to the [American Revolution](https://www.britannica.com/event/American-Revolution), which began with the refusal to pay a tax imposed by the king of Great Britain. Monarchs tried to stop this reaction of the [aristocracy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/aristocracy), and both rulers and the privileged classes sought allies among the nonprivileged bourgeois and the peasants.

Although scholarly debate continues about the exact causes of the Revolution, the following reasons are commonly adduced: (1) the [bourgeoisie](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/bourgeoisie) resented its exclusion from political power and positions of honor; (2) the peasants were acutely aware of their situation and were less and less willing to support the anachronistic and burdensome feudal system; (3) the philosophes had been read more widely in France than anywhere else; (4) French participation in the [American Revolution](https://www.britannica.com/event/American-Revolution) had driven the government to the brink of [bankruptcy](https://www.britannica.com/money/bankruptcy); (5) France was the most populous country in Europe, and crop failures in much of the country in 1788, coming on top of a long period of economic difficulties, [compounded](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounded) existing restlessness; and (6) the French [monarchy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monarchy), no longer seen as [divinely ordained](https://www.britannica.com/topic/divine-right-of-kings), was unable to adapt to the political and societal pressures that were being exerted on it.

# Aristocratic revolt, 1787–89

The opening stages of the Revolution occurred when the controller general of finances, [Charles-Alexandre de Calonne](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Alexandre-de-Calonne), arranged the summoning of an assembly of “notables” (prelates, great noblemen, and a few representatives of the bourgeoisie) in February 1787 to propose reforms designed to eliminate the budget deficit by increasing the taxation of the privileged classes. The assembly refused to take responsibility for the reforms and suggested the calling of the [Estates-General](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Estates-General), which represented the [clergy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/clergy-Christianity), the [aristocracy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/aristocracy), and the [Third Estate](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Third-Estate) (the commoners) and which had not met since 1614. The efforts made by Calonne’s successors to enforce fiscal reforms in spite of resistance by the privileged classes led to the so-called revolt of the “aristocratic bodies,” notably that of the _[parlements](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Parlement)_ (the most important courts of justice), whose powers were curtailed by the edict of May 1788.

[![Jacques Necker](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/9854-050-32CCAD6F/Jacques-Necker.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/9854-050-32CCAD6F/Jacques-Necker.jpg)

[Jacques Necker](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/9854-050-32CCAD6F/Jacques-Necker.jpg) Jacques Necker, print by Henri Grévedon after a painting by Louis-Joseph-Siffrède Duplessis, 1822; in the Rijksmuseum, Amsterdam.(more)

During the spring and summer of 1788, there was unrest among the populace in [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris), [Grenoble](https://www.britannica.com/place/Grenoble), [Dijon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dijon), [Toulouse](https://www.britannica.com/place/Toulouse-France), [Pau](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pau), and [Rennes](https://www.britannica.com/place/Rennes). The king, [Louis XVI](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-XVI), had to yield. He reappointed reform-minded [Jacques Necker](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jacques-Necker) as the finance minister and promised to [convene](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/convene) the Estates-General on May 5, 1789. He also, in practice, granted freedom of the press, and [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) was flooded with pamphlets addressing the reconstruction of the state. The elections to the Estates-General, held between January and April 1789, coincided with further disturbances, as the harvest of 1788 had been a bad one. There were practically no exclusions from the voting; and the electors drew up _cahiers de doléances_, which listed their [grievances](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/grievances) and hopes. They elected 600 deputies for the Third Estate, 300 for the nobility, and 300 for the clergy.

### What was the cause of World War II?

World War II began in Europe on September 1, 1939, when [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany) invaded Poland. Great Britain and France responded by declaring war on Germany on September 3. The war between the U.S.S.R. and Germany began on June 22, 1941, with [Operation Barbarossa](https://www.britannica.com/event/Operation-Barbarossa), the German invasion of the [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union). The war in the Pacific began on December 7/8, 1941, when [Japan attacked the American naval base at Pearl Harbor](https://www.britannica.com/event/Pearl-Harbor-attack) and other American, Dutch, and British military installations throughout Asia.

### What countries fought in World War II?

The main combatants were the [Axis powers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Axis-Powers) (Germany, Italy, and Japan) and the [Allies](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Allied-Powers-international-alliance) (France, Great Britain, the United States, the Soviet Union, and, to a lesser extent, China).

### Who were the leaders during World War II?

The [Allied powers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Allied-Powers-international-alliance) were led by [Winston Churchill](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Winston-Churchill) (United Kingdom); [Joseph Stalin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Stalin) (Soviet Union); [Charles de Gaulle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-de-Gaulle-president-of-France) (France); and [Franklin D. Roosevelt](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Franklin-D-Roosevelt) and [Harry S. Truman](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Harry-S-Truman) (United States). The [Axis powers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Axis-Powers) were led by [Adolf Hitler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adolf-Hitler) (Germany), [Benito Mussolini](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Benito-Mussolini) (Italy), and [Hideki Tojo](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Tojo-Hideki) (Japan).

### What were the turning points of World War II?

The [war in the Pacific](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pacific-War) turned against Japan during the [Battle of Midway](https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-Midway) (June 3–6, 1942), an American victory that destroyed the Japanese first-line [carrier](https://www.britannica.com/technology/aircraft-carrier) force and, together with the [Battle of Guadalcanal](https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-Guadalcanal), ended Japan’s ability to prosecute an offensive war.

The tide of the war in Europe shifted with the [Soviet](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union) victory at the [Battle of Stalingrad](https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-Stalingrad) (February 1943). More than one million Soviet troops and tens of thousands of civilians died in the defense of the city, but the destruction of two entire German armies marked the beginning of the end of the [Third Reich](https://www.britannica.com/place/Third-Reich).

### How did World War II end?

The [Allied](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Allied-Powers-international-alliance) [landings at Normandy](https://www.britannica.com/event/Normandy-Invasion) on June 6, 1944, opened a second front in Europe, and Germany’s abortive [offensive at the Ardennes](https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-the-Bulge) in the winter of 1944–45 marked the [Third Reich](https://www.britannica.com/place/Third-Reich)’s final push in the west. The [Red Army](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Red-Army) advanced from the east and effectively claimed all the territory under its control for the [Soviet](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union) sphere. The Allied armies converged on Berlin. [Adolf Hitler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adolf-Hitler) committed suicide on April 30, 1945, and the war in Europe ended on May 8.

The American “island hopping” campaign had destroyed key Japanese installations throughout the [Pacific](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pacific-War) while allowing bypassed islands to wither on the vine. Hundreds of thousands were killed in firebombings of Japanese cities, and the [atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki](https://www.britannica.com/event/atomic-bombings-of-Hiroshima-and-Nagasaki) in August 1945 knocked Japan out of the war.

### How many people died during World War II?

Estimates of the total number of people killed during World War II have ranged from 35,000,000 to 60,000,000—a significant span, because statistics about the war’s casualties are inexact. The Soviet Union and China are believed to have suffered the most total casualties, while an estimated 5,800,000 Poles died, which represents about 20 percent of Poland’s prewar population. About 4,200,000 Germans died, and about 1,972,000 Japanese died. In all, the scale of human losses during World War II was vast. **World War II**, conflict that involved virtually every part of the world during the years 1939–45. The war was in many respects a continuation, after an uneasy 20-year hiatus, of the disputes left unsettled by [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I). The principal belligerents were the [Axis powers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Axis-Powers)— [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany), [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy), and [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan)—and the [Allies](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Allied-powers-World-War-II)— [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), [Great Britain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Britain-island-Europe), the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), the [Soviet Union](https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union), and [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) (after the war, these countries would become the five permanent members of the [United Nations Security Council](https://www.britannica.com/topic/United-Nations-Security-Council)). The [total nature](https://www.britannica.com/topic/total-war) of World War II meant that civilian populations not only contributed to the war effort but also became direct targets of [aerial attack](https://www.britannica.com/event/strategic-bombing-during-World-War-II). Moreover, in 1941 the Nazi regime unleashed a war of extermination against Jews, Slavs, and other people deemed inferior by Hitler’s ideology, while Stalinist Russia extended its [campaign of terror against Ukrainians](https://www.britannica.com/event/Holodomor) to the conquered Poles. The 40,000,000–50,000,000 deaths incurred in World War II make it the bloodiest conflict, as well as the largest war, in history.

## The outbreak of war

By the early part of 1939 the [German](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany) dictator [Adolf Hitler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adolf-Hitler) had become determined to invade and occupy [Poland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Poland). Poland, for its part, had guarantees of French and British military support should it be attacked by Germany. Hitler intended to invade Poland anyway, but first he had to neutralize the possibility that the Soviet Union would resist the invasion of its western neighbor. Secret negotiations led on August 23–24 to the signing of the [German-Soviet Nonaggression Pact](https://www.britannica.com/event/German-Soviet-Nonaggression-Pact) in [Moscow](https://www.britannica.com/place/Moscow). In a secret protocol of this pact, the Germans and the Soviets agreed that Poland should be divided between them, with the western third of the country going to Germany and the eastern two-thirds being taken over by the U.S.S.R.

[![Adolf Hitler](https://cdn.britannica.com/56/70056-050-8DD89416/Adolf-Hitler-troops-Eastern-Front-1939.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/56/70056-050-8DD89416/Adolf-Hitler-troops-Eastern-Front-1939.jpg)

[Adolf Hitler](https://cdn.britannica.com/56/70056-050-8DD89416/Adolf-Hitler-troops-Eastern-Front-1939.jpg) Adolf Hitler reviewing German troops in Poland, September 1939.(more)

In September 1939 the [Allies](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Allied-powers-World-War-II), namely Great Britain, France, and Poland, were together superior in industrial resources, population, and military manpower, but the German military, or [Wehrmacht](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Wehrmacht), because of its armament, training, [doctrine](https://www.britannica.com/topic/doctrine), discipline, and fighting spirit, was the most efficient and effective fighting force for its size in the world. The index of military strength in September 1939 was the number of divisions that each nation could mobilize. Against Germany’s 100 infantry divisions and six armoured divisions, France had 90 [infantry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/infantry) divisions in metropolitan France, Great Britain had 10 infantry divisions, and Poland had 30 infantry divisions, 12 [cavalry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cavalry) [brigades](https://www.britannica.com/topic/brigade), and one armoured brigade (Poland had also 30 reserve infantry divisions, but these could not be mobilized quickly). A division contained from 12,000 to 25,000 men.

It was the qualitative superiority of the German infantry divisions and the number of their armoured divisions that made the difference in 1939. The firepower of a German infantry division far exceeded that of a French, British, or Polish division; the standard German division included 442 [machine guns](https://www.britannica.com/technology/machine-gun), 135 [mortars](https://www.britannica.com/technology/mortar-weapon), 72 [antitank](https://www.britannica.com/technology/antitank-weapon) guns, and 24 howitzers. Allied divisions had a firepower only slightly greater than that of World War I. Germany had six armoured divisions in September 1939; the Allies, though they had a large number of tanks, had no armoured divisions at that time.

The six armoured, or [panzer](https://www.britannica.com/topic/panzer-division), divisions of the Wehrmacht comprised some 2,400 tanks. And though Germany would subsequently expand its tank forces during the first years of the war, it was not the number of tanks that Germany had (the Allies had almost as many in September 1939) but the fact of their being organized into divisions and operated as such that was to prove decisive. In accordance with the doctrines of General [Heinz Guderian](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Heinz-Guderian), the German tanks were used in massed formations in conjunction with motorized artillery to punch holes in the enemy line and to isolate segments of the enemy, which were then surrounded and captured by motorized German infantry divisions while the tanks ranged forward to repeat the process: deep drives into enemy territory by panzer divisions were thus followed by mechanized infantry and foot soldiers. These tactics were supported by [dive bombers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dive-bomber) that attacked and disrupted the enemy’s supply and communications lines and spread panic and confusion in its rear, thus further paralyzing its defensive capabilities. Mechanization was the key to the German [blitzkrieg](https://www.britannica.com/topic/blitzkrieg), or “lightning war,” so named because of the unprecedented speed and mobility that were its salient characteristics. Tested and well-trained in maneuvers, the German panzer divisions constituted a force with no equal in Europe.

The value of the French Air Force in 1939 was reduced by the number of obsolescent planes in its order of battle: 131 of the 634 fighters and nearly all of the 463 bombers. [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) was desperately trying to buy high-performance aircraft in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) in 1939.

At sea the odds against Germany were much greater in September 1939 than in August 1914, since the Allies in 1939 had many more large surface warships than Germany had. At sea, however, there was to be no clash between the Allied and the German massed fleets but only the individual operation of German pocket battleships and commerce raiders.

## [Technology of war](https://www.britannica.com/technology/military-technology), 1918–39

[![Maginot Line](https://cdn.britannica.com/90/130590-050-F7CF76C6/entrance-Schoenenbourg-Fort-region-Maginot-Line-Bas-Rhin.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/90/130590-050-F7CF76C6/entrance-Schoenenbourg-Fort-region-Maginot-Line-Bas-Rhin.jpg)

[Maginot Line](https://cdn.britannica.com/90/130590-050-F7CF76C6/entrance-Schoenenbourg-Fort-region-Maginot-Line-Bas-Rhin.jpg) Main entrance to the Schoenenbourg Fort on the Maginot Line, Bas-Rhin department, Alsace region, France.(more)

When [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I) ended, the experience of it seemed to vindicate the power of the defensive over the offensive. It was widely believed that a superiority in numbers of at least three to one was required for a successful offensive. Defensive concepts underlay the construction of the [Maginot Line](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maginot-Line) between France and Germany and of its lesser counterpart, the [Siegfried Line](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Siegfried-Line), in the interwar years. Yet by 1918 both of the requirements for the supremacy of the offensive were at hand: [tanks](https://www.britannica.com/technology/tank-military-vehicle) and [planes](https://www.britannica.com/technology/airplane). The battles of [Cambrai](https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-Cambrai-1917) (1917) and [Amiens](https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-Amiens) (1918) had proved that when tanks were used in masses, with surprise, and on firm and open terrain, it was possible to break through any [trench](https://www.britannica.com/topic/trench-warfare) system.

The Germans learned this crucial, though subtle, lesson from World War I. The Allies on the other hand felt that their victory confirmed their methods, weapons, and leadership, and in the interwar period the French and British armies were slow to introduce new weapons, methods, and doctrines. Consequently, in 1939 the British Army did not have a single armoured division, and the French tanks were distributed in small packets throughout the infantry divisions. The Germans, by contrast, began to develop large tank formations on an effective basis after their rearmament program began in 1935.

In the air the technology of war had also changed radically between 1918 and 1939. [Military aircraft](https://www.britannica.com/technology/military-aircraft) had increased in size, speed, and range, and for operations at sea, [aircraft carriers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/aircraft-carrier) were developed that were capable of accompanying the fastest surface ships. Among the new types of planes developed was the [dive bomber](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dive-bomber), a plane designed for accurate low-altitude bombing of enemy strong points as part of the tank-plane-infantry combination. Fast low-wing [monoplane](https://www.britannica.com/technology/monoplane) fighters were developed in all countries; these aircraft were essentially flying platforms for eight to 12 [machine guns](https://www.britannica.com/technology/machine-gun) installed in the wings. Light and medium [bombers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bomber-aircraft) were also developed that could be used for the [strategic bombardment of cities and military strongpoints](https://www.britannica.com/topic/strategic-bombing). The threat of bomber attacks on both military and civilian targets led directly to the development of [radar](https://www.britannica.com/technology/radar) in [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England). Radar made it possible to determine the location, the distance, and the height and speed of a distant aircraft no matter what the weather was. By December 1938 there were five radar stations established on the coast of England, and 15 additional stations were begun. So, when war came in September 1939, Great Britain had a warning chain of radar stations that could tell when hostile planes were approaching.

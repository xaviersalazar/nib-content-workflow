**Mongol empire**, [empire](https://www.britannica.com/topic/empire-political-science) founded by [Genghis Khan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Genghis-Khan) in 1206. Originating from the [Mongol](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mongol) heartland in the [Steppe](https://www.britannica.com/place/the-Steppe) of central [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia), by the late 13th century it spanned from the [Pacific Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean) in the east to the [Danube River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Danube-River) and the shores of the [Persian Gulf](https://www.britannica.com/place/Persian-Gulf) in the west. At its peak, it covered some 9 million square miles (23 million square km) of territory, making it the largest [contiguous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contiguous) land empire in [world history](https://www.britannica.com/topic/world-history).

## Origin and growth

[![Genghis Khan](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/121673-050-4FE5679B/Genghis-Khan-ink-colour-silk-Taipei-Taiwan.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/121673-050-4FE5679B/Genghis-Khan-ink-colour-silk-Taipei-Taiwan.jpg)

[Genghis Khan](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/121673-050-4FE5679B/Genghis-Khan-ink-colour-silk-Taipei-Taiwan.jpg) Genghis Khan, ink and colour on silk; in the National Palace Museum, Taipei, Taiwan.(more)

The year 1206, when Temüjin, son of Yesügei, was elected Genghis Khan of a federation of tribes on the banks of the Onon River, must be regarded as the beginning of the Mongol empire. This federation not only consisted of Mongols in the proper sense—that is, [Mongol-speaking](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mongol-language) tribes—but also other [Turkic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Turkic-peoples) tribes. Before 1206 Genghis Khan was but one of the tribal leaders fighting for supremacy in the steppe regions south and southeast of [Lake Baikal](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lake-Baikal); his victories over the Kereit and then the Naiman Turks, however, gave him undisputed authority over the whole of what is now [Mongolia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mongolia). A series of campaigns, some of them carried out simultaneously, followed.

## Initial conquests

[![Jiaohe](https://cdn.britannica.com/99/137499-050-3B5A1B0D/Portion-ruins-city-Jiaohe-China-Uygur-Autonomous.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/99/137499-050-3B5A1B0D/Portion-ruins-city-Jiaohe-China-Uygur-Autonomous.jpg)

[Jiaohe](https://cdn.britannica.com/99/137499-050-3B5A1B0D/Portion-ruins-city-Jiaohe-China-Uygur-Autonomous.jpg) Portion of the ruins of the ancient city of Jiaohe, Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region, China. The city lay along the ancient Silk Road and was destroyed by Genghis Khan in the 13th century.(more)

The first attack (1205–09) was directed against the [Tangut](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tangut) kingdom of Hsi Hsia ( [Xi Xia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Xi-Xia)), a northwestern border-state of [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China), and ended in a declaration of [allegiance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/allegiance) by the Xi Xia king. A subsequent campaign was aimed at north China, which at that time was ruled by the Tungusic [Jin dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jin-dynasty-China-Mongolia-1115-1234). The fall of [Beijing](https://www.britannica.com/place/Beijing) in 1215 marked the loss of all the territory north of the [Huang He](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yellow-River) (Yellow River) to the Mongols; during the following years the Jin empire was reduced to the role of a buffer state between the Mongols in the north and the Chinese [Song empire](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Song-dynasty) in the south. Other campaigns were launched against central Asia. In 1218 the Khara-Khitai state in east [Turkistan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Turkistan) was absorbed into the empire.

The assassination of [Muslim](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam) subjects of Genghis Khan by the Khwārezmians in Otrar led to a war with the sultanate of [Khwārezm](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Khwarezm-Shah-dynasty) (Khiva) in west Turkistan (1219–25). [Bukhara](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bukhara), [Samarkand](https://www.britannica.com/place/Samarkand-Uzbekistan), and the capital [Urgench](https://www.britannica.com/place/Urgench-Uzbekistan) were taken and sacked by Mongol armies (1220–21). Advance troops (after crossing the [Caucasus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Caucasus)) even penetrated into southern [Russia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Russia) and raided cities in [Crimea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Crimea) (1223). The once prosperous region of Khwārezm suffered for centuries from the effects of the Mongol invasion which brought about not only the destruction of the prosperous towns but also the disintegration of the [irrigation system](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation) on which agriculture in those parts depended. A similarly destructive campaign was launched against Xi Xia in 1226–27 because the Xi Xia king had refused to assist the Mongols in their expedition against Khwārezm. The death of Genghis Khan during that campaign (1227) increased the vindictiveness of the Mongols. The Xi Xia [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture), a mixture of Chinese and Tibetan elements, with [Buddhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism) as the state [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion), was virtually [annihilated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/annihilated).

[![Cai Wenji](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/59678-050-E965D32E/encampment-Mongol-detail-scroll-Cai-Wenji-Chinese.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/59678-050-E965D32E/encampment-Mongol-detail-scroll-Cai-Wenji-Chinese.jpg)

[Cai Wenji](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/59678-050-E965D32E/encampment-Mongol-detail-scroll-Cai-Wenji-Chinese.jpg) A Mongol encampment, detail from the Cai Wenji scroll, a Chinese hand scroll of the Nan (Southern) Song dynasty.(more)

In 1227 the Mongol dominions stretched over the vast regions between the [Caspian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Caspian-Sea) and [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China-Sea) seas, bordering in the north on the sparsely populated forest belt of [Siberia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Siberia) and in the south on the [Pamirs](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pamirs), [Tibet](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tibet), and the central plains of China. This empire contained a multitude of different peoples, religions, and civilizations, and it is only natural to seek the motivating force behind this unparalleled expansion. Certainly the traditional antagonism between pastoral, nomadic steppe-dwellers and settled agricultural civilizations has to be taken into account. Raids by nomads from the steppe had always occurred from time to time wherever powerful nomadic tribes lived in the proximity of settled populations, but they had not usually taken on the dimensions of a bid for world [hegemony](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hegemony) or domination as in the case of Genghis Khan’s invasions.

![Silk Road](https://cdn.britannica.com/83/193183-050-A10F4603/Silk-Road.jpg)

[Silk Road](https://cdn.britannica.com/83/193183-050-A10F4603/Silk-Road.jpg)(more)

The idea of a heavenly mission to rule the world was certainly present in Genghis Khan’s own mind and in the minds of many of his successors, but this ideological imperialism had no foundation in [nomadic society](https://www.britannica.com/topic/nomadism) as such. It was most probably due to influences from China where the “one world, one ruler” [ideology](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ideology) had a long tradition. The creation of nomad empires in the steppes and the attempts to extend their rule over the more settled parts of central Asia and finally over the whole known world may also have been influenced by the desire to control the routes of intercontinental land trade. The desire for plunder also cannot be ignored, and it was certainly not by accident that the first attacks by nomad federations were usually directed against those states which benefited from the control of trade routes in central Asia such as the famous [Silk Road](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Silk-Road-trade-route).

## Military practices

[![Rashīd al-Dīn: Mongol warriors from History of the World](https://cdn.britannica.com/21/43321-050-7E84E9E2/miniature-warriors-Mongol-Rashid-al-Din-History-of.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/21/43321-050-7E84E9E2/miniature-warriors-Mongol-Rashid-al-Din-History-of.jpg)

[Rashīd al-Dīn: Mongol warriors from _History of the World_](https://cdn.britannica.com/21/43321-050-7E84E9E2/miniature-warriors-Mongol-Rashid-al-Din-History-of.jpg) Mongol warriors, miniature from Rashīd al-Dīn's _History of the World_, 1307; in the Edinburgh University Library, Scotland.(more)

The amazing military achievements of the Mongols under Genghis Khan and his [successors](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/successors) were due to superior [strategy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/strategy-military) and [tactics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tactics) rather than to numerical strength. Mongol armies were chiefly composed of [cavalry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cavalry) which afforded them a high degree of mobility and speed. Their movements and maneuvers were directed by signals and a well-organized messenger service. In battle they relied mainly on bows and arrows and resorted to man-to-man fighting only after having disorganized the enemy’s ranks. Mongol armaments and tactics were more suited to open plains and flat countries than to mountainous and wooded regions. For the [siege](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/siege) of walled cities they frequently secured assistance from artisans and engineers of technically advanced conquered peoples such as Chinese, Persians, and Arabs.

Another factor contributing to the overwhelming success of their expeditions was the skilful use of spies and [propaganda](https://www.britannica.com/topic/propaganda). Before attacking they usually asked for voluntary surrender and offered peace. If this was accepted, the population was spared. If, however, resistance had to be overcome, wholesale slaughter or at least [enslavement](https://www.britannica.com/topic/slavery-sociology) invariably resulted, sparing only those whose special skills or abilities were considered useful. In the case of voluntary surrender, tribesmen or soldiers were often incorporated into the Mongol forces and treated as federates. Personal loyalty of federate rulers to the Mongol [khan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/khan-title) played a great role, as normally no formal treaties were concluded. The “Mongol” armies, therefore, often consisted of only a minority of ethnic Mongols.

# Organization of [Genghis Khan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Genghis-Khan)’s empire

[![Genghis Khan](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/159895-050-AB367B08/Detail-statue-Genghis-Khan-Mongolia-Ulaanbaatar.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/159895-050-AB367B08/Detail-statue-Genghis-Khan-Mongolia-Ulaanbaatar.jpg)

[Genghis Khan](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/159895-050-AB367B08/Detail-statue-Genghis-Khan-Mongolia-Ulaanbaatar.jpg) Detail of a statue of Genghis Khan, near Ulaanbaatar (Ulan Bator), Mongolia.(more)

During the early stages of [Mongol](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mongol) [supremacy](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/supremacy), the [empire](https://www.britannica.com/topic/empire-political-science) established by Genghis absorbed civilizations in which a strong, unified, and well-organized state power had developed. The social organization of the Mongols was, however, characterized by pastoralism and a decentralized patrilineal system of clans. Antagonism existed between a society of this nature and the subjugated advanced civilizations, between a relatively small number of foreign conquerors and a numerically strong conquered population. In the early phases of conquest, the Mongols usually attempted to impose the social structure of the steppes upon their new subjects. It was customary for the Mongols to enslave a conquered tribe and to present whole [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) to distinguished military leaders as a sort of personal appanage. These slaves became sooner or later an [integral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integral) part of the conquering tribe. In the conquered areas a similar procedure was adopted. Groups of the settled population, usually those living in a certain territory, became the personal property of Mongol military leaders who exploited the local economic forces as they liked. No use was made of the existing state machinery or [bureaucracy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/bureaucracy), and the former political divisions were entirely disregarded. Nor was there any attempt to organize the numerous local Mongol leaders who enjoyed a high degree of independence from the court of the khans. Ruthless exploitation under strong military pressure was therefore characteristic of the early phase of Mongol domination, which may be said to have lasted until about 1234, some seven years after Genghis Khan’s death.

The central power rested with the [khan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/khan-title), who was assisted by military and political councilors. No departmental administration was, however, established during the early stages of Genghis Khan’s empire. The highly hierarchized military organization of the Mongols had no political or administrative counterpart. The influence of the councilors, who were appointed by the khan regardless of their nationality, was nevertheless great. It was a former Jin subject, the [Khitan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Khitan-people) [Yelü Chucai](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Yelu-Chucai) (1190–1244), a man of high talents with an excellent Chinese education, who [dissuaded](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/dissuaded) Genghis from converting the whole of north [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) into pastureland. Other councilors were [Uyghurs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Uyghur), and for some time the [Uyghur language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Uyghur-language) was as much used in the court chancery as Mongol. The Uyghur script was also adopted for writing Mongol. The oldest known document in the [Mongol language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mongol-language) is a stone inscription carved in approximately 1224.

The economy of the conquered areas was not properly organized during the period of conquest. The abolition of highly organized governments gave an opportunity for the exploitation of local production by the Mongol appanage-holders who relied to a great extent on non-Mongol tax-farmers. There was no single financial system for the whole empire or even for large parts of it. The absence of civil organization at the top, the great independence of the various appanages, and the high priority accorded to military affairs had a strongly disintegrating effect and were, at least in the early phases of Mongol rule, [detrimental](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/detrimental) to economic progress and prosperity. The Mongol empire was, under Genghis and his successors, not yet a state in the normal sense of the word but a vast agglomeration of widely different territories held together by military domination.

As the empire grew through new conquests after Genghis’s death, the same pattern repeated itself: a period of military, and at the same time decentralized, rule marked the first stage of Mongol domination. The result was a noticeable variation of practice within the empire. Newly conquered areas were still subject to direct exploitation [bearing](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/bearing) the imprint of a nomadic and military mentality, but, in those areas which had been subjugated earlier, attempts were made to build up a state machinery and bureaucracy in order to consolidate Mongol rule. This was done mostly in accordance with the traditional administrative system of the individual territory.

This general tendency, together with the absence of an original Mongol concept for ruling a settled population, accounts for the entirely different development that occurred in various countries. This resulted in an empire that may not have been “Mongol” but was a Chinese, Persian, or central Asian empire with a [Mongol dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yuan-dynasty). This [trend](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/trend) was expressed more in some locations than others because the absorptive power of the various civilizations differed in intensity. In China, for instance, the Mongols could maintain their rule better than elsewhere because the strong Chinese tradition of centralized state power supplied a stable framework of governmental organization.

The original absence of a state concept on the part of the Mongols is reflected in the ruling clan’s attitude to the empire. The empire was considered to be not the khan’s personal property but the heirloom of the imperial clan as a whole. Already in Genghis’s lifetime the empire was divided among his four favourite sons into _ulus_, a Mongol word which denotes the supremacy over a certain number of tribes rather than a clearly defined territory. Tolui, the youngest, received the eastern part—the original homeland of the Mongols together with the [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) parts of north China. [Ögödei](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ogodei) became ruler of the western part of the steppes (modern northern [Xinjiang](https://www.britannica.com/place/Xinjiang) and western Mongolia). [Chagatai](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Chagatai) received the lands of Khara-Khitai (modern northern [Iran](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iran) and southern Xinjiang). The eldest son, [Jöchi](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jochi), followed by his son [Batu](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Batu-Mongol-ruler), ruled over southwest [Siberia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Siberia) and west Turkistan (an area later known as the territory of the [Golden Horde](https://www.britannica.com/place/Golden-Horde)). To these four Mongol empires a fifth was added when [Hülegü](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hulegu), a son of Tolui, completed the conquest of Iran, [Iraq](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iraq), and [Syria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Syria) and became the founder of the [Il-Khanid dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Il-Khanid-dynasty) in Iran. The [unity](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/unity) of the Mongol empire was therefore from the beginning undermined by disintegrating factors, and the history of the empire after Genghis’s death may consequently be subdivided into two periods, the first being characterized by relative unity in the empire ruled by a great khan who was recognized by all branches of the royal clan, the second showing a more or less complete independence of the separate empires, which thereafter had no common history.

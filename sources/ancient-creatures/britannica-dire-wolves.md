**dire wolf**, ( _Aenocyon dirus_), [canine](https://www.britannica.com/animal/canine) that existed during the [Pleistocene Epoch](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pleistocene-Epoch) (2.6 million to 11,700 years ago). It is probably the most common mammalian [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) to be found preserved in the [La Brea Tar Pits](https://www.britannica.com/place/La-Brea-Tar-Pits) in southern [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state). The [dire](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/dire) wolf differed from the modern [gray wolf](https://www.britannica.com/animal/gray-wolf) ( _Canis lupus_) in several ways: it was larger and it had a more massive [skull](https://www.britannica.com/science/skull), a smaller [brain](https://www.britannica.com/science/brain), and larger teeth than modern wolves, with relatively light limbs. Standing 96.5 cm (38 inches) tall at the shoulder, many dire wolves grew up to 178 cm (70 inches) in length and weighed between 59 and 68 kg (roughly 130 and 150 pounds). The species was considerably widespread throughout [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) and parts of western [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America), and skeletal remains have been found in [Florida](https://www.britannica.com/place/Florida), the [Mississippi River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mississippi-River) valley, the Valley of [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico), [Bolivia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bolivia), [Peru](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru), and [Venezuela](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venezuela).

[![Dire wolf](https://cdn.britannica.com/10/12310-004-384CF25F/wolf-detail-mural-Rancho-La-Brea-Calif-1922.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/10/12310-004-384CF25F/wolf-detail-mural-Rancho-La-Brea-Calif-1922.jpg)

[Dire wolf](https://cdn.britannica.com/10/12310-004-384CF25F/wolf-detail-mural-Rancho-La-Brea-Calif-1922.jpg) Dire wolf ( _Aenocyon dirus_, also known as _Canis dirus_) from Rancho La Brea, California; detail of a mural by Charles R. Knight, 1922.(more)

The [lineage](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/lineage) that included the dire wolves and their ancestors emerged some 5.7 million years ago during the late [Miocene Epoch](https://www.britannica.com/science/Miocene-Epoch) (which lasted from 23 million to about 5.3 million years ago), and it evolved independently of the lineage that produced modern wolves and their close relatives. Genetic studies report that dire wolves, which first appeared in North America, are more closely related to the [jackals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/jackal) and [African wild dogs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/African-hunting-dog) of Africa and [dholes](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dhole) of eastern and southern [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia) than they are to their modern counterparts from the [Americas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Americas). So far, there is no evidence that they interbred with [gray wolves](https://www.britannica.com/animal/gray-wolf) (which originated several million years later in [Eurasia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Eurasia) before dispersing to North America).

Did scientists really bring dire wolves back from extinction?Breaking news: Dire wolves are still extinct. (Sorry.)(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dire-wolf/images-videos)

The [biotechnology](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biotechnology) company Colossal Biosciences announced in 2025 that it had brought back the dire wolf by altering parts of the gray wolf genome—that is, the coding regions of DNA, which encode all the genes ( _see also_ [de-extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/de-extinction)). The company claimed that technicians making 20 edits to 14 [genes](https://www.britannica.com/science/gene) in gray wolf [embryos](https://www.britannica.com/science/embryo-human-and-animal) and implanting them in [dog](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dog) surrogates resulted in three pups with dire wolf [fur](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fur-animal-skin) color and fur texture, noting that these limited edits were enough to support their claim that they had resurrected the species ( _see also_ [gene editing](https://www.britannica.com/science/gene-editing)). Other geneticists working in [academia](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/academia) and elsewhere remained skeptical; however, they argued that bringing back the species would require thousands of changes to thousands of genes and that the company’s announcement was premature.

[La Brea Tar Pits, Los Angeles, California](https://cdn.britannica.com/82/129882-050-3A55EA92/Tar-bubble-La-Brea-Pits-Calif-Los.jpg) Tar bubble in the La Brea Tar Pits, Los Angeles, California, U.S.(more)

**La Brea Tar Pits**, [tar](https://www.britannica.com/science/coal-tar) (Spanish _brea_) pits, in Hancock Park (Rancho La Brea), [Los Angeles](https://www.britannica.com/place/Los-Angeles-California), [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state), U.S. The area was the site of “pitch springs” oozing [crude oil](https://www.britannica.com/science/crude-oil) that was used by local Indians for waterproofing. [Gaspar de Portolá](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gaspar-de-Portola)’s expedition in 1769 explored the area, which [encompasses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompasses) about 20 acres (8 hectares). The tar pits are thick, sticky pools of viscous [asphalt](https://www.britannica.com/science/asphalt-material) (the lowest grade of crude oil) that has oozed to the surface from a large petroleum reservoir. They have yielded the fossilized skulls and bones of trapped prehistoric animals as well as one partial [human skeleton](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skeleton) and many human [artifacts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/artifacts). Until the 1870s, scientists studying the tar pits believed that the animals found trapped in the tar were of recent origin. Interest in the area became intense at the turn of the 20th century, however, when the remains of an extinct giant ground sloth were found. Excavations, which continue, have also uncovered the remains of other Pleistocene mammals such as the imperial [mammoth](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mammoth-extinct-mammal), the mastodon, the short-faced bear, the [sabre-toothed cat](https://www.britannica.com/animal/saber-toothed-cat) (California’s official state fossil), and the camel; seeds, insects, and [fish](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fish) have also been unearthed. Park exhibits include life-size figures of many such long-extinct creatures and an observation pit. The Page Museum at the La Brea Tar Pits, an arm of the Natural History Museum of Los Angeles County, contains more than one million prehistoric [specimens](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/specimens) exhumed from the pits.

### What did woolly mammoths eat?

Woolly mammoths were [herbivores](https://www.britannica.com/science/herbivore) (plant eaters) that ate the stems and leaves of tundra [plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant) and shrubs. Their teeth were made up of alternating plates of [enamel](https://www.britannica.com/science/enamel-tooth) and a denture that often became worn down by constant back-to-front chewing motions.

### Why did woolly mammoths go extinct?

Woolly mammoths went extinct due to a warming [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology) that reduced their [habitat](https://www.britannica.com/science/habitat-biology) and hunting by [humans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being). Scientific evidence suggests that small [populations](https://www.britannica.com/science/population-biology-and-anthropology) of woolly mammoths may have survived in mainland [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) until between 10,500 and 7,600 years ago. Other evidence suggests that woolly mammoths persisted until 5,600 years ago on St. Paul Island, in Alaska’s [Pribilof Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pribilof-Islands), in the [Bering Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bering-Sea) and as late as 4,300 years ago on [Wrangel Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wrangel-Island) in the [Chukchi Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chukchi-Sea) when they ultimately succumbed to the effects of [inbreeding](https://www.britannica.com/science/inbreeding) and loss of genetic diversity.

### How are woolly mammoths related to modern elephants?

Woolly mammoths are closely related to present-day [Asian elephants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Asian-elephant). They evolved from steppe mammoths between 800,000 and 600,000 years ago, with their last common ancestor with Asian elephants existing about six million years ago.

### How large is a woolly mammoth?

Woolly mammoths stood about 3 to 3.7 meters (about 10 to 12 feet) tall and weighed between 5,500 and 7,300 kg (between about 6 and 8 tons).

### Are scientists bringing back the woolly mammoth?

At least one [biotechnology](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biotechnology) company is attempting to bring back the woolly mammoth using samples collected from woolly mammoth remains. From the remains of frozen carcasses and teeth, scientists have sequenced woolly mammoth DNA. Some have suggested that advances in [genetics](https://www.britannica.com/science/genetics) and reproductive [cloning](https://www.britannica.com/science/cloning) technologies since the 1990s could allow scientists to resurrect the [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) ( [de-extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/de-extinction)). Colossal Biosciences—the biotechnology company that claimed to have resurrected the [dire wolf](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dire-wolf) in 2025—is attempting to bring back the woolly mammoth by modifying the [genes](https://www.britannica.com/science/gene) in the [DNA](https://www.britannica.com/science/DNA) of an [Asian elephant](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Asian-elephant). Critics argue that while the animal that results may have some physical characteristics in common with the woolly mammoth (such as a shaggy coat), it would be little more than a slightly modified Asian elephant.

**woolly mammoth**, ( _Mammuthus primigenius_), extinct [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) of [elephant](https://www.britannica.com/animal/elephant-mammal) found in [fossil](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil) deposits of the [Pleistocene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pleistocene-Epoch) and [Holocene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Holocene-Epoch) epochs (from about 2.6 million years ago to the present) in [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe), northern [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia), and [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America). The woolly [mammoth](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mammoth-extinct-mammal) was known for its large size, [fur](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fur-animal-skin), and imposing tusks. Thriving during the Pleistocene [ice ages](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice-age-geology), woolly mammoths died out after much of their [habitat](https://www.britannica.com/science/habitat-biology) was lost as [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth)’s [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology) warmed in the aftermath of the last ice age. The species is named for the appearance of its long thick coat of fur.

## Natural history

[![woolly mammoth (Mammuthus primigenius)](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/219289-050-FCC279FE/Woolly-Mammoth-Skeleton-Extinct-National-Museum-of-Natural-History-Smithsonian.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/219289-050-FCC279FE/Woolly-Mammoth-Skeleton-Extinct-National-Museum-of-Natural-History-Smithsonian.jpg)

[woolly mammoth ( _Mammuthus primigenius_)](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/219289-050-FCC279FE/Woolly-Mammoth-Skeleton-Extinct-National-Museum-of-Natural-History-Smithsonian.jpg) Woolly mammoths ( _Mammuthus primigenius_) stood about 3 to 3.7 metres (about 10 to 12 feet) tall and weighed between 5,500 and 7,300 kg (between about 6 and 8 tons).(more)

Woolly mammoths stood about 3 to 3.7 meters (about 10 to 12 feet) tall and weighed between 5,500 and 7,300 kg (between about 6 and 8 tons). They had a yellowish brown undercoat about 2.5 cm (about 1 inch) thick beneath a coarser outer covering of dark brown hair that grew more than 70 cm (27.5 inches) long in some individuals. Under the extremely thick skin was a layer of insulating [fat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fat) at times 8 cm (3 inches) thick. Its skull was high and domelike, with large downward-directed curved tusks. The woolly mammoth’s [teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/tooth-anatomy) were made up of alternating plates of [enamel](https://www.britannica.com/science/enamel-tooth) and a denture that often became worn down by constant back-to-front chewing motions. The woolly mammoth’s ears were small, which exposed a smaller amount of surface area and was likely an [adaptation](https://www.britannica.com/science/adaptation-biology-and-physiology) to the cold climates in the Northern Hemisphere. A mound of fat, which served as an [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) and [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) reserve, was present as a hump on the back.

The woolly mammoth lived in [steppe](https://www.britannica.com/place/the-Steppe) [tundra](https://www.britannica.com/science/tundra) habitat (also called mammoth steppe, an [ecosystem](https://www.britannica.com/science/ecosystem) made up of low [shrubs](https://www.britannica.com/plant/shrub), sedges, and [grasses](https://www.britannica.com/plant/grass)), which was widespread across Eurasia and North America during the Pleistocene, but there is some evidence that some populations also inhabited [forests](https://www.britannica.com/science/forest) of the present-day [Midwestern United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/Midwest). The woolly mammoth was herbivorous, consuming the [stems](https://www.britannica.com/science/stem-plant) and [leaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy) of tundra [plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant) and shrubs.

Mammoths are closely related to present-day [Asian elephants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Asian-elephant) ( _Elephas maximus_), and these groups broke away from their last common ancestor about six million years ago. Morphological and genetic studies suggest that woolly mammoths evolved from steppe mammoths ( _Mammuthus trogontherii_) between about 800,000 and 600,000 years ago in Asia. Genetic evidence suggests that woolly mammoths spread to Europe about 200,000 years ago and from Asia across the [Bering Land Bridge](https://www.britannica.com/place/Beringia) to North America about 125,000 years ago.

## Extinction

Woolly mammoths were largely [extinct](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology) by about 10,000 years ago, due to the pressures of a warming climate (which reduced the habitat of these cold-adapted mammals) combined with hunting by humans. Scientific evidence suggests that small populations of woolly mammoths may have survived in mainland North America until between 10,500 and 7,600 years ago. Other evidence suggests that woolly mammoths persisted until 5,600 years ago on [St. Paul Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Saint-Paul-Island-Alaska), [Alaska](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alaska), in the [Bering Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bering-Sea) and as late as 4,300 years ago on [Wrangel Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wrangel-Island), an [Arctic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arctic) island located off the coast of northern [Russia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Russia), before succumbing to extinction from [inbreeding](https://www.britannica.com/science/inbreeding) and loss of genetic diversity.

[woolly mammoth](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/167271-050-7A2425C3/Lyuba-mammoth-display-Kalinigrad-Russia-Siberia.jpg) Lyuba, a well-preserved female woolly mammoth ( _Mammuthus primigenius_), on display at the Field Museum, Chicago. It was about one month old when it died in Siberia about 42,000 years ago. It was discovered in Russia in 2007.(more)

The woolly mammoth is by far the best-known of all mammoths. The relative abundance and, at times, excellent preservation of carcasses of this species found in the [permafrost](https://www.britannica.com/science/permafrost) (permanently frozen ground) of [Siberia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Siberia) have provided much information about mammoths’ structure and habits. These carcasses are so well preserved that sled dogs have been fed thawed woolly mammoth [meat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/meat) dating to more than 30,000 years ago, and fossil mammoth ivory was previously so abundant that it was exported from Siberia to [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China) and Europe from medieval times.

## DNA sequencing and potential de-extinction

These remains and fossils of teeth have allowed scientists to collect and sequence woolly mammoth [DNA](https://www.britannica.com/science/DNA). Some have suggested that advances in [genetics](https://www.britannica.com/science/genetics) and reproductive [cloning](https://www.britannica.com/science/cloning) technologies since the 1990s could allow scientists to resurrect the woolly mammoth ( _see also_ [de-extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/de-extinction)). The oldest preserved mammoth DNA, which also has the distinction of being the oldest known [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) DNA, dates back to more than one million years ago and may belong to a direct ancestor of the woolly mammoth.

The [woolly mammoth](https://www.britannica.com/animal/woolly-mammoth) ( _Mammuthus primigenius_) was a massive creature, standing about 3 to 3.7 meters (about 10 to 12 feet) tall. Its towering stature was complemented by its hefty weight, which ranged between 5,500 and 7,300 kg (approximately 6 to 8 tons). Such dimensions make the woolly mammoth comparable in size to modern-day [elephants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/elephant-mammal).

Apart from its size, the woolly mammoth also had several distinctive features. It was well-adapted to the cold climates of the [Pleistocene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pleistocene-Epoch) [ice ages](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice-age-geology). It had a yellowish-brown undercoat about 2.5 cm (about 1 inch) thick, covered by a coarser layer of dark brown hair that could grow more than 70 cm (27.5 inches) long. Beneath its thick skin, a layer of fat up to 8 cm (3 inches) thick provided additional insulation, helping the mammoth survive in frigid environments. The mammoth’s ears were relatively small, an adaptation that minimized heat loss in cold climates. The hump of [fat](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fat) on its back functioned as a reservoir of [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) and [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water). It had a high and domelike skull, with large, downward-curving tusks that could be used for foraging and defense.

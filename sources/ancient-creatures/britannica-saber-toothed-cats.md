- What is a sabre-toothed cat?







A [sabre-toothed cat](https://www.britannica.com/animal/saber-toothed-cat/Introduction#ref1) is any member of the extinct carnivores belonging to either the family Nimravidae or the subfamily Machairodontinae of the cat family (Felidae). They are named for their elongated, bladelike canine teeth in their upper jaw.



The best-known genus of sabre-toothed cats is _Smilodon_, also known as the "sabre-toothed tiger." They existed from the Eocene through the Pleistocene Epoch (56 million to 11,700 years ago). _Smilodon_ was a large, short-limbed cat that lived in North and South America during the Pleistocene Epoch and was about the size of a modern African lion. They used their long upper canine teeth (up to 20 cm long) for stabbing and slashing attacks on large herbivores.

- When and where did sabre-toothed cats live?







Sabre-toothed cats, known for their elongated bladelike canine teeth, existed from the Eocene through the Pleistocene Epoch (56 million to 11,700 years ago). These carnivores belonged to either the extinct family Nimravidae or the subfamily Machairodontinae of the cat family (Felidae).



Nimravidae existed from about 37 million to 7 million years ago and include the genera _Hoplophoneus_, _Nimravus_, _Dinictis_, and _Barbourofelis_. The Machairodontinae, which are more closely related to felids, existed from about 12 million to less than 10,000 years ago, and include the genera _Smilodon_, _Homotherium_, and _Meganteron_.

- Why did sabre-toothed cats become extinct?







Sabre-toothed cats, which existed from the Eocene through the Pleistocene Epoch (56 million to 11,700 years ago), eventually went extinct. The extinction pattern of the last of these cats closely followed that of the mastodons. Sabre-toothed cats died out in the Old World during the late Pliocene, as did the mastodons. However, in North and South America, where mastodons persisted throughout the Pleistocene, sabre-toothed cats continued successfully to the end of the epoch.



Several hypotheses exist regarding the extinction of large mammals. These include habitat loss or change, direct climatic effects, changes in summer and winter conditions, and the "prehistoric overkill hypothesis," which blames human hunting for the demise of large animals wherever humans arrived.

**sabre-toothed cat**, any of the [extinct](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology) catlike [carnivores](https://www.britannica.com/animal/carnivore-mammal) belonging to either the extinct family Nimravidae or the subfamily Machairodontinae of the cat family ( [Felidae](https://www.britannica.com/animal/feline)). Named for the pair of elongated bladelike [canine teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/canine-tooth) in their upper [jaw](https://www.britannica.com/science/jaw), they are often called sabre-toothed tigers or sabre-toothed lions, although the modern [lion](https://www.britannica.com/animal/lion) and [tiger](https://www.britannica.com/animal/tiger) are true cats of the subfamily Felinae.

Sabre-toothed cats existed from the [Eocene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Eocene-Epoch) through the [Pleistocene Epoch](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pleistocene-Epoch) (56 million to 11,700 years ago). According to the [fossil record](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil-record), the Nimravidae were [extant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/extant) from about 37 million to 7 million years ago. Only distantly related to felids, they include the genera _Hoplophoneus_, _Nimravus_, _Dinictis_, and _Barbourofelis_. The Machairodontinae, extant from about 12 million to less than 10,000 years ago, include the more familiar _Smilodon_ as well as _Homotherium_ and _Meganteron_. Sabre-toothed cats roamed [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) and [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) throughout the [Miocene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Miocene-Epoch) and [Pliocene epochs](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pliocene-Epoch) (23 million to 2.6 million years ago). By Pliocene times, they had spread to [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia) and [Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Africa). During the Pleistocene, sabre-toothed cats were also present in [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America).

The most widely known genus of sabre-toothed cats is _[Smilodon](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Smilodon)_, the “sabre-toothed tiger.” A large, short-limbed cat that lived in North and South America during the Pleistocene [Epoch](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Epoch), it was about the size of the modern African lion ( _Panthera leo_) and represents the peak of sabre-tooth [evolution](https://www.britannica.com/science/evolution-scientific-theory). Its immense upper [canine teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/canine-tooth), up to 20 cm (8 inches) long, were probably used for stabbing and slashing attacks, possibly on large [herbivores](https://www.britannica.com/science/herbivore) such as the [mastodon](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mastodon). Several physical [adaptations](https://www.britannica.com/science/adaptation-biology-and-physiology) of _Smilodon_ suggest such a [hunting](https://www.britannica.com/sports/hunting-sport) technique: its skull was modified to accommodate the attachment of strong neck muscles for bringing the head down; the lower canines were reduced; and the molars formed shearing blades with no trace of grinding surfaces. In addition, the jaw could be opened to about a 90° angle to free the upper canines for action; however, some paleontologists suggest that since the muscles in the jaw would have had to stretch significantly to enable such a wide [gape](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/gape), they would have been relatively weak compared with those of modern cats. The bones of many _Smilodon_ specimens have been recovered from the [La Brea Tar Pits](https://www.britannica.com/place/La-Brea-Tar-Pits) in [Los Angeles](https://www.britannica.com/place/Los-Angeles-California), California; the cats were apparently mired in the [tar](https://www.britannica.com/art/tar-musical-instrument) as they preyed on other animals that had also become trapped.

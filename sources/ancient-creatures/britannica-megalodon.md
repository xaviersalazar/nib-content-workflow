- What was the megalodon?







The [megalodon](https://www.britannica.com/animal/megalodon), _Carcharocles megalodon_, meaning "giant tooth," was the largest shark and fish ever known. It lived from the early Miocene Epoch (23.03 million years ago) to the end of the Pliocene Epoch (2.58 million years ago).



Megalodon was an apex predator that preyed on fish, baleen whales, toothed whales, sirenians, and seals. Adults likely had no predators, while juveniles were vulnerable to large predatory sharks.



Debate continues about the taxonomy and evolution of megalodon and its relationship to modern [white sharks](https://www.britannica.com/animal/great-white-shark). Some scientists believe that megalodon and modern white sharks evolved within the same lineage, while others classify megalodon within the lineage of megatooth sharks.



Megalodon's extinction may be attributed to climate change and shifting food-chain dynamics.

- When did the megalodon live?

It is considered to be the largest shark and fish that ever lived.



The megalodon's geographic distribution expanded throughout the Miocene but declined during the Pliocene. - How big was the megalodon compared to modern sharks?







The megalodon was the largest fish ever known. Based on the size of fossil teeth, the megalodon likely resembled the modern great white shark in appearance. However, megalodon teeth were much larger, with the largest tooth measuring 17.8 cm (6.9 inches) in length, almost three times longer than those of modern white sharks.



Estimates suggest that adult megalodons reached a mean length of between 13 and 14 metres (roughly 42.7 and 45.9 feet), with the largest specimens measuring up to an estimated 24.3 metres (79.7 feet) long. The largest of the modern predatory sharks is the great white shark, at 6 metres (20 feet).

- How do scientists learn about megalodon if it is extinct?







Scientists learn about the extinct megalodon primarily through its [fossilized teeth](https://www.britannica.com/animal/www.britannica.com/animal/megalodon/Introduction#ref340366). The word megalodon means "giant tooth" in Greek. Since sharks lack bones, these teeth are the most abundant remains, providing key insights into megalodon's size, estimated at up to 58.7 feet long.



By studying the shape and size of these teeth, scientists can infer relationships to modern sharks, diet, and even potential nursery areas. The largest megalodon tooth ever found was 6.9 inches long, almost three times longer than the average tooth of a modern great white shark. Additionally, fossil remains have been found off the coast of every continent except Antarctica.

- What are some reasons scientists think the megalodon became extinct?







Scientists suggest that the [megalodon](https://www.britannica.com/animal/megalodon) shark became extinct about 2.6 million years ago. The reasons for this are likely linked to a mix of environmental changes and shifting food-chain dynamics.



The closing of seaways separating North and South America disrupted marine food chains. This likely affected baleen whales, which scientists believe were the megalodon's main food source. Additionally, competition from smaller predatory sharks, such as the great white shark, and early killer whales likely contributed to the megalodon's decline.

**megalodon**, ( _Carcharocles megalodon_), member of an [extinct](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology) [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) of megatooth [shark](https://www.britannica.com/animal/shark) (Otodontidae) that is considered to be the largest shark, as well as the largest [fish](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fish), that ever lived. [Fossils](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil) attributed to megalodon have been found dating from the early [Miocene Epoch](https://www.britannica.com/science/Miocene-Epoch) (which began 23.03 million years ago) to the end of the [Pliocene Epoch](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pliocene-Epoch) (2.58 million years ago). The word _megalodon_, a compound of Greek root words, means “giant tooth.”

## Natural history

## Distribution

![megalodon (Carcharocles megalodon) geographic distribution](https://cdn.britannica.com/36/200136-050-C4B56F05/Patterns-megalodon-distribution-epochs-Pliocene-Miocene-fossil.jpg)

[megalodon ( _Carcharocles megalodon_) geographic distribution](https://cdn.britannica.com/36/200136-050-C4B56F05/Patterns-megalodon-distribution-epochs-Pliocene-Miocene-fossil.jpg) Patterns of megalodon ( _Carcharocles megalodon_) distribution during the Miocene and Pliocene epochs can been estimated using the locations of collected fossil teeth.(more)

Fossil remains of megalodon have been found in shallow tropical and temperate seas along the coastlines and [continental shelf](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-shelf) regions of all [continents](https://www.britannica.com/science/continent) except [Antarctica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctica). During the early and middle parts of the Miocene Epoch (which lasted from 23 million to 5.3 million years ago), large seaways separated [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) from [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) and [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) and [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia) from [Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Africa) and the [Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/Middle-East), which likely facilitated movement from one [ocean basin](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean-basin) to another. Throughout the Miocene, megalodon distribution expanded from pockets located in the [Caribbean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Caribbean-Sea) and [Mediterranean seas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea), in the [Bay of Bengal](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bay-of-Bengal), and along the coasts of [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state) and southern [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia) to encompass [waters](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) off the coasts of northern Europe, South America, southern Africa, [New Zealand](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Zealand), and east Asia. During the Pliocene Epoch, however, megalodon’s geographic range contracted significantly, and it was [extinct](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology) by the end of the epoch.

## Physical features

![megalodon size](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/200251-050-79878ECB/Studies-body-mass-megalodons-females-males-sharks.jpg)

[megalodon size](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/200251-050-79878ECB/Studies-body-mass-megalodons-females-males-sharks.jpg) Studies estimate that the body mass of adult megalodons ( _Carcharocles megalodon_) ranged from roughly 30,000 kg (about 66,000 pounds) to more than 65,000 kg (about 143,000 pounds), adult females being larger (in both length and mass) than adult males. In contrast, most white sharks ( _Carcharodon carcharias_) weigh between 680 and 1,800 kg (1,500 and 4,000 pounds).(more)

Megalodon was the largest [fish](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fish) ever known, a designation based on discoveries of hundreds of fossil [teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/tooth-anatomy), two vertebral columns, and a handful of individual vertebrae. Tooth-shape similarities between megalodon and modern [great white sharks](https://www.britannica.com/animal/white-shark) ( _Carcharodon carcharias_) suggest that the two species may have been close relatives, and thus megalodon likely resembled that species in appearance—that is, as a bulky torpedo-shaped fish with a conical snout, large pectoral and dorsal fins, and a strong crescent-shaped [tail](https://www.britannica.com/science/tail-zoology). Estimates of body length are calculated using the statistical relationship between the size of megalodon’s fossil teeth and the teeth and body mass of modern white sharks and other living relatives. This data suggests that mature adult megalodons had a mean length of between 13 and 14 metres (about 42.7 and 45.9 feet), with the largest specimens measuring up to an estimated 24.3 metres (79.7 feet) long. Studies estimate that adult body mass ranged from roughly 30 metric tons (1 metric ton = 1,000 kg; about 66,000 pounds) to more than 65 metric tons (about 143,000 pounds), adult females being larger (in both length and mass) than adult males.

Megalodon teeth are similar to those of modern white sharks in that they are triangular, serrated, and symmetrical. They differ from modern white shark teeth in that they are larger and thicker, the serrations on each tooth occur in regular intervals, and they possess a bourlette (a darker, chevron-shaped region near the tooth’s root). The largest extant megalodon tooth measures 17.8 cm (6.9 inches) in length, almost three times longer than those of modern white sharks (which are typically about 5.4 cm \[2.1 inches\] long). In addition, megalodon possessed a ferocious bite; its bite diameter was 3 metres (about 9.8 feet), several times larger than the bite diameter of averaged-sized white sharks.

Megalodon is thought to have managed its body [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature) in a manner similar to that of modern white sharks, in that it was not exclusively [cold-blooded](https://www.britannica.com/science/cold-bloodedness) like most fish. White sharks generate [heat](https://www.britannica.com/science/heat) through the contraction of their swimming [muscles](https://www.britannica.com/science/muscle), and this heat raises the temperature of parts of the shark’s body above that of the surrounding [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water), an [adaptation](https://www.britannica.com/science/adaptation-biology-and-physiology) called [regional endothermy](https://www.britannica.com/science/regional-endothermy) (which is a type of [warm-bloodedness](https://www.britannica.com/science/warm-bloodedness)). This adaptation might have allowed megalodon to swim and hunt in colder waters, giving it exclusive access to prey in those locations.

## Reproduction and territoriality

Megalodon is thought to have produced live young. It is not known, however, whether the [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) was ovoviviparous (in which [eggs](https://www.britannica.com/science/egg-biology) are retained within the mother until they hatch) or [viviparous](https://www.britannica.com/science/viviparity) (in which fertilized [embryos](https://www.britannica.com/science/embryo-human-and-animal) derive continuous nourishment from the mother). Estimates of body size using juvenile teeth suggest that newly birthed young may have been at least 2 metres (6.6 feet) in length.

Few details are known about megalodon [courtship](https://www.britannica.com/science/courtship), but the species appears to have used nurseries for its young. A 2010 study identified a megalodon nursery along the Panamanian coast, which was characterized by the presence of juvenile teeth from various stages of life. Scientists posit that this shallow warm-water nursery provided young megalodons with access to a diverse array of smaller, more-abundant prey and enabled adults to better intercept attacks from other predatory shark species, such as [hammerhead sharks](https://www.britannica.com/animal/hammerhead-shark). As the young sharks grew older, it is thought that they would make forays into deeper water to pursue larger [animals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal).

Little is known about how individuals dispersed after they matured. Since megalodon is thought to have occupied an ecological [niche](https://www.britannica.com/science/niche-ecology) similar to that of the white shark, some studies have assumed that megalodon likely ranged over areas comparable in size to the range of modern white sharks—about 1,000 square km (386 square miles).

# Predators and prey

In addition to being the world’s largest [fish](https://www.britannica.com/animal/fish), megalodon was one of the largest marine carnivores in Earth’s history. ( [Basilosaurids](https://www.britannica.com/animal/basilosaurid), [mosasaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mosasaur), and [pliosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/pliosaur) may have been just as large.) Megalodon was an [apex predator](https://www.britannica.com/science/apex-predator), or top [carnivore](https://www.britannica.com/science/carnivore-consumer), in the marine environments it inhabited ( _see also_ [keystone species](https://www.britannica.com/science/keystone-species)). It preyed upon fish, [baleen whales](https://www.britannica.com/animal/baleen-whale), [toothed whales](https://www.britannica.com/animal/toothed-whale) (such as ancestral forms of modern [sperm whales](https://www.britannica.com/animal/sperm-whale), [dolphins](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dolphin-mammal), and [killer whales](https://www.britannica.com/animal/killer-whale)), [sirenians](https://www.britannica.com/animal/sirenian) (such as [dugongs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dugong-mammal) and [manatees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/manatee)), and [seals](https://www.britannica.com/topic/seal-authentication). The young likely sought out smaller prey, while adults hunted larger [whales](https://www.britannica.com/animal/whale). Mature megalodons likely did not have any predators, but newly birthed and juvenile individuals may have been vulnerable to other large predatory [sharks](https://www.britannica.com/animal/shark), such as great [hammerhead sharks](https://www.britannica.com/animal/hammerhead-shark) ( _Sphyrna mokarran_), whose ranges and nurseries are thought to have overlapped with those of megalodon from the end of the [Miocene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Miocene-Epoch) and throughout the [Pliocene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pliocene-Epoch).

## Paleontology

The mysteries of the megalodon sharkThe natural history of the megalodon ( _Carcharocles megalodon_), a gigantic predatory shark that plied tropical and temperate seas during the Miocene and Pliocene epochs.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/animal/megalodon/images-videos)

Much debate continues to surround the [taxonomy](https://www.britannica.com/science/taxonomy) and [evolution](https://www.britannica.com/science/evolution-scientific-theory) of megalodon ( _Carcharocles megalodon_ \[in some classifications _Otodus megalodon_\]), as well as its relationship to modern [white sharks](https://www.britannica.com/animal/white-shark) ( _Carcharodon carcharias_). Megalodon was first described in 1835 by Swiss-born American naturalist, geologist, and teacher [Louis Agassiz](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-Agassiz), who named the [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) _Carcharodon megalodon_. Megalodon would be known by this scientific name until the late 1990s, when a growing group of scientists placed it in the genus _Carcharocles_.

Although some paleontologists assert that megalodon and modern white sharks evolved within the same lineage ( _Carcharodon_ in the [shark](https://www.britannica.com/animal/shark) family Lamnidae) on the basis of their serrated [teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/tooth-anatomy), others classify megalodon within the lineage of megatooth sharks (Otodontidae) whose origins trace back to the [Cretaceous Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period) (145 million to 66 million years ago). A tooth-analysis study performed in 2012 suggested that modern white sharks evolved from lamnid sharks (Lamnidae) some 5 million years ago during the late [Miocene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Miocene-Epoch) and early [Pliocene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pliocene-Epoch) epochs. The study notes that the pattern of serrations and other similarities in tooth structure between megalodon and modern white sharks could be a product of convergent evolution (wherein similar [characters](https://www.britannica.com/science/character-biology) evolve independently in different lineages).

### Is _Mosasaurus_ bigger than a megalodon?

No. While several [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) of _Mosasaurus_ appear in the [fossil record](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil-record), members of only one, _M. hoffmanni_, challenge the length of the largest [megalodon](https://www.britannica.com/animal/megalodon) specimens known—which measure 17.9 metres (58.7 feet) long. In contrast, the longest mosasaurs, based on a specimen of _M. hoffmanni_, are estimated to have been 17 meters (about 56 feet) in length.

### Are mosasaurs considered dinosaurs?

No. Mosasaurs are marine [reptiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reptile) more closely related to modern [lizards](https://www.britannica.com/animal/lizard) and [snakes](https://www.britannica.com/animal/snake) than to [dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur). Dinosaurs are [archosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/archosaur), a reptile subgroup that includes [pterosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/pterosaur) (flying reptiles), as well as all living and extinct [crocodiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/crocodile-order) and [birds](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal).

### Why did the Mosasaurus go extinct?

The group died out during the course of the [mass extinction event](https://www.britannica.com/science/mass-extinction-event) triggered by the extraterrestrial impact that formed the [Chicxulub crater](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chicxulub) about 66 million years ago.

**mosasaur**, (family Mosasauridae), extinct group of aquatic [lizards](https://www.britannica.com/animal/lizard) that attained a high degree of [adaptation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adaptation) to the marine [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) and were distributed worldwide during the [Cretaceous Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period) (145.5 million to 66 million years ago). Mosasaurs are a [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) group, spanning more than ten genera and several [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon). The group died out during the course of the [mass extinction event](https://www.britannica.com/science/mass-extinction-event) triggered by the extraterrestrial impact that formed the [Chicxulub crater](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chicxulub) about 66 million years ago ( _see also_ [K–T extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/K-T-extinction)).

The mosasaurs competed with other marine [reptiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reptile)—the [plesiosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/plesiosaur) and [ichthyosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ichthyosaur)—for food, which consisted largely of [ammonoids](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ammonoid), fish, and [cuttlefish](https://www.britannica.com/animal/cuttlefish). Many mosasaurs of the Late Cretaceous were large. The longest mosasaurs, based on a specimen of _Mosasaurus hoffmanni_, are estimated to have been 17 meters (about 56 feet) in length, but the most common forms were no larger than modern [porpoises](https://www.britannica.com/animal/porpoise).

Mosasaurs had a snakelike body with a large skull and a long snout. Their limbs were modified into paddles having shorter limb bones and more numerous finger and toe [bones](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy) than those of their ancestors. The tail region of the body was long, and its end was slightly downcurved in a manner similar to that of the early [ichthyosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ichthyosaur). The [backbone](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/backbone) consisted of more than 100 vertebrae. The structure of the skull was very similar to that of the modern [monitor lizards](https://www.britannica.com/animal/monitor-lizard), to which mosasaurs are related. The jaws bore many conical, slightly recurved [teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/tooth-anatomy) set in individual sockets. The jawbones are notable in that they were jointed near mid-length (as in some of the advanced monitors) and connected in front by [ligaments](https://www.britannica.com/science/ligament) only. This arrangement enabled the animals not only to open the mouth by lowering the mandible but also to extend the lower jaws sideways while feeding on large prey.

**pliosaur**, a group of large carnivorous marine [reptiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reptile) [characterized](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characterized) by massive heads, short necks, and streamlined tear-shaped bodies. Pliosaurs have been found as fossils from the [Jurassic](https://www.britannica.com/science/Jurassic-Period) and [Cretaceous periods](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period) (about 200 million to 65.5 million years ago). They are classified in the order [Plesiosauria](https://www.britannica.com/animal/plesiosaur), along with their long-necked relatives, the [plesiosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/plesiosaur). Pliosaurs possessed powerful jaws and large teeth, and they used four large fins to swim through Mesozoic seas.

One notable pliosaur is _Liopleurodon_, a [genus](https://www.britannica.com/science/genus-taxon) found in Middle Jurassic deposits in England and northern France. _Liopleurodon_ is significant in that several fossils of variable quality that range in length from 5 to 25 metres (16 to 85 feet) have been placed in this [genus](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/genus), leading many authorities to question whether such specimens should be reclassified into other genera.

On the other hand, some groups did indeed grow quite large. For example, _[Kronosaurus](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Kronosaurus)_, an Early Cretaceous pliosaur from [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia), grew to about 12 metres (about 40 feet) long; the skull alone measured about 3.7 metres (12.1 feet) long. An even larger pliosaur from the Jurassic, dubbed “ [Predator X](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Predator-X),” was unearthed in [Svalbard](https://www.britannica.com/place/Svalbard) in 2009. Although it remains unclassified at present, some details are known. Its length and weight are estimated at 15 metres (about 50 feet) long and 45 tonnes (almost 100,000 pounds), respectively. The jaws of this creature are thought to have produced a bite force of 33,000 pounds per square inch, perhaps the highest bite force of any known [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal).

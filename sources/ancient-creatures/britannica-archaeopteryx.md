- What is Archaeopteryx?







_Archaeopteryx_ is a genus of feathered dinosaur dating back roughly 150 million years ago during the Late Jurassic Epoch. These fossils were discovered in Bavaria, Germany, starting in 1861.



Once considered the oldest known fossil bird, later discoveries of similar bird-like fossils, such as _Xiaotingia zhengi_, have led some paleontologists to reclassify _Archaeopteryx_ as a dinosaur.



_Archaeopteryx_ shared anatomical features with coelurosaurs (carnivorous dinosaurs). The presence of feathers on the first specimens suggested it was a bird. Unlike modern birds, it had well-developed teeth and a long tail with feathers on each side, and its three fingers bore claws and moved independently. Though it had wings, _Archaeopteryx_ likely used bursts of powered flight over short distances.

- When and where were Archaeopteryx fossils discovered?







_Archaeopteryx_ fossils, once considered the oldest known bird fossils, date back roughly 150 million years to the Late Jurassic Epoch. The first _Archaeopteryx_ fossil was discovered in 1861 in the Solnhofen Limestone Formation in Bavaria, Germany. The fine-grained limestone of this area allowed for exceptional preservation of fossil material, including feather impressions. _Archaeopteryx_ shared many anatomical features with coelurosaurs (carnivorous dinosaurs); the presence of feathers on the fossils was a key identifier for classifying it as a bird.

- Why is Archaeopteryx considered important in the study of evolution?







_Archaeopteryx_ is significant in the study of evolution because it has features of both reptiles and birds, bridging the evolutionary gap between them. It had reptilian features such as teeth, a long tail, and unfused fingers with claws, but it also had avian features such as feathers and wings.



_Archaeopteryx_, discovered in 1861, was once known as the earliest fossil bird. It provides insights into the evolution of flight, even though it may not have been able to sustain long-distance flight. The arrangement of melanosomes in its feathers suggests they provided structural support for bursts of flight, and skeletal structures indicate it evolved from small carnivorous dinosaurs.

- How did Archaeopteryx help scientists understand the link between dinosaurs and birds?







_Archaeopteryx_, a feathered dinosaur from the Late Jurassic Epoch, has played a significant role in understanding the evolutionary link between dinosaurs and birds. The discovery of _Archaeopteryx_ fossils, starting in 1861, revealed a mix of reptilian and avian characteristics.



Though _Archaeopteryx_ had well-developed wings and feathers similar to modern birds, it also shared many anatomical features with coelurosaurs (carnivorous dinosaurs). Unlike today's birds, _Archaeopteryx_ had teeth, clawed fingers, and a long tail similar to those of smaller dinosaurs.



These unique characteristics of _Archaeopteryx_ helped scientists like [Thomas Henry Huxley](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thomas-Henry-Huxley) and [John H. Ostrom](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-H-Ostrom) establish that birds evolved from small carnivorous dinosaurs.

- What have scientists learned about the environment and time period in which Archaeopteryx lived?







_Archaeopteryx_ lived about 150 million years ago during the Late Jurassic Epoch. The _Archaeopteryx_ fossils were discovered in the Solnhofen Limestone Formation in Bavaria, Germany. This limestone was formed in a shallow tropical marine environment, likely a coral lagoon, allowing exceptional fossil preservation.



During the Jurassic period, the supercontinent Pangea split apart, creating the central Atlantic Ocean and the Gulf of Mexico. Increased plate tectonic movement caused volcanic activity and mountain-building. Warm-adapted plant fossils suggest an expanded tropical zone, with a smaller temperature difference between the Equator and the poles compared to today. Global temperatures were generally warm, with the warmest temperatures in the Late Jurassic.

**Archaeopteryx**, [genus](https://www.britannica.com/science/genus-taxon) of [feathered dinosaur](https://www.britannica.com/animal/feathered-dinosaur) that was once thought to be the oldest known [fossil](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil) [bird](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal). The specimens date to approximately 150 million years ago, during the Late [Jurassic Epoch](https://www.britannica.com/science/Jurassic-Period) (163.5 million to 145 million years ago), and all were found in the [Solnhofen Limestone](https://www.britannica.com/science/Solnhofen-Limestone) Formation in [Bavaria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bavaria), Germany, starting in 1861. However, late 20th- and early 21st-century discoveries of other birdlike fossils of similar age, including _[Xiaotingia zhengi](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Xiaotingia-zhengi)_ from the [Liaoning deposits](https://www.britannica.com/animal/feathered-dinosaur#ref288166) in China, have prompted several paleontologists to call for the reclassification of _Archaeopteryx_ as a [dinosaur](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur).

Much of what is known about _Archaeopteryx_ comes from a series of well-preserved fossil specimens. The Solnhofen Limestone is a very fine-grained Jurassic [limestone](https://www.britannica.com/science/limestone) formed in a shallow tropical marine [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) (probably a coral [lagoon](https://www.britannica.com/science/lagoon-geography)), where lime-rich muds slowly accumulated and permitted fossil material to be exceptionally well preserved. Several of the fossils show clear impressions of [feathers](https://www.britannica.com/science/feather). The sizes of the specimens range from that of a [blue jay](https://www.britannica.com/animal/blue-jay) to that of a large [chicken](https://www.britannica.com/animal/poultry-agriculture).

_Archaeopteryx_ shared many anatomic characters with coelurosaurs, a group of [theropods](https://www.britannica.com/animal/theropod) (carnivorous dinosaurs). In fact, only the identification of feathers on the first known specimens indicated that the [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) was a bird. Unlike living birds, however, _Archaeopteryx_ had well-developed [teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/tooth-anatomy) and a long well-developed [tail](https://www.britannica.com/science/tail-zoology) similar to those of smaller dinosaurs, except that it had a row of feathers on each side. The three fingers [bore](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/bore) [claws](https://www.britannica.com/science/claw) and moved independently, unlike the fused fingers of living birds.

_Archaeopteryx_ had well-developed [wings](https://www.britannica.com/science/wing-anatomy), and the structure and arrangement of its wing feathers—similar to that of most living birds—indicate that it could fly. However, evidence suggests that the animal’s powered [flight](https://www.britannica.com/science/flight-animal-locomotion) differed from that of most modern birds. The bones of _Archaeopteryx_ were strong enough to handle low torsional forces, which allowed for bursts of powered flight over short distances to elude predators, rather than high torsional forces, which are required for rapid flapping and soaring. A study of melanosomes (the pigmented, [melanin](https://www.britannica.com/science/melanin)-producing granules present in specialized [skin](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skin) cells called [melanocytes](https://www.britannica.com/science/melanocyte)) in the animal’s feathers revealed that the feathers were black and that the arrangement of the granules within the [feather’s](https://www.britannica.com/science/feather) microstructure probably provided increased structural support to the wings, similar to the way it does in modern birds. Skeletal structures related to flight are incompletely developed, however, which suggests that _Archaeopteryx_ may not have been able to [sustain](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/sustain) flight for great distances. _Archaeopteryx_ is known to have evolved from small carnivorous [dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur), as it retains many features such as teeth and a long tail. It also retains a wishbone, a [breastbone](https://www.britannica.com/science/sternum), hollow thin-walled [bones](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy), [air sacs](https://www.britannica.com/science/air-sac) in the [backbones](https://www.britannica.com/science/vertebral-column), and feathers, which are also found in the nonavian coelurosaurian relatives of birds. These structures, therefore, cannot be said to have evolved for the purpose of flight, because they were already present in dinosaurs before either birds or flight evolved.

Several paleontologists note that some birdlike dinosaurs of similar age or older also possessed features identical or nearly identical to those of _Archaeopteryx_. Many features, such as the presence of [feathers](https://www.britannica.com/science/feather), three-fingered hands, a wishbone, and long [robust](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/robust) forelimbs, which are often considered diagnostic of birds, also appear in _X. zhengi_ and _Aurornis xui_, [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) thought to have lived some 5 million and 10 million years before _Archaeopteryx_, respectively, as well as others. Thus, these paleontologists claim that _Archaeopteryx_ cannot in fact be the world’s most primitive bird, and many of the features used to describe birds could be applied to the Paraves, a more [inclusive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inclusive) collection of [theropod](https://www.britannica.com/animal/theropod) dinosaurs that includes birds and the deinonychosaurs (a group that contains the troodontids and the [dromaeosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dromaeosaur)).

According to nearly all [ornithologists](https://www.britannica.com/science/ornithology), [birds](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal) are not only the descendants of ancient [dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur), they _are_, in fact, dinosaurs themselves! Dinosaurs are a branch of [reptiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reptile) that emerged during the middle of the [Triassic Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Triassic-Period) (about 252 million to 201 million years ago) and thrived during the [Jurassic](https://www.britannica.com/science/Jurassic-Period) and [Cretaceous](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period) periods (201 million to 66 million years ago). Birds evolved from a group of dinosaurs called [theropods](https://www.britannica.com/animal/theropod), which are known for the ability to walk on their hind legs rather than on all fours. The slow transformation of part of this lineage into the forms we recognize as birds was driven largely by the emergence of [feathers](https://www.britannica.com/science/feather) and their development from primitive single-stalked, hairlike designs into complex structures made up of a central stalk and elements that branch laterally from it.

Feathers probably first appeared among the therapods during the Jurassic Period as a body covering to help with insulation or coloring that would be useful in [courtship](https://www.britannica.com/science/courtship) or [camouflage](https://www.britannica.com/topic/camouflage-military-tactic) ( _see also_ [concealing coloration](https://www.britannica.com/science/concealing-coloration)). In one lineage, feathers, in conjunction with forelimbs that evolved into wings, also bestowed the ability of gliding and muscle-powered [flight](https://www.britannica.com/science/flight-animal-locomotion), so identifying the first [feathered dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/feathered-dinosaur) capable of these activities has become a tantalizing exercise. Although another group of reptiles called the [pterosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/pterosaur) had already developed the ability to fly as early as the Late Triassic Epoch (237 million to 201 million years ago), where, when, and in which group of feathered dinosaurs powered flight first appeared remain mysteries. The presence of feathers endures as a major defining characteristic of birds today—along with lightweight, hollow [bones](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy), [warm-bloodedness](https://www.britannica.com/science/warm-bloodedness), and the ability of female birds to deposit hard-shelled [eggs](https://www.britannica.com/science/egg-biology).

Traditionally, [_Archaeopteryx_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Archaeopteryx), which was first discovered in 1861 in the [Solnhofen Limestone](https://www.britannica.com/science/Solnhofen-Limestone) of [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany), is considered the world’s oldest birdlike feathered dinosaur. Despite a string of challenges to this title brought on by others discovered in the late 20th and early 21st centuries, most contemporary studies still list _Archaeopteryx_ as the earliest known form with modern birdlike features, and it was also capable of powered flight. Nevertheless, the other candidates listed in the interactive below not only highlight innovative approaches to dealing with the challenges of traveling through the [air](https://www.britannica.com/science/air), but they also offer insight into the evolution of the structures we have come to associate with modern birds.

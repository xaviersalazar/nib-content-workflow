**sign language**, any means of [communication](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communication) through bodily movements, especially of the hands and arms, used when spoken communication is impossible or not desirable. The practice is probably older than speech. Sign language may be as coarsely expressed as mere grimaces, shrugs, or pointings; or it may employ a delicately [nuanced](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nuanced) combination of coded manual signals reinforced by facial expression and perhaps augmented by words spelled out in a manual alphabet. Wherever vocal communication is impossible, as between speakers of mutually unintelligible languages or when one or more would-be communicators is deaf, sign language can be used to bridge the gap.

## Language barrier

[![American Sign Language](https://cdn.britannica.com/06/152506-050-089E5FC2/people-American-Sign-Language.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/06/152506-050-089E5FC2/people-American-Sign-Language.jpg)

[American Sign Language](https://cdn.britannica.com/06/152506-050-089E5FC2/people-American-Sign-Language.jpg) Deaf or hard-of-hearing people conversing in American Sign Language (ASL).(more)

[Chinese](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chinese-languages) and [Japanese](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Japanese-language), whose languages use the same body of characters but pronounce them entirely differently, can communicate by means of a sign language in which one watches while the other traces mutually understood characters in his or her palm. Evidence of long use of sign language to communicate around mutually unintelligible languages exists for Africa, Australia, and [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America). The most generally known model is that of the Plains [Indians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/American-Indian-languages) of 19th-century North America. Although their languages were dissimilar, the mode of life and [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) of all groups had many shared elements, and, consequently, finding common symbols was easy. Thus, a cupped hand leaping and bobbing away from the “speaker” was familiar to all as the rump of a bounding deer; a circle drawn against the sky meant the moon—or something as pale as the moon. Two fingers astride the other index finger represented a person on horseback; two fingers spread and darting from the mouth like the forked tongue of a snake meant lies or treachery; and the gesture of brushing long hair down over the neck and shoulder signified a woman. This sign language became so familiar that long and complex narratives—in monologue or dialogue—could be signed and understood within large groups of Indians otherwise unable to communicate.

## Inability to [speak](https://www.britannica.com/science/speech-disorder)

The [Indian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indigenous-peoples-of-the-Americas) sign language was codified by use into an explicit vocabulary of gestures representing or depicting objects, actions, and ideas, but it made no attempt to “spell out” or otherwise represent words that could not be conveyed by gestures. Several forms of sign language were developed to enable signers to spell out words and sounds, however. Most of these are as complex and flexible as spoken languages.

It was long thought in many [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) that the deaf were ineducable, and the few teachers willing to try were available only to the wealthy. In the mid-18th century, however, the first educator of poor [deaf](https://www.britannica.com/science/deafness) children, [Charles-Michel, abbé de l’Epée](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Michel-abbe-de-l-Epee), developed a system for spelling out French words with a manual alphabet and expressing whole concepts with simple signs. From l’Epée’s system developed French Sign Language (FSL), still in use in France today and the [precursor](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/precursor) of [American Sign Language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/American-Sign-Language) (ASL) and many other national sign languages.

FSL was brought to the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) in 1816 by Thomas Gallaudet, founder of the American School for the Deaf in Hartford, Connecticut. The new sign language was combined with the various systems already in use in the United States to form ASL, which today is used by more than 500,000 deaf people in the United States and Canada; it is the fourth most common [language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/language) in the United States. National sign languages such as ASL have more in common with one another than with the spoken languages of their country of origin, since their signs represent concepts and not English or French or Japanese words. One system, Cued Speech, first developed by the American physicist R. Orin Cornett in 1966, does, however, successfully employ hand signs representing only sounds (not concepts), used in conjunction with lipreading. It has been adapted to more than 40 languages.

## Abstinence from speech

Members of religious orders who have taken vows of silence, as well as others who for reasons of piety or humility have forsworn speech, have need of sign language. Often, in a silent monastic order, for instance, natural gestures such as passing food or pointing to some needed object have [sufficed](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/sufficed) for effective communication, leaving little need for specially coded signs. [Meher Baba](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Meher-Baba), an Indian religious figure, abstained from speech in the last decades of his life but “dictated” voluminous writings to [disciples](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disciples), at first by pointing to letters on an English-language alphabet board; but, after evolving a suitable sign language of gestures, he relied on that alone. The [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) English cleric [Venerable Bede](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Bede-the-Venerable) worked out a coded sign language based on manual signs representing numbers, with the numbers in turn signifying letters of the [Latin alphabet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Latin-alphabet) in sequence—i.e., 1 for _A_, 7 for _G_, etc. It is not known, however, whether he devised the system to communicate with the deaf or merely to maintain silence.

- What is deaf history?







Deaf history encompasses the experiences, movements, and cultural evolution of deaf individuals and communities across time. In the 1800s, educators like Laurent Clerc integrated French Sign Language into American classrooms, which shaped [American Sign Language (ASL)](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sign-language/Introduction#ref6667) and deaf education.



However, the late 19th century saw the rise of oralism, which suppressed sign language in favor of assimilation through speech training. Despite this, deaf communities maintained their culture through associations and periodicals, establishing organizations such as the [National Association of the Deaf (NAD)](https://www.britannica.com/topic/www.britannica.com/science/deaf-history/The-19th-century#ref322929) in 1880 to advocate for their rights.



The 20th century brought renewed emphasis on sign language and deaf identity, spurred by William Stokoe's research in the 1960s. This led to the [total communication movement](https://www.britannica.com/science/deaf-history/The-20th-century#ref322934) and the [bilingual-bicultural approach](https://www.britannica.com/science/deaf-history/The-20th-century#ref322934) in education. The "Deaf President Now!" protest in 1988 at Gallaudet University highlighted the community's determination to have deaf individuals in leadership positions.

- How have deaf people communicated throughout history?







Throughout history, deaf people have communicated in diverse ways, adapting to societal attitudes and technological advancements. For centuries, sign language has been a fundamental means of communication within Deaf communities. Early forms of sign language were developed to spell out words and concepts, such as the French Sign Language (FSL), which influenced the creation of American Sign Language (ASL).



Formal deaf education in the U.S. began in 1817, initially using Signed English, but ASL soon became dominant. The oralist movement, which prioritized lip-reading and speech, gained traction, suppressing sign language in schools. However, the rediscovery of sign language in the 1960s led to a renaissance, advocating for its use in education and recognizing ASL as a genuine language. Today, Deaf communities thrive, utilizing technology like communication devices to integrate and communicate independently.

- When and why were schools for the deaf established?







Deaf schools were first established in the early 1800s to provide religious training to deaf people, ensuring they understood the word of God. In 1817, Laurent Clerc and Thomas Hopkins Gallaudet founded the American School for the Deaf in West Hartford, Connecticut. Clerc's influence and use of French Sign Language significantly shaped contemporary American Sign Language (ASL).



Later in the 19th century, the focus shifted towards training deaf individuals to become good citizens, leading to the rise of oralism, which aimed to assimilate deaf people into spoken-language communities. This approach saw a decline in deaf teachers and emphasized speech training, viewing sign language as a primitive relic.

**deaf history**, the experience and education of deaf persons and the development of deaf communities and culture through time. The history of deaf people (those affected by varying degrees of [deafness](https://www.britannica.com/science/deafness)) has been written as a history of [hearing](https://www.britannica.com/science/hearing-sense) perceptions of deaf people, as a history of the education of deaf people, and as the history of the lives and communities of deaf people. This history embodies some of the major strands of [disability studies](https://www.britannica.com/topic/disability-studies) scholarship: the reactions of outsiders to those with a physical difference, shifting understandings of normalcy, and the existence of a community of people who create lives based on a different sensory universe than that of those around them.

## Early deaf communities

Deaf people are unique among individuals with a sensory difference in that they are also a linguistic minority. They have long formed communities whenever they come together in a specific geographic location. Most scholars attribute the development of deaf communities to the establishment of schools for the deaf and the desire of alumni to associate with one another afterward. But there is also evidence that whenever a significant number of deaf people exist in one geographic location, they will form social relationships with one another and with hearing people who use [sign language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sign-language). The island of [Martha’s Vineyard](https://www.britannica.com/place/Marthas-Vineyard), off the Massachusetts coast in the United States, was an example of such a community ( _see_ [deafness on Martha’s Vineyard](https://www.britannica.com/science/deafness-on-Marthas-Vineyard)). From the 17th to the mid-20th century, a significant population of deaf people coexisted alongside their hearing counterparts in certain towns on the island. In those towns, nearly everyone was able to use some form of sign language, and [deafness](https://www.britannica.com/science/deafness) was an accepted, unremarkable fact of daily life.

Communities such as that found on Martha’s Vineyard are likely rare. There were few, if any, politically organized European communities of deaf people in the early modern era (the 16th and 17th centuries). There were, however, early small-scale attempts by European religious orders to educate the deaf children of rich noble families. Spanish Benedictine monk [Pedro Ponce de León](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pedro-Ponce-de-Leon) was the most prominent of those early teachers. In the 1540s he taught the deaf brothers Don Francisco de Velasco and Don Pedro de Velasco, as well as 10 to 12 other deaf people, at his monastery. Ponce’s work would be replicated in other small-scale schools throughout Europe, but state sponsorship of deaf education would begin only in the 18th century.

## The 18th century

In Europe the [Enlightenment](https://www.britannica.com/event/Enlightenment-European-history) brought about a new faith in reason and a new curiosity on the part of scholars about the ability of deaf people to achieve rational and abstract thought. In that period the education of deaf people attracted prominent attention, and historians have generally pointed to Paris as the crucible of deaf education in the modern era. In Paris, Charles-Michel, abbé de l’Épée, founded what would eventually become the first state-supported school for deaf children, later known as the Institut National des Jeunes Sourds (INJS). Beginning with a class for two deaf sisters, de l’Épée’s school served as a model and a source of inspiration for the establishment of other European schools. Those schools generally followed the INJS’s use of a signed language to teach deaf children in their national spoken and written language. A school established in Leipzig, Germany, in 1778 by [Samuel Heinicke](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Samuel-Heinicke) exemplified the oral method (oralism), a method emphasizing training in speechreading (or lipreading) and articulation as a means for deaf people to learn their national language.

The respective methods used by de l’Épée and Heinicke became touchstones in a centuries-long “methods debate” in the field of deaf education. Within that debate, one side supported the use of sign language to teach deaf children both subject matter and written language, while the other side saw the use of sign language as hindering deaf people’s ability to learn speechreading and orally spoken language. (That latter claim has been disproved. Linguists have recognized that the use of sign language actually enhances second-language acquisition in both deaf and hearing children.) In general, both sides supported teaching deaf people to speak; the difference lay in how much sign language would be used and how much emphasis would be given to [speech](https://www.britannica.com/topic/speech-language) training. The users of sign method (or manual method) decried what they felt was an overemphasis on speech training to the exclusion of academic content. De l’Épée and Heinicke entered a correspondence in the 1780s debating the merits of their respective methods, a debate judged by the rector and fellows of the Academy of Zürich to have been won by de l’Épée. That was hardly the end of the matter, and the “methods debate” has figured prominently in nearly every history of deaf people to date.

- What is punctuation?







Punctuation involves adding standardized marks or signs in writing to clarify meaning and separate structural units. It contrasts or accentuates the meaning of the written text. Modern English punctuation, complete since the 17th century, includes spaces between words, indentations for new paragraphs, and uppercase letters at the beginning of sentences, proper nouns, or titles.



Common marks include the period to end sentences or mark abbreviations, the colon to introduce lists or summaries, the semicolon to connect related sentences, and the comma to indicate syntactic turning points. Other marks include parentheses, question marks, exclamation marks, quotation marks, hyphens, and apostrophes.

- Why do we use punctuation marks in writing?







Punctuation marks are essential in writing to enhance understanding and ensure correct reading, whether silently or aloud. Derived from the Latin _punctus_, meaning "point," punctuation clarifies grammar and mirrors the rhythm of speech.



From the 15th to the 17th century, the purpose of punctuation transitioned from indicating pauses for readers to serving as guides to a sentence's grammatical construction. While good punctuation clarifies the structure of sentences, bad punctuation obscures meaning. Today, the system of punctuation includes spaces between words, paragraph indentations, and capitalization and marks like periods, colons, semicolons, commas, parentheses, question marks, exclamation points, hyphens, and apostrophes.

- How does a period differ from a comma when ending or separating sentences?







Periods and commas serve distinct roles in sentence construction. A [period](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/period#dictionary-entry-1), also known as a full stop, marks the end of a complete declarative sentence. It indicates a full pause and the conclusion of a thought.



A [comma](https://www.britannica.com/topic/punctuation/Punctuation-in-English-since-1600#ref53182), on the other hand, is a lighter stop used to indicate syntactic turning points within a sentence. It separates elements in a series, sets off nonessential clauses, and can combine with coordinating conjunctions. A comma indicates a brief pause, helping to clarify relationships between sentence parts.

- How can correct punctuation change the meaning of a sentence?







Punctuation marks clarify a text's grammar, speed, and rhythm. Correct punctuation can drastically change a sentence's meaning.



Consider these examples:



  - "Let’s eat, Grandma!" versus "Let’s eat Grandma!" The comma saves Grandma from being eaten.
  - "Tea that is caffeinated keeps me up at night" versus "My refrigerator, which is only about 5 years old, started leaking." The first sentence contains an essential clause that is not set off by commas, while the second contains a nonessential clause set off by commas.
  - "The hat does not fit, it's too tight" is a comma splice that can be corrected by separating the two independent clauses with a period, using a semicolon, or adding a conjunction.

Punctuation, or lack thereof, can help identify an essential or nonessential clause.

**punctuation**, the use of spacing, [conventional](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conventional) signs, and certain typographical devices as aids to the understanding and correct reading, both silently and aloud, of handwritten and printed texts. The word is derived from the Latin _punctus_, “point.” From the 15th century to the early 18th the subject was known in [English](https://www.britannica.com/topic/English-language) as pointing; and the term _punctuation_, first recorded in the middle of the 16th century, was reserved for the insertion of vowel points (marks placed near consonants to indicate preceding or following vowels) in Hebrew texts. The two words exchanged meanings between 1650 and 1750.

Since the late 16th century the theory and practice of punctuation have varied between two main schools of thought: the elocutionary school, following late [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) practice, treated points or stops as indications of the pauses of various lengths that might be observed by a reader, particularly when he was reading aloud to an audience; the syntactic school, which had won the argument by the end of the 17th century, saw them as something less arbitrary, namely, as guides to the grammatical construction of sentences. Pauses in speech and breaks in [syntax](https://www.britannica.com/topic/syntax) tend in any case to coincide; and although writers are now agreed that the main purpose of punctuation is to clarify the grammar of a text, they also require it to take account of the speed and rhythm of actual speech.

[Syntactic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Syntactic) punctuation is, by definition, bad when it obscures rather than clarifies the construction of sentences. Good punctuation, however, may be of many kinds: to take two extreme examples, [Henry James](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-James-American-writer) would be unintelligible without his numerous commas, but [Ernest Hemingway](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernest-Hemingway) seldom needs any stop but the period. In poetry, in which the elocutionary aspect of punctuation is still important, and to a lesser degree in fiction, especially when the style is close to actual speech, punctuation is much at the [author’s](https://www.britannica.com/art/author) discretion. In nonfictional [writing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/writing) there is less room for experiment. Stimulating variant models for general use might be the light punctuation of [George Bernard Shaw’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Bernard-Shaw) prefaces to his plays and the heavier punctuation of [T.S. Eliot’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/T-S-Eliot) literary and political essays.

## Punctuation in [Greek](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-language) and Latin to 1600

The punctuation now used with English and other western European languages is [derived](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/derived) ultimately from the punctuation used with Greek and Latin during the classical period. Much work remains to be done on the history of the subject, but the outlines are clear enough. Greek inscriptions were normally written continuously, with no divisions between words or sentences; but, in a few inscriptions earlier than the 5th century bc, phrases were sometimes separated by a vertical row of two or three points. In the oldest Greek literary texts, written on papyrus during the 4th century bc, a horizontal line called the _paragraphos_ was placed under the beginning of a line in which a new topic was introduced. This is the only form of punctuation mentioned by Aristotle. [Aristophanes of Byzantium](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristophanes-of-Byzantium), who became librarian of the Museum at Alexandria about 200 bc, is usually credited with the invention of the critical signs, marks of quantity, accents, breathings, and so on, still employed in Greek texts, and with the beginnings of the Greek system of punctuation. [Rhetorical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Rhetorical) theory divided discourse into sections of different lengths. [Aristophanes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristophanes) marked the end of the short section (called a _comma_) by a point after the middle of its last letter, that of the longer section ( _colon_) by a point after the bottom of the letter, and that of the longest section ( _periodos_) by a point after the top of the letter. Since books were still being written in tall [majuscule](https://www.britannica.com/topic/majuscule) letters, like those used in inscriptions and like modern capital letters, the three positions were easily distinguishable. Aristophanes’ system was seldom actually used, except in a [degenerated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/degenerated) version involving only two points. In the 8th or 9th century it was supplemented by the Greek form of question mark (;). The modern system of punctuating Greek texts was established by the Italian and French printers of the Renaissance, whose practice was incorporated in the Greek types cut by [Claude Garamond](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Claude-Garamond) for Francis I of France between 1541 and 1550. The colon is not used in Greek, and the semicolon is represented by a high point. Quotation marks and the exclamation mark were added more recently.

In almost all Roman inscriptions points were used to separate words. In the oldest [Latin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Latin-language) documents and books, dating from the end of the 1st century bc to the beginning of the 2nd century ad, words were divided by points, and a change of topic was sometimes indicated by paragraphing: the first letter or two of the new paragraph projected into the margin, instead of being indented as has been [done](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/done) since the 17th century. Roman scholars, including the 4th-century grammarian [Donatus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aelius-Donatus) and the 6th-century patron of monastic learning [Cassiodorus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cassiodorus), recommended the three-point system of Aristophanes, which was perfectly workable with the majuscule Latin scripts then in use. In practice, however, Latin books in their period were written continuously—the point between words had been abandoned. The ends of sentences were marked, if at all, by only a gap (which might be followed by an enlarged letter) or by an occasional point. The only books that were well punctuated at that time were copies of the [Vulgate](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Vulgate) [Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible), for which its translator, St. [Jerome](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Jerome) (died 419/420), [devised](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/devised) punctuation _per cola et commata_ (“by phrases”), a rhetorical system, based on manuscripts of [Demosthenes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Demosthenes-Greek-statesman-and-orator) and [Cicero](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cicero), which was especially designed to assist reading aloud. Each phrase began with a letter projecting into the margin and was in fact treated as a minute paragraph, before which the reader was expected to take a new breath.

During the 7th and 8th centuries, which saw the transition from majuscule to [minuscule](https://www.britannica.com/topic/minuscule) handwriting (minuscule scripts were usually smaller than majuscule and had projections above and below the body of the letters, as in modern lowercase letters), [scribes](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/scribes) to whom the Latin language was no longer as well known as it had been—especially Irish, [Anglo-Saxon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Anglo-Saxon), and German scribes, to whom it was a foreign language—began to separate words. It was only in the 13th century that monosyllables, especially prepositions, were finally detached from the word following them. The introduction of spaces between words was critical to the development of silent reading, a practice that began only about the 10th century. To mark sentences, a space at the end became the rule; and an enlarged letter, often a majuscule, generally stood at the beginning of sentences and paragraphs alike. The use of points was somewhat confused by [St. Isidore of Sevilla](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Isidore-of-Sevilla) (died 636), whose encyclopaedia recommended an [aberrant](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/aberrant) version of the three-point system; but a point, high or low, was still used within or after sentences. The ends of sentences were often marked by a group of two or three marks, one of which might be a comma and not a simple point.

[St. Jerome’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Jerome) concern for the punctuation of sacred texts was shared by [Charlemagne](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charlemagne), king of the Franks and [Holy Roman emperor](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Holy-Roman-emperor), and his Anglo-Saxon adviser [Alcuin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alcuin), who directed the palace school at Aachen from 782 to 796. An important element in the educational revival over which they presided was the improvement of spelling and punctuation in biblical and liturgical manuscripts. It is in the earliest [specimens](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/specimens) of the new [Carolingian minuscule](https://www.britannica.com/art/Carolingian-minuscule) script, written at Corbie and Aachen about 780–800, that the first evidence for a new system of punctuation appears. It soon spread, with the script itself, throughout Europe, reaching its perfection in the 12th century. Single interior stops in the form of points or commas and final groups of stops continued in use; but they were joined by the mark later known as _punctus elevatus_ (![font character, punccirc](https://cdn.britannica.com/36/213936-004-C3342BA0/punccirc.jpg)) and by the question mark ( _punctus interrogativus_), of much the same shape as the modern one but inclined to the right. The source of these two new marks was apparently the system of [musical notation](https://www.britannica.com/art/musical-notation), called [neumes](https://www.britannica.com/art/neume), which is known to have been used for [Gregorian chant](https://www.britannica.com/art/Gregorian-chant) from at least the beginning of the 9th century. _Punctus elevatus_ and _punctus interrogativus_ indicated not only a pause and a syntactic break but also an appropriate inflection of the voice. By the 12th century another mark, _punctus circumflexus_ (![font character, puncel](https://cdn.britannica.com/37/213937-004-ECEB0D80/puncel.jpg)), had been added to _elevatus_ to indicate a rising inflection at the end of a subordinate clause, especially when the grammatical sense of the sentence was still not complete. Liturgical manuscripts in particular, between the 10th and the 13th centuries, made full use of this inflectional system: it is the origin of the “colon” still used to divide verses of the Psalms in breviaries and prayer books. In the later Middle Ages it was especially the Cistercian, Dominican, and Carthusian orders and the members of religious [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) such as the [Brethren of the Common Life](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Brethren-of-the-Common-Life) who troubled to preserve a mode of punctuation admirably adapted to the constant reading aloud, in church and refectory, that characterized the religious life. The [hyphen](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hyphen), to mark words divided at the ends of lines, appeared late in the 10th century; single at first, it was often doubled in the period between the 14th and 18th centuries.

Most late medieval punctuation was haphazard by comparison with 12th-century work—notably in the university textbooks produced at Paris, Bologna, and Oxford in the 13th and 14th centuries. In them, as elsewhere, a form of paragraph mark representing _c_ for _capitulum_ (“chapter”) is freely used at the beginning of sentences. Within the same period the plain point and _punctus elevatus_ are joined by the virgule (/) as an [alternative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alternative) form of light stop. [Vernacular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Vernacular) literature followed the less formal types of Latin literature; and the printers, as usual, followed the scribes. The first printed texts of the Bible and the liturgy are, as a rule, carefully punctuated on the inflectional principle. The profusion of points and virgules in the English books of the printer [William Caxton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Caxton) pays remarkably little attention to [syntax](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/syntax). Parentheses appeared about 1500. During the 15th century some English legal documents were already being written without punctuation; and British and American lawyers still use extremely light punctuation in the hope of avoiding possible [ambiguities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ambiguities).

The beginnings of postmedieval punctuation can be traced to the excellent manuscripts of classical and contemporary Latin texts copied in the new humanistic scripts by Italian scribes of the 15th century. To about 1450, the point and the _punctus elevatus_ seem to have been preferred for minor pauses; after that date they are often replaced by the virgule and what is now called the colon (:). The virgule, originally placed high, sank to the baseline and developed a curve—it turned, in fact, into a modern comma. The Venetian editor and printer [Aldus Manutius](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aldus-Manutius) (Aldo Manuzio; died 1515) made improvements in the humanistic system, and in 1566 his grandson of the same name [expounded](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/expounded) a similar system in his _Orthographiae ratio_ (“System of Orthography”); it included, under different names, the modern comma, semicolon, colon, and full point, or period. Most importantly, the younger Aldo stated plainly for the first time the view that clarification of syntax is the main object of punctuation. By the end of the 17th century the various marks had received their modern names, and the [exclamation mark](https://www.britannica.com/topic/exclamation-mark), quotation marks, and the dash had been added to the system.

# Punctuation in English since 1600

By the end of the 16th century writers of English were using most of the marks described by the younger Aldo in 1566; but their purpose was elocutionary, not syntactic. When [George Puttenham](https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Puttenham), in his [treatise](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/treatise) _[The Arte of English Poesie](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Arte-of-English-Poesie)_ (1589), and Simon Daines, in _Orthoepia Anglicana_ (1640), specified a pause of one unit for a comma, of two units for a semicolon, and of three for a colon, they were no doubt trying to bring some sort of order into a basically confused and unsatisfactory situation. The punctuation of Elizabethan [drama](https://www.britannica.com/art/dramatic-literature), of the devotional prose of [John Donne](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Donne) or of [Richard Hooker](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Richard-Hooker), and indeed of Bunyan’s _Pilgrim’s Progress_ (1678) was almost wholly elocutionary; and it lacked the inflectional element that had been the making of 12th-century punctuation. It was [Ben Jonson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ben-Jonson-English-writer), in his _English Grammar_, a work composed about 1617 and published posthumously in 1640, who first recommended syntactic punctuation in [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England). An early example is the 1625 edition of Francis Bacon’s _Essayes_; and from the Restoration onward syntactic punctuation was in general use. Influential [treatises](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/treatises) on syntactic punctuation were published by Robert Monteith in 1704 and Joseph Robertson in 1795. Excessive punctuation was common in the 18th century: at its worst it used commas with every subordinate clause and separable phrase. Vestiges of this attitude are found in a handbook published in London as late as 1880. It was the lexicographers [Henry Watson Fowler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/H-W-Fowler) and [Francis George Fowler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-George-Fowler), in _The King’s English_, published in 1906, who established the current British practice of light punctuation. Punctuation in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) has followed much the same path as in Britain, but the rules laid down by American authorities have in general been more rigid than the British rules.

The system of punctuation now used by writers of English has been complete since the 17th century. Three of its most important components are the space left blank between words; the indentation of the first line of a new paragraph; and the uppercase, or capital, letter written at the beginning of a sentence and at the beginning of a proper name or a title. The marks of punctuation, also known as points or stops, and the chief parts that they play in the system are as follows.

The end of a grammatically complete sentence is marked by a full point, full stop, or period. The period may also be used to mark abbreviations. The colon (:), which was once used like a full point and was followed by an [uppercase letter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/majuscule), now serves mainly to indicate the beginning of a list, summary, or quotation. The semicolon (;) ranks halfway between a comma and a full point. It may be substituted for a period between two grammatically complete sentences that are closely connected in sense; in a long or complicated sentence, it may precede a coordinate conjunction (such as _or_, _and_, or _but_). A comma (,) is the “lightest” of the four basic stops. As the most usual means of indicating the syntactic turning points in a sentence, it is exposed to abuse. It may be used to separate the elements of a series, before a relative clause that does not limit or define its [antecedent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/antecedent), in pairs to set off or isolate words or phrases, or in combination with coordinating conjunctions.

Other punctuation marks used in modern English include parentheses, which serve, like a pair of commas, to isolate a word or phrase; question, exclamation, and quotation marks; the hyphen; and the apostrophe (the use of which became standardized only in the 19th century).

## Punctuation in French, Spanish, German, and Russian

Since the modern punctuation of all the western European languages stems from the practice of the great Italian and [French](https://www.britannica.com/topic/French-language) printers of the 15th and 16th centuries, national differences are not considerable. In French, guillemets (<< >>) or dashes are used to mark quotations. In [Spanish](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Spanish-language), since the middle of the 18th century, an inverted mark of interrogation or exclamation has stood at the beginning of sentences as well as the normal mark at the end; and quotations may be marked either as in French or as in English. [German](https://www.britannica.com/topic/German-language) punctuation, which is still based on rules propounded in 1781, is more rigorously [syntactic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/syntactic) than the rest: all relative clauses and all clauses beginning with _dass_ (“that”) must be preceded by a comma. Quotations are marked either by pairs of commas (,,“) or by reversed guillemets (>\> <<). Letter spacing, as well as italic type, is used for emphasis. Early [Russian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Russian-language) punctuation was based on Greek practice, since the [Cyrillic alphabet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Cyrillic-alphabet) is derived from the Greek; and by the 17th century several quite elaborate systems had evolved in different areas. Since the 18th century Russia has used a form of western European punctuation that has much in common with German practice: notably an even wider obligatory use of commas with subordinate and indeed coordinate clauses, and letter spacing (as well as italics) for [emphasis](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/emphasis). German quotation marks, French guillemets, and dashes may be used for direct speech.

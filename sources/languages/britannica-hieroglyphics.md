**hieroglyphic writing**, system that employs characters in the form of pictures. Those individual [signs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sign-communications), called [hieroglyphs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hieroglyph), may be read either as pictures, as symbols for objects, or as symbols for [sounds](https://www.britannica.com/topic/phoneme).

Why Ancient Egyptian Writing Is More Than Just HieroglyphicsHieroglyphics weren't the only form of ancient Egyptian writing—just the first.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hieroglyphic-writing/images-videos)

The name _hieroglyphic_ (from the Greek word for “sacred carving”) is first [encountered](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/encountered) in the writings of [Diodorus Siculus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Diodorus-Siculus) (1st century bce). Earlier, other Greeks had spoken of sacred signs when referring to [Egyptian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Egyptian-language) writing. Among the Egyptian scripts, the Greeks labeled as hieroglyphic the script that they found on temple walls and public monuments, in which the characters were pictures sculpted in stone. The Greeks distinguished this script from two other forms of Egyptian writing that were written with ink on papyrus or on other smooth surfaces. These were known as the [hieratic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hieratic-script), which was still employed during the time of the [ancient Greeks](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Greece) for religious texts, and the [demotic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/demotic-script), the cursive script used for ordinary documents.

Hieroglyphic, in the strict meaning of the word, [designates](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/designates) only the [writing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/writing) on [Egyptian monuments](https://www.britannica.com/art/Egyptian-art). The word has, however, been applied since the late 19th century to the writing of other peoples, insofar as it consists of picture signs used as writing characters. For example, the name _hieroglyphics_ is always used to designate the monumental inscriptions of the [Indus civilization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indus-civilization) and of the [Hittites](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hittite), who also possessed other scripts, in addition to the [Mayan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-hieroglyphic-writing), the Incan, and [Easter Island](https://www.britannica.com/place/Easter-Island) writing forms and also the signs on the Phaistos Disk on Crete.

## Development of Egyptian hieroglyphic writing

The most ancient hieroglyphs date from the end of the 4th millennium bce and [comprise](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprise) [annotations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/annotations) incised onto pottery jars and ivory plaques deposited in tombs, presumably for the purpose of identification of the dead. Although by no means can all of these earliest signs be read today, it is nonetheless probable that these forms are based on the same system as the later classical hieroglyphs. In individual cases, it can be said with certainty that it is not the copied object that is designated but rather another word phonetically similar to it. This circumstance means that hieroglyphs were from the very beginning phonetic symbols. An earlier stage consisting exclusively of [picture writing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pictography) using actual illustrations of the intended words cannot be shown to have existed in Egypt; indeed, such a stage can with great probability be ruled out. No development from pictures to letters took place; hieroglyphic writing was never solely a system of picture writing. It can also be said with certainty that the jar marks (signs on the bottom of clay vessels) that occur at roughly the same period do not represent a [primitive](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/primitive) form of the script. Rather, these designs developed in parallel fashion to hieroglyphic writing and were influenced by it.

It is not possible to prove the connection of hieroglyphs to the [cuneiform](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cuneiform) characters used by the Sumerians in southern Mesopotamia. Such a relationship is improbable because the two scripts are based on entirely different systems. What is conceivable is a general tendency toward words being fixed by the use of signs, without transmission of particular systems.

## Invention and uses of hieroglyphic writing

The need to identify a pictorial representation with a royal individual or a specific, unique event, such as a hunt or a particular battle, led to the application of hieroglyphic writing to a monumental [context](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context). Hieroglyphs added to a scene signified that this illustration represented a particular war rather than an unspecified one or war in general; the writing reflected a new attitude toward time and a view of history as unique events in time. Beginning in the 1st [dynasty](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynasty) (c. 2925–c. 2775 bce), images of nonroyal persons were also [annotated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/annotated) with their names or titles, a further step toward expressing individuality and uniqueness. The so-called annalistic ivory tablets of the first two [dynasties](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynasties) were pictorial representations of the events of a year with specifically designated personal names, places, and incidents. For example, accompanying a scene of the pharaoh’s triumph over his enemies is the [annotation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/annotation) “the first occasion of the defeat of the Libyans.” Simultaneously, the writing of the Egyptians began to appear unaccompanied by pictorial representations, especially on [cylinder seals](https://www.britannica.com/art/cylinder-seal). These roller-shaped incised stones were rolled over the moist clay of jar stoppers. Their inscription prevented the sealed jar from being covertly opened and at the same time described its contents and designated the official responsible for it. In the case of wine, its origin from a specific vineyard and often also the destination of the shipment were designated, and, as a rule, so was the name of the reigning king.

From the stone inscriptions of the 1st dynasty, only individual names are known, these being mainly the names of kings. # Relationship of writing and art

The form of these hieroglyphs of the [Archaic period](https://www.britannica.com/art/Archaic-period) (the 1st to 2nd dynasty) corresponds exactly to the art [style](https://www.britannica.com/art/style-art) of this age. Although definite traditions or conventions were quickly formed with respect to the choice of perspective—e.g., a hand was depicted only as a palm, an eye or a mouth inscribed only in front view—the proportions remained flexible. The prerequisite of every [writing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/writing) system is a basic standardization, but such a standardization is not equivalent to a canon (an established body of rules and principles) in the degree of stylistic conformity that it requires. A recognized canon of Egyptian hieroglyphic writing arose in the 3rd [dynasty](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynasty) and was maintained until the end of the use of the script.

In that hieroglyphic signs represented pictures of living beings or inanimate objects, they retained a close connection to the fine arts. The same models formed the basis of both writing and art, and the style of the writing symbols usually changed with the art style. This correspondence occurred above all because the same craftsmen painted or incised both the writing symbols and the pictures. Deviations from the fine arts occurred when the writing, which was more closely bound to convention, retained patterns that the fine arts had eliminated. The face in front view is an example of this. This representation, apart from very special instances, was eventually rejected as an artistic form, the human face being shown only in profile. The front view of the face was, however, retained as a [hieroglyph](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hieroglyph) from the [Archaic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Archaic) period to the end of the use of hieroglyphic writing. Similar cases involve the depiction of various tools and [implements](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implements). Although some of the objects themselves fell out of use in the course of history—e.g., the general use of clubs as weapons—their representations, mainly misunderstood, were preserved in the hieroglyphic script. The hieroglyphs corresponding to objects that had disappeared from daily life were therefore no longer well known and were occasionally distorted beyond recognition. But the style of representation in the hieroglyphs still remained closely bound to the art of the respective epoch. Thus, there appeared taut, slender hieroglyphic forms or sensuous, fleshy ones or even completely bloated characters, according to the art style of the period.

## Media for hieroglyphic writing

[![hieroglyphs](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/124388-050-EFAFCE59/Hieroglyphs-temple-Ombos-Egypt.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/124388-050-EFAFCE59/Hieroglyphs-temple-Ombos-Egypt.jpg)

[hieroglyphs](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/124388-050-EFAFCE59/Hieroglyphs-temple-Ombos-Egypt.jpg) Hieroglyphs on the temple at ancient Ombos, near modern Kawm Umbu, Egypt.(more)

In historical times (2800 bce–300 ce), hieroglyphic writing was used for inscribing stone monuments and appears in Egyptian [relief](https://www.britannica.com/art/relief-sculpture) techniques, both high relief and bas-relief; in painted form; on metal, sometimes in cast form and sometimes incised; and on wood. In addition, hieroglyphs appear in the most varied kinds of metal and wood [inlay](https://www.britannica.com/art/inlay) work. All these applications correspond exactly with the techniques used in fine art.

Hieroglyphic texts are found primarily on the walls of temples and tombs, but they also appear on memorials and gravestones, on statues, on coffins, and on all sorts of vessels and implements. Hieroglyphic writing was used as much for [secular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/secular) texts—historical inscriptions, songs, legal documents, scientific documents—as for religious subject matter—cult rituals, [myths](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myths), hymns, grave inscriptions of all kinds, and prayers. These inscriptions were, of course, only a decorative monumental writing, unsuitable for everyday purposes. For popular use, [hieratic script](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hieratic-script) was developed, an abbreviated form of the picture symbols such as would naturally develop in writing with brush and ink on smooth surfaces such as papyrus, wood, and limestone.

## Writing and [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ancient-Egyptian-religion)

The influence of religious concepts upon hieroglyphic writing is attested in at least two common usages. First, in the 3rd millennium, certain signs were [avoided](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/avoided) or were used in garbled form in grave inscriptions for fear that the living beings represented by these signs could harm the deceased who lay helpless in the grave. Among these taboo [symbols](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religious-symbolism) were human figures and dangerous animals, such as scorpions and snakes. Second, in all periods and for all uses of the writing, symbols to which a positive religious significance was attached were regularly placed in front of other signs, even if they were to be read after them. Among these were hieroglyphs for God or individual gods as well as those for the king or the palace. Thus, for example, the two signs ![hieroglyphic writing](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/212788-004-3F9617AF/Hieroglyphic-writing.jpg), denoting the word combination “servant of God” (priest), are written so that the symbol for God, ![hieroglyphic writing](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/212789-004-EED8A47A/Hieroglyphic-writing.jpg), stands in front of that for servant, ![hieroglyphic writing](https://cdn.britannica.com/90/212790-004-F76F45D2/Hieroglyphic-writing.jpg), although the former is to be read last. Moreover, theology traced the invention of hieroglyphic writing back to the god [Thoth](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Thoth), although this [myth](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myth) of its divine origin did not have an effect on the development of the script. In the late period, Egyptian texts referred to hieroglyphic inscriptions as “writing of God’s words”; earlier, in contrast, they were simply called “pictures.”

## [Literacy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/literacy) and knowledge of hieroglyphic writing

At all periods only a limited circle understood the hieroglyphic script. Only those who needed the knowledge in their professions acquired the arts of writing and reading. These people were, for example, officials and priests (insofar as they had to be able to read rituals and other sacred texts), as well as craftsmen whose work included the making of inscriptions. Under Greek and especially under Roman rule, the knowledge declined and was entirely confined to temples where priests instructed their pupils in the study of hieroglyphic writing. From the time of the rule of the Ptolemies (305 to 30 bce), national [consciousness](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consciousness) became more and more narrowly bound up with religion, and the tradition-filled hieroglyphic writing was an outward sign of pharaonic civilization—in the fullest sense, a symbol. There was no lack of attempts to replace the hieroglyphic writing, cumbersome and ever more divergent from the [spoken language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/speech-language), with the simpler and more convenient Greek script. Such experiments, however, remained ineffective precisely because of the emotional value that the old writing system had when the country was under the foreign domination of the [Macedonian Greeks](https://www.britannica.com/place/Macedonia-ancient-kingdom-Europe) and the [Romans](https://www.britannica.com/place/Roman-Empire).

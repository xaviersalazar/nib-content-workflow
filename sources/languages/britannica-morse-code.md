### What is Morse Code?

The term Morse Code refers to either of two systems for representing letters of the [alphabet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/alphabet-writing), [numerals](https://www.britannica.com/science/number-mathematics), and [punctuation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/punctuation) marks by an arrangement of dots, dashes, and spaces. The [codes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/code-communications) are transmitted as electrical pulses of varied lengths or analogous mechanical or visual signals, such as flashing lights. The two systems are the original “American” Morse Code and the later International Morse Code, which became the global standard.

### How was Morse Code invented?

One of the Morse code systems was invented in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) by American artist and inventor [Samuel F.B. Morse](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Samuel-F-B-Morse) during the 1830s for electrical [telegraphy](https://www.britannica.com/technology/telegraph). A variant called the International Morse Code was devised by a conference of European nations in 1851 to account for letters with diacritic marks.

### Is Morse Code still used?

The original “American” Morse Code invented by [Samuel F.B. Morse](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Samuel-F-B-Morse) is hardly in use today. However, International Morse Code is still used by [U.S. Navy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/United-States-Navy) intelligence specialists, [amateur radio](https://www.britannica.com/technology/amateur-radio) operator afficionados who form the International Morse Code Preservation Society, and aviators who communicate abbreviated identifiers via Morse Code.

### How does Morse Code work?

International Morse Code uses combinations of dots and short dashes for all letters. In addition, the International Morse Code uses dashes of constant length rather than the variable lengths used in the original Morse Code. For example, the universal distress signal “SOS” is communicated by three dots, three dashes, and three dots—three dots denoting the letter “S” and three dashes denoting the letter “O.”

This version was further improved by American scientist and businessman [Alfred Lewis Vail](https://www.britannica.com/money/Alfred-Lewis-Vail), Morse’s assistant and partner. Soon after its introduction in Europe, it became apparent that the original Morse Code was inadequate for the transmission of much non-English text, since it lacked codes for letters with [diacritic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/diacritic) marks. To remedy this deficiency, a variant called the [International Morse Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/International-Morse-Code) was devised by a conference of European nations in 1851. This newer code is also called Continental Morse Code.

The two systems are similar, but the International Morse Code is simpler and more precise. For example, the original Morse Code used patterns of dots and spaces to represent a few of the letters, whereas the International Morse uses combinations of dots and short dashes for all letters. In addition, the International Morse Code uses dashes of constant length rather than the variable lengths used in the original Morse Code.

The International Morse Code has, except for some minor changes in 1938, remained the same since its [inception](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/inception). (The American telegraph industry never abandoned the original Morse Code, and so its use continued until the spread of [teleprinters](https://www.britannica.com/technology/teleprinter) in the 1920s and ’30s.) International Morse Code was used in [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II) and in the [Korean](https://www.britannica.com/event/Korean-War) and [Vietnam](https://www.britannica.com/event/Vietnam-War) wars. It was used heavily by the [shipping](https://www.britannica.com/technology/shipping-water-transportation) industry and for the safety of the seas up until the early 1990s. Although amateur [radio](https://www.britannica.com/topic/radio) made up only a small part of Morse Code usage, it did prepare many hundreds of operators for military duty in communications. In the early 2000s most countries had dropped the ability to [decipher](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/decipher) Morse Code from the requirements for obtaining an [amateur radio](https://www.britannica.com/technology/amateur-radio) license.

### What did Samuel F.B. Morse invent?

Samuel F.B. Morse developed an electric [telegraph](https://www.britannica.com/technology/telegraph) (1832–35) and then invented, with his friend [Alfred Vail](https://www.britannica.com/money/Alfred-Lewis-Vail), the [Morse Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Morse-Code) (1838). The latter is a system for representing letters of the alphabet, numerals, and punctuation marks by arranging dots, dashes, and spaces. The codes are transmitted through either a telegraph machine or visual signals.

### What was Samuel F.B. Morse’s early life like?

Samuel F.B. Morse was the son of the distinguished geographer and Congregational clergyman [Jedidiah Morse](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jedidiah-Morse). He attended Yale College (now [Yale University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yale-University)), and, although he was an indifferent scholar, his interest was aroused by lectures on the then little-understood subject of [electricity](https://www.britannica.com/science/electricity). To the distress of his austere parents, he also enjoyed painting.

### What were Samuel F.B. Morse’s achievements?

Samuel F.B. Morse was both an accomplished inventor and a painter. He developed an electric [telegraph](https://www.britannica.com/technology/telegraph) (1832–35) and then codeveloped the [Morse Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Morse-Code) (1838). During this time he also painted some of the finest portraits ever done by an American artist.

**Samuel F.B. Morse** (born April 27, 1791, [Charlestown](https://www.britannica.com/place/Charlestown-section-Boston-Massachusetts), [Massachusetts](https://www.britannica.com/place/Massachusetts), U.S.—died April 2, 1872, [New York](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-state), New York) was an American painter and inventor who developed an electric [telegraph](https://www.britannica.com/technology/telegraph) (1832–35). In 1838 he and his friend Alfred Vail developed the [Morse Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Morse-Code).

He was the son of the distinguished geographer and [Congregational](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Congregationalism) clergyman [Jedidiah Morse](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jedidiah-Morse). From [Phillips Academy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Phillips-Academy) in [Andover](https://www.britannica.com/place/Andover-Massachusetts), Massachusetts, where he had been an unsteady and [eccentric](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/eccentric) student, his parents sent him to Yale College (now [Yale University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yale-University)) in [New Haven](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Haven-Connecticut), Connecticut. Although he was an indifferent scholar, his interest was aroused by lectures on the then little-understood subject of [electricity](https://www.britannica.com/science/electricity). To the distress of his [austere](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/austere) parents, he also enjoyed [painting](https://www.britannica.com/art/painting) [miniature portraits](https://www.britannica.com/art/miniature-painting).

After graduating from Yale in 1810, Morse became a clerk for a [Boston](https://www.britannica.com/place/Boston) book publisher. But painting continued to be his main interest, and in 1811 his parents helped him go to England in order to study that art with American painter [Washington Allston](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Washington-Allston). During the [War of 1812](https://www.britannica.com/event/War-of-1812), between Great Britain and the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), Morse reacted to the English [contempt](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contempt) for Americans by becoming passionately pro-American. Like the majority of Americans of his time, however, he accepted English artistic standards, including the “historical” style of painting—the [Romantic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Romantic) portrayal of [legends](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legends) and historical events with personalities gracing the foreground in grand poses and brilliant colours.

When, on his return home in 1815, Morse found that Americans did not appreciate his historical canvases, he reluctantly took up portraiture again to earn a living. He began as an itinerant painter in [New England](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-England), New York, and [South Carolina](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Carolina). After 1825, on settling in [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City), he painted some of the finest portraits ever done by an American artist. He combined technical competence and a bold rendering of his subjects’ character with a touch of the [Romanticism](https://www.britannica.com/art/Romanticism#ref8417) he had imbibed in England.

Although often poor during those early years, Morse was sociable and at home with [intellectuals](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectuals), the wealthy, the religiously orthodox, and the politically [conservative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conservative). In addition, he possessed the gift of friendship. Among his friends in his middle years were a French hero of the [American Revolution](https://www.britannica.com/event/American-Revolution), the [marquis de Lafayette](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Marquis-de-Lafayette), whose attempts to promote liberal reform in [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) Morse ardently [endorsed](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/endorsed), and the novelist [James Fenimore Cooper](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Fenimore-Cooper). Morse and Cooper shared several traits: both were [ardent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ardent) U.S. republicans, though both had aristocratic social tastes, and both suffered from the American preference for European art.

Morse also had the gift of leadership. As part of a campaign against the licentiousness of the theatre, he helped launch, in 1827, the New York _[Journal of Commerce](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Journal-of-Commerce)_, which refused theatre advertisements. He also was a founder of the [National Academy of Design](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Academy-of-Design), organized to increase U.S. respect for painters, and was its first president from 1826 to 1845.

In 1832, while returning by ship from studying art in Europe, Morse [conceived](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conceived) the idea of an electric telegraph as the result of hearing a conversation about the newly discovered [electromagnet](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnet). Although the idea of an electric telegraph had been put forward in 1753 and electric telegraphs had been used to send messages over short distances as early as 1774, Morse believed that his was the first such proposal. He probably made his first working model by 1835.

Meanwhile, Morse was still devoting most of his time to painting, teaching art at the University of the City of New York (later [New York University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/New-York-University)), and to politics (he ran on anti- [immigrant](https://www.britannica.com/topic/immigration) and anti- [Roman Catholic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism) tickets for mayor of New York in 1836 and 1841). But by 1837 he had turned his full attention to the new [invention](https://www.britannica.com/technology/invention-technology). A colleague at the university, chemist Leonard [Gale](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Gale), introduced Morse to [Joseph Henry](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Henry)’s work on [electromagnetism](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnetism). The powerful electromagnets that Henry had devised allowed Morse to send messages over 16 km (10 miles) of wire, a much longer distance than the 12 metres (40 feet) over which his first model could transmit. A friend, Alfred Vail, offered to provide materials and labour to build models in his family’s ironworks in [Morristown](https://www.britannica.com/place/Morristown-New-Jersey), [New Jersey](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Jersey). Gale and Vail became partners in Morse’s telegraph rights. By 1838 he and Vail had developed the system of dots and dashes that became known throughout the world as the [Morse Code](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Morse-Code). In 1838, while unsuccessfully attempting to interest [Congress](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Congress) in building a telegraph line, he acquired Maine Congressman F.O.J. Smith as an additional partner. After failing to organize the construction of a Morse line in Europe, Morse alone among his partners persevered in promoting the telegraph, and in 1843 he was finally able to obtain financial support from Congress for the first telegraph line in the United States, from [Baltimore](https://www.britannica.com/place/Baltimore) to Washington. In 1844 the line was completed, and on May 24 he sent the first message, “What hath God wrought.”

Morse was immediately involved in legal claims by his partners and by rival inventors. A natural controversialist like his father, he fought vigorously in this and other controversies, such as those in art with painter [John Trumbull](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Trumbull-American-painter), in religion with [Unitarians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Unitarianism) and Roman Catholics, in politics with the Irish and [abolitionists](https://www.britannica.com/topic/abolitionism-European-and-American-social-movement), and in [daguerreotypy](https://www.britannica.com/technology/daguerreotype)—of which he was one of the first [practitioners](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/practitioners) in America—with [Louis-Jacques-Mandé Daguerre](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louis-Daguerre)’s pupil, François Gouraud. The legal battles over the telegraph culminated in an 1854 [U.S. Supreme Court](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Supreme-Court-of-the-United-States) decision that established his [patent](https://www.britannica.com/topic/patent) rights. As telegraph lines lengthened on both sides of the Atlantic, his wealth and fame increased. By 1847 Morse had bought Locust Grove, an estate overlooking the [Hudson River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hudson-River) near [Poughkeepsie](https://www.britannica.com/place/Poughkeepsie), New York, where, early in the 1850s, he built an Italian villa-style mansion. He spent his summers there with his large family of children and grandchildren, returning each winter season to his brownstone home in New York City.

In his [old age](https://www.britannica.com/science/old-age), Morse, a patriarch with a flowing beard, became a philanthropist. He gave generously to [Vassar College](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Vassar-College), of which he was a founder and trustee; to his [alma mater](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/alma%20mater), Yale College; and to churches, theological seminaries, [Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible) societies, mission societies, and temperance societies, as well as to poor artists.

Even during Morse’s own lifetime, the world was much changed by the telegraph. In the decades after his death in 1872, his fame as an inventor was obscured by the invention of the [telephone](https://www.britannica.com/technology/telephone), [radio](https://www.britannica.com/topic/radio), [television](https://www.britannica.com/technology/television-technology), and the [Internet](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Internet), while his reputation as an artist has grown. At one time he did not wish to be remembered as a portrait painter, but his powerful and sensitive portraits, among them those of Lafayette, the American writer [William Cullen Bryant](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Cullen-Bryant), and other prominent men, have been exhibited throughout the United States. The number of Morse telegraphic operators has decreased sharply, but his memory is [perpetuated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/perpetuated) by the Morse Telegraph Club (1942), an association dedicated to the history of telegraphy. His 1837 telegraph instrument is preserved by the [Smithsonian Institution](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Smithsonian-Institution)’s National Museum of American History in Washington, [D.C.](https://www.britannica.com/place/Washington-DC), while his estate, Locust Grove, is now designated a national historic landmark.

**language**, a system of [conventional](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conventional) spoken, manual (signed), or written symbols by means of which [human](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being) beings, as members of a [social group](https://www.britannica.com/topic/social-group) and participants in its [culture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/culture), express themselves. The functions of language include [communication](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communication), the expression of [identity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/personal-identity), [play](https://www.britannica.com/topic/play-behavior), imaginative expression, and [emotional](https://www.britannica.com/science/emotion) release.

## Definitions of language

Many definitions of language have been proposed. Henry Sweet, an English phonetician and language scholar, stated: “Language is the expression of ideas by means of speech-sounds combined into words. Words are combined into sentences, this combination answering to that of ideas into thoughts.” The American linguists Bernard Bloch and George L. Trager formulated the following definition: “A language is a system of arbitrary vocal symbols by means of which a social group cooperates.” Any [succinct](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/succinct) [definition](https://www.britannica.com/topic/definition) of language makes a number of presuppositions and begs a number of questions. The first, for example, puts excessive weight on “thought,” and the second uses “arbitrary” in a specialized, though [legitimate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legitimate), way.

_Every physiologically and mentally typical person acquires in childhood the ability to make use, as both sender and receiver, of a system of communication that comprises a circumscribed set of symbols (e.g., sounds, gestures, or written or typed characters)._ In [spoken language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/speech-language), this [symbol](https://www.britannica.com/topic/symbol) [set](https://www.britannica.com/topic/set-mathematics-and-logic) consists of noises resulting from movements of certain organs within the throat and mouth. In [signed languages](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sign-language), these symbols may be hand or body movements, gestures, or facial expressions. By means of these symbols, people are able to impart information, to express feelings and emotions, to influence the activities of others, and to comport themselves with varying degrees of friendliness or hostility toward persons who make use of substantially the same set of symbols.

_Different systems of communication constitute different languages; the degree of difference needed to establish a different language cannot be stated exactly._ No two people speak exactly alike; hence, one is able to recognize the voices of friends over the telephone and to keep distinct a number of unseen speakers in a radio broadcast. [Yet](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Yet), clearly, no one would say that they speak different languages. Generally, systems of communication are recognized as different languages if they cannot be understood without specific learning by both parties, though the precise limits of mutual intelligibility are hard to draw and belong on a scale rather than on either side of a definite dividing line. Substantially different systems of communication that may impede but do not prevent mutual comprehension are called [dialects](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dialect) of a language. In order to describe in detail the actual different language patterns of individuals, the term _[idiolect](https://www.britannica.com/topic/idiolect)_, [meaning](https://www.britannica.com/topic/meaning) the habits of expression of a single person, has been coined.

_Typically, people acquire a single language initially—their first language, or native tongue, the language used by those with whom, or by whom, they are brought up from infancy._ Subsequent “second” languages are learned to different degrees of competence under various conditions. Complete mastery of two languages is designated as [bilingualism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bilingualism); in many cases—such as upbringing by parents using different languages at home or being raised within a multilingual community—children grow up as bilinguals. In traditionally monolingual [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures), the learning, to any extent, of a second or other language is an activity superimposed on the prior mastery of one’s first language and is a different process intellectually.

_Language, as described above, is species-specific to human beings._ Other members of the animal kingdom have the ability to communicate, through vocal noises or by other means, but the most important single feature characterizing human language (that is, every individual language), against every known mode of animal communication, is its [infinite](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infinite) productivity and [creativity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/creativity). Human beings are unrestricted in what they can communicate; no area of experience is accepted as necessarily incommunicable, though it may be necessary to adapt one’s language in order to cope with new discoveries or new modes of [thought](https://www.britannica.com/topic/thought). [Animal communication](https://www.britannica.com/science/animal-communication) systems are by contrast very tightly circumscribed in what may be communicated. Indeed, displaced reference, the ability to communicate about things outside immediate temporal and spatial contiguity, which is fundamental to speech, is found elsewhere only in the so-called language of [bees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bee). Bees are able, by carrying out various conventionalized movements (referred to as [bee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bee) dances) in or near the hive, to indicate to others the locations and strengths of food sources. But food sources are the only known theme of this communication system. Surprisingly, however, this system, nearest to human language in function, belongs to a species remote from humanity in the animal kingdom. On the other hand, the animal performance superficially most like human speech, the mimicry of parrots and of some other birds that have been kept in the company of humans, is wholly [derivative](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/derivative) and serves no independent communicative function. Humankind’s nearest relatives among the primates, though possessing a vocal physiology similar to that of humans, have not developed anything like a spoken language. Attempts to teach [sign language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sign-language) to chimpanzees and other apes through imitation have achieved limited success, though the interpretation of the significance of ape signing ability remains controversial.

_In most accounts, the primary purpose of language is to facilitate communication, in the sense of transmission of information from one person to another._ However, [sociolinguistic](https://www.britannica.com/science/sociolinguistics) and [psycholinguistic](https://www.britannica.com/science/psycholinguistics) studies have drawn attention to a range of other functions for language. Among these is the use of language to express a national or local identity (a common source of conflict in situations of multiethnicity around the world, such as in Belgium, [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), and Quebec). Also important are the “ludic” (playful) function of language—encountered in such phenomena as [puns](https://www.britannica.com/art/pun), [riddles](https://www.britannica.com/art/riddle), and [crossword puzzles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crossword-puzzle)—and the range of functions seen in imaginative or symbolic [contexts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contexts), such as [poetry](https://www.britannica.com/art/poetry), [drama](https://www.britannica.com/place/Drama-Greece), and religious expression.

Language interacts with every aspect of human life in society, and it can be understood only if it is considered in relation to society. This article attempts to survey language in this light and to consider its various functions and the purposes it can and has been made to serve. Because each language is both a working system of communication in the period and in the [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) wherein it is used and also the product of its history and the source of its future development, any account of language must consider it from both these points of view.

# Historical attitudes toward language

As is evident from the discussion above, [human](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being) life in its present form would be impossible and inconceivable without the use of language. People have long recognized the force and significance of language. [Naming](https://www.britannica.com/topic/name)—applying a word to pick out and refer to a fellow human being, an animal, an object, or a [class](https://www.britannica.com/topic/social-class) of such beings or objects—is only one part of the use of language, but it is an essential and prominent part. In many [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) people have seen in the ability to name a means to control or to possess; this explains the reluctance, in some [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities), with which names are revealed to strangers and the [taboo](https://www.britannica.com/topic/taboo-sociology) restrictions found in several parts of the world on using the names of persons recently dead. Such restrictions echo widespread and perhaps universal taboos on naming directly things considered obscene, blasphemous, or very fearful.

Perhaps not surprisingly, several independent traditions ascribe a divine or at least a supernatural origin to language or to the language of a particular [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community). The biblical account, representing ancient [Jewish](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-language) beliefs, of [Adam](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adam-and-Eve-biblical-literary-figures)’s naming the creatures of the earth under God’s guidance is one such example:

> So out of the ground the Lord God formed every beast of the field and every bird of the air, and brought them to the man to see what he would call them; and whatever the man called every living creature, that was its name. (Genesis 2:19)

[Norse mythology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Germanic-religion-and-mythology) preserves a similar story of divine participation in the creation of language, and in [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India) the god [Indra](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indra) is said to have invented [articulate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/articulate) [speech](https://www.britannica.com/topic/speech-language). In the debate on the nature and origin of language given in [Plato](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Plato)’s Socratic [dialogue](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dialogue) _[Cratylus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Cratylus-by-Plato)_, [Socrates](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Socrates) is made to speak of the gods as those responsible for first fixing the names of things in the proper way.

A similar divine aura pervades early accounts of the origin of [writing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/writing). The Norse god [Odin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Odin-Norse-deity) was held responsible for the invention of the [runic alphabet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/runic-alphabet). The inspired stroke of genius whereby the ancient [Greeks](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-alphabet) adapted a variety of the [Phoenician](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Phoenician-alphabet) consonantal script so as to represent the distinctive [consonant](https://www.britannica.com/topic/consonant) and [vowel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vowel) sounds of Greek, thus producing the first [alphabet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/alphabet-writing) such as is known today, was linked with the mythological figure [Cadmus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Cadmus), who, coming from Phoenicia, was said to have founded Thebes and introduced writing into Greece ( _see_ [Phoenician language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Phoenician-language)). By a traditional account, the [Arabic alphabet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Arabic-alphabet), together with [the language itself](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Arabic-language), was given to Adam by God.

[Pieter Bruegel the Elder: _The Tower of Babel_](https://cdn.britannica.com/60/6160-050-4F39391D/Tower-of-Babel-oil-painting-Pieter-Brueghel.jpg) _The Tower of Babel_, oil painting by Pieter Bruegel the Elder, 1563; in the Kunsthistorisches Museum, Vienna.(more)

The later biblical tradition of the [Tower of Babel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tower-of-Babel) (Genesis 11:1–9) exemplifies three aspects of early thought about language: (1) divine interest in and control over its use and development, (2) a recognition of the power it gives to humans in relation to their [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment), and (3) an explanation of linguistic [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity), of the fact that people in [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) communities speak different and mutually unintelligible languages, together with a survey of the various speech communities of the world known at the time to the Hebrew people.

The origin of language has never failed to provide a subject for speculation, and its inaccessibility adds to its fascination. Informed investigations of the probable conditions under which language might have originated and developed are seen in the late 18th-century essay of the German philosopher [Johann Gottfried von Herder](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johann-Gottfried-von-Herder), [“Abhandlung über den Ursprung der Sprache”](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Abhandlung-uber-den-Ursprung-der-Sprache) (“Essay on the Origin of Language”), and in numerous other treatments. But people have tried to go farther, to discover or to reconstruct something like the actual forms and structure of the first language. This lies forever beyond the reach of [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science), in that spoken language in some form is almost certainly coeval with _[Homo sapiens](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Homo-sapiens)_. The earliest records of written language, the only linguistic fossils humanity can hope to have, go back no more than 4,000 to 5,000 years. Some people have tried to claim that the cries of animals and birds, or nonlexical expressions of excitement or anger, evolved into human speech, as if [onomatopoeia](https://www.britannica.com/topic/onomatopoeia) were the essence of language; these claims have been [ridiculed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/ridiculed) for their inadequacy (by, for example, the Oxford [philologist](https://www.britannica.com/science/philology) [Max Müller](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Max-Muller) in the 19th century) and have been given nicknames such as “bowwow” and “pooh-pooh” theories.

On several occasions attempts have been made to identify one particular existing language as representing the original or oldest tongue of humankind, but, in fact, the universal process of linguistic change rules out any such hopes from the start. The Greek historian [Herodotus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Herodotus-Greek-historian) told a (possibly satirical) story in which King [Psamtik I](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Psamtik-I) of Egypt (reigned 664–610 bce) caused a child to be brought up without ever hearing a word spoken in his presence. On one occasion it ran up to its guardian as he brought it some bread, calling out “bekos, bekos”; this, being said to be the Phrygian word for bread, proved that [Phrygian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Phrygian-language) was the oldest language. The naiveté and absurdity of such an account have not prevented the repetition of this experiment elsewhere at other times.

In Christian Europe the [position](https://www.britannica.com/topic/position-prosody) of Hebrew as the language of the [Hebrew Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-Bible) ( [Old Testament](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Old-Testament)) gave valid grounds through many centuries for regarding [Hebrew](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-language), the language in which God was assumed to have addressed Adam, as the parent language of all humankind. Such a view continued to be expressed even well into the 19th century. Only since the mid-1800s has linguistic science made sufficient progress finally to clarify the impracticability of speculation along these lines.

When people have begun to reflect on language, its relation to [thinking](https://www.britannica.com/topic/thought) becomes a central concern. Several cultures have independently viewed the main function of language as the expression of thought. Ancient Indian grammarians speak of the [soul](https://www.britannica.com/topic/soul-religion-and-philosophy) apprehending things with the intellect and inspiring the [mind](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mind) with a desire to speak, and in the Greek [intellectual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectual) tradition [Aristotle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle) declared, “Speech is the representation of the experiences of the mind” ( _[On Interpretation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/On-Interpretation)_). Such an attitude passed into Latin theory and thence into [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) doctrine. Medieval grammarians [envisaged](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/envisaged) three stages in the speaking process: things in the world exhibit properties; these properties are understood by the minds of humans; and, in the manner in which they have been understood, so they are communicated to others by the resources of language. [Rationalist](https://www.britannica.com/topic/rationalism) writers on language in the 17th century gave essentially a similar account: speaking is expressing thoughts by signs invented for the purpose, and words of different classes (the different parts of speech) came into being to correspond to the different aspects of thinking.

Such a view of language continued to be accepted as generally adequate and gave rise to the sort of [definition](https://www.britannica.com/topic/definition) proposed by Henry Sweet and quoted above. The main objection to it is that it either gives so wide an interpretation to thought as virtually to empty the word of any specific content or gives such a narrow interpretation of language as to [exclude](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/exclude) a great deal of normal usage. A recognition of the part played by speaking and writing in social cooperation in everyday life has highlighted the many and varied functions of language in all cultures, apart from the functions strictly involved in the [communication](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communication) of thought, which had been the main focus of attention for those who approached language from the standpoint of the philosopher. To allow for the full range of language used by speakers, more-comprehensive definitions of language have been proposed on the lines of the second one quoted at the beginning of this article—namely, “A language is a system of arbitrary vocal symbols by means of which a [social group](https://www.britannica.com/topic/social-group) cooperates.” Despite the breadth of this definition, however, its use of the word _vocal_ excludes all languages that are not vocalized, particularly manual (signed) languages.

A rather different [criticism](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criticism) of accepted views on language began to be made in the 18th century, most notably by the French philosopher [Étienne Bonnot de Condillac](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Etienne-Bonnot-de-Condillac) in “Essai sur l’origine des connaissances humaines” (1746; “Essay on the Origin of Human Knowledge”) and by [Johann Gottfried von Herder](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johann-Gottfried-von-Herder). These thinkers were concerned with the origin and development of language in relation to thought in a way that earlier students had not been. The medieval and rationalist views implied that humans, as rational, thinking creatures, invented language to express their thoughts, fitting words to an already developed structure of intellectual competence. With the examination of the actual and the probable historical relations between thinking and communicating, it became more [plausible](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/plausible) to say that language emerged not as the means of expressing already formulated judgments, questions, and the like but as the means of thought itself, and that humans’ rationality developed together with the development of their capacity for communicating.

The relations between thought and communication are certainly not fully explained today, and it is clear that it is a great oversimplification to define thought as subvocal speech, in the manner of some [behaviourists](https://www.britannica.com/science/behaviourism-psychology). But it is no less clear that propositions and other [alleged](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alleged) logical structures cannot be wholly separated from the language structures said to express them. Even the symbolizations of modern [formal logic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/formal-logic) are ultimately derived from statements made in some natural language and are interpreted in that light.

The [intimate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intimate) connection between language and thought, as opposed to the earlier assumed unilateral dependence of language on thought, opened the way to a recognition of the possibility that different [language](https://www.britannica.com/science/Whorfian-hypothesis) structures might in part favour or even determine different ways of understanding and thinking about the world. All people inhabit a broadly similar world, or they would be unable to [translate](https://www.britannica.com/topic/translation-linguistics) from one language to another, but they do not all inhabit a world exactly the same in all particulars, and translation is not merely a matter of substituting different but equivalent labels for the contents of the same inventory. From this stem the [notorious](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/notorious) difficulties in translation, especially when the systematizations of science, law, [morals](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/morals), social structure, and so on are involved. The extent of the interdependence of language and thought—linguistic relativity, as it has been termed—is still a matter of debate, but the fact of such interdependence can hardly fail to be acknowledged.

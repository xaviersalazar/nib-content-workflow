**habit**, in psychology, any regularly repeated behaviour that requires little or no thought and is learned rather than [innate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/innate). A habit—which can be part of any activity, ranging from eating and sleeping to thinking and reacting—is developed through [reinforcement](https://www.britannica.com/science/reinforcement) and [repetition](https://www.britannica.com/topic/repetition-human-psychology). Reinforcement encourages the repetition of a behaviour, or response, each time the stimulus that provoked the behaviour recurs. The behaviour becomes more automatic with each repetition. Some habits, however, may form on the basis of a single experience, particularly when emotions are involved. Habits, as discussed by [William James](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-James) in his _[Principles of Psychology,](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Principles-of-Psychology-by-James)_ are useful as the means for conserving higher mental processes for more demanding tasks, but they promote behavioral inflexibility.

Related Topics: [learned behaviour](https://www.britannica.com/science/learned-behavior)_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/habit-behavior)

Five methods are commonly used to break unwanted habits: the replacement of the old response with a new response— _e.g.,_ eating fruit instead of candy to satisfy a craving for sweetness; the repetition of the behaviour until fatigue or another unpleasant response takes over— _e.g.,_ being forced to smoke cigarettes until nauseated so that a repulsion for cigarettes replaces the desire to smoke; the change of [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) to separate the individual from the stimulus that is prompting the response; the gradual introduction of the stimulus that is provoking the behaviour— _e.g.,_ overcoming a child’s fear of adult dogs by giving him a puppy; and punishment, which is probably the least effective method.

**behaviourism**, a highly influential academic school of [psychology](https://www.britannica.com/science/psychology) that dominated psychological theory between the two world wars. Classical behaviourism, prevalent in the first third of the 20th century, was concerned exclusively with measurable and observable data and excluded ideas, emotions, and the consideration of inner mental experience and activity in general. In behaviourism, the organism is seen as “responding” to conditions (stimuli) set by the outer [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) and by inner biological processes.

The previously dominant school of [thought](https://www.britannica.com/topic/thought), [structuralism](https://www.britannica.com/science/structuralism-psychology), conceived of psychology as the [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science) of [consciousness](https://www.britannica.com/topic/consciousness), experience, or mind; although bodily activities were not excluded, they were considered significant chiefly in their relations to mental phenomena. The characteristic method of structuralism was thus [introspection](https://www.britannica.com/topic/introspection)—observing and reporting on the working of one’s own [mind](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mind).

The early formulations of behaviourism were a reaction by U.S. psychologist [John B. Watson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-B-Watson) against the introspective psychologies. In _Behaviorism_ (1924), Watson wrote that “Behaviorism claims that ‘consciousness’ is neither a definable nor a usable concept; that it is merely another word for the ‘soul’ of more ancient times. The old psychology is thus dominated by a subtle kind of religious philosophy.” Watson believed that behaviourism “attempted to make a fresh, clean start in psychology, breaking both with current theories and with traditional concepts and terminology” (from _Psychology from the Standpoint of a Behaviorist_, 3rd ed., 1929). Introspection was to be discarded; only such observations were to be considered admissible as could be made by independent observers of the same object or event—exactly as in [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science) or [chemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemistry). In this way psychology was to become “a purely objective, experimental branch of natural science.” However abstract these proposals may seem, they have had a revolutionary influence on modern psychology and [social science](https://www.britannica.com/topic/social-science) and on our [conception](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conception) of ourselves.

Watson’s objectivist leanings were presaged by many developments in the history of thought, and his work typified strong trends that had been emerging in [biology](https://www.britannica.com/science/biology) and psychology since the late 19th century. Thus, Watson’s desire to “bury subjective subject matter” received widespread support. Between the early 1920s and mid-century, the methods of behaviourism dominated U.S. psychology and had wide international [repercussions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/repercussions). Although the chief [alternatives](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alternatives) to behaviourism (e.g., [Gestalt](https://www.britannica.com/science/Gestalt-psychology) psychology and [psychoanalysis](https://www.britannica.com/science/psychoanalysis)) advocated methods based on experiential data, even these alternatives accommodated the objectivist approach by emphasizing a need for objective validation of experientially based [hypotheses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hypotheses).

The period 1912–30 (roughly) may be called that of classical behaviourism. Watson was then the dominant figure, but many others were soon at work giving their own systematic twists to the development of the program. Classical behaviourism was dedicated to proving that phenomena formerly believed to require introspective study (such as thinking, imagery, emotions, or feeling) might be understood in terms of stimulus and [response](https://www.britannica.com/science/reflex-physiology). Classical behaviourism was further characterized by a strict determinism based on the belief that every response is elicited by a specific stimulus.

A derivative form of classical behaviourism known as neobehaviourism evolved from 1930 through the late 1940s. In this approach, psychologists attempted to translate the general [methodology](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/methodology) prescribed by Watson into a detailed, experimentally based theory of adaptive behaviour. This era was dominated by [learning](https://www.britannica.com/science/learning) theorists [Clark L. Hull](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Clark-L-Hull) and [B.F. Skinner](https://www.britannica.com/biography/B-F-Skinner); Skinner’s thought was the direct descendant of Watson’s [intellectual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectual) heritage and became dominant in the field after the mid-1950s. Other important behaviourists included Hull-influenced [Kenneth W. Spence](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kenneth-Wartinbee-Spence); [Neal Miller](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Neal-E-Miller), who claimed that neuroscience is the most productive avenue in psychological research; [cognitive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cognitive) theorist [Edward C. Tolman](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edward-C-Tolman); and [Edwin R. Guthrie](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edwin-Ray-Guthrie). Tolman and others brought about a liberalization of strict behaviourist doctrine. The posture toward objectivism remained fundamentally the same, even while admitting the existence of intervening (i.e., mental) variables, accepting verbal reports, and branching into areas such as [perception](https://www.britannica.com/topic/perception).

A natural outgrowth of behaviourist theory was [behaviour therapy](https://www.britannica.com/science/behaviour-therapy), which rose to prominence after [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II) and focused on modifying observable behaviour, rather than the thoughts and feelings of the patient (as in psychoanalysis). In this approach, emotional problems are thought to result from faulty [acquired](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/acquired) behaviour patterns or the failure to learn effective responses. The aim of behaviour therapy, also known as behaviour modification, is therefore to change behaviour patterns. _See also_ [conditioning](https://www.britannica.com/science/conditioning).

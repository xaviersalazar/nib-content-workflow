**phobia**, an extreme, [irrational](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/irrational) fear of a specific object or situation. A phobia is classified as a type of [anxiety disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/anxiety-disorder), since anxiety is the chief symptom experienced by the sufferer. Phobias are thought to be learned emotional responses. It is generally held that phobias occur when fear produced by an original threatening situation is transferred to other similar situations, with the original fear often repressed or forgotten. An excessive, unreasoning fear of water, for example, may be based on a forgotten childhood experience of almost drowning. The person accordingly tries to avoid that situation in the future, a response that, while reducing anxiety in the short term, [reinforces](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/reinforces) the person’s association of the situation with the onset of anxiety.

[Behaviour therapy](https://www.britannica.com/science/behaviour-therapy) is often successful in overcoming phobias. In such therapy, the phobic person is gradually exposed to the anxiety-provoking object or situation in a controlled manner until he eventually ceases to feel anxiety, having realized that his fearful expectations of the situation remain unfulfilled. In this way, the strong associative links between the feared situation, the person’s experience of anxiety, and his subsequent avoidance of that situation are broken and are replaced by a less-maladaptive set of responses. Psychotherapy may also be useful in the treatment of phobias.

Although psychiatrists classify phobias as a single type of anxiety disorder, hundreds of words have been coined to specify the nature of the fear by [prefixing](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/prefixing) “phobia” with the Greek word for the object feared. Among the more common examples are [acrophobia](https://www.britannica.com/science/acrophobia), fear of high places; [claustrophobia](https://www.britannica.com/science/claustrophobia), fear of closed places; nyctophobia, fear of the dark; ochlophobia, fear of crowds; [xenophobia](https://www.britannica.com/science/xenophobia), fear of strangers; and zoophobia, fear of animals. [Agoraphobia](https://www.britannica.com/science/agoraphobia), the fear of being in open or public places, is a particularly crippling [illness](https://www.britannica.com/science/disease) that may prevent its victims from even leaving home. School phobia may afflict schoolchildren who are overly attached to a parent. _See also_ [anxiety](https://www.britannica.com/science/anxiety).

**anxiety disorder**, any of several [disorders](https://www.britannica.com/science/mental-disorder) that are characterized by a feeling of fear, dread, or [apprehension](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/apprehension) that arises without a clear or appropriate cause. [Anxiety](https://www.britannica.com/science/anxiety) normally is an [adaptive mechanism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/defense-mechanism) that signals a potentially harmful internal or external change and thereby enables individuals to avoid harm or to cope with [stress](https://www.britannica.com/science/stress-psychology-and-biology). When anxiety occurs for seemingly no reason or in an exaggerated manner in response to relatively [innocuous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/innocuous) stimuli and disrupts a person’s life, an anxiety disorder may be diagnosed.

Anxiety disorders are some of the most-common [psychiatric](https://www.britannica.com/science/psychiatry) disorders in the world. However, despite effective treatments, many affected individuals are not diagnosed or treated adequately. Anxiety disorders often are chronic and disabling conditions associated with enormous [emotional](https://www.britannica.com/science/emotion) and [monetary](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/monetary) costs.

## Common anxiety disorders

## [Generalized anxiety disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/generalized-anxiety-disorder)

People with generalized anxiety disorder have persistent worry and anxiety symptoms for at least a six-month period. The condition affects women more than men, with the average age of onset being about 30 years. It tends to run a chronic course. Generalized anxiety disorder often is accompanied by other conditions, such as [depression](https://www.britannica.com/science/depression-psychology), [panic disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/panic-disorder), social anxiety disorder, and substance abuse. Certain indices of disability in generalized anxiety disorder, particularly role impairment, are comparable in severity to those of [major depressive disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/major-depressive-disorder). The [concurrent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/concurrent) presence of depression or other conditions in generalized anxiety disorder increases not only the person’s burden of disability but also the economic costs (due to absenteeism, increased [health](https://www.britannica.com/topic/health) care use, and hospitalizations) associated with the disorder.

## [Obsessive-compulsive disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/obsessive-compulsive-disorder)

[Obsessive-compulsive disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/obsessive-compulsive-disorder) (OCD) is characterized by the presence of repetitive, highly intrusive, anxiety-provoking thoughts, impulses, or images known as [obsessions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/obsessions). In about 80 percent of cases, obsessions lead to mental and motor compulsions (repetitive ritualized thoughts or acts) to reduce the anxiety. People with OCD may spend several hours each day engaged in compulsions, to the exclusion of normal life activities. OCD usually [manifests](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifests) in children or young adults, tends to run a chronic waxing-and-waning course, and is associated with significant disability in many life areas. Many OCD patients report low self-esteem, tend to seek help very late, and have high rates of major depression during their lifetime. People with severe OCD may have serious impairments in social functioning and may be financially dependent on others.

## [Panic disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/panic-disorder)

Panic disorder is characterized by sudden, sometimes spontaneous [attacks](https://www.britannica.com/science/panic-attack) of terrifying anxiety accompanied by [symptoms](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/symptoms) such as the experience of terror, [heart](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart) palpitations, and [sweating](https://www.britannica.com/science/sweat). Fear of the attacks themselves generates a pattern of avoidance that can severely constrict the person’s life. During attacks, fear that the symptoms may signal a [heart attack](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart-attack), [stroke](https://www.britannica.com/science/stroke-medical-condition), or other medical problem leads to repeated emergency room visits and expensive workups. People with panic disorder tend to use medical care more frequently than people without the condition. The degree to which patients with panic disorder are impaired may be influenced by various factors, such as age, education, panic disorder [diagnosis](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diagnosis), or the presence of another psychiatric condition (specifically, major depression).

## [Post-traumatic stress disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/post-traumatic-stress-disorder)

Persons who experience life-threatening trauma (e.g., [sexual abuse](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sexual-abuse), combat, natural disasters) may be affected by recurrent intrusive thoughts and [dreams](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dream-sleep-experience) of the traumatic event, abnormally increased alertness and anxiety, and emotional numbing as well as avoidance of situations that remind them of their trauma. These symptoms are characteristic of [post-traumatic stress disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/post-traumatic-stress-disorder) (PTSD), which is a very common condition that tends to run a chronic course. It often is associated with other anxiety disorders, depression, and substance abuse. Patients may be irritable and angry, which can lead to difficulty in interpersonal relationships.

Veterans with [war](https://www.britannica.com/topic/war)-related PTSD have high rates of health complaints, medical conditions, and health care use. Similar [impairments](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/impairments) of subjective distress, poor physical and emotional health, and impaired social and role functioning are experienced by nonveteran PTSD patients (e.g., [rape](https://www.britannica.com/topic/rape-crime) victims). PTSD often co-occurs with chronic [pain](https://www.britannica.com/science/pain) and other medical conditions (such as [HIV](https://www.britannica.com/science/HIV)) and is associated with intensified pain, greater disability, and worse medical outcomes. PTSD is associated with an elevated [suicide](https://www.britannica.com/topic/suicide) rate.

## [Social anxiety disorder](https://www.britannica.com/science/social-anxiety-disorder) and specific phobias

Social anxiety disorder typically develops in [adolescence](https://www.britannica.com/science/adolescence), is more common in women, and usually runs a chronic course. Symptoms may include a fear of strangers, a fear of humiliation or of being judged by others, and an avoidance of social situations where attention centres on the individual. When confronted with these situations, the individual may experience blushing, [diarrhea](https://www.britannica.com/science/diarrhea), elevated [heart rate](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart-rate), [nausea](https://www.britannica.com/science/nausea-pathology), sweating, or trembling. Social anxiety disorder often is accompanied by depression and an increased risk of [alcohol abuse](https://www.britannica.com/science/alcoholism). Patients with subthreshold symptoms of social anxiety disorder can suffer from significant disability, potentially increasing concurrent depression.

Specific [phobias](https://www.britannica.com/science/phobia) involve irrational fear that is focused on a particular entity (e.g., [dogs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dog)) or situation (e.g., driving through a [tunnel](https://www.britannica.com/technology/tunnel)). Symptoms of anticipatory anxiety, distress when in the presence of the feared object or situation, and active avoidance of triggering situations are characteristic of individuals with specific phobias.

## [Treatment](https://www.britannica.com/science/therapeutics) of anxiety disorders

There are several treatment options for anxiety disorders, including [cognitive behavioral therapy](https://www.britannica.com/science/cognitive-behavior-therapy) and [medications](https://www.britannica.com/science/drug-chemical-agent) such as anti-anxiety, [benzodiazepine](https://www.britannica.com/science/benzodiazepine) drugs like [alprazolam (Xanax)](https://www.britannica.com/science/alprazolam). However, the shame, fear, and stigma that often accompany [diagnoses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diagnoses) cause many patients to avoid seeking care in the first place. Other obstacles to treatment may include failure of the health system to detect an anxiety disorder. Even when treated, many patients are left with residual symptoms and disability. However, there is clear evidence that both effective psychotherapeutic and medical treatment can improve personal and occupational functioning, raise [quality of life](https://www.britannica.com/topic/quality-of-life), and reduce unnecessary medical use costs for most anxiety disorder cases. In addition, specifically targeting functional gains rather than only symptom reduction with treatments has been proposed as a means of greatly improving functional outcomes for persons with anxiety disorders. Routine use of simple validated rating scales can objectively document functional impairment, aid in evaluating the true [efficacy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/efficacy) of treatments, and assist patients in their application for disability benefits or appropriate workplace accommodations.

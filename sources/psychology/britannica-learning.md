**learning**, the [alteration](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/alteration) of behaviour as a result of individual experience. When an organism can perceive and change its behaviour, it is said to learn.

## Types of learning

The array of learned behaviour includes [discrimination](https://www.britannica.com/science/discrimination-psychology) learning (where a subject learns to respond to a limited range of sensory characteristics, such as a particular shade of coloration), [habituation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/habituation) (the cessation of responses to repeated stimulation), [concept formation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/concept-formation) (the process of sorting experiences according to related features), [problem solving](https://www.britannica.com/topic/thought/Types-of-thinking#ref275924), [perceptual learning](https://www.britannica.com/topic/perceptual-learning) (the effects of past experience on sensory perceptions), and [psychomotor learning](https://www.britannica.com/science/psychomotor-learning) (the development of neuromuscular patterns in response to sensory signals). [Association](https://www.britannica.com/science/association-psychology), [conditioning](https://www.britannica.com/science/conditioning), [imitation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/imitation-behavior), [insight](https://www.britannica.com/topic/insight-learning), and [imprinting](https://www.britannica.com/topic/imprinting-learning-behavior) represent other types of learning.

Learning theorists from the 17th through the mid-20th century attempted to develop a scientific proof of certain principles that governed all processes of learning. Rigorous, “objective” [methodology](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/methodology) was attempted so that the behaviour of all organisms could be comprehended under a unified system of laws modeled on those posited in the physical sciences. By the 1970s, however, flaws and gaps in these [comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive) theories led many psychologists to conclude that learning could not be represented by a single universal theory.

The last attempts to [integrate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrate) all knowledge of [psychology](https://www.britannica.com/science/psychology) into one grand theory occurred in the 1930s. These were represented in the works of [Edwin R. Guthrie](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edwin-Ray-Guthrie), [Clark L. Hull](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Clark-L-Hull), and [Edward C. Tolman](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edward-C-Tolman). Guthrie reasoned that responses (not [perceptions](https://www.britannica.com/topic/perception) or mental states) were the central building blocks of learning. Hull argued that “habit strength,” a result of practiced stimulus-response (S-R) activities promoted by reward, was the essential aspect of learning, which he viewed as a gradual process. Tolman portrayed learning as a process that derives from behaviour. Many of these themes remain vital to contemporary research.

[Association](https://www.britannica.com/science/association-psychology) is one such theme. Its essence lies in the observation that a subject perceives something in the [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) (sensations) and the result is an awareness of what is out there (ideas). Associations leading to ideas were said to include closeness of objects or events in space or time, similarity, frequency, salience, and attractiveness. [Associative learning](https://www.britannica.com/topic/associative-learning), the ability of an animal to connect a previously irrelevant [stimulus](https://www.britannica.com/science/stimulus) with a particular response, occurs mainly through the process of [conditioning](https://www.britannica.com/science/conditioning), in which [reinforcement](https://www.britannica.com/science/learning-theory/Major-themes-and-issues#ref70240) [crystallizes](https://www.britannica.com/science/conditioned-reflex) new behaviour patterns. The earliest well-known conditioning experiment was performed by 19th-century Russian physiologist [Ivan Petrovich Pavlov](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ivan-Pavlov), who conditioned dogs to salivate to the sound of bells.

[S-R theories](https://www.britannica.com/science/stimulus-response-theory) failed to account for many learned phenomena, however, and seemed overly [reductive](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/reductive) because they ignored a subject’s inner activities. Tolman headed another, less “objective” camp that held that associations involved a stimulus and a subjective sensory impression (S-S).

Another current theme is that of [reinforcement](https://www.britannica.com/science/reinforcement), which accounts for the finding that a subject’s performance will improve when his or her activities are rewarded. The theoretical mechanisms of such reinforcement are controversial.

Many psychologists discount any universal applicability of association theory, saying that other considerations are of greater importance to learning. [Gestalt](https://www.britannica.com/science/Gestalt-psychology) psychologists, for instance, believe that the key learning processes involve a restructuring of relationships in the environment, not simply an associative experience with them. [Psycholinguists](https://www.britannica.com/science/psycholinguistics) (those who study the psychological aspects of [language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/language) ability) argue that language learning involves too many words and combinations to be satisfactorily explained by association theory. Instead, they argue that some basic organizing structure underlies language learning, perhaps an [inherited](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/inherited) native “grammar.”

Other major issues of contemporary theories of learning include the role of motivation in performance; the [transfer of training](https://www.britannica.com/topic/transfer-of-training) between a task already learned and one yet to be learned; learning stages; and the processes and nature of [recall](https://www.britannica.com/topic/recall-memory), forgetting, and [information retrieval](https://www.britannica.com/technology/information-retrieval) or [memory](https://www.britannica.com/science/memory-psychology). Other scientists are exploring nonquantifiable concepts such as image, [cognition](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cognition-thought-process), awareness, and volition.

## Physiological basis of learning

The mechanisms of learning and remembering seem to depend on relatively enduring changes in the [nervous system](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-nervous-system). Apparently the effects of learning are first retained in the [brain](https://www.britannica.com/science/brain) by some [reversible process](https://www.britannica.com/science/reversibility), after which a more permanent neural change takes place. Two types of neurological processes have therefore been suggested. The short-term function of [memory](https://www.britannica.com/science/memory-psychology), temporary and reversible, may be achieved through a physiological mechanism (e.g., synaptic electrical or chemical change) that keeps the memory trace alive over a limited period of time. The ensuing, more permanent (long-term) storage may depend on changes in the physical or chemical structure of [neurons](https://www.britannica.com/science/neuron); synaptic changes seem to be particularly important. New studies using [positron emission tomography](https://www.britannica.com/topic/positron-emission-tomography) (PET) and [magnetic resonance imaging](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-resonance-imaging)(MRI) scans may [illuminate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/illuminate) the physiological basis of learning.

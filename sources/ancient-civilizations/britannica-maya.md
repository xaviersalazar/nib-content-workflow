### When did Maya civilization begin?

As early as 1500 BCE the Maya had settled in villages and were practicing agriculture. The Classic Period of Maya culture lasted from about 250 CE until about 900. At its height, Maya civilization consisted of more than 40 cities, each with a population between 5,000 and 50,000. During the Post-Classic Period (900–1519), cities in the [Yucatán Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yucatan-Peninsula) continued to flourish for several centuries after the great cities of lowland Guatemala had become depopulated.

### Where did the Maya live?

Maya civilization occupied much of the northwestern part of the isthmus of Central America, from [Chiapas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chiapas) and Yucatán, now part of southern [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico), through [Guatemala](https://www.britannica.com/place/Guatemala), [Honduras](https://www.britannica.com/place/Honduras), [Belize](https://www.britannica.com/place/Belize), and [El Salvador](https://www.britannica.com/place/El-Salvador) and into [Nicaragua](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nicaragua). Maya people still live in the same region today. In the early 21st century some 30 [Mayan languages](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mayan-languages) were spoken by more than five million people, most of whom were bilingual in Spanish.

### What did the Maya eat?

The Maya had developed an agriculture based on the cultivation of [corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corn-plant) (maize), [beans](https://www.britannica.com/plant/bean), and [squash](https://www.britannica.com/plant/squash) by about 1500 BCE; by 600 CE cassava (sweet manioc) was also grown. They practiced mainly slash-and-burn agriculture, but they used advanced techniques of irrigation and terracing.

### Were the Maya polytheistic or monotheistic?

Pre-Columbian Maya religion was polytheistic. Important gods included [Itzamná](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Itzamna), the supreme Maya deity and original creator god who had several forms; the Feathered Serpent, known to the Maya as [Kukulcán](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quetzalcoatl) (and to the Toltecs and Aztecs as Quetzalcóatl); and Bolon Tzacab, who is thought to have functioned as a god of royal descent. Today most Maya are nominal [Roman Catholics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism)—though, beginning in the late 20th century, many converted to Evangelical Protestantism.

**Maya**, [Indigenous people of Mesoamerica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mesoamerican-Indian) occupying a nearly [continuous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/continuous) territory in southern [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico), [Guatemala](https://www.britannica.com/place/Guatemala), and northern [Belize](https://www.britannica.com/place/Belize). In the early 21st century some 30 [Mayan languages](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mayan-languages) were spoken by more than five million people, most of whom were bilingual in [Spanish](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Spanish-language). Before the [Spanish conquest of Mexico and Central America](https://www.britannica.com/place/Central-America/The-Spanish-conquest#ref40843), the Maya possessed one of the most complex societies in the [Western Hemisphere](https://www.britannica.com/place/Western-Hemisphere) ( _see_ [pre-Columbian civilizations: The earliest Maya civilization of the lowlands](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pre-Columbian-civilizations/Late-Formative-Period-300-bce-100-ce#ref69356)). They practiced agriculture, built great stone buildings and pyramid temples, worked gold and copper, and used a form of [hieroglyphic writing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-hieroglyphic-writing) that has now largely been deciphered.

As early as 1500 bce the Maya had settled in villages and had developed an agriculture based on the cultivation of [corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corn-plant) (maize), [beans](https://www.britannica.com/plant/bean), and [squash](https://www.britannica.com/plant/squash); by 600 ce [cassava](https://www.britannica.com/plant/cassava) (sweet manioc) was also grown. ( _See also_ [origins of agriculture: Early development: The Americas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agriculture/The-Americas#ref10763).) They began to build ceremonial centers, and by 200 ce these had developed into cities containing temples, pyramids, palaces, courts for playing ball, and plazas. The ancient Maya [quarried](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/quarried) immense quantities of building stone (usually limestone), which they cut by using harder stones such as [chert](https://www.britannica.com/science/chert). They practiced mainly [slash-and-burn agriculture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/slash-and-burn-agriculture), but they used advanced techniques of [irrigation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation) and [terracing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/terrace-cultivation). They also developed a system of [hieroglyphic writing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-hieroglyphic-writing) and highly sophisticated [calendrical](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-calendar) and astronomical systems. The Maya made [paper](https://www.britannica.com/technology/paper) from the inner bark of wild fig trees and wrote their hieroglyphs on books made from this paper. Those books are called [codices](https://www.britannica.com/topic/codex-manuscript). The Maya also developed an elaborate and beautiful tradition of [sculpture](https://www.britannica.com/art/sculpture) and [relief carving](https://www.britannica.com/art/relief-sculpture). Architectural works and stone inscriptions and reliefs are the chief sources of knowledge about the early Maya. Early Maya [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) showed the influence of the earlier [Olmec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Olmec) civilization.

The rise of the Maya began about 250 ce, and what is known to [archaeologists](https://www.britannica.com/science/archaeology) as the [Classic Period](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Classic-Period) of Maya culture lasted until about 900 ce. At its height, Maya civilization consisted of more than 40 cities, each with a population between 5,000 and 50,000. Among the principal cities were [Tikal](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tikal), [Uaxactún](https://www.britannica.com/place/Uaxactun), [Copán](https://www.britannica.com/place/Copan), [Bonampak](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bonampak), [Dos Pilas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dos-Pilas), Calakmul, [Palenque](https://www.britannica.com/place/Palenque-ancient-city-Mexico), and Río Bec. At its peak the Maya population may have reached two million people, most of whom were settled in the lowlands of what is now Guatemala. After 900 ce, however, the Classic Maya civilization declined precipitously, leaving the great cities and ceremonial centers vacant and overgrown with jungle vegetation. Some scholars have suggested that armed conflicts and the exhaustion of agricultural land were responsible for the sudden decline. Discoveries in the 21st century led scholars to posit a number of additional reasons for the destruction of Maya civilization. One cause was probably the war-related disruption of river and land trade routes. Other contributors may have been [deforestation](https://www.britannica.com/science/deforestation) and [drought](https://www.britannica.com/science/drought). During the Post-Classic Period (900–1519), cities such as [Chichén Itzá](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chichen-Itza), [Uxmal](https://www.britannica.com/place/Uxmal), and [Mayapán](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mayapan) in the [Yucatán Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yucatan-Peninsula) continued to flourish for several centuries after the great lowland cities had become depopulated. By the time the Spaniards conquered the area in the early 16th century, most of the Maya had become village-dwelling agriculturists who practiced the religious rites of their forebears.

The major [extant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/extant) Maya cities and ceremonial centers feature a variety of pyramidal temples or palaces overlain with limestone blocks and richly ornamented with narrative, ceremonial, and astronomical reliefs and inscriptions that have ensured the stature of Maya art as premier among [Indigenous American](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indigenous-peoples-of-the-Americas) [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures). But the true nature of Maya society, the meaning of its hieroglyphics, and the chronicle of its [history](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history) remained unknown to scholars for centuries after the Spaniards discovered the ancient Maya building sites.

Systematic explorations of Maya sites were first undertaken in the 1830s, and a small portion of the writing system was deciphered in the early and mid-20th century. Those discoveries shed some light on [Maya religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mayan-religion), which was based on a pantheon of nature gods, including those of the Sun, the Moon, rain, and corn. A priestly class was responsible for an elaborate cycle of rituals and ceremonies. Closely related to Maya religion—indeed, inextricable from it—was the impressive development of [mathematics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics) and [astronomy](https://www.britannica.com/science/astronomy). In mathematics, positional notation and the use of the [zero](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Zero-Japanese-aircraft) represented a [pinnacle](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/pinnacle) of [intellectual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectual) achievement. Maya astronomy underlay a complex [calendrical system](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-calendar) involving an accurately determined solar year (18 months of 20 days each, plus a 5-day period considered unlucky by the Maya), a sacred calendar of 260 days (13 cycles of 20 named days), and a variety of longer cycles culminating in the Long Count, a continuous marking of time, based on a zero date in 3113 bce. Maya astronomers compiled precise tables of positions for the [Moon](https://www.britannica.com/science/moon-natural-satellite) and [Venus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venus-planet) and were able to accurately predict [solar eclipses](https://www.britannica.com/science/eclipse/Solar-eclipse-phenomena#ref11199).

On the basis of these discoveries, scholars in the mid-20th century mistakenly thought that Maya society was composed of a priestly class of peaceful stargazers and calendar keepers supported by a devout peasantry. The Maya were thought to be utterly absorbed in their [religious](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) and cultural pursuits, in favorable contrast to the more warlike and sanguinary [Indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Indigenous) empires of central Mexico. But the progressive decipherment of nearly all of the [Maya hieroglyphic writing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-hieroglyphic-writing) has provided a truer if less-elevating picture of Maya society and culture. Many of the hieroglyphs depict the histories of the Maya dynastic rulers, who waged war on rival Maya cities and took their [aristocrats](https://www.britannica.com/topic/aristocracy) captive. Those captives were then tortured, mutilated, and [sacrificed](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-sacrifice) to the gods. Indeed, [torture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/torture) and [human sacrifice](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-sacrifice) were fundamental religious rituals of Maya society; they were thought to guarantee [fertility](https://www.britannica.com/science/fertility), demonstrate piety, and propitiate the gods, and, if such practices were neglected, cosmic disorder and [chaos](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/chaos) were thought to result. The drawing of human blood was thought to nourish the gods and was thus necessary for achieving contact with them; hence, the Maya rulers, as the intermediaries between the Maya people and the gods, had to undergo ritual bloodletting and self-torture.

The present-day Maya peoples can be divided on linguistic and geographic grounds into the following groups: the [Yucatec Maya](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yucatec-Maya), inhabiting Mexico’s Yucatán Peninsula and extending into northern Belize and northeastern Guatemala; the [Lacandón](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lacandon), very few in number, occupying a territory in southern Mexico between the [Usumacinta River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Usumacinta-River) and the Guatemalan border, with small numbers in Guatemala and Belize; the [K’ichean](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Kiche-language)-speaking peoples of the eastern and central highlands of Guatemala (Q’eqchi’, Poqomchi’, Poqomam, Uspanteko, [K’iche’](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Kiche), [Kaqchikel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Kaqchikel), [Tz’utujil](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tzutujil), Sakapulteko \[Sacapultec\], and Sipacapa \[Sipacapeño\]); the Mamean peoples of the western Guatemalan highlands (Mam, Teco \[Tektiteko\], Awakateko, and Ixil); the Q’anjobalan peoples of [Huehuetenango](https://www.britannica.com/place/Huehuetenango) and [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) parts of Mexico (Motocintlec \[Mocho’\], Tuzantec, Jakalteko, Akateko, [Tojolabal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tojolabal), and Chuj); the [Tzotzil](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tzotzil) and [Tzeltal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tzeltal) peoples of [Chiapas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chiapas) in southern Mexico; the [Cholan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chol) peoples, including the [Chontal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chontal) and Chol speakers in northern Chiapas and [Tabasco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tabasco-state-Mexico) and the linguistically related [Chortí](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chorti) of the extreme eastern part of Guatemala; and the [Huastec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Huastec) of northern [Veracruz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Veracruz-state-Mexico) and adjoining [San Luís Potosí](https://www.britannica.com/place/San-Luis-Potosi-state-Mexico) in east-central Mexico. The chief division in Maya cultures is between highland and lowland cultures. Yucatec, Lacandón, and Chontal-Chol are lowland groups. The Huastec, a linguistically and geographically separated group living in Veracruz and San Luis Potosí, who never were Maya culturally, and the other Maya peoples live in highlands across Guatemala.

Contemporary Maya are basically agricultural, raising crops of corn, beans, and squash. They live in [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) organized around central villages, which may be permanently occupied but more commonly are [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) centers with public buildings and houses that generally stand vacant; the people of the community live on farm homesteads except during fiestas and markets. Dress is largely traditional, particularly for women; men are more likely to wear modern ready-made clothing. Domestic [spinning](https://www.britannica.com/technology/spinning-yarn-manufacturing) and [weaving](https://www.britannica.com/technology/weaving), once common, are becoming rare, and most clothing is made of factory-woven cloth. Cultivation is with the [hoe](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hoe) and, where the soil is tough, the digging stick. The [Yucatec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yucatec-Maya) usually keep pigs and chickens and, rarely, oxen that are used for farming. Industries are few, and crafts are oriented toward domestic needs. Usually some cash crop or item of local manufacture is produced for sale outside the region in order to provide cash for items not otherwise obtainable.

Most Maya are [nominal](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nominal) [Roman Catholics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism)—though, beginning in the late 20th century, many converted to Evangelical [Protestantism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Protestantism). Their [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity), however, is generally overlaid upon the Indigenous religion. Its [cosmology](https://www.britannica.com/science/cosmology-astronomy) is typically Maya, and Christian figures are commonly identified with Maya deities. Public religion is basically Christian, with [masses](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mass-Christian-religious-service) and saint’s day celebrations. The Indigenous pre-Columbian religion is observed in domestic rites.

**Mesoamerican civilization**, the complex of [indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/indigenous) [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) that developed in parts of [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico) and [Central America](https://www.britannica.com/place/Central-America) prior to Spanish exploration and conquest in the 16th century. In the organization of its kingdoms and empires, the sophistication of its monuments and cities, and the extent and refinement of its [intellectual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectual) accomplishments, the Mesoamerican civilization, along with the comparable [Andean civilization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Andean-peoples) farther south, [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) a New World counterpart to those of [ancient Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt), [Mesopotamia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mesopotamia-historical-region-Asia), and [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China).

A brief treatment of Mesoamerican civilization follows. For full treatment, _see_ [pre-Columbian civilizations](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pre-Columbian-civilizations).

Archaeologists have dated human presence in Mesoamerica to possibly as early as 21,000 bce (though the dating of the Valsequillo finds on which that early date is based remains controversial). By 11,000 bce, [hunting-and-gathering peoples](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hunter-gatherer) occupied most of the New World south of the glacial ice cap covering northern [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America). The cooler climate of this period as compared with that of the present day supported a [grassland](https://www.britannica.com/science/grassland) vegetation, especially in the highland valleys, that was ideal for large herds of [grazing](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/grazing) animals. The shift toward sedentary [agriculture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agriculture) apparently began after about 7000 bce, when a dramatic [global warming](https://www.britannica.com/science/global-warming) caused the [glaciers](https://www.britannica.com/science/glacier) to retreat and tropical forests to overtake the Mesoamerican grasslands.

The gradual domestication of successful food plants—most notably a mutant [corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corn-plant) (maize) with husks, dating to c. 5300 bce—over succeeding millennia gave rise to more or less permanent village farming life by about 1500 bce. In addition to corn, crops included [beans](https://www.britannica.com/plant/bean), [squashes](https://www.britannica.com/plant/squash), [chili peppers](https://www.britannica.com/plant/chili-pepper), and [cotton](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cotton-fibre-and-plant). As agricultural productivity improved, the rudiments of civilization emerged during the period designated by archaeologists as the [Early Formative](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Early-Formative-period) (1500–900 bce). [Pottery](https://www.britannica.com/art/pottery), which had appeared in some areas of the region as early as 2300 bce, perhaps introduced from Andean cultures to the south, took on varied and [sophisticated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/sophisticated) forms. The idea of the temple-pyramid seems to have taken root during this period.

Corn cultivation in one area—the humid and fertile lowlands of southern [Veracruz](https://www.britannica.com/place/Veracruz-state-Mexico) and [Tabasco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tabasco-state-Mexico), in Mexico—was sufficiently productive to permit a major diversion of human energy into other activities, such as the arts and commerce. Struggles for control of this rich but limited farmland resulted in a dominant landowning class that shaped the first great Mesoamerican civilization, the [Olmec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Olmec).

[![Olmec “colossal head”](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/102292-050-23468198/head-La-Venta-Park-Museum-Villahermosa-Mexico-Tabasco.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/102292-050-23468198/head-La-Venta-Park-Museum-Villahermosa-Mexico-Tabasco.jpg)

[Olmec “colossal head”](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/102292-050-23468198/head-La-Venta-Park-Museum-Villahermosa-Mexico-Tabasco.jpg) Olmec “colossal head” at La Venta Park-Museum in Villahermosa, Tabasco, Mexico.(more)

[San Lorenzo](https://www.britannica.com/place/San-Lorenzo-ancient-city-Mexico), the oldest known Olmec centre, dates to about 1150 bce, a time when the rest of Mesoamerica was at best on a [Neolithic](https://www.britannica.com/event/Neolithic) level. The site is most noted for its extraordinary stone monuments, especially the “colossal heads” measuring up to 9 feet (nearly 3 metres) in height and possibly representing players in a [ritual](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/ritual) ball game ( _see_ [tlachtli](https://www.britannica.com/sports/tlachtli)).

The period known as the [Middle Formative](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Middle-Formative-period) (900–300 bce), during which the [La Venta](https://www.britannica.com/place/La-Venta) urban complex rose and flourished, was one of increased cultural regionalism. The [Zapotec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zapotec) people, for example, attained a high level of development at [Monte Albán](https://www.britannica.com/place/Monte-Alban), producing the first writing and written [calendar](https://www.britannica.com/science/calendar) in Mesoamerica. However, at this site, as well as in the Valley of Mexico, the Olmec presence can be widely detected.

In the subsequent [Late Formative](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Late-Formative-period) and Classic periods, lasting until about 700–900 ce, the well-known [Maya](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-people), Zapotec, [Totonac](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Totonac), and [Teotihuacán](https://www.britannica.com/place/Teotihuacan) civilizations developed distinctive variations on their shared Olmec heritage. The Maya, for example, brought [astronomy](https://www.britannica.com/science/astronomy), [mathematics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics), calendar making, and [hieroglyphic writing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-hieroglyphic-writing), as well as monumental architecture, to their highest expression in the New World. At the same time, Teotihuacán, in the Valley of Mexico, became the capital of a political and commercial empire [encompassing](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompassing) much of Mesoamerica.

Teotihuacán power diminished after about 600, and for the next several centuries numerous states vied for supremacy. The [Toltec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Toltec) s of [Tula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tula-ancient-city-Mexico), in central Mexico, prevailed from about 900 to 1200 (the Early Postclassic Period). Following Toltec decline, a further period of unrest in the Late Postclassic Period lasted until 1428, when the [Aztec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Aztec) defeated the rival city of Azcapotzalco and became the dominant force in central Mexico. This last native Mesoamerican empire fell to the Spaniards, led by [Hernán Cortés](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hernan-Cortes), in 1521.

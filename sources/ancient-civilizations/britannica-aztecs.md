**Aztec**, [Nahuatl-speaking](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nahuatl-language) people who in the 15th and early 16th centuries ruled a large [empire](https://www.britannica.com/topic/empire-political-science) in what is now central and southern [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico). The name Aztec is derived from Aztlán (variously translated as “White Land,” “Land of White Herons,” or “Place of Herons”), an [allusion](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/allusion) to their origins, probably in northwestern Mexico. They were also called the Tenochca, from an [eponymous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/eponymous) ancestor, Tenoch, and the Mexica, probably from Metzliapán (“Moon Lake”), the mystical name for [Lake Texcoco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lake-Texcoco). From Tenochca was derived the name of their great city, [Tenochtitlán](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tenochtitlan), founded on an island in Lake Texcoco, in the Valley of Mexico. From Mexica came the name for the [city that superseded the Aztec capital](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico-City) and for the surrounding valley, which was applied later to the whole Mexican nation. The Aztecs referred to themselves as Culhua-Mexica, to link themselves with Colhuacán, an important urban in the Valley of Mexico. _See also_ [pre-Columbian civilizations: Aztec culture to the time of the Spanish conquest](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pre-Columbian-civilizations/Aztec-culture-to-the-time-of-the-Spanish-conquest#ref69395).

## Origins of the Aztec people

The origin of the Aztec people is uncertain, but elements of their own tradition suggest that they were [hunter-gatherers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hunter-gatherer) on the northern Mexican plateau before their appearance in Mesoamerica in perhaps the 12th century ce; Aztlán, however, may be legendary. It is possible that their migration southward was part of a general movement of peoples that followed, or perhaps helped trigger, the collapse of the highly developed [Toltec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Toltec) civilization of central Mexico and its capital, [Tula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tula-ancient-city-Mexico), a spectacular urban center that featured [pyramids](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pyramid-architecture), temples, public buildings, and statuary.

At the beginning of the 12th century, [catastrophe](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/catastrophe) befell Toltec civilization when Tula was attacked and destroyed, as were other important Toltec centers. Groups of hunter-gatherers, including a subdivision of the [Chichimec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chichimec) people under the leadership of Xólotl, took advantage of the situation and traveled from the arid plateau of northern Mexico toward the fertile, heavily settled central zone. Xólotl’s Chichimec joined forces with the remaining Toltecs, resulting in a period of relative peace and development in the Valley of Mexico. During this time the Aztecs, who, according to [legend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legend), had been wandering in search of a new place to settle, established a precarious home near the ruins of Tula. There they improved their approach to [agriculture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agriculture) and acquired other technological knowledge.

However, their stay was temporary. Aztec tradition holds that the god [Huitzilopochtli](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Huitzilopochtli) instructed them to depart again in search of a permanent home, the location of which would be revealed by the appearance of an [eagle](https://www.britannica.com/animal/eagle-bird) perched on a [nopal](https://www.britannica.com/plant/prickly-pear) cactus with a serpent in its beak (an image that is memorialized on [Mexico’s national flag](https://www.britannica.com/topic/flag-of-Mexico)). A long [pilgrimage](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/pilgrimage) ensued that ended in 1325 on a small island in [Lake Texcoco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lake-Texcoco), where, it is said, elder members of the people spotted the eagle, the cactus, and the serpent. There they built a temple and, around it, on islands in Lake Texcoco, the first dwellings of what was to become the powerful city of [Tenochtitlán](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tenochtitlan).

# Establishment of the Aztec empire

Under the ruler Itzcóatl (1428–40), [Tenochtitlán](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tenochtitlan) formed alliances with the neighboring states of [Texcoco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Texcoco) and Tlacopan and became the dominant power in central [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico). Later, by commerce and conquest, Tenochtitlán came to rule an [empire](https://www.britannica.com/topic/empire-political-science) of 400 to 500 small states, [comprising](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprising) by 1519 some 5,000,000 to 6,000,000 people spread over 80,000 square miles (207,200 square km). At its height, Tenochtitlán itself covered more than 5 square miles (13 square km) and had upward of 140,000 inhabitants, making it the most densely populated settlement ever achieved by a [Mesoamerican civilization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mesoamerican-civilization). The empire that the Aztecs would establish was equaled in the New World only by that of the [Incas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Inca) of Peru, and the brilliance of their civilization is comparable to that of other great ancient [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) of America and the Old World.

## The Spanish conquest

The Aztec empire was still expanding when its growth was halted in 1519 by the appearance of [Spanish](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Western-colonialism/Spains-American-empire#ref25870) explorers. [Hernán Cortés](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hernan-Cortes) led a force of some 500 European soldiers into central Mexico, and made a prisoner of the ninth emperor, [Montezuma II](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Montezuma-II)(reigned 1502–20), who died in Spanish custody. Among the reasons for the Spanish conquest of the Aztec empire was that Montezuma, at least initially, suspected that Cortés was a returning god. Cortés was a skillful leader, but he also benefited from his force’s possession of superior arms ( [crossbows](https://www.britannica.com/technology/crossbow), [muskets](https://www.britannica.com/technology/musket), steel swords, and body armor), as well as [horses](https://www.britannica.com/animal/horse) and [dogs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dog) that were trained for battle. Deadly European [diseases](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-disease) against which the Aztecs had no immunity also took their toll. (For an account of how such advantages allowed for the European conquest of the Americas more generally, _see_ [_Guns, Germs, and Steel_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Guns-Germs-and-Steel).) Finally, the Spaniards took great advantage of the hatred that peoples who had been conquered by the Aztecs held for their imperial overlords. Thousands of [Indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Indigenous) warriors joined the Spanish invasion, which likely would not have succeeded without their participation.

Montezuma’s successors, [Cuitláhuac](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cuitlahuac) and [Cuauhtémoc](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cuauhtemoc), were unable to stave off Cortés and his forces. After a brutal two-year campaign, by August 13, 1521, the Spanish had taken control of Tenochtitlán. With its capture, the Aztec empire came to an end.

## Aztec agriculture

The basis of Aztec success in creating a great state and ultimately an empire was their remarkable system of agriculture, the high productivity of which made for a rich and populous state. Aztec agriculture featured intensive cultivation of all available land, as well as [elaborate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/elaborate) systems of irrigation and reclamation of [swampland](https://www.britannica.com/science/swamp) through the use of raised fields known as [_chinampas_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/chinampa) (“floating gardens”). Rich soil from the bottom of a lake was piled up to form ridges between rows of ditches or canals. As a result of the Valley of Mexico’s mild climate and ample water for [irrigation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/irrigation), the chinampas yielded multiple harvests annually. A system of lakes (Texcoco, Chalco, [Xochimilco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Xochimilco), Xaltoca, and Zumpango) that were connected naturally and by means of artificial canals contributed to the strategic importance of the Valley of Mexico by providing extensive water transportation that furthered the early economic and political unification of the valley.

## Aztec sociopolitical organization

The Aztec empire also was [characterized](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characterized) by a complex sociopolitical organization, the nature of which continues to be debated by [anthropologists](https://www.britannica.com/science/anthropology). Some academics point to the division of the [tribe](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tribe-anthropology) into _calpulli_(“big houses”), pseudo [family](https://www.britannica.com/topic/family-kinship) units established in Tenochtitlán, as evidence of an egalitarian organization; others emphasize that the proof of social stratification is undeniable. Because of the existence of an Aztec hereditary [nobility](https://www.britannica.com/topic/aristocracy), it has been argued that Aztec society was “feudal”; however, the relation of these noble groups to the Aztec kings, to society in general, and to land ownership was distinct from Old World [feudalism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/feudalism), partly because the Aztec monarch’s rule was more absolute. Records from Montezuma II’s reign indicate that the empire was organized into provinces and that tribute was paid according to the production of each region. A gigantic political, military, and religious [bureaucracy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bureaucracy) was built up, with governors, [tax](https://www.britannica.com/money/taxation) collectors, courts of [justice](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/justice), military garrisons, mail and messenger services, and other civil offices.

## Aztec religion

[![Aztec earth goddess](https://cdn.britannica.com/00/155700-050-3F545633/Coatlicue-stone-sculpture-National-Museum-of-Anthropology.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/00/155700-050-3F545633/Coatlicue-stone-sculpture-National-Museum-of-Anthropology.jpg)

[Aztec earth goddess](https://cdn.britannica.com/00/155700-050-3F545633/Coatlicue-stone-sculpture-National-Museum-of-Anthropology.jpg) Coatlicue, stone sculpture; in the National Museum of Anthropology, Mexico City.(more)

[Aztec religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Aztec-religion) was syncretistic, absorbing elements from many other Mesoamerican cultures. At base, it shared many of the cosmological beliefs of earlier peoples, notably the [Maya](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maya-people), such as that the present earth was the last in a series of creations and that it occupied a position between systems of 13 heavens and 9 underworlds. Prominent in the Aztec pantheon were [Huitzilopochtli](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Huitzilopochtli), god of war; [Tonatiuh](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tonatiuh), god of the sun; [Tlaloc](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tlaloc), god of rain; and [Quetzalcóatl](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quetzalcoatl), the Feathered Serpent, who was part deity and part [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) hero. [Human sacrifice](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-sacrifice), particularly by offering a victim’s [heart](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart) to Tonatiuh, was commonly practiced, as was bloodletting. Moreover, in order to guarantee human existence, the Aztecs, as “people of the sun,” had to nourish Huitzilopochtli with human blood. For them, ideologically at least, war was therefore a religious obligation that provided prisoners who could be sacrificed to the [sun god](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sun-worship). Of course, there was a more [mundane](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/mundane) purpose as well, and it would be a serious mistake to think of Aztec warfare as functioning primarily in the religious sphere. Still, as their power grew, the Aztecs ritually killed prisoners from all parts of what is now Mexico in Tenochtitlán.

Closely entwined with Aztec religion was the calendar, on which the elaborate round of [rituals](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ritual) and ceremonies were performed by a large professional [priesthood](https://www.britannica.com/topic/priesthood) (each temple and god had its attendant priestly order). Many of these ceremonies were public, in that the populace played the role of spectators. Elements in all the ceremonies were very similar and included ritual ablutions to prepare the priests for the contact; offerings and sacrifices to gain the gods’ favor; and theatrical dramas of [myths](https://www.britannica.com/topic/myth) by masked performers in the form of dances, songs, and processionals. The [Aztec calendar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Aztec-calendar) was the one common to much of Mesoamerica, and it [comprised](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprised) a solar year of 365 days and a sacred year of 260 days; the two yearly cycles running in parallel produced a larger cycle of 52 years.

## Nahuatl: the Aztec language

The language of the Aztecs was [Nahuatl](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nahuatl-language) (also called Aztec), part of the [Uto-Aztecan linguistic family](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Uto-Aztecan-languages) that, at the time of the early explorations of America by Europeans, was influencing languages as far north as the [Yellowstone River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yellowstone-River) and as far south as [Panama](https://www.britannica.com/place/Panama). The most important of the Uto-Aztecan languages, Nahuatl was the language of both the Aztec and [Toltec](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Toltec) civilizations. Once the Aztecs achieved political ascendancy, Nahuatl became the [lingua franca](https://www.britannica.com/topic/lingua-franca) of an area almost as large as present-day Mexico.

A large body of literature in Nahuatl that was produced by the Aztecs survives from the 16th century. It was recorded in an [orthography](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/orthography) that was introduced by Spanish priests and based on that of [Spanish](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Spanish-language). Classical (16th-century) Nahuatl employed a set of 15 consonants and four long and short vowels and was notable for its use of a _tl_ sound produced as a single consonant and for the use of the [glottal stop](https://www.britannica.com/topic/glottal-stop).

**Phoenician**, person who inhabited one of the city-states of ancient [Phoenicia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Phoenicia), such as [Byblos](https://www.britannica.com/place/Byblos), [Sidon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sidon), [Tyre](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tyre), or [Beirut](https://www.britannica.com/place/Beirut), or one of their colonies. Located along eastern [Mediterranean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea) trade routes, the Phoenician city-states produced notable merchants, traders, and colonizers. By the 2nd millennium bce they had settled in the [Levant](https://www.britannica.com/place/Levant), [North Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Africa), [Anatolia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Anatolia), and [Cyprus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cyprus). They traded wood, cloth, dyes, embroideries, wine, and decorative objects; ivory and wood carving became their specialties, and the work of Phoenician goldsmiths and metalsmiths was well known. Their [alphabet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Phoenician-alphabet) became the basis of the [Greek alphabet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-alphabet).

Top Questions


### Is ancient Greece a country?

No, ancient Greece was a civilization. The Greeks had cultural traits, a [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-religion), and a [language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-language) in common, though they spoke many dialects. The basic political unit was the [city-state](https://www.britannica.com/topic/city-state). Conflict between city-states was common, but they were capable of banding together against a common enemy, as they did during the Persian Wars (492–449 BCE). Powerful city-states such as [Athens](https://www.britannica.com/place/Athens) and [Sparta](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sparta) exerted influence beyond their borders but never controlled the entire Greek-speaking world.

### Where was ancient Greece located?

Ancient Greek civilization was concentrated in what is today [Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece) and along the western coast of Turkey. However, ancient Greek colonists established cities all around the [Mediterranean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea) and along the coast of the [Black Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Black-Sea).

### Was ancient Greece a democracy?

Each ancient Greek city-state had its own government. Common forms of government included [tyranny](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tyranny) and [oligarchy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/oligarchy). In 507 BCE, under the leadership of [Cleisthenes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cleisthenes-of-Athens), the citizens of Athens began to develop a system of popular rule that they called [democracy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/democracy), which would last nearly two centuries. In their governing body, the Assembly ( [Ecclesia](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ecclesia-ancient-Greek-assembly)), all adult male citizens, perhaps 10 to 15 percent of the total population, were eligible to vote.

### When did ancient Greece start and end?

Ancient Greek civilization flourished from the period following [Mycenaean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mycenae) civilization, which ended about 1200 BCE, to the death of [Alexander the Great](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alexander-the-Great), in 323 BCE. By that time, Greek cultural influence had spread around the Mediterranean and, through Alexander the Great’s campaign of conquest, as far afield as India.

### Why is ancient Greece important?

The political, philosophical, artistic, and scientific achievements of ancient Greek civilization formed a legacy with unparalleled influence on Western civilization. Greek political ideas have influenced modern forms of government, [Greek pottery](https://www.britannica.com/art/Greek-pottery) and sculpture have inspired artists for millennia, and [Greek epic, lyric, and dramatic poetry](https://www.britannica.com/art/Greek-literature) is still read around the world.

**ancient Greek civilization**, the period following [Mycenaean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mycenae) civilization, which ended about 1200 bce, to the death of [Alexander the Great](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alexander-the-Great), in 323 bce. It was a period of political, philosophical, artistic, and scientific achievements that formed a [legacy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legacy) with unparalleled influence. The larger historical period spanning from the output of [ancient Greek](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Greece) author [Homer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Homer-Greek-poet) in the 8th century bce to the decline of the [Roman Empire](https://www.britannica.com/place/Roman-Empire) in the 5th century ce is known as " [Classical antiquity](https://www.britannica.com/event/Classical-antiquity)," [encompassing](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompassing) Greco-Roman [culture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/culture), playing a major role in the [Mediterranean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea) sphere of influence and in the creation of Western civilization, and shaping areas as [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) as [law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/law), [architecture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/architecture), [art](https://www.britannica.com/art/visual-arts), [language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/language), [poetry](https://www.britannica.com/art/poetry), [rhetoric](https://www.britannica.com/topic/rhetoric), politics, and [philosophy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/philosophy).

## The post-Mycenaean period and Lefkandi

![ancient Greece](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/1033-050-895AB1EE/Ancient-Greece.jpg)

[ancient Greece](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/1033-050-895AB1EE/Ancient-Greece.jpg)(more)

The period between the catastrophic end of the Mycenaean civilization and about 900 bce is often called a [Dark Age](https://www.britannica.com/event/Dark-Ages). It was a time about which Greeks of the Classical age had confused and actually false notions. [Thucydides](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thucydides-Greek-historian), the great ancient historian of the 5th century bce, wrote a sketch of Greek [history](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history) from the [Trojan War](https://www.britannica.com/event/Trojan-War) to his own day, in which he notoriously fails, in the appropriate chapter, to signal any kind of dramatic rupture. (He does, however, speak of [Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece) “settling down gradually” and colonizing [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy/The-people#ref26974), [Sicily](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sicily), and what is now western [Turkey](https://www.britannica.com/place/Turkey). This surely implies that Greece was settling down after something.) Thucydides does indeed display sound knowledge of the series of [migrations](https://www.britannica.com/science/migration-animal) by which Greece was resettled in the post-Mycenaean period. The most famous of these was the “ [Dorian invasion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dorian-invasion),” which the Greeks called, or connected with, the legendary “return of the [descendants](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/descendants) of [Heracles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Heracles).” Although much about that invasion is problematic—it left little or no archaeological trace at the point in time where tradition puts it—the problems are of no concern here. Important for the understanding of the [Archaic](https://www.britannica.com/art/Archaic-period) and Classical periods, however, is the powerful belief in Dorianism as a linguistic and religious concept. Thucydides casually but significantly mentions soldiers speaking the “Doric dialect” in a narrative about ordinary military matters in the year 426. That is a surprisingly abstract way of looking at the subdivisions of the Greeks, because it would have been more natural for a 5th-century Greek to identify soldiers by home cities. Equally important to the understanding of this period is the hostility to [Dorians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dorian), usually on the part of [Ionians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ionian), another linguistic and religious subgroup, whose most-famous city was [Athens](https://www.britannica.com/place/Athens). So extreme was this hostility that Dorians were prohibited from entering Ionian sanctuaries; [extant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/extant) today is a 5th-century example of such a prohibition, an inscription from the island of [Paros](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paros).

Phenomena such as the tension between Dorians and Ionians that have their origins in the Dark Age are a reminder that Greek civilization did not emerge either unannounced or uncontaminated by what had gone before. The Dark Age itself is beyond the scope of this article. One is bound to notice, however, that archaeological finds tend to call into question the whole concept of a Dark Age by showing that certain features of Greek civilization once thought not to antedate about 800 bce can actually be pushed back by as much as two centuries. One example, chosen for its relevance to the emergence of the Greek [city-state](https://www.britannica.com/topic/city-state), or [polis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/polis), will [suffice](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/suffice). In 1981 [archaeology](https://www.britannica.com/science/archaeology) pulled back the curtain on the “darkest” phase of all, the Protogeometric Period ( _c._ 1075–900 bce), which takes its name from the [geometric shapes](https://www.britannica.com/art/Proto-Geometric-style) painted on [pottery](https://www.britannica.com/art/Greek-pottery). A grave, rich by the standards of any period, was uncovered at a site called Lefkandi on [Euboea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Euboea-island-Greece), the island along the eastern flank of [Attica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Attica-ancient-district-Greece) (the territory controlled by Athens). The grave, which dates to about 1000 bce, contains the (probably cremated) remains of a man and a woman. The large [bronze](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bronze-alloy) vessel in which the man’s ashes were deposited came from [Cyprus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cyprus), and the [gold](https://www.britannica.com/science/gold-chemical-element) items buried with the woman are splendid and sophisticated in their workmanship. Remains of [horses](https://www.britannica.com/animal/horse) were found as well; the animals had been buried with their snaffle bits. The grave was within a large collapsed house, whose form anticipates that of the Greek [temples](https://www.britannica.com/topic/temple-building) two centuries later. Previously it had been thought that those temples were one of the first [manifestations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifestations) of the “monumentalizing” associated with the beginnings of the city-state. Thus, that find and those made in a set of nearby cemeteries in the years before 1980 attesting further contacts between [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt) and [Cyprus](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cyprus) between 1000 and 800 bce are important evidence. They show that one corner of one island of Greece, at least, was neither impoverished nor isolated in a period usually thought to have been both. The difficulty is to know just how exceptional Lefkandi was, but in any view it has revised former ideas about what was and what was not possible at the beginning of the 1st millennium bce.

## [Colonization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/colonialism-international-relations) and city-state formation

The term _colonization_, although it may be convenient and widely used, is misleading. When applied to [Archaic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Archaic) Greece, it should not necessarily be taken to imply the state-sponsored sending out of definite numbers of settlers, as the later Roman origin of the word implies. For one thing, it will be seen that state formation may itself be a product of the colonizing movement.

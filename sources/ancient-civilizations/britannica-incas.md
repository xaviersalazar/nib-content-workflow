### What was the extent of the Inca empire at the time of the Spanish conquest?

At the time of the Spanish conquest in 1532, the Inca empire extended along the Pacific coast and Andean highlands, from the northern border of modern [Ecuador](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ecuador) to the Maule River in central [Chile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chile).

### What role did religion play in Inca society?

[Inca religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Inca-religion) was a highly organized state religion, combining [animism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/animism), fetishism, and the worship of nature gods. It included elaborate forms of [divination](https://www.britannica.com/topic/divination) and [sacrifices](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacrifice-religion), with the sun god Inti being the head of the pantheon.

### How did the Inca empire facilitate communication and transportation?

The Inca built a vast network of [roads](https://www.britannica.com/technology/road), with north-south routes along the coast and the Andes, that also included tunnels and [suspension bridges](https://www.britannica.com/technology/suspension-bridge). A relay service carried messages using knotted cords called [quipu](https://www.britannica.com/technology/quipu) at a rate of 150 miles (241 km) a day.

**Inca**, [Indigenous](https://www.britannica.com/topic/South-American-Indian) people who, at the time of the Spanish conquest in 1532, ruled an [empire](https://www.britannica.com/topic/empire-political-science) that extended along the [Pacific](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean) coast and [Andean highlands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Andes-Mountains), from the northern border of modern [Ecuador](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ecuador) to the Maule River in central [Chile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chile). ## People and society

Inca society was highly stratified. The emperor ruled with the aid of an aristocratic [bureaucracy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/bureaucracy), exercising authority with harsh and often repressive controls. Inca technology and architecture were highly developed. Their irrigation systems, palaces, temples, and fortifications can still be seen throughout the Andes. The economy was based on [agriculture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/agriculture), its staples being [corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corn-plant) (maize), white and sweet [potatoes](https://www.britannica.com/plant/potato), [squash](https://www.britannica.com/plant/squash), [tomatoes](https://www.britannica.com/plant/tomato), [peanuts](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Peanuts) (groundnuts), [chili peppers](https://www.britannica.com/plant/chili-pepper), [coca](https://www.britannica.com/plant/coca), [cassava](https://www.britannica.com/plant/cassava), and [cotton](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cotton-fibre-and-plant). They raised [guinea pigs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/guinea-pig), [ducks](https://www.britannica.com/animal/duck), [llamas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/llama), [alpacas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/alpaca), and [dogs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dog). Clothing was made of llama wool and cotton. Houses were of stone or [adobe](https://www.britannica.com/topic/adobe) mud.

The Inca built a vast network of [roads](https://www.britannica.com/technology/road) throughout their empire. It [comprised](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprised) two north-south roads: one running along the coast for about 2,250 miles (3,600 km) and the other inland along the Andes for a comparable distance, with many interconnecting links. Many short rock tunnels and vine-supported [suspension bridges](https://www.britannica.com/technology/suspension-bridge) were constructed. Use of the system was strictly limited to government and military business; a well-organized relay service carried messages in the form of knotted cords called [quipu](https://www.britannica.com/technology/quipu) (Quechua: _khipu_) at a rate of 150 miles (241 km) a day. The network greatly [facilitated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/facilitated) the Spanish conquest of the Inca empire.

The Inca religion combined features of [animism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/animism), fetishism, and the worship of nature gods. The pantheon was headed by [Inti](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Inti-Inca-Sun-god), the [sun god](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sun-worship), and also included [Viracocha](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Viracocha), a creator god and [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) hero, and [Apu Illapu](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Illapu), the rain god. Under the empire the Inca religion was a highly organized state religion, but even though worship of the sun god and the rendering of service were required of subject peoples, their native religions were tolerated. Inca rituals included elaborate forms of [divination](https://www.britannica.com/topic/divination) and the [sacrifice](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacrifice-religion) of humans and animals. These religious institutions were destroyed by the Spanish conquerors’ campaign against [idolatry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/idolatry).

The descendants of the Inca are today’s [Quechua](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quechua)-speaking peoples of the Andes, who make up a large share of [Peru’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru) population and also live in [Bolivia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bolivia), [Ecuador](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ecuador), [Colombia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Colombia), [Argentina](https://www.britannica.com/place/Argentina), and [Chile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chile). The Quechua people have carried their rich traditions into the 21st century, blending ancestral customs with modern economic life. Many rural Quechua [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) continue to farm, herd [llamas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/llama) or [alpacas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/alpaca), weave textiles, and organize festivals rooted in [Inca religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Inca-religion). [Tourism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tourism) has also become an economic lifeline in some rural areas, providing income through homestays, handicrafts, and guiding, though this brings both opportunities and pressures to adapt traditions.

Customs around religion and spirituality remain deeply interwoven with daily life. Quechua belief systems honor mountain spirits (Apus) and Pachamama (Mother Earth), and ritual offerings are important. Christian (often [Roman Catholic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism), increasingly [Protestant](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Protestantism), or evangelical in some regions) ceremonies are generally [syncretic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religious-syncretism)—blending with [Indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Indigenous) cosmology rather than replacing it. Traditional festivals, textile symbolism, music, and dance also play key roles in [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) cohesion and identity. Many communities work to keep their cultural practices alive, teaching songs, dances, and rituals to younger generations and continuing to produce textiles rich with ancestral symbolism. Local celebrations and language-revitalization efforts help ensure that spiritual customs and artistic traditions remain a vibrant part of contemporary Andean life.

# History

[![Cuzco, Peru: Sacsahuamán](https://cdn.britannica.com/30/180730-050-25042FA2/Battlements-fortress-Inca-Sacsahuaman-Peru-Cuzco.jpg?w=300)\\

Like other Andean [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures), the Inca left no written records. Inca origins and early [history](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history) are largely shrouded in [legends](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legends) that may be more mythical than factual. Their later history, particularly from the reign of Pachacuti Inca Yupanqui (Pachakuti ’Inka Yupanki) onward, is largely based on fact, even though it presents what the Inca wanted people to know. Whether these historical traditions are true, in the sense that they accurately relate what happened, is not so important as is the fact that the Inca used them to justify their various imperial conquests.

The present knowledge of Inca society has been derived from a combination of archaeological studies, [oral tradition](https://www.britannica.com/topic/oral-tradition) preserved by official “memorizers,” and the written accounts sent to Spain by early Spanish observers. Although the Inca kept detailed accounts of their dynastic history, knotted onto the [quipu](https://www.britannica.com/technology/quipu) records kept by professional accountants, nonquantitative [data](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/data) remains to be deciphered. The major local ethnic lords also kept records. Some of the Spaniards transcribed events that were told to them, and systematic efforts were made by the Spanish in 1549 and in the 1570s to investigate the Andean past. These accounts became the main sources of the fragmentary information available to modern researchers.

The Inca established their capital at [Cuzco](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cuzco) (Peru) in the 12th century. They began their conquests in the early 15th century and within 100 years had gained control of an Andean population of about 12 million people. According to their tradition, the Inca originated in the village of [Paqari-tampu](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paqari-tampu), about 15 miles (24 km) south of Cuzco. The founder of the Inca [dynasty](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynasty), [Manco Capac](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Manco-Capac) (Manqo Qhapaq), led the tribe to settle in Cuzco, which remained thereafter their capital. Until the reign of the fourth emperor, [Mayta Capac](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mayta-Capac) (Mayta Qhapaq), in the 14th century, there was little to distinguish the Inca from the many other tribes inhabiting small domains throughout the [Andes](https://www.britannica.com/place/Andes-Mountains). Under Mayta Capac the Inca began to expand, attacking and looting the villages of neighboring peoples and probably assessing some sort of tribute. Under [Capac Yupanqui](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Capac-Yupanqui-emperor-of-Incas) (Qhapaq Yupanki), the next emperor, the Inca first extended their influence beyond the Cuzco valley, and under [Viracocha Inca](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Viracocha-Inca) (Wiraqocha ’Inka), the eighth, they began a program of permanent conquest by establishing garrisons among the settlements of the peoples whom they had conquered.

[![Pachacuti Inca Yupanqui](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/200095-050-73658638/Inca-emperor-Pachacuti-Inca-Yupanqui.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/200095-050-73658638/Inca-emperor-Pachacuti-Inca-Yupanqui.jpg)

[Pachacuti Inca Yupanqui](https://cdn.britannica.com/95/200095-050-73658638/Inca-emperor-Pachacuti-Inca-Yupanqui.jpg) Pachacuti Inca Yupanqui (Pachakuti 'Inka Yupanki), 10th Inca emperor, oil on canvas, probably mid-18th century, based on a 1615 Spanish engraving; in the Brooklyn Museum, New York.(more)

The earliest date that can be confidently assigned to Inca dynastic history is 1438, when [Pachacuti Inca Yupanqui](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pachacuti-Inca-Yupanqui) (Pachakuti ’Inka Yupanki), a son of Viracocha Inca, usurped the throne from his brother Inca Urcon (’Inka ’Urqon). Under Pachacuti Inca Yupanqui (1438–71) the Inca conquered territory south to the [Titicaca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lake-Titicaca) Basin and north to present-day [Quito](https://www.britannica.com/place/Quito), making subject peoples of the powerful Chanca, the [Quechua](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quechua), and the [Chimú](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chimu). A policy of forced resettlement of large [contingents](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contingents) from each conquered people helped ensure political stability by distributing ethnic groups throughout the [empire](https://www.britannica.com/topic/empire-political-science) and thus making the organization of revolt very difficult. Local governors were responsible for exacting the labor tax on which the empire was based; the tax could be paid by service in the army, on [public works](https://www.britannica.com/technology/public-utility), or in agricultural work.

Under [Topa Inca Yupanqui](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Topa-Inca-Yupanqui) (Thupa ’Inka Yupanki; 1471–93) the empire reached its southernmost extent in central [Chile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chile), and the last vestiges of resistance on the southern Peruvian coast were eliminated. His death was followed by a struggle for succession, from which [Huayna Capac](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Huayna-Capac) (Wayna Qhapaq; 1493–1525) emerged successful. Huayna Capac pushed the northern boundary of the empire to the Ancasmayo River before dying in an [epidemic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/epidemic) that may have been brought by a tribe from the east that had picked it up from the Spanish at [La Plata](https://www.britannica.com/place/La-Plata). His death set off another struggle for succession, which was still unresolved in 1532, when the Spanish arrived in [Peru](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru).

[![Atahuallpa](https://cdn.britannica.com/35/197035-050-FD1BCB30/Atahuallpa-emperor-oil-canvas-engraving-Inca-Spanish.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/35/197035-050-FD1BCB30/Atahuallpa-emperor-oil-canvas-engraving-Inca-Spanish.jpg)

[Atahuallpa](https://cdn.britannica.com/35/197035-050-FD1BCB30/Atahuallpa-emperor-oil-canvas-engraving-Inca-Spanish.jpg) Atahuallpa ('Ataw Wallpa 'Inka), 14th Inca emperor, oil on canvas, probably mid-18th century, based on a 1615 Spanish engraving; in the Brooklyn Museum, New York.(more)

Upon their arrival, the Spaniards took advantage of the civil war, simultaneously aiding and hindering [Atahuallpa](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Atahuallpa) (’Ataw Wallpa ’Inka; 1532–33) in his effort to consolidate the empire under his control. They ultimately executed him in August 1533, and his successor, Topa Huallpa (Thupa Wallpa; 1533), died soon afterward under mysterious circumstances. The ongoing instability and disintegration of the empire played into the hands of the Spanish as they [consolidated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/consolidated) their control, capturing Cuzco in November 1533 and establishing colonial [Lima](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lima) in 1535. A remnant Inca state lasted until 1572 in a remote mountainous area called Vitcos.

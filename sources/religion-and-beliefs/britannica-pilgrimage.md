- What is a religious pilgrimage?







A religious pilgrimage is a journey undertaken for a spiritual motive, often to a specific place sanctified by association with a divinity or holy personage. This practice is found across all major world religions, including Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism. Pilgrimages can commemorate the origins of a faith, connect sacred time with sacred place, and offer a separation from the everyday world. Motivations vary, from seeking salvation and miraculous cures to fulfilling vows or performing penance. While some journeys are prescribed by sacred texts, like the Hajj in Islam, others are more personal quests for spiritual merit.

- Why do people go on pilgrimages?







People embark on pilgrimages for a variety of religious reasons. These journeys are often undertaken to visit specific places sanctified by association with a divinity or holy figure. For instance, Christians visit the Holy Land to trace biblical events, while Muslims revere Mecca as the birthplace of Muhammad.



Pilgrimages can be motivated by a pious search for salvation, a desire for miraculous cures, the fulfillment of vows, or as penance for wrongdoing. In some traditions, like Islam, pilgrimage (Hajj) is a required pillar of faith. While not always explicitly mandated, sacred texts in various religions recommend visits to holy places. These journeys offer a way to commemorate the origins of one's faith and connect with the divine.

- How do different religions practice pilgrimage?







Pilgrimage, a journey undertaken for religious reasons, is a practice found across diverse faiths, each with unique customs and sacred destinations.



In Islam, the [hajj](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hajj) to Mecca is a mandatory pilgrimage for adult Muslims who are physically and financially able, involving specific rituals like circumambulating the Kaaba and walking between Mount Safa and Mount Marwah.



Buddhists undertake pilgrimages to sites associated with the Buddha's life, such as [Lumbini](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lumbini) (birthplace) and [Bodh Gaya](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bodh-Gaya) (enlightenment site). Many Buddhist countries also feature mountain pilgrimages and extensive temple routes, like the Shikoku pilgrimage in Japan.



Hinduism emphasizes journeys to sacred sites called _tirthas_, often rivers, mountains, or cities linked to deities. Pilgrims bathe in sacred rivers, perform rites for ancestors, and visit temples, with major sites including Varanasi and the four great abodes of the gods.



Jainism's pilgrimage sites are often located at places marking significant events in the lives of Tirthankaras, with prominent destinations like Parasnath Hill and Shatrunjaya Hills.



Christian pilgrimage, exemplified by the [Camino de Santiago](https://www.britannica.com/event/Camino-de-Santiago), historically involved journeys to sites like the tomb of St. James to seek remission of sins. Christians also visit the Holy Land to retrace biblical events.

- What challenges do pilgrims often face during their journey?







Pilgrims undertaking journeys, such as the Amarnath yatra, often encounter significant challenges. These can include security threats, unpredictable weather conditions, and the ecological strain placed on sensitive environments. Historically, some pilgrimages have faced dangers like terrorist attacks and natural disasters, including severe snowstorms and flash floods. These obstacles require careful planning and preparedness from those embarking on such spiritual travels.

- How can a pilgrimage affect a person's beliefs or life?







A pilgrimage, a journey undertaken for religious reasons, can profoundly affect a person's beliefs and life by offering a unique connection to their faith. These journeys often involve separating from the everyday world to visit a place sanctified by its association with a divine figure or holy event. This physical act can reinforce spiritual beliefs, provide a sense of commemoration for the origins of one's faith, and offer opportunities for reflection and personal transformation. **pilgrimage**, a journey undertaken for a [religious](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religious-experience) motive. Although some pilgrims have wandered continuously with no fixed destination, pilgrims more commonly seek a specific place that has been sanctified by association with a divinity or other holy personage. The institution of pilgrimage is evident in all world [religions](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) and was also important in the religions of ancient [Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Greece) and [Rome](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Rome).

## General considerations

[![The Kaaba in Mecca during the hajj](https://cdn.britannica.com/25/231525-050-3EE7C7C9/Mecca-Saudi-Arabia-Muslim-pilgrims.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/25/231525-050-3EE7C7C9/Mecca-Saudi-Arabia-Muslim-pilgrims.jpg)

[The Kaaba in Mecca during the hajj](https://cdn.britannica.com/25/231525-050-3EE7C7C9/Mecca-Saudi-Arabia-Muslim-pilgrims.jpg) Pilgrims surrounding the Kaaba (Kaʿbah) during the hajj, Mecca, Saudi Arabia.(more)

Great centers of pilgrimage attract visitors from widely dispersed cultural backgrounds and geographic locations, often enabling them to [commemorate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/commemorate) the origins of their particular [faith](https://www.britannica.com/topic/faith). Since the 2nd or 3rd century ce, Christians have traced the events of the [Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible), including the life of [Jesus Christ](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus) himself, through visits to the Holy Land. [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mecca) is revered by Muslims as the dwelling place of [Adam](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adam-and-Eve-biblical-literary-figures) after his expulsion from [paradise](https://www.britannica.com/topic/paradise-religion) and as the birthplace of [Muhammad](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Muhammad) (570–632), the prophet of [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam). According to [Hindu](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism) tradition, [Varanasi](https://www.britannica.com/place/Varanasi) (Benares) was founded at the dawn of creation and is the earthly home of Lord [Shiva](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shiva).

The Christian [New Testament](https://www.britannica.com/topic/New-Testament) does not specifically enjoin believers to go on pilgrimage, but sacred texts in other religious faiths do. A [hajj](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hajj) (formal pilgrimage) to Mecca is one of the five [Pillars of Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pillars-of-Islam). According to the Bible, immediately after he was given the [Ten Commandments](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ten-Commandments), the Hebrew prophet [Moses](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Moses-Hebrew-prophet) received instructions on [Mount Sinai](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Sinai-mountain-Egypt) that required the Jewish people to appear before the Lord three times a year. The [Mahabharata](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mahabharata), an important Hindu [epic](https://www.britannica.com/art/epic) dating from the 1st millennium bce, recommends visits to many holy places in [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), mentioning shrines in an order corresponding to the Sun’s movement across the sky. The [Buddha](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Buddha-founder-of-Buddhism) himself prescribed certain places of pilgrimage, choosing sites linked with key events in his life.

## Meaning and motivations of pilgrimage

[![The Western Wall in Jerusalem](https://cdn.britannica.com/19/93419-050-6381B963/Western-Wall-remains-Jerusalem-Second-Temple-Dome.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/19/93419-050-6381B963/Western-Wall-remains-Jerusalem-Second-Temple-Dome.jpg)

[The Western Wall in Jerusalem](https://cdn.britannica.com/19/93419-050-6381B963/Western-Wall-remains-Jerusalem-Second-Temple-Dome.jpg) Pilgrims visiting the Western Wall in Jerusalem, the only remains of the Second Temple, overlooked by the Dome of the Rock.(more)

Given its presence in so many different cultural and historical [contexts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contexts), no single meaning can be attributed to the act of pilgrimage. Structural similarities are discernible, however, across [disparate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disparate) traditions of sacred travel. Pilgrimage usually entails some separation (alone or in a group) from the everyday world of home, and pilgrims may mark their new identity by wearing special clothes or abstaining from physical comforts. Frequently, pilgrimages link sacred place with sacred time. The hajj always occurs on the 8th, 9th, and 10th days of the last month of the Muslim year. Historically, [Jews](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judaism) would visit the temple of [Jerusalem](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jerusalem) during three annual festivals, as prescribed in [Deuteronomy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Deuteronomy): [Passover](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Passover) (in remembrance of the beginning of the [Exodus](https://www.britannica.com/event/Exodus-Old-Testament) from bondage in Egypt), [Shavuot](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shavuot) (marking the giving of the Law to Moses), and [Sukkoth](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sukkoth-Judaism) (recalling the temporary shelters used by the Israelites who fled from Egypt). Hindu pilgrimages mark particular phases of the life cycle of the individual and often occur at points in the year identified as [auspicious](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/auspicious) by astrological calculations.

Apart from involving movement across physical and cultural landscapes toward a sacred goal, pilgrimages frequently involve [ritual](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ritual) movements at the site itself. Performing the [Stations of the Cross](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Stations-of-the-Cross), which reenact the events of Jesus’ Passion, is a frequent activity at Roman Catholic shrines. Muslims walk seven times around the [Kaʿbah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Kaaba-shrine-Mecca-Saudi-Arabia), or central cube-shaped shrine, at Mecca. [Buddhists](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism) circumambulate dome-shaped reliquaries called [stupas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/stupa). Among Hindus, perhaps the ideal pilgrimage would involve a journey around the four most sacred sites in India (the _dhama_ s), covering the country in an auspicious clockwise direction.

A further common feature of pilgrimages is the availability of small souvenirs— [relics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/relic), containers of holy water, icons, and so on—that allow the sacredness of a shrine to be transported back to the pilgrim’s home. Finally, pilgrimage sites tend to have a material focus, even though the nature of that focus varies according to the assumptions of the [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion). Muslim and Jewish sites avoid [iconic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/iconic) representations of divinity, but images are important in Catholic and Orthodox Christian sites. Statues of gods are central to Hindu [worship](https://www.britannica.com/topic/worship) and necessary for _[darshan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/darshan)_, the [beneficial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/beneficial) gaze that passes between pilgrim and deity.

[![Ganges River](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/102231-050-21280A4A/pilgrims-Ganges-River.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/102231-050-21280A4A/pilgrims-Ganges-River.jpg)

[Ganges River](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/102231-050-21280A4A/pilgrims-Ganges-River.jpg) Hindu pilgrims bathing in the Ganges River.(more)

Numerous factors determine the location of sacred sites. Shrines memorialize some great miracle or divine appearance from the past but may also appropriate the places that are holy to an older or rival faith. Thus, as [Buddhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism) was established as the dominant religion in [Tibet](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tibet), deities associated with [indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/indigenous) traditions were subordinated to those of the new religion, just as sacred spots such as [Mount Kailas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Kailash) (in the [Kailas Range](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kailas-Range)) were turned into Buddhist pilgrimage sites. Similarly, the missionary efforts of colonial powers in Africa and Latin America led to the creation of modified religious landscapes, often combining pagan and Christian imagery and [myth](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myth), as is evident in the case of [Our Lady of Guadalupe](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Our-Lady-of-Guadalupe-patron-saint-of-Mexico) in Mexico. Pilgrimage centers are frequently situated in striking and remote geographical areas. In [Hinduism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism) ritual bathing often takes place at the [confluences](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/confluences) of rivers, which are imbued with sacred meaning. The Ganges is regarded as the holiest Hindu river because it is believed to issue from the very locks of Shiva’s hair.

A factor that unites pilgrimage locations across different religions is the sense, variously expressed, that a given place can provide privileged access to a divine or [transcendent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/transcendent) sphere. This idea is well expressed in the Hindu concept of the _tirtha_, a Sanskrit term [encompassing](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompassing) the notion of a ford or intersection between two realms. The same word is used by [Jains](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jainism) for any site where a prophet was born or died.

In all religious traditions, [hierarchies](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hierarchies) of sites are evident, as some places are regarded as more sacred than others. For Hindus, Varanasi, one of seven especially holy cities, has the power to bestow _[moksha](https://www.britannica.com/topic/moksha-Indian-religion)_, or freedom from the cycle of reincarnation. For the [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) Catholic, a visit to Jerusalem or Rome would have gained more remission of [sin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sin-religion) (through the granting of [indulgences](https://www.britannica.com/topic/indulgence)) than a journey to a lesser place. In [North Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Africa), [marabout](https://www.britannica.com/topic/marabout) shrines, consisting of the tomb of a holy man, lack the pan-Islamic status of Mecca or [Medina](https://www.britannica.com/place/Medina-Saudi-Arabia) but provide access to sacred figures, living or dead, who mediate God’s grace ( _baraka_) to clients.

Motivations for pilgrimage vary, within as well as between traditions. Sacred travel is frequently linked to a pious search for ultimate [salvation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/salvation-religion) but can also be prompted by more earthly aims, such as gaining miraculous cures, fulfilling vows, or doing penance for wrongdoing. In medieval times the English shrine of Walsingham, associated with the [Virgin Mary](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mary-mother-of-Jesus) and the [Annunciation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Annunciation-Christianity), attracted women seeking solutions to problems of [lactation](https://www.britannica.com/science/lactation) and [infertility](https://www.britannica.com/science/infertility) and may have encouraged self-identification with the Holy Mother. More recently, the [apparition of the Virgin Mary](https://www.britannica.com/event/Our-Lady-of-Fatima) to three peasant children in Portugal in 1917 has made the small village of [Fátima](https://www.britannica.com/place/Fatima) into an international pilgrimage site, one tied to several supposed cures and [miracles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/miracle).

In addition, pilgrimage has frequently been linked to, and sometimes explicitly combined with, travel for overtly [secular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/secular) purposes. From the earliest times, the hajj was an annual fair as well as a religious activity, and it had important commercial functions under the rulers of the [Ottoman dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ottoman-Empire). A Muslim pilgrim might well have financed the journey to Mecca through trade, and over many centuries annual caravans to the city benefited from the relative security available to large groups of travelers. The [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) of ancient Greece and Rome did not establish boundaries between religious ritual and the secular world in ways evident in the modern West, and festivals easily combined celebration of the gods with urban commercial activities. The [Crusades](https://www.britannica.com/event/Crusades) initiated by Pope [Urban II](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Urban-II) in 1095 allied elements of pilgrimage with [chivalry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/chivalry) and the gaining of booty. Before the final armed assault on Muslim-controlled Jerusalem in July 1099, the crusaders fasted and walked barefoot around the city. Priests carried relics and preached to the military pilgrims on the [Mount of Olives](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-of-Olives). The [Camino de Santiago](https://www.britannica.com/event/Camino-de-Santiago), a series of medieval [pilgrimage](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/pilgrimage) routes to the purported tomb of [St. James the Greater](https://www.britannica.com/biography/St-James-son-of-Zebedee) in [Santiago de Compostela](https://www.britannica.com/place/Santiago-de-Compostela), Spain, has seen in the 21st century a significant increase in pilgrims who undertake the journey for nonreligious reasons.

# Pilgrimage and the world

[![Bodh Gaya, India](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/58641-050-6B238BA8/Prayer-flags-Bodh-Gaya-pilgrim-bodhi-tree.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/58641-050-6B238BA8/Prayer-flags-Bodh-Gaya-pilgrim-bodhi-tree.jpg)

[Bodh Gaya, India](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/58641-050-6B238BA8/Prayer-flags-Bodh-Gaya-pilgrim-bodhi-tree.jpg) Prayer flags and pilgrim under the bodhi tree at Bodh Gaya, India, the site of the Buddha's Enlightenment.(more)

Just as the purposes and motives of pilgrimage vary, so do the relationships between pilgrims and political rulers. Many institutions connected with sacred travel have been controlled or sponsored by such authorities, who gain further legitimacy by association with [transcendent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/transcendent) realms. The pilgrimage of [St. Helena](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Helena) (c. 248–c. 328), mother of the Roman emperor [Constantine](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Constantine-I-Roman-emperor), to the Holy Land was probably an [adaptation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adaptation) of the traditional imperial progress through the provinces. Helena’s personal journey, during which she discovered the [True Cross](https://www.britannica.com/topic/True-Cross), was also a public event and coincided with the emperor’s extensive building programs on sacred sites. Many centuries earlier, in the 3rd century bce, the Mauryan emperor [Ashoka](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ashoka), who had converted to Buddhism, visited many of the important sites of the [Buddha’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Buddha-founder-of-Buddhism) life. In common with Helena, he combined piety with [astute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/astute) imperial patronage.

Pilgrimages have also prompted behaviors that have proved deeply threatening to political and religious authorities. As a practice that involves the search for divine favor, temporary release from everyday life, and the potential for mass movement of populations across the landscape, pilgrimage contains volatile cultural and social elements. In [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) England fears were raised over false and disorderly pilgrims as well as over the possibility that apparently pious travelers were abandoning their families for selfish reasons; after periods of [plague](https://www.britannica.com/science/plague), restrictions on movement were extensive. Pilgrimage sites often [juxtapose](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/juxtapose) orthodox and more popular perspectives on [worship](https://www.britannica.com/topic/worship), resulting in conflicts between elites and the masses.

Many important sites are sacred to more than one denomination or even [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion), with competition and conflict arising as much as cooperation. The [Dome of the Rock](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dome-of-the-Rock) in [Jerusalem](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jerusalem) was constructed by Muslims on the site of the Jewish [Temple](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Temple-of-Jerusalem) in the 7th century. It could thus be interpreted either as expressing [Islam’s](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam) [continuities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/continuities) with its Jewish roots or as physically effacing [Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judaism) from a sacred center. Also in Jerusalem is the [Church of the Holy Sepulchre](https://www.britannica.com/place/Holy-Sepulchre), enclosing the traditional sites of [Christ’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus) [crucifixion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crucifixion-capital-punishment) and burial. Even today, different Christian denominations look after separate parts of the church and maintain different areas of worship.

Pilgrimage has had its opponents in all of the world religions. Pilgrims have been accused of placing too much [faith](https://www.britannica.com/topic/faith) in images and particular places (as opposed to trusting in the omnipresence of God), of becoming distracted by the temptations of travel, and of seeking purely material blessings rather than spiritual enlightenment. Within Islam, some mystical [Sufi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sufism) traditions have emphasized the importance of inner inspiration over that of journeying to [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mecca) itself. In the 16th century, the Protestant reformers [Martin Luther](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Martin-Luther) and [John Calvin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Calvin) argued that papal granting of [indulgences](https://www.britannica.com/topic/indulgence) and credulous [belief](https://www.britannica.com/topic/belief) in miracles reflected the corruption of the [Roman Catholic Church](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism). The emergence of [Protestantism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Protestantism) in Europe saw attacks on many pilgrimage shrines and images. Guru [Nanak](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Guru-Nanak) (1469–1539), the founder of [Sikhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sikhism), maintained somewhat ambivalent attitudes toward pilgrimage. He traveled extensively and visited many sacred sites during his life but remained opposed to the use of images in worship as well as to empty formalism in religion.

Yet the power of pilgrimage as a [metaphor](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/metaphor) may be retained even in [contexts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contexts) apparently unfavorable to its practice. In _[The Pilgrim’s Progress](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Pilgrims-Progress)_ (1678), the Puritan [John Bunyan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Bunyan) (1628–88) translated the physical act of journeying and the search for [salvation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/salvation-religion) into an allegorical struggle with the self. His central character, Christian, moves through many travails from the City of Destruction toward the Celestial City.

## Modern developments

[![Lourdes](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/262113-050-1E87A6A4/Catholic-pilgrims-touch-the-rock-of-the-Grotto-of-Massabielle.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/262113-050-1E87A6A4/Catholic-pilgrims-touch-the-rock-of-the-Grotto-of-Massabielle.jpg)

[Lourdes](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/262113-050-1E87A6A4/Catholic-pilgrims-touch-the-rock-of-the-Grotto-of-Massabielle.jpg) Catholic pilgrims touching the rock of the Massabielle Grotto in Lourdes, France. The grotto was the site of Marian apparitions received by Bernadette Soubirous in 1858.(more)

In the present day, pilgrimage has maintained and even increased its appeal around the world. Travel is easier, cheaper, and safer than ever before. Growing levels of [literacy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/literacy) and access to communications technology have increased knowledge of and curiosity about distant places. The [tourism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tourism) industry has continued to grow since the 19th century and represents an activity that is often combined with, or even shades into, pious journeying. In some cases, pilgrimage has assumed the role of defending religious [conviction](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conviction) against the incursions of [atheism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/atheism). Thus, the many sites dedicated to the Virgin Mary that have emerged in Europe over the past two centuries bear witness to a continued faith in miracles as opposed to the seemingly cold rationality of the natural sciences. Although the church initially treated it with [skepticism](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/skepticism), the famous site of [Lourdes](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lourdes) in southern France, where [Bernadette of Lourdes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Bernadette-of-Lourdes) received [apparitions of the Virgin Mary](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Marian-apparition) in 1858, became a powerful example of supernatural intervention for French [ecclesiastical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ecclesiastical) authorities to cite as they defended the Roman Catholic faith against proponents of [secularism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/secularism). In the early 21st century, Lourdes attracted millions of visitors each year.

As populations continue to move around the globe, pilgrimage to newly founded sites has provided a way for migrant [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) to retain contact with their homelands. For instance, the proliferation of new Hindu pilgrimage sites in [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) and Europe demonstrates the flexibility of pilgrimage as an institution. [Pittsburgh](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pittsburgh), [Toronto](https://www.britannica.com/place/Toronto), [Houston](https://www.britannica.com/place/Houston), and other major cities contain important temples that indicate the enduring ability of an ancient religious landscape to be translated into new social and cultural territories.

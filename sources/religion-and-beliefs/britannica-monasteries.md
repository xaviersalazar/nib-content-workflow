- What is monasticism?







[Monasticism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monasticism/Introduction#ref1) is an institutionalized religious practice where members follow a strict rule exceeding ordinary religious requirements. Monastics, commonly celibate and universally ascetic, separate from society as hermits or join communities with similar intentions.



The term, derived from the Greek _monachos_ ("living alone"), can be misleading as many monastics live in communities. Monasticism implies celibacy, crucial in Abrahamic religions like Christianity, Judaism, and Islam. [Christian monasticism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity/Monasticism#ref67516) emphasizes world-denying asceticism and maximum love for God and neighbor, with monks historically playing a role in preserving learning and providing social services.

- Why do people choose to live a monastic life?







People choose to live a monastic life for various reasons, often linked to theological convictions that spiritual fulfillment is unattainable in society. Monasticism involves separating from society to pursue a radical religious life, either as a [hermit](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monasticism/Introduction#ref38673) or within a community, to seek spiritual growth.



Motivations include overcoming imperfections like sin and ignorance to reveal the true self. This quest for spiritual intensification requires withdrawal from everyday life to control the body and mind through practices differing from normal routines. Monastics commit to poverty, chastity, and obedience, renouncing worldly pleasures to contemplate God and pursue Christian perfection.

- What daily routines or activities are common in monastic communities?







Monastic communities commonly center their daily routines around prayer, work, and study. Monks commit to a life of prayer, participating in communal chanting of liturgical prayers at specific times.



Manual labor is also typical, with monks engaging in domestic tasks, craft work, gardening, or fieldwork to make the monastery self-sufficient. Additionally, scriptural and spiritual study occupies a portion of the day. Monks often copy ancient manuscripts, preserving knowledge while also running internal and external schools.



These activities reflect monastic vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience, with an emphasis on humility and renunciation of worldly pleasures in pursuit of Christian perfection.

- What are the main differences between monks and nuns?







Monks and nuns are both members of monastic orders, but monks are men, while nuns are women. In most monastic traditions, the status of nuns is lower than that of monks. For example, in Jainism, nuns are obliged to defer to monks, even if the nuns have greater learning or spiritual attainment. Monasteries sometimes serve as training centers for religious leaders; however, there is a distinction between training secular clergy and regular clergy. Christian monastic training produces priests as well as monastics.

- How does monasticism vary between different religions, like Christianity and Buddhism?







Monasticism, an institutionalized practice found in religions like [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity/Monasticism#ref67516) and [Buddhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism/Buddhism-in-the-West#ref68690), involves individuals living by a rule that exceeds the laity's or spiritual leaders' expectations, often in celibacy and asceticism.



In Christianity, monasticism emphasizes maximum love for God and neighbor, with monks often ordained as priests. [Basil the Great](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity/Monasticism#ref67516) shaped monastic community life in the Byzantine Church, and [Benedict of Nursia's rule](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monasticism/Cenobitic#ref38691) influenced Western monasticism, emphasizing poverty, chastity, obedience, and stability.



Buddhist monasticism involves a symbiotic relationship between monks and the lay community. Monks adhere to the vinaya, a code of conduct with over 200 rules, and the [sangha](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism/Buddhism-in-the-West#ref68689) has evolved from wandering mendicants to settled communities in permanent monasteries.

- What impact has monasticism had on society and culture throughout history?







Monasticism has significantly shaped society and culture throughout history by preserving and transmitting secular and religious traditions. Monks and monastic orders have played a vital role in the arts, sciences, and education. Monasteries have also been instrumental in preserving books and learning, especially from the 5th to the 8th centuries, and served as centers for producing copies of manuscripts.



Beyond their role in learning, monastic institutions have also been involved in medical, political, and military functions. The relationship between monks and the lay community has been essential in structuring societies and addressing both spiritual and practical concerns. Monasticism's influence extends to art, with the expansion of monasticism in the 10th and 11th centuries contributing to the development of Romanesque art and architecture.

**monasticism**, an institutionalized [religious](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) practice or movement whose members attempt to live by a rule that requires works that go beyond those of either the laity or the ordinary spiritual leaders of their religions. Commonly celibate and universally [ascetic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/asceticism), the monastic individual separates himself or herself from society either by living as a [hermit](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hermit-religion) or anchorite (religious recluse) or by joining a [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) ( _coenobium_) of others who profess similar intentions. First applied to [Christian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity) groups in antiquity, the term _monasticism_ is now used to denote similar, though not identical, practices in religions such as [Buddhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism), [Hinduism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism), [Jainism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jainism), and [Daoism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Taoism).

The word _monasticism_ is derived from the [Greek](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-language) _monachos_ (“living alone”), but this etymology highlights only one of the elements of monasticism and is somewhat misleading, because a large proportion of the world’s monastics live in cenobitic (common life) [communities](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religious-order). The term _monasticism_ implies [celibacy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/celibacy), or living alone in the sense of lacking a spouse, which became a socially and historically crucial feature of the monastic life.

Even this aspect of monasticism does not extend beyond the [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) and languages that perpetuate the religious terminology of the so-called Abrahamic (tracing their origin to the [biblical](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible) figure [Abraham](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abraham)) or [prophetic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prophecy) religions of [Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judaism), [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity), and [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam). In the [Islamic world](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-world), [Arabic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Arabic-language) and [Persian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Persian-language) terms that can be translated as _[monk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monk-monasticism)_ or _monastic_ do not mean “solitary,” as in the Greek. Instead, they are etymologically derived from other terms associated with monastic life in Islam (e.g., _zuhd_, “asceticism”). None of the many Indic terms for _monk_ (e.g., the [Sanskrit](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sanskrit-language) _apabhramsha_ or the [Pali](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pali-language) _prakrit_) mean “single” or “living alone,” though monastics in those traditions—most notably [Hinduism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism), [Buddhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism), and [Jainism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jainism)—live alone or in groups that are set off from the rest of society. The etymologies of the Indian and some of the Arabic and Persian terminology connote poverty, ecstatic states of mind, dress conventions, and so on, while other terms imply single, celibate living.

## Nature and significance

Monastics have been instrumental in creating, preserving, and [enhancing](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhancing) institutions of religious and [secular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/secular) [learning](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religious-education) and in transmitting cultural goods, [artifacts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/artifacts), and [intellectual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectual) skills down through the generations. Monastic institutions have also fulfilled medical, political, and military functions, though since 1500 the latter two have become completely secularized in most societies.

A definition of monasticism that covers all its forms would be so broad that particulars would have to be [relegated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/relegated) to the analysis of specific monastic systems. Such a definition might be: religiously [mandated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/mandated) behaviour (orthopraxy), together with its institutions, [ritual](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ritual), and belief systems, whose agents, members, or participants undertake voluntarily (often through a vow) religious works that go beyond those required by the religious teachings of the society at large. Such behaviour derives from the example of religious and spiritual founders who interpreted more radically the tenets that apply to all believers or to the whole society. Beyond such a statement, one can speak only of the principal characteristics of the monastic life and its institutions, since none of them is universal. Celibacy is fundamental to the majority of the world’s monastic orders but is by no means universal, as shown by the case of Buddhism in modern Japan.

Another characteristic, [asceticism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/asceticism), is universal, provided the term is defined widely enough so as to include all supererogatory (voluntarily undertaken rather than wholly prescribed) religious practices. The truly universal characteristic of monasticism follows from its definition: the monastic separates from society, either to [abide](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/abide) alone as a religious recluse (hermit or anchorite) or to join a community of those who have separated themselves from their surroundings with similar intentions—i.e., the full-time pursuit of the religious life in its most radical and often in its most demanding guise.

Monasticism does not exist in societies that lack a written transmitted lore. [Nonliterate societies](https://www.britannica.com/topic/indigenous-religion) cannot have monastic institutions, because the monastic responds to an established written body of religious doctrine, which has undergone [criticism](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criticism) and then generated countercriticism in a dialectic process that presupposes a literate, codified manipulation of the doctrine. The monastic founders and their successors may either support or oppose the official religious tradition, but the presence of such a tradition is indispensable as the matrix of all monastic endeavour.

## Overcoming imperfections

All monasticism has its mainstay in theological [convictions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/convictions) that life in society cannot generate the spiritual consummation [stipulated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/stipulated) by the [religion’s](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) founder. In some traditions, especially in those of South Asian [provenance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/provenance), the true “ [self](https://www.britannica.com/topic/self)” is held to be clogged and concealed by imperfections—by [sin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sin-religion), ignorance, or other theologically suggested impediments. The ego with which the layperson and the seeking neophyte identifies is not the true self, which must be discovered or uncovered. Barriers—differently conceived as matter, individuated mind, or a soul- [mind](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mind) [aggregate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aggregate) defiled by sin, ignorance, and perversion—must be broken through, or a veil lifted, so that the true self, the [primordial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/primordial) spirit, may shine forth. In most traditions this breakthrough is held to be unattainable through a conventionally good life in society, and thus a new approach must be sought. The body and the mind, which are part or all of the impediment, have to be controlled, [disciplined](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disciplined), and chastised; hence, monastics advocate either [asceticism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/asceticism) or a set of psychophysical practices that differ radically from the normal routines of life.

## Spiritual [perfection](https://www.britannica.com/topic/perfection)

The quest for spiritual intensification is elitist—even when, as within Christian monastic orders, humility is required. Withdrawal from society is necessary because the instrumentalities of perfection cannot normally be acquired and activated in the surroundings of everyday life. The basis of monastic life is a set of spiritual precepts that either [articulate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/articulate) the supreme value or provide support for the body and the mind on their journey toward whatever supreme consummation may be [envisioned](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/envisioned). Intense contemplation, often accompanied by physical rigours, [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) [ascetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ascetic) practice—i.e., [prayer](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prayer), [worship](https://www.britannica.com/topic/worship), incantation, propitiation, and various forms of self-abasement or self-inflation. Monastics pursue all these forms of orthopraxy in enormously varied forms and degrees.

## Emancipation of the self

## [Salvation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/salvation-religion)

The ultimate purpose of the monastic endeavour is to attain a state of freedom from bondage, where both bondage and freedom are defined in theological terms. The languages of most [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) with monastic traditions possess special terms to denote bondage and freedom; a few languages adapt terms of common parlance that are then understood by members of society to refer to theologically adumbrated types of bondage and freedom. For example, the term _[salvation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/salvation-religion)_ in the Christian [context](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context) means deliverance from the powers of evil that arise from [original sin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/original-sin) and that beset a person’s body, mind, and [soul](https://www.britannica.com/topic/soul-religion-and-philosophy). Notions of salvation, liberation, and emancipation are generated by, or closely related to, the way in which a society conceives of the individual’s status within the larger universe.

These concepts presuppose a specific cosmological view against which to frame the answers to the question—formulated or unformulated—“What is it that is bound and that can, should, or must be freed to achieve the most desirable state within or vis-à-vis the totality of things—e.g., the cosmos, God, and other absolutes?” The question implies spatial and temporal [parameters](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/parameters) that need to be [articulated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/articulated). In some of the [indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/indigenous) South Asian religions, salvation can be achieved during one’s lifetime, but whether this actually happens or is delayed is irrelevant to Indian notions of liberation (Sanskrit: _[moksha](https://www.britannica.com/topic/moksha-Indian-religion)_). In [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity) and [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam), but not in [Rabbinic Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rabbinic-Judaism), salvation cannot be fully achieved as long as the body exists. Thus, _salvation_ and its [semantic](https://www.britannica.com/science/semantics) equivalents in other languages refer to both the present and the future in the South Asian religions but to the future above all in two of the Abrahamic ones. The life of the monastic consists of full-time seeking of salvation, in contrast to that of the “part-time” quest of the general believer.

## Redemption

The concept of redemption as deliverance from the spiritual effect of past transgressions may or may not be identical with salvation, though the terms are synonymous in many [contexts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contexts), notably within Christianity. As part of a vocation, the monastic seeks redemption from his or her sins and usually intercedes for others to advance their redemption. This is accomplished through personal [sacrifice](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacrifice-religion) and may involve forms of self- [mortification](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mortification). The practice of self-mortification, which intensifies or stabilizes the austerities required of the monastic, is found in all monastic traditions. Whether the autocentric or the [vicarious](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vicarious) aspect of the quest is emphasized depends entirely on the doctrinal framework within which the monastic functions. In either case, however, monastics improve their chances of redemption because, in mortifying their own bodies and minds for the benefit of others, they also help their own advancement along the spiritual path. When a [Jain](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jainism) [monk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monk-monasticism) volunteers to lie upon a bed infested with vermin that suck his blood, he may do so to diminish a client’s or patron’s burden of bad [karma](https://www.britannica.com/topic/karma) (the notion that every deed, good or bad, receives due reward or retribution), but at the same time he practices the monastic virtues prescribed for him as a monk. When a [Franciscan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Franciscans) friar (a follower of [Francis of Assisi](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Francis-of-Assisi), the 12th–13th-century Italian [mendicant](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mendicant-Roman-Catholicism) leader) serves the poor and the sick, he also exercises his own virtues of service and humility, all of which are signs or instruments of his own redemption.

## [Liberation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/moksha-Indian-religion)

When liberation ( [_moksha_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/moksha-Indian-religion)) from cycles of birth and death constitutes the foundation of a belief system, as in the basic Indian pattern of [samsara](https://www.britannica.com/topic/samsara) (the ineluctable process of death and rebirth that can be broken only through supererogatory efforts of asceticism), monastics become disseminators of methods of liberation. In [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), [Tibet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tibetan-Buddhism), and [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia) the monk stood at the centre of religious life, whereas in the Western Christian world he was and is marginal to the main liturgical and ideological thrust, [albeit](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/albeit) not always deprived of high [social status](https://www.britannica.com/topic/social-status). In principle, the importance of the monastic life in a religious system (if not always in the social system) is related to its [eschatology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/eschatology) (doctrine of last things). Thus, if the state of existence after salvation is continuous with the present life, as in the [Abrahamic religions](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Abrahamic-religions), then the monastic will have less prominence than would be had in belief systems, such as those of [South Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Asia), in which salvation implies a different state that cancels finitude and [eradicates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/eradicates) all traces of separate individual existence.

- What is a rosary?







A rosary is a religious exercise involving prayers counted on a string of beads or a knotted cord, also referring to the beads or cord themselves. Adopted by Christians in the 3rd century, it is now a popular method of public and private prayer in Roman Catholicism. The most common form is the Rosary of the Blessed Virgin, recited using a chaplet with beads arranged in five decades, each separated by a larger bead.



The Rosary of the Blessed Virgin consists of reciting 15 decades of Hail Marys while holding a small bead. Different prayers (the Gloria Patri and the Our Father) are said on the larger beads separating the decades while meditating on particular mysteries. These mysteries are events from the life, death, and glorification of Jesus Christ and Mary, divided into the joyous, sorrowful, and glorious mysteries. In 2002, Pope John Paul II added the luminous mysteries, celebrating events in Jesus’ ministry.

**rosary**, (from [Latin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Latin-language) _rosarium_, “rose garden”), religious exercise in which [prayers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prayer) are recited and counted on a string of beads or a knotted cord. By extension, the beads or cord may also be called a rosary. The practice is widespread, occurring in virtually every major religious tradition in the world.

## In Buddhism

Known as _mala_ s, prayer beads are a traditional tool in [Buddhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism) and are especially common among Tibetan Buddhists. It was likely adapted from Hinduism. A _mala_ typically features 108 beads, which are said to represent humanity’s mortal desires, and often ends in a tassel or amulet. The beads are commonly used to count the recitation of [mantras](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mantra) but can also be used to direct meditative breathing or to count prostrations. The beads themselves are often painted in specific colours and can be made from [bodhi](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Bo-tree) wood, seeds, shells, metal, or other materials, depending on the focus of the meditation.

## In Christianity

In [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity) the practice was adopted in the 3rd century by Eastern Christian monks, and various forms of the rosary were developed. In [Roman Catholicism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism/Structure-of-the-church#ref43647) the rosary became a popular method of public and private prayer. The most common rosary is the one devoted to [Mary](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mary-mother-of-Jesus), the [Rosary of the Blessed Virgin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rosary-of-the-Blessed-Virgin), the prayers of which are recited with the aid of a chaplet, or rosary. The beads of the chaplet are arranged in five decades (sets of 10), each decade separated from the next by a larger bead. The two ends of the chaplet are joined by a small string holding a crucifix, two large beads, and three small beads.

Traditionally, the Rosary of the Blessed Virgin requires three turns around the chaplet. It consists of the recitation of 15 decades of [Hail Marys](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hail-Mary-prayer) (150 Hail Marys), each one said while holding a small bead. On the larger beads separating the decades, different prayers are said (the [Gloria Patri](https://www.britannica.com/topic/lesser-doxology) and the [Our Father](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lords-Prayer)) and particular mysteries are meditated upon. The 15 [mysteries](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mystery-religious-concept-or-rite) are events from the life, death, and glorification of [Jesus Christ](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus) and Mary; they are divided into three sets of five—the joyous, the sorrowful, and the glorious mysteries. The introductory and concluding prayers of the rosary vary.

In 2002 Pope [John Paul II](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-John-Paul-II) added a fourth set of mysteries, the “luminous mysteries,” or mysteries of light. The five new mysteries celebrate events in Jesus’ ministry, including his [baptism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/baptism); his miracle at Cana, where he turned water into wine; his proclamation of the [kingdom of God](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Kingdom-of-God); the [Transfiguration](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Transfiguration-Christianity), in which he revealed his divinity to three of his Apostles; and his establishment of the [Eucharist](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eucharist) at the [Last Supper](https://www.britannica.com/event/Last-Supper-Christianity).

The origin of the Rosary of the Blessed Virgin is not certain, though it has been associated with [St. Dominic](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Dominic), founder of the [Dominican](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dominican-order) order in the early 13th century. The devotion probably developed gradually as a substitute for the recitation of the [Psalms](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Psalms) or for the divine office sung by monks at the various canonical hours each day. It reached its definitive form in the 15th century through the preaching of the Dominican Alan de la Roche and his associates, who organized Rosary Confraternities at Douai in France and at Cologne. In 1520 Pope [Leo X](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leo-X) gave the rosary official approbation, and it has been repeatedly commended by the Roman Catholic Church. Since the 1960s, however, public recitation of the rosary has become less frequent. St. John Paul II’s addition of new mysteries, which are not required for reciting the rosary, was intended to revive interest in the practice; some traditional Catholics, however, rejected the new mysteries, believing that they upset the relationship between the original number of mysteries and their corresponding psalms.

In [Eastern Orthodoxy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eastern-Orthodoxy) the prayer rope predates the Catholic rosary and is mainly a monastic devotion. Rosaries of 33, 100, or 300 knots or beads are the common sizes, and they are used to count repetitions of the [Prayer of the Heart](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jesus-prayer) (the [Jesus Prayer](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jesus-prayer)). The [Russian Orthodox](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Russian-Orthodox-Church) _vertitza_ (“string”), _chotki_ (“chaplet”), or _lievstoka_ (“ladder”) is made of 103 beads, separated into irregular sections by 4 large beads and joined together so that the lines of beads run parallel, thus suggesting the form of a ladder; the parallel lines symbolize the ladder seen by [Jacob](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jacob-Hebrew-patriarch) in his dream and remind the faithful of the spiritual climb toward greater devotion and virtue. In the Romanian church the chaplet is called _matanie_ (“reverence”) because the monk makes a profound bow at the beginning and end of each prayer counted on the beads.

The [Anglican](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Anglicanism) prayer beads are a blend of the Orthodox and Catholic rosaries. They have four sections (“weeks”) of seven beads each, four larger “cruciform” beads separating the weeks, and an invitatory bead and a cross at the base. A prayer is said first on the cross and then on each of the 33 beads—33, according to tradition, equaling the number of years in Jesus’ earthly life—and the “circle of prayers” is typically performed three times (symbolic of the [Trinity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Trinity-Christianity)), making the total number of prayers 100, which represents the fullness of creation.

## In Hinduism

The _japa mala_ of [Hinduism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism) is used to direct and count the recitation of mantras during meditation. It usually consists of 108 beads strung in a circle to represent the cyclic nature of life. Many also feature a larger _guru_ or _bindu_ bead that marks the start and end of the strand. Members of the [Hare Krishna](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hare-Krishna) movement often use beads in their chanting of the names of [Krishna](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Krishna-Hindu-deity), and other Hindu traditions use the beads to recite the names of specific gods. Some sects believe that the beads are infused with different energies based on the mantras they have been used for and thus have an array of rosaries for different meditations.

## In Islam

In [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam) the rosary ( [_subḥa_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/subhah)) consists of three groups of beads whose total is 100. Each bead represents one of the “most beautiful names of God,” and the rosary serves to count these names. The rosary is also used in an act of prayer and is carried by all classes of Muslims, especially pilgrims.

### What is the difference between Christianity and Roman Catholicism?

[Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity) is a world religion that stems from the life, teachings, and death of [Jesus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus). [Roman Catholicism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism) is the largest of the three major branches of Christianity. Thus, all Roman Catholics are Christian, but not all Christians are Roman Catholic. Of the estimated 2.5 billion Christians in the world, about 1.3 billion are Roman Catholics. Broadly, Roman Catholicism differs from other Christian churches and denominations in its beliefs about the [sacraments](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacrament), the roles of the [Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible) and tradition, the importance of the [Virgin Mary](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mary-mother-of-Jesus) and the [saints](https://www.britannica.com/topic/saint), and the [papacy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/papacy).

### Who is the leader of the Roman Catholic Church?

The [pope](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pope), the [bishop](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bishop-Christianity) of [Rome](https://www.britannica.com/place/Rome), serves as the leader of the Roman Catholic Church. On May 8, 2025, [Leo XIV](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leo-XIV) (formerly Cardinal Robert Prevost) was elected to the [papacy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/papacy). He succeeded Pope [Francis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-I-pope) (formerly Cardinal Jorge Bergoglio), who reigned from March 13, 2013, to April 21, 2025.

### Who founded Roman Catholicism?

As a branch of [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity), Roman Catholicism can be traced to the life and teachings of [Jesus Christ](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus) in Roman-occupied Jewish Palestine about 30 ce. According to Roman Catholic teaching, each of the [sacraments](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacrament) was instituted by Christ himself. [Roman Catholicism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism) also holds that Jesus established his disciple [St. Peter](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Peter-the-Apostle) as the first [pope](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pope) of the nascent church (Matthew 16:18).

### What are the Roman Catholic sacraments?

In [Roman Catholicism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism) and certain other [Christian](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity) churches, the [sacraments](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacrament) are a key and essential part of the faith. In Roman Catholic teaching, the sacraments serve to perpetuate the union of God and humankind. They are “the visible form of an invisible grace,” as [St. Augustine](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Augustine) famously described them. Roman Catholicism celebrates seven sacraments: [baptism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/baptism), the [Eucharist](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eucharist), [confirmation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/confirmation), reconciliation ( [confession](https://www.britannica.com/topic/confession-religion)), [marriage](https://www.britannica.com/topic/marriage), [anointing of the sick](https://www.britannica.com/topic/anointing-of-the-sick), and [holy orders](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ordination). Some, such as baptism, confirmation, marriage, and ordination, are generally to be received only once in a Roman Catholic’s life. For others, such as the Eucharist and reconciliation, frequent participation is encouraged.

### Why is Roman Catholicism so prominent in Latin America?

Roman Catholicism is the major religion of nearly every country in [Latin America](https://www.britannica.com/place/Latin-America). This can be attributed in large part to the lingering effects of Spanish and Portuguese colonization of the region and the Roman Catholic missions that accompanied those endeavors. Often, the missions served as tools for the suppression of [Indigenous peoples](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indigenous-peoples-of-the-Americas), forcing “civility” in the form of the [Spanish](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Spanish-language) or [Portuguese](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Portuguese-language) language, Western dress, and a Europeanized agricultural lifestyle. However, sometimes Roman Catholic missions stood opposed to the colonizing forces and protected people from enslavement and helped them achieve economic autonomy (which was a major factor in the expulsion of the [Jesuits](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jesuits) from the Americas in 1767). Although the countries of Latin America eventually gained independence from [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain) and [Portugal](https://www.britannica.com/place/Portugal), the religious legacy of [colonialism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Western-colonialism) has persisted.

**Roman Catholicism**, Christian [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) that has been the decisive spiritual force in the history of Western civilization. Along with [Eastern Orthodoxy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eastern-Orthodoxy) and [Protestantism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Protestantism), it is one of the three major branches of [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity). It is led by the [pope](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pope), as the [bishop of Rome](https://www.britannica.com/topic/papacy), and the [Holy See](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Holy-See) forms the [church’s](https://www.britannica.com/topic/church-architecture) central government, making decisions on issues of [faith](https://www.britannica.com/topic/faith) and [morality](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/morality) for the some 1.3 billion Catholics throughout the world.

The Roman Catholic Church traces its history to [Jesus Christ](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus) and the [Apostles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/list-of-the-Twelve-Apostles-of-Jesus). The number of Roman Catholics in the world is greater than that of nearly all other religious traditions. These incontestable statistical and historical facts suggest that some understanding of Roman Catholicism—its history, its institutional structure, its beliefs and practices, and its place in the world—is an indispensable component of cultural literacy, regardless of how one may individually answer the ultimate questions of life and death and faith. Without a grasp of what Roman Catholicism is, it is difficult to make historical sense of the [Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages), [intellectual](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intellectual) sense of the works of [St. Thomas Aquinas](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Thomas-Aquinas), literary sense of [_The Divine Comedy_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Divine-Comedy) of [Dante](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Dante-Alighieri), artistic sense of the [Gothic](https://www.britannica.com/art/Gothic-architecture) cathedrals, or musical sense of many of the [compositions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compositions) of [Haydn](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Haydn) and [Mozart](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wolfgang-Amadeus-Mozart) ( _see_ [Catholic imagination](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Catholic-imagination)).

At one level, of course, the interpretation of Roman Catholicism is closely related to the interpretation of Christianity as such. By its own reading of history, Roman Catholicism originated with the very beginnings of Christianity. An essential component of the definition of any one of the other branches of Christendom, moreover, is its relation to Roman Catholicism: How did Eastern Orthodoxy and Roman Catholicism come into [schism](https://www.britannica.com/event/East-West-Schism-1054)? Was the break between the [Church of England](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Church-of-England) and Rome inevitable? Conversely, such questions are essential to the definition of Roman Catholicism itself, even to a definition that adheres strictly to the official Roman Catholic view, according to which the Roman Catholic Church has maintained an unbroken [continuity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/continuity) since the days of the Apostles, while all other denominations, from the ancient [Copts](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Copt) to the latest storefront church, are deviations from it.

Like any intricate and ancient phenomenon, Roman Catholicism can be described and interpreted from a variety of perspectives and by several [methodologies](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/methodologies). Thus, the Roman Catholic Church itself is a complex institution, for which the usual diagram of a pyramid, extending from the pope at the apex to the believers in the pew, is vastly oversimplified. Within that institution, moreover, sacred congregations, archdioceses and [dioceses](https://www.britannica.com/topic/diocese), provinces, [religious orders](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religious-order-Roman-Catholicism) and societies, seminaries and colleges, [parishes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/parish-religion) and confraternities, and countless other organizations all invite the social scientist to the consideration of power relations, leadership roles, social [dynamics](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynamics), and other sociological phenomena that they uniquely represent.

The Holy See is assisted by the [Roman Curia](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Curia), a group of dicasteries (also known as departments), congregations, and councils with specific functions and responsibilities relating to church matters such as liturgy and [worship](https://www.britannica.com/topic/worship), religious [education](https://www.britannica.com/topic/education), missionary activities, doctrine of the faith, or bishops and [clergy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/clergy-Christianity). This administrative structure is often likened to a [president](https://www.britannica.com/topic/president-government-official) and [prime minister](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prime-minister) system, with the pope serving as president or head of state and the [cardinal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cardinal-Roman-Catholicism) secretary of state serving as prime minister or [head of government](https://www.britannica.com/topic/head-of-government).

The pope and Holy See reside in [Vatican City](https://www.britannica.com/place/Vatican-City), an enclave in [Rome](https://www.britannica.com/place/Rome), situated on the west bank of the [Tiber River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tiber-River). Vatican City is the world’s smallest fully independent [nation-state](https://www.britannica.com/topic/nation-state). The [Vatican Palace](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Vatican-palace), the papal residence in Vatican City north of [St. Peter’s Basilica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Saint-Peters-Basilica), one of the most renowned works of [Renaissance architecture](https://www.britannica.com/art/Renaissance-architecture), is a major site of tourism. The lavish building is home to a number of public chapels, notably the [Sistine Chapel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sistine-Chapel); the four Stanze di Raffaello ( [Raphael](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Raphael-Italian-painter-and-architect)’s Rooms), with extensive [frescoes](https://www.britannica.com/art/fresco-painting) by the artist and his successors; [Vatican Museums and Galleries](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Vatican-Museums-and-Galleries); and the [Vatican Apostolic Library](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Vatican-Apostolic-Library).

As a world religion among world religions, Roman Catholicism [encompasses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompasses), within the range of its multicolored life, features of many other world faiths; thus, only the [methodology](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/methodology) of comparative religion can address them all. Furthermore, because of the influence of [Plato](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Plato) and [Aristotle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle) on those who developed it, Roman Catholic doctrine must be studied philosophically even to understand its theological vocabulary. Nevertheless, a historical approach is especially appropriate to this task, not only because two millennia of history are represented in the Roman Catholic Church but also because the [hypothesis](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hypothesis) of its continuity with the past, and the divine truth embodied in that continuity, are central to the church’s understanding of itself and essential to the [justification](https://www.britannica.com/topic/justification-Christianity) of its authority.

For a more detailed treatment of the [early church](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-early-Christianity), _see_ [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity). The present article concentrates on the historical forces that transformed the primitive Christian movement into a church that was recognizably “catholic”—that is, possessing identifiable norms of doctrine and life, fixed structures of authority, and a universality (the original meaning of the term _catholic_) by which the church’s membership could extend, at least in principle, to all of humanity.

## The emergence of Catholic [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity)

At least in an inchoate form, all the elements of catholicity—doctrine, authority, universality—are evident in the [New Testament](https://www.britannica.com/topic/New-Testament). The [Acts of the Apostles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Acts-of-the-Apostles-New-Testament) begins with a depiction of the demoralized band of the [disciples](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disciples) of [Jesus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus) in [Jerusalem](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jerusalem), but by the end of its account of the first decades, the Christian [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) has developed some [nascent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nascent) [criteria](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criteria) for determining the difference between authentic (“apostolic”) and inauthentic teaching and behavior. It has also moved beyond the geographic borders of [Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judaism), as the dramatic sentence of the closing chapter announces: “And thus we came to Rome” (Acts 28:14). The later epistles of the New Testament [admonish](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/admonish) their readers to “guard what has been entrusted to you” (1 [Timothy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Letters-of-Paul-to-Timothy) 6:20) and to “contend for the faith that was once for all handed down to the holy ones” ( [Jude](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Letter-of-Jude) 3), and they speak about the Christian community itself in exalted and even cosmic terms as the church, “which is \[Christ’s\] body, the fullness of the one who fills all things in every way” ( [Ephesians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Letter-of-Paul-to-the-Ephesians) 1:23). It is clear even from the New Testament that these [catholic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/catholic) features were proclaimed in response to internal challenges as well as external ones; indeed, scholars have concluded that the early church was far more pluralistic from the very beginning than the somewhat idealized portrayal in the New [Testament](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Testament) might suggest.

As such challenges continued in the 2nd and 3rd centuries ce, further development of catholic teaching became necessary. The schema of [apostolic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/apostolic-church) authority formulated by the bishop of Lyon, [St. Irenaeus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Irenaeus) (c. 130–c. 200), sets forth systematically the three main sources of authority for catholic Christianity: the Scriptures of the New Testament (alongside the [Hebrew Scriptures](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-Bible), or “Old Testament,” which Christians interpret as prophesying the coming of Jesus); the episcopal centers established by the Apostles as the seats of their identifiable successors in the governance of the church (traditionally at Alexandria, Antioch, Jerusalem, and Rome); and the apostolic tradition of [normative](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/normative) doctrine as the “rule of faith” and the standard of Christian conduct. Each of the three sources depended on the other two for validation; thus, one could determine which purportedly scriptural writings were genuinely apostolic by appealing to their conformity with acknowledged apostolic tradition and to the usage of the apostolic churches, and so on. This was not a [circular argument](https://www.britannica.com/topic/circular-argument) but an appeal to a single catholic authority of apostolicity, in which the three elements were inseparable. Inevitably, however, there arose conflicts—of doctrine and jurisdiction, of worship and pastoral practice, and of social and political strategy—among the three sources, as well as between equally “apostolic” bishops. When [bilateral](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/bilateral) means of resolving such conflicts proved insufficient, there could be recourse to either the precedent of convoking an apostolic council (Acts 15) or to what Irenaeus had already called “the preeminent authority of this church \[of Rome\], with which, as a matter of necessity, every church should agree.” Catholicism was on the way to becoming Roman Catholic.

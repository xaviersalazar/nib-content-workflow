**Hinduism**, major world [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) originating on the [Indian subcontinent](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indian-subcontinent) and [comprising](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprising) several and varied systems of [philosophy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/philosophy), belief, and [ritual](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ritual). Although the name Hinduism is relatively new, having been coined by British writers in the first decades of the 19th century, it refers to a rich [cumulative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cumulative) tradition of texts and practices, some of which date to the 2nd millennium bce or possibly earlier. If the [Indus valley civilization](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indus-civilization) (3rd–2nd millennium bce) was the earliest source of these traditions, as some scholars hold, then Hinduism is the oldest living religion on Earth. Its many [sacred](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacred) texts in [Sanskrit](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sanskrit-language) and [vernacular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vernacular) languages served as a vehicle for spreading the religion to other parts of the world, though ritual and the visual and performing arts also played a significant role in its transmission. From about the 4th century ce, Hinduism had a dominant presence in [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia), one that would last for more than 1,000 years.

In the early 21st century, Hinduism had nearly one billion adherents worldwide and was the religion of about 80 percent of [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India)’s population. Despite its global presence, however, it is best understood through its many distinctive regional [manifestations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifestations).

## Overview

## The term Hinduism

The term Hinduism became familiar as a designator of religious ideas and practices distinctive to [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India) with the publication of books such as _Hinduism_ (1877) by Sir Monier Monier-Williams, the notable Oxford scholar and author of an influential Sanskrit dictionary. Initially it was an outsiders’ term, building on centuries-old usages of the word Hindu. Early travelers to the [Indus valley](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indus-River), beginning with the Greeks and Persians, spoke of its inhabitants as “Hindu” (Greek: _‘indoi_), and, in the 16th century, residents of India themselves began very slowly to employ the term to distinguish themselves from the Turks. Gradually the distinction became primarily religious rather than ethnic, geographic, or cultural.

Since the late 19th century, Hindus have reacted to the term Hinduism in several ways. Some have rejected it in favor of [indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/indigenous) formulations. Others have preferred “ [Vedic religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Vedic-religion),” using the term Vedic to refer not only to the ancient religious texts known as the [Vedas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Veda) but also to a fluid corpus of sacred works in multiple languages and an orthoprax (traditionally sanctioned) way of life. Still others have chosen to call the religion _[sanatana dharma](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sanatana-dharma)_ (“eternal law”), a formulation made popular in the 19th century and emphasizing the timeless elements of the tradition that are perceived to [transcend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/transcend) local interpretations and practice. Finally, others, perhaps the majority, have simply accepted the term _Hinduism_ or its [analogues](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogues), especially _hindu dharma_ (Hindu [moral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/moral) and religious law), in various [Indic languages](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indo-Aryan-languages).

Since the early 20th century, textbooks on Hinduism have been written by Hindus themselves, often under the rubric of _sanatana dharma_. These efforts at self-explanation add a new layer to an elaborate tradition of explaining practice and doctrine that dates to the 1st millennium bce. The roots of Hinduism can be traced back much farther—both textually, to the schools of commentary and debate preserved in epic and Vedic writings from the 2nd millennium bce, and visually, through artistic representations of [yaksha](https://www.britannica.com/topic/yaksha) s (luminous spirits associated with specific locales and natural phenomena) and [naga](https://www.britannica.com/topic/naga-Hindu-mythology) s (cobralike divinities), which were [worshipped](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/worshipped) from about 400 bce. The roots of the tradition are also sometimes traced back to the female terra-cotta figurines found ubiquitously in excavations of sites associated with the Indus valley civilization and sometimes interpreted as goddesses.

# General nature of Hinduism

More strikingly than any other major religious [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community), Hindus accept—and indeed celebrate—the organic, multileveled, and sometimes pluralistic nature of their traditions. This expansiveness is made possible by the widely shared Hindu view that truth or reality cannot be [encapsulated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encapsulated) in any creedal formulation, a perspective expressed in the Hindu [prayer](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prayer) “May good thoughts come to us from all sides.” Thus, Hinduism maintains that truth must be sought in multiple sources, not dogmatically proclaimed.

Anyone’s view of the truth—even that of a _[guru](https://www.britannica.com/topic/guru-Hinduism)_ regarded as possessing superior authority—is fundamentally conditioned by the specifics of time, age, gender, state of [consciousness](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consciousness), social and geographic location, and stage of attainment. These multiple perspectives [enhance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhance) a broad view of religious truth rather than diminish it; hence, there is a strong tendency for contemporary Hindus to affirm that [tolerance](https://www.britannica.com/topic/toleration) is the foremost religious virtue. On the other hand, even [cosmopolitan](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cosmopolitan) Hindus living in a global [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) recognize and value the fact that their [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) has developed in the specific [context](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context) of the [Indian subcontinent](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indian-subcontinent). Such a tension between universalist and particularist impulses has long animated the Hindu tradition. When Hindus speak of their religious identity as _[sanatana dharma](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sanatana-dharma)_, they emphasize its continuous, seemingly eternal ( _sanatana_) existence and the fact that it describes a web of customs, obligations, traditions, and ideals ( [dharma](https://www.britannica.com/topic/dharma-religious-concept)) that far exceeds the Western tendency to think of religion primarily as a system of beliefs. A common way in which English-speaking Hindus often distance themselves from that frame of mind is to insist that Hinduism is not a religion but a way of life.

## The five tensile strands

Across the sweep of Indian religious history, at least five elements have given shape to the Hindu religious tradition: doctrine, practice, society, story, and devotion. These five elements, to adopt a typical Hindu [metaphor](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/metaphor), are understood as relating to one another as strands in an elaborate braid. Moreover, each strand develops out of a history of conversation, elaboration, and challenge. Hence, in looking for what makes the tradition cohere, it is sometimes better to locate central points of tension than to expect clear agreements on Hindu thought and practice.

## Doctrine

The first of the five strands of Hinduism is doctrine, as expressed in a vast textual tradition anchored to the [Veda](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Veda) (“Knowledge”), the oldest core of Hindu religious utterance, and organized through the centuries primarily by members of the learned [Brahman](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Brahman-social-class) class. Here several [characteristic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characteristic) tensions appear. One concerns the relationship between the [divine](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacred) and the world. Another tension concerns the disparity between the world-preserving ideal of dharma and that of _[moksha](https://www.britannica.com/topic/moksha-Indian-religion)_ (release from an inherently flawed world). A third tension exists between individual destiny, as shaped by [karma](https://www.britannica.com/topic/karma) (the influence of one’s actions on one’s present and future lives), and the individual’s [deep](https://www.britannica.com/technology/diya) bonds to family, society, and the divinities associated with these concepts.

These books are Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy.



The Torah confirms events recognized as acts of God and is considered especially present in the first five books of the Bible. The Torah is vital because it embodies the divine will and provides a guide for Jewish communities and individuals to live in accordance with that will.

- What are major Jewish holidays and their meanings?







The major holidays in [Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judaism) include:



  - **Sabbath (Shabbat)** A weekly day of rest and holiness, observed from sunset Friday to sunset Saturday, commemorating God's rest after creation.

  - **Rosh Hashanah** The Jewish New Year, a two-day holiday in the fall for reflection and repentance. Marked by sounding the shofar and eating symbolic foods like apples and honey.

  - **Yom Kippur** The Day of Atonement, concluding the High Holy Days. A day of prayer, fasting, and seeking forgiveness.

  - **Sukkot** A seven-day holiday commemorating the Israelites' wandering in the wilderness, during which Jews build and dwell in temporary shelters called sukkahs.

  - **Hanukkah** The eight-day Festival of Lights, celebrating the rededication of the Second Temple after the Maccabean revolt. Celebrated by lighting candles on a menorah.

  - **Purim** Commemorates the salvation of Persian Jews through Queen Esther. Marked by reading the Book of Esther, sharing gifts, and charitable giving.

  - **Passover (Pesach)** A major seven- or eight-day holiday celebrating the Exodus from Egypt, marked by a seder meal and abstaining from leavened products.

  - **Shavuot** Originally a harvest festival, it also commemorates the giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai. Observances include studying the Torah.

**Judaism**, [monotheistic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monotheism) [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) developed among the ancient [Hebrews](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew). Judaism is characterized by a belief in one [transcendent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/transcendent) God who revealed himself to [Abraham](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abraham), [Moses](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Moses-Hebrew-prophet), and the [Hebrew](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew) prophets and by a religious life in accordance with [Scriptures](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-Bible) and rabbinic traditions. Judaism is the complex phenomenon of a total way of life for the Jewish people, [comprising](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprising) [theology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/theology), law, and innumerable cultural traditions.

## The history of Judaism

It is history that provides the key to an understanding of Judaism, for its primal affirmations appear in early historical narratives. Thus, the [Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible) reports contemporary events and activities for essentially religious reasons. The biblical authors believed that the [divine](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacred) presence is encountered primarily within history. God’s presence is also experienced within the natural realm, but the more immediate or [intimate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intimate) disclosure occurs in human actions. Although other ancient [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) also perceived a divine presence in history, the understanding of the ancient [Israelites](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Israelite) proved to be the most lasting and influential. It is this particular claim—to have experienced God’s presence in human events—and its subsequent development that is the [differentiating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/differentiating) factor in Jewish thought.

Moreover, the ancient Israelites’ entire mode of existence was affected by their belief that throughout history they stood in a unique relationship with the divine. The people of [Israel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Israel-Old-Testament-kingdom) believed that their response to the divine presence in history was central not only for themselves but for all humankind. Furthermore, God—as person—had revealed in a particular encounter the pattern and structure of communal and individual life to this people. Claiming [sovereignty](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/sovereignty) over the people because of his continuing action in history on their behalf, he had established a [covenant](https://www.britannica.com/topic/covenant-religion) ( _berit_) with them and required from them obedience to his teaching, or law ( [Torah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Torah)). This obedience was a further means by which the divine presence was made manifest—expressed in concrete human existence. The corporate life of the [chosen](https://www.britannica.com/topic/chosen-people) [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) was thus a summons to the rest of humankind to recognize God’s presence, sovereignty, and purpose—the establishment of peace and well-being in the universe and in humankind.

History, moreover, disclosed not only God’s purpose but also humankind’s inability to live in accord with it. Even the chosen community failed in its obligation and had to be summoned back, time and again, to its responsibility by the prophets—the divinely called spokespersons who warned of [retribution](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/retribution) within history and argued and reargued the case for [affirmative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/affirmative) human response. Israel’s role in the divine economy and thus Israel’s particular culpability were dominant themes sounded against the motif of fulfillment, the ultimate triumph of the divine purpose, and the establishment of divine sovereignty over all humankind.

## Nature and characteristics

In nearly 4,000 years of historical development, the Jewish people and their religion have displayed a remarkable adaptability and [continuity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/continuity). In their encounter with the great civilizations, from ancient [Babylonia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Babylonia) and [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt) to Western Christendom and modern [secular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/secular) culture, they have [assimilated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/assimilated) foreign elements and [integrated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrated) them into their own social and religious systems, thus maintaining an unbroken religious and cultural tradition. Furthermore, each period of Jewish history has left behind it a specific element of a Judaic heritage that continued to influence subsequent developments, so that the total Jewish heritage at any given time is a combination of all these successive elements along with whatever adjustments and accretions have occurred in each new age.

The various teachings of Judaism have often been regarded as specifications of the central idea of [monotheism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monotheism). One God, the creator of the world, has freely elected the Jewish people for a [unique](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/unique) covenantal relationship with himself. This one and only God has been affirmed by virtually all professing Jews in a variety of ways throughout the ages.

Jewish monotheism has had both universalistic and particularistic features. Along universal lines, it has affirmed a God who created and rules the entire world and who at the end of history will redeem all Israel (the classical name for the Jewish people), all humankind, and indeed the whole world. The ultimate goal of all nature and history is an unending reign of cosmic intimacy with God, entailing universal [justice](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/justice) and peace. Between creation and redemption lies the particularistic [designation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/designation) of the Jewish people as the locus of God’s activity in the world, as the people chosen by God to be “a kingdom of priests and a holy nation” ( [Exodus](https://www.britannica.com/event/Exodus-Old-Testament) 19:6). This arrangement is designated a [covenant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/covenant) and is structured by an elaborate and intricate law. Thus, the Jewish people are both entitled to special privileges and burdened with special responsibilities from God. As the prophet [Amos](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Amos) (8th century bce) expressed it: “You alone have I intimately known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities” (Amos 3:2). The universal goal of the Jewish people has frequently expressed itself in [messianism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/messiah-religion)—the idea of a universal, political realm of justice and peace. In one form or another, messianism has permeated Jewish thinking and action throughout the ages, and it has strongly influenced the outlook of many secular-minded Jews ( _see also_ [eschatology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/eschatology)).

Law embraces practically all domains of Jewish life, and it became the principle means by which Judaism was to bring about the reign of God on earth. It is a total guide to religious and [ethical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ethical) conduct, involving ritualistic observance as well as individual and social [ethics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ethics-philosophy). It is a liturgical and ethical way constantly expatiated on by the prophets and priests, by rabbinic sages, and by philosophers. Such conduct was to be performed in the service of God, the transcendent and immanent ruler of the universe, the Creator and the propelling force of nature, and the one giving guidance and purpose to history. According to Judaic belief, this divine guidance is [manifested](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifested) through the history of the Jewish people, which will culminate in the messianic age. Judaism, whether in its “normative” form or in its sectarian deviations, never completely departed from this basic ethical and historical monotheism.

## Periodization

The division of the millennia of Jewish history into periods is a procedure frequently dependent on [philosophical](https://www.britannica.com/topic/philosophy-of-history) [predilections](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/predilections). The Christian world long believed that until the rise of [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity) the history of Judaism was but a “preparation for the Gospel” ( _preparatio evangelica_) that was followed by the “manifestation of the Gospel” ( _demonstratio evangelica_) as revealed by [Christ](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus) and the [Apostles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Apostle). This formulation could be theologically [reconciled](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reconciled) with the assumption that Christianity had been preordained even before the creation of the world.

In the 19th century, biblical scholars moved the decisive division back to the period of the [Babylonian Exile](https://www.britannica.com/event/Babylonian-Captivity) and the restoration of the Jews to the kingdom of [Judah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judah-Hebrew-tribe) (6th–5th century bce). They asserted that after the first fall of [Jerusalem](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jerusalem) (586 bce) the ancient “Israelitic” religion gave way to a new form of the “Jewish” [faith](https://www.britannica.com/topic/faith), or Judaism, as formulated by the reformer [Ezra](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ezra-Hebrew-religious-leader) (5th century bce) and his school. In _Die Entstehung des Judentums_ (1896; “The Origin of Judaism”) the German historian Eduard Meyer argued that Judaism originated in the Persian period, or the days of [Ezra](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ezra-Hebrew-religious-leader) and [Nehemiah](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Nehemiah) (5th century bce); indeed, he attributed an important role in shaping the emergent religion to Persian imperialism.

These theories, however, have been discarded by most scholars in the light of a more [comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive) knowledge of the [ancient Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Middle-East) and the abandonment of a theory of gradual evolutionary development that was dominant at the beginning of the 20th century. Most Jews share a long-accepted notion that there never was a real break in continuity and that Mosaic-prophetic-priestly Judaism was continued, with only a few modifications, in the work of the Pharisaic and rabbinic sages well into the modern period. Even today the various Jewish groups—whether [Orthodox](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Orthodox-Judaism), [Conservative](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Conservative-Judaism), or [Reform](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Reform-Judaism)—all claim direct spiritual descent from the Pharisees and the rabbinic sages. In fact, however, many developments have occurred within so-called [normative](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/normative) or [Rabbinic Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rabbinic-Judaism).

# Biblical Judaism (20th–4th century bce)

## The ancient Middle Eastern setting

[![Sacrifice of Isaac](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/192146-050-B6DD19E2/Sacrifice-of-Isaac-Abraham-Giambattista-Pittoni-Italian.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/192146-050-B6DD19E2/Sacrifice-of-Isaac-Abraham-Giambattista-Pittoni-Italian.jpg)

[_Sacrifice of Isaac_](https://cdn.britannica.com/46/192146-050-B6DD19E2/Sacrifice-of-Isaac-Abraham-Giambattista-Pittoni-Italian.jpg) The painting _Sacrifice of Isaac_ (1713) by Italian artist Giambattista Pittoni depicts Abraham ready to obey God's command to sacrifice his son Isaac. (more)

The [Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible) depicts the family of the [Hebrew](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew) [patriarchs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/patriarch-Eastern-Orthodoxy)— [Abraham](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abraham), [Isaac](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isaac), and [Jacob](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jacob-Hebrew-patriarch) (all early 2nd millennium bce)—as having its chief seat in the northern Mesopotamian town of [Harran](https://www.britannica.com/place/Harran), which then belonged to the Hurrian kingdom of [Mitanni](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mitanni). From there Abraham, the founder of the Hebrew people, is said to have migrated to [Canaan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canaan-historical-region-Middle-East) (comprising roughly the region of modern [Israel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Israel-Old-Testament-kingdom) and Lebanon), which was a vortex of west Asian, Egyptian, and east Mediterranean [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) throughout the biblical period and later ages. From Canaan the Hebrew ancestors of the people of Israel (named after the patriarch Jacob, also called Israel) migrated to Egypt, where they lived in servitude; a few generations later they returned to occupy part of Canaan.

[Israelite](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Israelite) [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) initially resembled that of its surroundings; it was neither wholly original nor wholly primitive. The Hebrews were seminomadic herdsmen and occasionally farmers. Their tribal structure resembled that of the West Semitic steppe dwellers known from the 18th-century-bce tablets excavated at the north-central Mesopotamian city of [Mari](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mari-ancient-city-Syria); their family customs and law have parallels in the Old Babylonian and Hurro-Semite law of the early and middle 2nd millennium. The [conception](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conception) of a messenger of God that underlies biblical prophecy was [Amorite](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Amorite) (West Semitic) and also found in the tablets at Mari. Mesopotamian religious and cultural [conceptions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conceptions) are reflected in biblical cosmogony, primeval history (including the Flood story in [Genesis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Genesis-Old-Testament) 6:9–8:22), and law collections. The Canaanite component of Israelite culture consisted of the [Hebrew language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-language) and a rich literary heritage—whose Ugaritic form (which flourished in the northern Syrian city of [Ugarit](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ugarit) from the mid-15th century to approximately 1200 bce) [illuminates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/illuminates) the Bible’s poetry, style, mythological [allusions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/allusions), and religious or cultic terms. Egypt provides many [analogues](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogues) for Hebrew hymnody and wisdom literature. All the cultures among which the patriarchs lived had cosmic gods who fashioned the world and preserved its order, all had a developed [ethical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ethical) system expressed in law and [moral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/moral) [admonitions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/admonitions), and all had elaborate religious rites and [myths](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myths).

Although plainer when compared with some of the learned literary creations of Mesopotamia, Canaan, and Egypt, the earliest biblical writings are so imbued with contemporary ancient Middle Eastern elements that the once-held assumption that Israelite [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) began on a preliterate level must be rejected. Late-born amid high civilizations, the Israelite religion had from the start features characteristic of all the known religions of the area. Implanted on the [land bridge](https://www.britannica.com/science/land-bridge) between Africa and Asia, it was exposed to crosscurrents of foreign thought throughout its history.

## The pre-Mosaic period: the religion of the patriarchs

[![Guercino: Abraham Casting Out Hagar and Ishmael](https://cdn.britannica.com/49/11349-050-D840C981/Abraham-Driving-Out-Hagar-oil-canvas-Ishmael.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/49/11349-050-D840C981/Abraham-Driving-Out-Hagar-oil-canvas-Ishmael.jpg)

[Guercino: _Abraham Casting Out Hagar and Ishmael_](https://cdn.britannica.com/49/11349-050-D840C981/Abraham-Driving-Out-Hagar-oil-canvas-Ishmael.jpg) _Abraham Casting Out Hagar and Ishmael_, oil on canvas by Guercino, 1657; in the Brera Picture Gallery, Milan.(more)

Israelite tradition identified YHWH (by scholarly [convention](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/convention) pronounced [Yahweh](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yahweh)), the God of Israel, with the creator of the world, who had been known and worshipped from the beginning of time. Abraham did not discover this God but entered into a new covenantal relationship with him, in which Abraham was promised the land of Canaan and numerous progeny. God fulfilled that promise, it is believed, through the actions of the Hebrew leader [Moses](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Moses-Hebrew-prophet) (14th–13th century bce): he liberated the people of Israel from Egypt, imposed covenantal obligations on them at [Mt. Sinai](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Sinai-mountain-Egypt), and brought them to the Promised Land.

[![Titian: Adam and Eve in the Garden of Eden](https://cdn.britannica.com/53/153-050-93A1C4EF/Adam-and-Eve-oil-painting-Garden-of.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/53/153-050-93A1C4EF/Adam-and-Eve-oil-painting-Garden-of.jpg)

[Titian: _Adam and Eve in the Garden of Eden_](https://cdn.britannica.com/53/153-050-93A1C4EF/Adam-and-Eve-oil-painting-Garden-of.jpg) _Adam and Eve in the Garden of Eden_, oil painting by Titian, c. 1550; in the Prado, Madrid.(more)

Historical and anthropological studies present [formidable](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/formidable) objections to the [continuity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/continuity) of YHWH [worship](https://www.britannica.com/topic/worship) from [Adam](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adam-and-Eve-biblical-literary-figures) (the biblical first man) to Moses. The Hebrew tradition itself, moreover, does not unanimously support even the more modest claim of the continuity of YHWH worship from Abraham to Moses. This lack of continuity is demonstrated in [Exodus](https://www.britannica.com/event/Exodus-Old-Testament) 6:3, which says that God revealed himself to the patriarchs not as YHWH but as [El Shaddai](https://www.britannica.com/biography/El-Shaddai)—an [archaic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/archaic) epithet of unknown meaning that is not specifically Israelite but is found throughout the patriarchal narratives and in the [Book of Job](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Book-of-Job). The epithet El Elyon (God Most High) also appears frequently in the patriarchal narratives. Neither of these epithets is used in postpatriarchal narratives (excepting the [Book of Ruth](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Book-of-Ruth)). Other [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds) with El are unique to [Genesis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Genesis-British-rock-group): El Olam (God the Everlasting One), El Bethel (God Bethel), and El Roʾi (God of Vision). An additional peculiarity of the patriarchal stories is their use of the phrase “God of my \[your, his\] father.” All these epithets have been taken as evidence that patriarchal religion differed from the worship of YHWH that began with Moses. A relation to a [patron](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/patron) god was defined by revelations starting with Abraham (who never refers to the God of his father) and continuing with a succession of “founders” of his worship. Attached to the founder and his family, as befits the patron of wanderers, this unnamed [deity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/deity) acquired various Canaanite epithets (El, Elyon, Olam, Bethel, Qone Eretz \[“Possessor of the Land”\]) only after their immigration into Canaan. Whether the name of YHWH was known to the patriarchs is doubtful. It is significant that while the epithets Shaddai and El occur frequently in pre-Mosaic and Mosaic-age names, YHWH appears as an element only in the names of [Yehoshuaʿ](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joshua) (Joshua) and perhaps of Jochebed—persons who were closely associated with Moses.

The patriarchs are depicted as objects of God’s blessing, protection, and providential care. Their response is loyalty and obedience and observance of a cult (i.e., a system of religious beliefs and practices) whose ordinary expression is [sacrifice](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacrifice-religion), vow, and [prayer](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prayer) at an altar, stone pillar, or [sacred](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacred) tree. Circumcision was a distinctive mark of the cult [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community). The [eschatology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/eschatology) (doctrine of ultimate destiny) of their [faith](https://www.britannica.com/topic/faith) was God’s promise of land and a great progeny. Any flagrant contradictions between patriarchal and later mores have presumably been censored; yet distinctive features of the post-Mosaic religion are absent. The God of the patriarchs shows nothing of YHWH’s “jealousy”; no religious tension or contrast with their neighbors appears, and idolatry is scarcely an issue. The patriarchal [covenant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/covenant) differed from the Mosaic, [Sinaitic covenant](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sinai-covenant) in that it was modeled upon a royal grant to favorites and imposed no obligations as conditions of the people’s happiness. Evidently not the same as the later religion of Israel, the patriarchal religion prepared the way for the later one through its familial basis, its personal call by the Deity, and its response of loyalty and obedience to him.

Little can be said of the relation between the religion of the patriarchs and the religions of Canaan. Known points of contact between them are the divine [epithets](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/epithets) mentioned above. Like the God of the fathers, [El](https://www.britannica.com/topic/El), the head of the Ugaritic pantheon, was depicted as both a judgmental and a compassionate deity. [Baal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Baal-ancient-deity) (Lord), the aggressive young agricultural deity of [Ugarit](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ugarit), is remarkably absent from Genesis. Yet the socioeconomic situation of the patriarchs was so different from the urban, mercantile, and monarchical background of the Ugaritic myths as to render any comparisons highly questionable.

## The Mosaic period: foundations of the Israelite religion

## The [Egyptian](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt) sojourn

According to Hebrew tradition, a [famine](https://www.britannica.com/science/famine) caused the migration to Egypt of the band of 12 Hebrew families that later made up a tribal league in the land of Israel. The schematic character of this tradition does not impair the historicity of a migration to Egypt, an enslavement by Egyptians, and an escape from Egypt under an inspired leader by some component of the later Israelite tribes. To disallow these events, it can be argued, would make their centrality as articles of faith in the later religious beliefs of Israel inexplicable.

[![15th-century German Bible illustration showing Moses leading a group of Israelites between parted green waves of the Red Sea, with Hebrew text above.](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/83533-050-FBBFF28B/Moses-children-illustration-Israel-Red-Sea-Bible.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/83533-050-FBBFF28B/Moses-children-illustration-Israel-Red-Sea-Bible.jpg)

[Moses and the Israelites](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/83533-050-FBBFF28B/Moses-children-illustration-Israel-Red-Sea-Bible.jpg) Moses leading the children of Israel through the Red Sea; illustration from a German Bible, 15th century.(more)

Tradition gives the following account of the birth of the nation. At the [Exodus](https://www.britannica.com/event/Exodus-Old-Testament) from Egypt (13th century bce), YHWH showed his faithfulness and power by liberating the Israelites from bondage and punishing their oppressors with plagues and drowning them in the sea. At Sinai he made the Israelites his people and gave them the terms of his covenant, regulating their conduct toward him and each other so as to make them a holy nation. After sustaining them miraculously during their 40-year trek in the wilderness, he enabled them to take the land that he had promised to their fathers, the patriarchs. Central to these events is Moses, who was commissioned by God to lead the Israelites out of Egypt, mediate God’s covenant with them, and bring them to Canaan.

Behind the [legends](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legends) and the multiform law collections, it is possible to discern a historical figure to whom the legends and the legislative activity can be attached. And it is precisely Moses’ unusual combination of roles that makes him credible as a historical figure. Like [Muhammad](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Muhammad) (c. 570–632 ce) at the birth of [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam), Moses fills oracular, legislative, executive, and military functions. He shapes the main institutions of Israel: the [priesthood](https://www.britannica.com/topic/priesthood) and the sacred shrine, the covenant and its rules, and the administrative apparatus of the tribal league. Although Moses is compared to a prophet in various texts in the [Pentateuch](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Torah) (the first five books of the Bible), he is never designated as one—the term being evidently unsuited for so [comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive) and unique a figure.

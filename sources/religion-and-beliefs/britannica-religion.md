**religion**, [human](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being) beings’ relation to that which they regard as holy, sacred, absolute, spiritual, divine, or worthy of especial reverence. It is also commonly regarded as consisting of the way people deal with ultimate concerns about their lives and their fate after [death](https://www.britannica.com/science/death). In many traditions, this relation and these concerns are expressed in terms of one’s relationship with or attitude toward gods or spirits; in more [humanistic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/humanism) or [naturalistic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/naturalism-philosophy) forms of religion, they are expressed in terms of one’s relationship with or attitudes toward the broader human [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) or the natural world. In many religions, texts are deemed to have scriptural status, and people are esteemed to be invested with spiritual or [moral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/moral) authority. Believers and worshippers participate in and are often enjoined to perform devotional or contemplative practices such as [prayer](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prayer), [meditation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/meditation-mental-exercise), or particular [rituals](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ritual). [Worship](https://www.britannica.com/topic/worship), moral conduct, right [belief](https://www.britannica.com/topic/belief), and participation in religious institutions are among the [constituent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituent) elements of the religious life.

- What is theology?







Theology, derived from the Greek _theos_ (God) and _logos_ (reason), is the study of God or the gods. It originated with pre-Socratic philosophers and was first termed by Plato as the study of eternal realities.



Christian theology understands _theologia_ as the study of God's divine nature and _oikonomia_ as the study of God's activities. It seeks to clarify religious experiences through reason, balancing systematic understanding with the recognition of God's transcendence.



Theology encompasses various disciplines, including historical studies, hermeneutics, systematic theology, ethics, and apologetics. It interprets faith, develops religious teachings, and addresses humanity's existence in the world.

- Why do people study theology?







People study [theology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/theology/Functions-of-theology#ref284061) to explore religious teachings, interpret humanity's existence, and understand the relationship between humanity and the world.



Theology covers a broad range of universal themes, including God, humanity, and the world, also addressing ethical considerations and the course of history. It critically analyzes religious truths, engages in historical studies, and examines the scriptures.



Theology seeks to reconcile faith with reason, coordinate religious doctrines with scientific worldviews, and address the truth claims of various religions.

- What are some topics that theology tries to answer?







Theology addresses fundamental questions about God, humanity, and the world. It explores the nature of God, human existence, and the relationship between them.



Theology seeks to interpret humanity's role in the world, establishing ethical norms for interactions with others, societal structures, and political institutions. It also grapples with the concept of salvation, exploring how humans can achieve spiritual fulfillment or redemption.



Furthermore, theology delves into eschatology, which is the study of the end times, including concepts of life after death, judgment, and the ultimate destiny of humanity and the universe. Moral theology identifies the principles that determine human behavior in light of Christian revelation.

- How does theology relate to different religions?







Theology relates to different religions through its themes and interpretations of the divine or sacred. Though originating in Christianity, theology's themes—God, humanity, the world, salvation, and eschatology—extend to other religions like Islam and Judaism.



Theology addresses universal themes, including God, humanity, and the world, within a framework of finality, also encompassing ethics. Doctrines and dogmas serve different functions across religious traditions, acting as clues to religious insight in Eastern mystical religions and as aids to theological reflection in Western personalistic religions.



Theologians of different religions interpret their faiths, making theology normative, though not absolutely authoritative in some religions. The academic study of religion uses empirical analysis, distinct from theology’s normative claims. The interrelation of theology and the history of religions involves dialogue and mutual understanding, acknowledging the diversity of religious experiences and interpretations.

- What is the difference between theology and philosophy?







Theology and philosophy differ in their approaches to understanding reality. [Philosophy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/philosophy/Introduction) uses rational, abstract, and methodical consideration of reality and human existence. It relies on timeless evidence and autonomous reason. [Theology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/theology), derived from the Greek words _theos_ (God) and _logos_ (reason), is the study or understanding of God. It is rooted in an authority that initiates thinking, such as revealed truth or spiritual testimony. While theology uses reason and systematically develops its tenets, its reflections are based on religious convictions.



In summary, philosophy explicates the totality of being, while theology is responsible to an authority that initiates its thinking, speaking, and witnessing.

- How has theology influenced history and culture?







Theology's influence on history and culture is significant because it encompasses worldly dimensions like cosmology, anthropology, and historiography. The prophetic theology of history in the Hebrew Bible notably shaped the concept of history itself, viewing it as a linear process directed toward a goal, contrasting with cyclical views.



Theology has also been integral to cultural and intellectual life, with universities and schools often initiated by the church to explore various dimensions of life. Moreover, much of modern philosophy has emerged from theological themes, retaining a dialectical relationship even in secularism. Ethical teachings in Christianity, derived from the Ten Commandments and the Sermon on the Mount, have also shaped legal systems and social norms.

## Nature of theology

The concept of theology that is applicable as a [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/history-of-science) in all religions and that is therefore neutral is difficult to distill and determine. The problem lies in the fact that, whereas theology as a concept had its origins in the tradition of the ancient Greeks, it obtained its content and method only within Christianity. Thus, theology, because of its peculiarly Christian profile, is not readily transferable in its narrow sense to any other religion. In its broader thematic concerns, however, theology as a subject matter is germane to other religions.

The [Greek philosopher](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-philosophy) [Plato](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Plato), with whom the concept emerges for the first time, associated with the term _theology_ a polemical intention—as did his pupil [Aristotle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle). For Plato, theology described the [mythical](https://www.britannica.com/topic/myth), which he allowed may have a temporary [pedagogical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/pedagogical) significance that is [beneficial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/beneficial) to the state but is to be cleansed from all offensive and [abstruse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/abstruse) elements with the help of political legislation. This identification of theology and mythology also remained customary in later Greek thought. In contrast to philosophers, “theologians” (e.g., the 8th-century-bce Greek poets [Hesiod](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hesiod) and [Homer](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Homer-Greek-poet), the cultic servants of the [oracle](https://www.britannica.com/topic/oracle-religion) at [Delphi](https://www.britannica.com/place/Delphi-ancient-city-Greece), and the rhetoricians of the Roman cult of emperor worship) testified to and proclaimed that which they viewed as divine. Theology thus became significant as the means of proclaiming the gods, of confessing to them, and of teaching and “preaching” this confession. In this practice of “theology” by the Greeks lies the prefiguration of what later would be known as theology in the history of Christianity. In spite of all the contradictions and [nuances](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nuances) that were to emerge in the understanding of this concept in various Christian confessions and schools of thought, a formal [criterion](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criterion) remains constant: theology is the attempt of adherents of a [faith](https://www.britannica.com/topic/faith) to represent their statements of [belief](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religious-belief) consistently, to explicate them out of the basis (or fundamentals) of their faith, and to assign to such statements their specific place within the context of all other worldly relations (e.g., nature and history) and spiritual processes (e.g., [reason](https://www.britannica.com/topic/reason) and [logic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/logic)).

Here, then, the above indicated difficulty becomes apparent. In the first place, theology is a spiritual or religious attempt of “believers” to explicate their faith. In this sense it is not neutral and is not attempted from the perspective of removed observation—in contrast to a general history of religions. The [implication](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implication) derived from the religious approach is that it does not provide a formal and indifferent scheme devoid of presuppositions within which all religions could be subsumed. In the second place, theology is influenced by its origins in the Greek and Christian traditions, with the implication that the transmutation of this concept to other religions is endangered by the very circumstances of origination. If one attempts, nevertheless, such a transmutation—and if one then speaks of a theology of primitive religions and of a theology of [Buddhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism)—one must be aware of the fact that the concept “theology,” which is uncustomary and also inadequate in those spheres, is applicable only to a very limited extent and in a very modified form. This is because some Eastern religions have atheistic qualities and provide no access to the _theos_(“god”) of theology. If one nonetheless speaks of theology in religions other than Christianity or [Greek religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-religion), one implies—in formal [analogy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogy) to what has been observed above—the way in which representatives of other religions understand themselves.

## Relationship to the history of religions

If theology explicates the way in which the believer understands his faith—or, if faith is not a dominating quality, the way in which a religion’s practitioners understand their religion—this implies that it claims to be normative, even if the claim does not, as in [Hinduism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism) and [Buddhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism), culminate in the pretension to be absolutely [authoritative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/authoritative). The normative element in these religions arises simply out of the authority of a divine teacher or out of a [revelation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/revelation) (e.g., a vision or auditory revelation) or some other kind of spiritual encounter as a result of which one feels committed. The academic [study of religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/study-of-religion), which [encompasses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompasses) also [religious psychology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/study-of-religion/Basic-aims-and-methods#ref38065), [religious sociology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/study-of-religion/Basic-aims-and-methods#ref38061), and the [history and phenomenology of religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/study-of-religion/Basic-aims-and-methods#ref38079) as well as the [philosophy of religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/philosophy-of-religion), has emancipated itself from the normative aspect in favour of a purely [empirical](https://www.britannica.com/science/empirical-method) analysis. This [empirical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/empirical) aspect, which corresponds to the modern [conception](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conception) of science, can be applied only if it functions on the basis of objectifiable (empirically verifiable) entities. [Revelation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Revelation-to-John) of the kind of event that would have to be characterized as [transcendent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/transcendent), however, can never be understood as such an objectifiable entity. Only those forms of religious life that are positive and arise out of experience can be objectified. Wherever such forms are given, the religious person is taken as the source of the religious phenomena that are to be interpreted. Understood in this manner, the study of religion represents a necessary step in the process of secularization.

Nevertheless, it cannot be said that theology and the history of religions only contradict one another. The “theologies”—for want of a better term—of the various religions are concerned with religious phenomena, and the adherents of the religions of the more “advanced” [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) are themselves constrained—especially at a time of increasing cultural interdependency—to take cognizance of and to interpret theologically the fact that besides their own religion there are many others. In this regard, then, there are not only [analytical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analytical) but also theological statements concerning religious phenomena, particularly in regard to the manner in which such statements are encountered in specific primitive or high religions. Thus, the objects of the history of religions and those of theology cannot be clearly separated. They are merely approached with different categories and [criteria](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criteria). If the history of religions does not surrender its neutrality—since such a surrender would thereby reduce the discipline to anthropology in an ideological sense (e.g., religion understood as mere projection of the psyche or of societal conditions)—theology will recognize the history of religions as a science providing valuable material and as one of the sciences in the universe of sciences.

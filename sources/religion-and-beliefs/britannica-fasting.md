- What is fasting?







Fasting is abstaining from food, drink, or both for a specific period, whether complete or partial, short or lengthy, or intermittent. People fast for various reasons, including health, religious, ritualistic, or ethical purposes.



Medically, fasting has been used as a treatment since the 5th century BCE. Religions worldwide use fasting to prepare people to approach deities, confess sins, or aid in reaching a transcendent state. Physiologically, during fasting, insulin secretion decreases while glucagon secretion increases, leading to the mobilization of stored nutrients to produce glucose.

- Why do people fast?

- What are some different types of fasting?







Fasting is abstaining from food, drink, or both for ethical, health, ritualistic, or religious purposes. Abstention can be complete or partial, short or lengthy, or intermittent.



Different types of fasting include:



  - **Complete Fasting** Some methods only allow for the consumption of water or calorie-free tea and can last over a month.
  - **Modified Fasting** Allows for the intake of 200 to 500 kilocalories per day, usually in the form of bread, vegetable broth, fruit juice, honey, or milk.
  - **Intermittent Fasting** Involves cyclic periods of calorie restriction, such as a 24-hour period of fasting followed by a 24-hour period of regular calorie consumption.

- How does fasting affect the body?







During fasting, the body undergoes several changes to maintain energy and glucose levels. Initially, insulin secretion decreases, and the liver produces glucose from glycogen stores. As fasting continues, the body starts breaking down fat into fatty acids and keto acids, which the brain uses for energy in place of glucose. This process reduces the need for glucose production and helps to conserve muscle tissue. Prolonged fasting leads to a state of starvation characterized by low insulin and high glucagon levels, along with increased free fatty acids and keto acids in the blood.

- What are some risks or concerns about fasting?







While fasting has potential therapeutic uses, it also carries risks. Some studies show that long-term intermittent fasting can promote glucose intolerance and the release of damaging oxidants from tissues.



Eating disorders, such as anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa, are serious health risks that reflect an undue concern with body weight. These disorders can lead to semi-starvation, impaired immunity, anemia, and diminished digestive function. Young athletes who restrict energy intakes to meet weight guidelines may experience negative health consequences, including bone loss.



During fasting, the body undergoes various metabolic changes to maintain glucose levels, including the breakdown of glycogen, production of glucose from amino acids and glycerol, and the conversion of fatty acids into keto acids.

- How is fasting practiced in different religions or cultures?







Fasting is practiced across religions and cultures for various reasons, ranging from health to spiritual purification. In ancient cultures, it prepared individuals to connect with deities. Some Native American tribes fasted before vision quests, while Siberian shamans fasted to gain visions and control spirits.

**fasting**, abstinence from [food](https://www.britannica.com/topic/food) or drink or both for [health](https://www.britannica.com/topic/health), ritualistic, religious, or [ethical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ethical) purposes. The abstention may be complete or partial, lengthy, of short duration, or [intermittent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intermittent). Fasting has been promoted and practiced from antiquity worldwide by physicians, by the founders and followers of many religions, by culturally designated individuals (e.g., hunters or candidates for initiation rites), and by individuals or groups as an expression of protest against what they believe are violations of social, ethical, or political principles.

## Medical fasting

Fasting has been used therapeutically since at least the 5th century bce, when Greek physician [Hippocrates](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hippocrates) recommended abstinence from food or drink for patients who exhibited certain symptoms of illness. Some physicians recognized a fasting instinct, whereby patients in certain disease states naturally experience a loss of [appetite](https://www.britannica.com/science/appetite). Some physicians believed that administering food during such states was unnecessary and possibly even [detrimental](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/detrimental), since fasting was thought to be an important natural part of the recovery process.

An understanding of the physiological effects of fasting began to evolve in the latter part of the 19th century, when some of the first organized studies of fasting were carried out in animals and humans. In the 20th century, as more became known about nutrition and the nutritional requirements of the [human body](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-body), methods of fasting became increasingly sophisticated, and a wide array of approaches emerged. For example, fasting was used as a treatment and as a form of disease prevention, and it was carried out in various settings (e.g., in a hospital or clinic or at home). Some fasting methods, particularly those applied in the treatment of chronic diseases, lasted more than a month, allowed for the [consumption](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consumption) of only water or [calorie](https://www.britannica.com/science/calorie)-free tea, and included exercise and [enemas](https://www.britannica.com/science/enema). Other methods, generally referred to as modified fasting, allowed for the intake of 200 to 500 kilocalories per day (daily calorie needs of adults range from about 1,600 to 3,000 kilocalories, depending on sex, age, and activity level) and sometimes included psychological or spiritual therapy; depending on the particular method used, calories usually were in the form of bread, vegetable broth, fruit juice, honey, or milk. Modified fasting was distinguished from a very low-calorie diet, which allowed up to 800 kilocalories per day and typically was aimed at inducing substantial weight loss. Intermittent fasting involved cyclic periods of calorie restriction, such as a 24-hour period of fasting followed by a 24-hour period of regular calorie consumption.

By the 21st century, although fasting clearly was applicable in some instances of disease, such as in certain [acute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/acute) diseases (particularly when accompanied by a loss of appetite), whether fasting in other instances was [beneficial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/beneficial) to human health remained unclear. For example, whereas research in humans had suggested that intermittent fasting carried out over 15 days improved insulin-mediated glucose uptake into tissues, studies in rodents had indicated that such fasting, carried out over the long-term, promoted glucose intolerance and the release of damaging oxidants from tissues.

## Fasting and religion

In the religions of ancient peoples and civilizations, fasting was a practice to prepare persons, especially priests and priestesses, to approach the deities. In the Hellenistic [mystery religions](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mystery-religion) (e.g., the [healing cult](https://www.britannica.com/topic/healing-cult) of the god [Asclepius](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Asclepius)), the gods were thought to reveal their divine teachings in dreams and visions only after a fast that required the total dedication of the devotees. Among the pre-Columbian peoples of Peru, fasting often was one of the requirements for penance after an individual had confessed sins before a priest. In many [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) the practice was considered a means to [assuage](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/assuage) an angered deity or to aid in resurrecting a deity who was believed to have died (e.g., a god of vegetation).

In the religions of some tribes of [Native Americans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Native-American), fasting was practiced before and during a [vision quest](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vision-quest). Among the [Evenk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Evenk-people) of Siberia, shamans (religious personages thought to have the power to heal and to communicate psychically) often received their initial visions not with a quest but rather after an unexplained illness. After the initial vision, however, they fasted and trained themselves to see further visions and to control spirits. Historically, priestly societies among the [Pueblo Indians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pueblo-Indians) of the American Southwest fasted during retreats before major ceremonies connected with seasonal changes.

Fasting for special purposes or before or during special sacred times remains a characteristic of major religions of the world. In [Jainism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jainism), for example, fasting according to certain prescribed rules and practicing certain types of [meditation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/meditation-mental-exercise) leads to trances that enable individuals to dissociate themselves from the world and reach a [transcendent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/transcendent) state. Some Buddhist monks of the [Theravada](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Theravada) school fast as part of their meditation practices. In [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), Hindu sadhus (holy men) are admired for their frequent personal fasts for various reasons.

Among the Western religions, only [Zoroastrianism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zoroastrianism) prohibits fasting, because of its belief that such a form of [asceticism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/asceticism) will not aid in strengthening the faithful in their struggle against evil. The other Western religions— [Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judaism), [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity), and [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam)—emphasize fasting during certain periods. [Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judaism), which developed many dietary laws and customs, observes several annual fast days, primarily on days of penitence (such as [Yom Kippur](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yom-Kippur), the Day of Atonement) or [mourning](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mourning). Christianity, especially [Roman Catholicism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism) and [Eastern Orthodoxy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eastern-Orthodoxy), has observed a 40-day fast period during [Lent](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lent), a spring period of penitence before [Easter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Easter-holiday), and during [Advent](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Advent), a penitential period before [Christmas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christmas). Among Roman Catholics the observance has been modified since the [Second Vatican Council](https://www.britannica.com/event/Second-Vatican-Council) (1962–65) to allow greater individual choice, with [mandatory](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/mandatory) fasting only on [Ash Wednesday](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ash-Wednesday-Christian-holy-day) and [Good Friday](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Good-Friday) during Lent. Protestant churches generally leave the decision to fast to individual church members. The month of [Ramadan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ramadan) in Islam is a period of penitence and total fasting from dawn to dusk.

## Fasting as a form of protest

In addition to its role in [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion), fasting may be used to express social and political views, particularly as a gesture of protest or solidarity. The classic example of this approach was set by [Mahatma Gandhi](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mahatma-Gandhi), who in the early 20th century conducted a fast in prison to atone for the violent excesses of those of his followers who did not practice his teaching of [satyagraha](https://www.britannica.com/topic/satyagraha-philosophy) (nonviolence) against British rule in India. Gandhi later often fasted in pursuit of similar objectives, including the removal of disabilities [imposed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/imposed) by the government on the [untouchables](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dalit). Fasting has frequently been practiced to protest against war and what are considered social evils and injustices, as in the fasts of the American Black comedian [Dick Gregory](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Dick-Gregory) from the 1960s in protest against the violation of [civil rights](https://www.britannica.com/topic/civil-rights) of American Indians and against U.S. military activity in [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia). In 1981, 10 Irish nationalists died in a Belfast prison during a [hunger strike](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hunger-strike) conducted to urge recognition of themselves and their associates as political prisoners.

### What is Ramadan?

Ramadan is the ninth month of the [Islamic calendar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-calendar) and a holy month of [fasting](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fasting) in [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam). Muslims fast from dawn until dusk, with eating and drinking permitted only before sunrise and after sunset. After sunset, Muslims share an evening meal called _ifṭār_. The observation of Ramadan is stipulated in the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran), and for Muslims it is a period of deep religious significance that emphasizes self-restraint. The holiday concludes at the end of the month with [Eid al-Fitr](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eid-al-Fitr), the “Feast of Fast-Breaking.”

### When is Ramadan?

The start of Ramadan is determined by the sighting of the waxing crescent moon. Some [Muslim](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam) communities use astronomical calculations, while others require visual confirmation of the waxing crescent moon to determine the beginning of the month. Months on the [Islamic calendar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-calendar), including Ramadan, last 29 or 30 days. Because the Islamic calendar year is overall shorter than the [Gregorian calendar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gregorian-calendar) year, Ramadan begins 10–12 days earlier each year, allowing it to fall in every [season](https://www.britannica.com/science/season) throughout a 33-year cycle.

### What are the religious practices during Ramadan?

During Ramadan Muslims [fast](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fasting), engage in introspection, perform [prayers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prayer), and read the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran). Although they fast during the day, Muslims enjoy a meal after sundown, called _ifṭār_, and a predawn meal, called _suḥūr_. [Laylat al-Qadr](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Laylat-al-Qadr), the “Night of Power,” is observed on a date within the final 10 nights of Ramadan to commemorate God’s revelation of the Qurʾān to the Prophet [Muhammad](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Muhammad) through the angel Gabriel ( [Jibrīl](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jibril)). The exact date and observances during Laylat al-Qadr vary among [Shiʿi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shii) and [Sunni](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sunni) Muslims. The month concludes with the festival of [Eid al-Fitr](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eid-al-Fitr), the “Feast of Fast-Breaking.”

### Are there exemptions for fasting during Ramadan?

Although [fasting](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fasting) for Ramadan is one of the mandatory [Five Pillars of Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pillars-of-Islam), there are exceptions or allowances for those unable to perform the fast. Exemptions are permitted for pregnant or nursing women, children, the elderly, the ill, the disabled, travelers, and those with mental health issues. Those who are ill or traveling can make up the fasting time at a later date. If one’s fast is invalidated by eating or drinking at the wrong time, the lost day of fasting can be made up later with an extra day of fasting. For some Muslims, volunteering, performing righteous works, or feeding the poor can also be substituted for fasting if necessary.

**Ramadan**, the ninth month of the [Muslim calendar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-calendar) and the holy month of [fasting](https://www.britannica.com/topic/fasting) in [Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam). During the month of Ramadan Muslims fast from dawn until dusk, and they are permitted to eat and drink only before sunrise and after sunset. Observing the daily fast for Ramadan comprises one of the [Five Pillars of Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pillars-of-Islam). In addition to fasting, the month is a period of introspection, communal [prayer](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prayer) ( _[ṣalāt](https://www.britannica.com/topic/salat)_) in the [mosque](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mosque), and reading of the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran). [God](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Allah) is said to forgive the past sins of those who observe the holy month with fasting, prayer, and faithful intention. The month of difficult fasting and focused religious observance concludes with the jubilant feasting holiday of [Eid al-Fitr](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eid-al-Fitr), “Feast of Fast-Breaking.”

## Timing

Did You Know?

The month of Ramadan begins with the appearance of the waxing crescent moon. In some Muslim communities the waxing crescent moon, and thus the start of Ramadan, is determined by astronomical calculation; in many others, clerics’ visual confirmation of the waxing crescent moon is required to declare the beginning of the month of fasting. In some instances cloud cover can create a delay in the start of the month. Therefore different Muslim communities in different parts of the globe might begin observing Ramadan on slightly different dates.

Ramadan, like all months in the Islamic calendar, begins and ends with the sighting of the slightest waxing crescent moon, just following a new moon. The determination of when the month of Ramadan starts, based on sighting the crescent moon, is made locally within different countries or even by different mosques, thus creating variability of the precise beginning of fasting. Some communities or countries—such as [Turkey](https://www.britannica.com/place/Turkey) (Türkiye)—base the beginning of Ramadan on astronomical calculations, while other communities require leaders to make visual confirmation of the crescent moon’s appearance, in which case cloud cover can create a delay. Islamic months last 29 or 30 days depending on the sighting of the next waxing crescent moon, so Muslims fasting for Ramadan do so for approximately 29 or 30 days. Because the Muslim calendar year is shorter than the [Gregorian calendar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gregorian-calendar) year, Ramadan begins 10–12 days earlier each year, allowing it to fall in every [season](https://www.britannica.com/science/season) throughout a 33-year cycle.

These timing stipulations based on daylight hours necessitate adjustments in special circumstances. Muslims observing Ramadan close to the north and south poles, where the sun never or barely rises in winter and never or barely sets in summer, would fast not at all or incessantly if these rules were followed to the letter. In these instances, Muslims, in consultation with clerics, might opt to follow [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mecca)’s timing or the timing of a nearby major city. A similar special case arises for Muslims in space: before [Malaysian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Malaysia) astronaut [Sheikh Muszaphar Shukor](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sheikh-Muszaphar-Shukor) embarked for the [International Space Station](https://www.britannica.com/topic/International-Space-Station) during Ramadan in October 2007, the Malaysian government assembled 150 clerics and scientists to find a solution to this space-age conundrum. The clerics decreed that a Muslim in orbit during Ramadan could either make up the fasting days after returning to Earth or perform the fast based on the daylight timings at their point of liftoff into space.

## Qurʾānic connections

[![Qurʾān and prayer beads](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/177351-050-EBE89772/Prayer-beads-Quran-Arabic-Islam-text-words.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/177351-050-EBE89772/Prayer-beads-Quran-Arabic-Islam-text-words.jpg)

The observation of Ramadan is stipulated in the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran) in the second [surah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/surah) (Al-Baqarah \[“The Cow”\]) in verses 183–87. [Verse 185](https://sacred-texts.com/isl/pick/002.htm) provides a clear overview of this observance:

> The month of Ramaḍān in which was revealed the Qur’an, a guidance for mankind, and clear proofs of the guidance, and the Criterion (of right and wrong). And whosoever of you is present, let him fast the month, and whosoever of you is sick or on a journey, (let him fast the same) number of other days. Allah desireth for you ease; He desireth not hardship for you; and (He desireth) that ye should complete the period, and that ye should magnify Allah for having guided you, and that peradventure ye may be thankful. —Translated by M.M. Pickthall

Islamic traditions recorded in [hadiths](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hadith)—sayings and traditions of Muhammad—further indicate that it was during Ramadan that there occurred previous divine [revelations](https://www.britannica.com/topic/revelation) to prophets in [Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judaism) and [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity): a scroll to [Abraham](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abraham) (Ibrāhīm), the [Torah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Torah) to [Moses](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Moses-Hebrew-prophet) (Mūsā), and the [Gospel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gospel-New-Testament) (Injīl) to [Jesus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus) (ʿĪsā).

God’s revelation of the Qurʾān to the Prophet Muhammad through the angel Gabriel ( [Jibrīl](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jibril)) is believed to have taken place on one of the final 10 nights of Ramadan in 610 ce, though the exact night is unclear. This moment of revelation in the month of Ramadan is celebrated as [Laylat al-Qadr](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Laylat-al-Qadr), the “Night of Power.” The date of this annual commemoration varies throughout the [Islamic world](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-world) but is most commonly observed on the 23rd night of Ramadan for [Shiʿi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shii) Muslims and on the 27th for [Sunni](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sunni) Muslims. A distinctive custom among Shiʿi Muslims is a ceremony of supplication called Rafʿ al-Maṣā︎ḥif, so called for the moment when worshippers raise a copy of the Qurʾān above their heads. Laylat al-Qadr holds additional significance as a night in which [angels](https://www.britannica.com/topic/angel-religion) are thought to descend to earth with a myriad of tasks, leading to a night of peace, blessings, and divine guidance ( _qadar_) until the dawn. It is thus commemorated with solemnity, devotion, and prayer, and some observers spend the festival in a [mosque](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mosque).

## Religious significance

[![Reading the Qurʾān](https://cdn.britannica.com/37/257937-050-821FBFFE/palestinian-man-reads-the-koran-at-great-omari-mosque-in-gaza-city-during-ramadan-2023.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/37/257937-050-821FBFFE/palestinian-man-reads-the-koran-at-great-omari-mosque-in-gaza-city-during-ramadan-2023.jpg)

[Reading the Qurʾān](https://cdn.britannica.com/37/257937-050-821FBFFE/palestinian-man-reads-the-koran-at-great-omari-mosque-in-gaza-city-during-ramadan-2023.jpg) A Palestinian man reading the Qurʾān at the Great Mosque of Gaza in Gaza city on the first day of the Muslim holy month of Ramadan on March 23, 2023.(more)

Observing the month of fasting has deep religious significance for Muslims. Ramadan is a time for Muslims to practice self-restraint, in keeping with _[ṣawm](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sawm)_ (Arabic: “to refrain”), one of the [pillars of Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pillars-of-Islam). Although _ṣawm_ is most commonly understood as the obligation to fast during Ramadan, it is broadly interpreted as the obligation to refrain between dawn and dusk from food, drink, sexual activity, and all forms of immoral behavior, including impure or unkind thoughts. False words or bad deeds or intentions are deemed as destructive of a fast as is eating or drinking.

The fast at Ramadan is a time for deep consideration of religious matters and focusing on God. As mentioned in the Qurʾān’s introduction of the requirement in 2:183: “Fasting is prescribed for you…so perhaps you will become mindful of God.” Fasting the whole month is said to absolve sins. Alms ( [_zakāt_](https://www.britannica.com/money/zakat-Islamic-tax)) and charity ( _ṣadaqah_) are said to count more during Ramadan. It is a holy time of the year during which it is said that the gates to [hell](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hell) are closed and those to [paradise](https://www.britannica.com/topic/heaven) are opened.

There is more to Ramadan practice than fasting, although that tends to be the focus. Eating a festive evening meal, called _[ifṭār](https://www.britannica.com/topic/iftar)_, after sundown is a central part of the holiday as well. After the sunset prayer, Muslims gather in their homes or mosques to break their fast, often joyfully shared with friends and extended family. The _ifṭār_ usually begins with [dates](https://www.britannica.com/plant/date-palm), as was the custom of Muhammad, or [apricots](https://www.britannica.com/plant/apricot) and water or sweetened milk. Sunni Muslims offer additional prayers at night called the _tarāwīḥ_, which are preferably performed in congregation at the mosque; Shiʿi Muslims, however, generally do not perform the _tarāwīḥ_ prayers. Through their nightly prayers, Muslims may recite the entire Qurʾān over the course of the month of Ramadan.

The last 10 days of the month of Ramadan are considered particularly auspicious, and during this time some Muslims choose to abstain from worldly activity and retire to a mosque to practice intense prayer retreat known as _iʿtikāf_. To accommodate such acts of worship, especially in the evening, work hours are adjusted during the day and sometimes reduced in some Muslim-majority countries. In countries where Muslims are not in the majority, some institutions, such as schools or employers, may be requested to make reasonable accommodations for those observing these intensive religious practices.

Festive lanterns light up the streets and homes at night during Ramadan. Before dawn, another meal called _suḥūr_ is eaten. Muslims in some communities sound drums or ring bells in the predawn hours in order to awaken everyone for this meal before beginning another day of fasting.

Ramadan Greetings

During Ramadan, Muslims greet each other with joyful and hopeful phrases, including:

- **Ramadan mubarak:** “Blessed Ramadan”

- **Ramadan kareem:** “Generous Ramadan”

- **Taqabbal Allah:** “May God accept your prayers and good deeds”


Fasting during Ramadan is not an overly strict requirement, and the Islamic tradition includes various exceptions. Those who are ill or traveling can make up the fasting time later. Upholding restraint through fasting can be invalidated by eating or drinking at the wrong time, but the lost day can be made up later with an extra day of fasting. Volunteering, performing righteous works, or feeding the poor can also be substituted for fasting if necessary. Able-bodied adults and older children fast during the daylight hours from dawn to dusk. Pregnant or nursing women, children, the old, the ill, the disabled, travelers on long journeys, and those with mental health issues are all exempt from the requirement of fasting. As the Qurʾān declares regarding exceptions (2:185): “Allah intends ease for you, not hardship.”

## Breaking the fast

The end of the Ramadan fasting month is merrily celebrated as [Eid al-Fitr](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eid-al-Fitr), the “Feast of Fast-Breaking,” which is one of the two major religious holidays of the Muslim calendar (the other, [Eid al-Adha](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eid-al-Adha), marks the end of the [hajj](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hajj), the [pilgrimage](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pilgrimage-religion) to [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mecca)). In some communities Eid al-Fitr is quite elaborate: children wear new clothes, special pastries are baked, gifts are exchanged, the graves of relatives are visited, and people gather for family meals and to pray in mosques.

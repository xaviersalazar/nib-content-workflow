- What is Islam?







Islam is a major global religion that began with the Prophet Muhammad in 7th century Arabia. _Islām_ means "surrender" in Arabic, highlighting the faith's core idea: believers (Muslims) should submit to Allah's (God's) will. Muslims believe Allah is the sole God, the world's creator, sustainer, and restorer.



The will of Allah is revealed through the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/art/Arabic-literature/The-Qur-an#ref261903), which Muslims believe to be the word of God. [Muhammad](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam/Tales-and-legends-concerning-religious-figures#ref69237) is considered the last in a line of prophets including Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Solomon, and Jesus.



The five primary duties for Muslims are the [Pillars of Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pillars-of-Islam/Introduction#ref1):


\\* Profession of faith ( [_shahādah_](https://www.britannica.com/art/Arabic-literature/The-Qur-an#ref261903))


\\* Prayer ( [_ṣalāt_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pillars-of-Islam/Introduction#ref1))


\\* Alms tax ( [_zakāt_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pillars-of-Islam/Introduction#ref1))


\\* Fasting during Ramadan ( [_ṣawm_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pillars-of-Islam/Introduction#ref1))


\\* Pilgrimage to Mecca ( [_hajj_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam/Prayer#ref69158)).

- Who was Muhammad in Islam?







In Islam, [Muhammad](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Muhammad) is considered the final prophet in a line of prophets that includes Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus. Muslims believe that through Muhammad, [Allah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Allah) revealed his complete message in the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran), which corrects and confirms the teachings of earlier prophets.



The [Prophet's](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Muhammad/Status-in-the-Qur-an-and-in-post-Qur-anic-Islam#ref337916) actions and sayings (Hadith) serve as a guide for Muslims, second only to the Qurʾān. The strict monotheism in Islam does not allow for mythological embellishment; however, legends woven around Prophet Muhammad are sources of inspiration.

- What is the Qur’an and why is it important in Islam?







The [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran/Introduction#ref1) is the holy scripture of Islam, believed by Muslims to be the direct word of God revealed to the Prophet Muhammad through the angel Gabriel between 610 and 632 CE. The word Qurʾān itself means "recitation," highlighting its importance as an oral text.



As the primary source of Islamic law and guidance, the Qurʾān covers a wide range of topics, including moral principles, legal matters, and stories of past prophets. Its central message is the absolute oneness of God, and it instructs believers to live their lives in preparation for the Day of Judgment. The Qurʾān is essential to the daily lives of Muslims, with its verses recited in prayers and studied for guidance.

- What are the Five Pillars of Islam?







The [Five Pillars of Islam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pillars-of-Islam/Introduction#ref1) are the core beliefs and practices of Islam. They are duties that are incumbent on every Muslim.



The Pillars consist of:


\\* **Shahādah:** The Muslim profession of faith: "There is no god but God; Muhammad is the Prophet of God". It must be recited aloud, correctly, and with understanding at least once in a lifetime.


\\* **Salat:** The daily ritual prayer performed in a prescribed manner five times each day.


\\* **Zakat:** An obligatory tax or alms levied to benefit the poor and needy.


\\* **Sawm:** Fasting during the month of Ramadan, abstaining from food and drink from sunrise to sunset.


\\* **Hajj:** The major pilgrimage to Mecca, Saudi Arabia, which every adult Muslim must make at least once in their lifetime if they are financially and physically able.

- Why do Muslims pray five times a day?







The five daily prayers, known as Salat, are a core component of Islam. Observed since the time of Prophet Muhammad, these prayers are performed at dawn (Salat al-fajr), midday (al-dhuhr), afternoon (al-asr), sunset (al-maghrib), and evening (al-isha).

**Islam**, major world [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) [promulgated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/promulgated) by the Prophet [Muhammad](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Muhammad) in [Arabia](https://www.britannica.com/place/history-of-Arabia-31558) in the 7th century ce. The Arabic term _islām_, literally “surrender,” [illuminates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/illuminates) the fundamental religious idea of Islam—that the believer (called a Muslim, from the active particle of _islām_) accepts surrender to the will of [Allah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Allah) (in Arabic, Allāh: God). Allah is viewed as the sole God—creator, sustainer, and restorer of the world. The will of Allah, to which human beings must submit, is made known through the [sacred](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacred) scriptures, the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran) (often spelled Koran in English), which Allah revealed to his messenger, Muhammad. In Islam Muhammad is considered the last of a series of prophets (including [Adam](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adam-and-Eve-biblical-literary-figures), [Noah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Noah), [Abraham](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abraham), [Moses](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Moses-Hebrew-prophet), [Solomon](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Solomon), and [Jesus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus)), and his message simultaneously [consummates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consummates) and completes the “revelations” attributed to earlier prophets.

Retaining its [emphasis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/stress-linguistics) on an uncompromising [monotheism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monotheism) and a strict [adherence](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adherence) to certain essential religious practices, the religion taught by Muhammad to a small group of followers spread rapidly through the [Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/Middle-East) to [Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Africa/Cultural-patterns#ref37203), [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe/Religions#ref34580), the [Indian subcontinent](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indian-subcontinent), the [Malay Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Malay-Peninsula), and [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China/Coinage#ref71752). By the early 21st century there were more than 1.5 billion Muslims worldwide. Although many sectarian movements have arisen within Islam, all Muslims are bound by a common [faith](https://www.britannica.com/topic/faith) and a sense of belonging to a single [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community).

This article deals with the fundamental beliefs and practices of Islam and with the connection of religion and society in the Islamic world. The history of the various peoples who embraced Islam is covered in the article [Islamic world](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-world).

## The foundations of Islam

## The legacy of Muhammad

From the very beginning of Islam, Muhammad had inculcated a sense of brotherhood and a bond of faith among his followers, both of which helped to develop among them a feeling of close relationship that was accentuated by their experiences of persecution as a [nascent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nascent) community in [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mecca). The strong attachment to the tenets of the Qurʾānic [revelation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/revelation) and the [conspicuous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conspicuous) socioeconomic content of Islamic religious practices cemented this bond of faith. In 622 ce, when the Prophet migrated to [Medina](https://www.britannica.com/place/Medina-Saudi-Arabia), his preaching was soon accepted, and the community-state of Islam emerged. During this early period, Islam acquired its characteristic [ethos](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ethos) as a religion uniting in itself both the spiritual and temporal aspects of life and seeking to regulate not only the individual’s relationship to God (through conscience) but human relationships in a social setting as well. Thus, there is not only an Islamic religious institution but also an [Islamic law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sharia), state, and other institutions governing society. Not until the 20th century were the religious (private) and the [secular](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/secular) (public) distinguished by some Muslim thinkers and separated formally in certain places such as [Turkey](https://www.britannica.com/place/Turkey).

This dual religious and social character of Islam, expressing itself in one way as a religious community commissioned by God to bring its own value system to the world through the _jihād_ (“exertion,” commonly translated as “holy war” or “holy struggle,” but in the Qurʾān the term has multiple meanings; _see also [jihad](https://www.britannica.com/topic/jihad)_), explains the astonishing success of the early generations of Muslims. Within a century after the Prophet’s death in 632 ce, they had brought a large part of the globe—from Spain across [Central Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Central-Asia) to India—under a new [Arab](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Arab) Muslim empire.

The period of Islamic conquests and empire building marks the first phase of the expansion of Islam as a religion. Islam’s essential [egalitarianism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/egalitarianism) within the community of the faithful and its official [discrimination](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/discrimination) against the followers of other religions won rapid converts. Jews and Christians were assigned a special status as [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities) possessing scriptures and were called the “people of the Book” ( _[ahl al-kitāb](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ahl-al-Kitab)_) and, therefore, were allowed religious [autonomy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/autonomy). They were, however, required to pay a per capita tax called _[jizyah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/jizya)_, as opposed to pagans, who were required to either accept Islam or die. The same status of the “people of the Book” was later extended in particular times and places to [Zoroastrians](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zoroastrianism) and [Hindus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism), but many “people of the Book” joined Islam in order to escape the disability of the _jizyah_. A much more massive expansion of Islam after the 12th century was inaugurated by the [Sufis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sufism) (Muslim mystics), who were mainly responsible for the spread of Islam in [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), Central Asia, Turkey, and [sub-Saharan Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/sub-Saharan-Africa) ( _see below_).

Beside the jihad and Sufi missionary activity, another factor in the spread of Islam was the far-ranging influence of Muslim traders, who not only introduced Islam quite early to the Indian east coast and South India but also proved to be the main catalytic agents (beside the Sufis) in converting people to Islam in [Indonesia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indonesia), Malaya, and China. Islam was introduced to Indonesia in the 14th century, hardly having time to consolidate itself there politically before the region came under Dutch [hegemony](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hegemony).

The vast variety of races and [cultures](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures) embraced by Islam (an estimated total of more than 1.5 billion persons worldwide in the early 21st century) has produced important internal differences. All segments of Muslim society, however, are bound by a common faith and a sense of belonging to a single community. With the loss of political power during the period of [Western colonialism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Western-colonialism) in the 19th and 20th centuries, the concept of the Islamic community ( _[ummah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ummah)_), instead of weakening, became stronger. The faith of Islam helped various Muslim peoples in their struggle to gain political freedom in the mid-20th century, and the unity of Islam contributed to later political solidarity.

## Sources of Islamic doctrinal and social views

Islamic doctrine, law, and thinking in general are based upon four sources, or fundamental principles ( _[uṣūl](https://www.britannica.com/topic/usul-al-fiqh)_): (1) the Qurʾān, (2) the [Sunnah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sunnah) (“Traditions”), (3) _ijmāʿ_ (“consensus”), and (4) _ijtihād_ (“individual thought”).

[![Qurʾān](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/189973-050-72EA9D2E/Muslim-girl-Quran.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/189973-050-72EA9D2E/Muslim-girl-Quran.jpg)

[Qurʾān](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/189973-050-72EA9D2E/Muslim-girl-Quran.jpg) Muslim girl studying the Qurʾān.(more)

The [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran) (literally, “reading” or “recitation”) is regarded as the [verbatim](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/verbatim) word, or speech, of God delivered to Muhammad by the archangel [Gabriel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gabriel-archangel). Divided into 114 suras (chapters) of unequal length, it is the fundamental source of Islamic teaching. The suras revealed at Mecca during the earliest part of Muhammad’s career are concerned mostly with [ethical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ethical) and spiritual teachings and the Day of Judgment. The suras revealed at Medina at a later period in the career of the Prophet are concerned for the most part with social legislation and the politico-moral principles for [constituting](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituting) and ordering the community.

_[Sunnah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sunnah)_ (“a well-trodden path”) was used by pre-Islamic Arabs to denote their tribal or [common law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/common-law). In Islam it came to mean the example of the Prophet—i.e., his words and deeds as recorded in [compilations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compilations) known as [Hadith](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hadith) (in Arabic, Ḥadīth: literally, “report”; a collection of sayings attributed to the Prophet). Hadith provide the written documentation of the Prophet’s words and deeds. Six of these collections, compiled in the 3rd century ah (9th century ce), came to be regarded as especially [authoritative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/authoritative) by the largest group in Islam, the Sunnis. Another large group, the [Shiʿah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shii), has its own Hadith contained in four [canonical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/canonical) collections.

The doctrine of _[ijmāʿ](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ijma)_, or [consensus](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consensus), was introduced in the 2nd century ah (8th century ce) in order to standardize legal theory and practice and to overcome individual and regional differences of opinion. Though conceived as a “consensus of scholars,” _ijmāʿ_ was in actual practice a more fundamental operative factor. From the 3rd century ah _ijmāʿ_ has amounted to a principle of stability in thinking; points on which consensus was reached in practice were considered closed and further substantial questioning of them prohibited. Accepted interpretations of the Qurʾān and the actual content of the Sunnah (i.e., Hadith and theology) all rest finally on the _ijmāʿ_ in the sense of the acceptance of the authority of their community.

_[Ijtihād](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ijtihad)_, meaning “to endeavor” or “to exert effort,” was required to find the legal or doctrinal solution to a new problem. In the early period of Islam, because _ijtihād_ took the form of individual opinion ( _[raʾy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ray-Islam)_), there was a wealth of conflicting and chaotic opinions. In the 2nd century ah _ijtihād_ was replaced by _[qiyās](https://www.britannica.com/topic/qiyas)_ (reasoning by strict analogy), a formal procedure of deduction based on the texts of the Qurʾān and the Hadith. The transformation of _ijmāʿ_ into a [conservative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conservative) mechanism and the acceptance of a definitive body of Hadith virtually closed the “gate of _ijtihād_” in [Sunni](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sunni) Islam while _ijtihād_ continued in Shiʿism. Nevertheless, certain outstanding Muslim thinkers (e.g., [al-Ghazālī](https://www.britannica.com/biography/al-Ghazali) in the 11th–12th century) continued to claim the right of new _ijtihād_ for themselves, and reformers in the 18th–20th centuries, because of modern influences, caused this principle once more to receive wider acceptance.

# Doctrines of the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran)

## God

The doctrine about God in the Qurʾān is rigorously monotheistic: God is one and unique; he has no partner and no equal. Trinitarianism, the Christian belief that God is three persons in one substance, is vigorously [repudiated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/repudiated). Muslims believe that there are no intermediaries between God and the [creation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/creation-myth) that he brought into being by his sheer command, “Be.” Although his presence is believed to be everywhere, he is not incarnated in anything. He is the sole creator and sustainer of the universe, wherein every creature bears witness to his unity and lordship. But he is also just and merciful: His [justice](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/justice) ensures order in his creation, in which nothing is believed to be out of place, and his mercy is unbounded and [encompasses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompasses) everything. His creating and ordering the universe is viewed as the act of prime mercy for which all things sing his glories. The God of the Qurʾān, described as majestic and [sovereign](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/sovereign), is also a personal God; he is viewed as being nearer to one than one’s own [jugular vein](https://www.britannica.com/science/jugular-vein), and, whenever a person in need or distress calls him, he responds. Above all, he is the God of guidance and shows everything, particularly humanity, the right way, “the straight path.”

This picture of God—wherein the attributes of power, justice, and mercy interpenetrate—is related to the concept of God shared by [Judaism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Judaism) and [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity) and also differs radically from the concepts of pagan Arabia, to which it provided an effective answer. The pagan Arabs believed in a blind and [inexorable](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/inexorable) fate over which humans had no control. For this powerful but insensible fate the Qurʾān substituted a powerful but provident and merciful God. The Qurʾān carried through its uncompromising [monotheism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monotheism) by rejecting all forms of [idolatry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/idolatry) and eliminating all gods and divinities that the Arabs worshipped in their sanctuaries ( _ḥaram_ s), the most prominent of which was the [Kaʿbah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Kaaba-shrine-Mecca-Saudi-Arabia) sanctuary in [Mecca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mecca) itself.

## The universe

In order to prove the unity of God, the Qurʾān lays frequent [stress](https://www.britannica.com/topic/stress-linguistics) on the design and order in the universe. There are no gaps or dislocations in nature. Order is explained by the fact that every created thing is endowed with a definite and defined nature whereby it falls into a pattern. This nature, though it allows every created thing to function in a whole, sets limits, and this idea of the limitedness of everything is one of the most fixed points in both the [cosmology](https://www.britannica.com/science/cosmology-astronomy) and [theology](https://www.britannica.com/topic/theology) of the Qurʾān. The universe is viewed, therefore, as [autonomous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/autonomous), in the sense that everything has its own [inherent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inherent) laws of behavior, but not as autocratic, because the patterns of behavior have been endowed by God and are strictly limited. “Everything has been created by us according to a measure.” Though every creature is thus limited and “measured out” and hence depends upon God, God alone, who reigns unchallenged in the heavens and the earth, is unlimited, independent, and self-sufficient.

## [Humanity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being)

According to the Qurʾān, God created two apparently parallel species of creatures, human beings and _[jinn](https://www.britannica.com/topic/jinni)_, the one from clay and the other from fire. About the _jinn_, however, the Qurʾān says little, although it is implied that the _jinn_ are [endowed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/endowed) with reason and responsibility but are more prone to evil than human beings are. It is with humanity that the Qurʾān, which describes itself as a guide for the [human race](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being), is centrally concerned. The story of the Fall of [Adam](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adam-and-Eve-biblical-literary-figures) (the first man) promoted in Judaism and Christianity is accepted, but the Qurʾān states that God forgave Adam his act of disobedience, which is not viewed in the Qurʾān as [original sin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/original-sin) in the Christian sense of the term.

In the story of the creation of humanity, [Iblīs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Iblis), or [Satan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Satan), who protested to God against the creation of human beings, because they “would [sow](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/sow) mischief on earth,” lost in the competition of knowledge against Adam. The Qurʾān, therefore, declares humanity to be the noblest of all creation, the created being who bore the [trust](https://www.britannica.com/money/trust-law) (of responsibility) that the rest of creation refused to accept. The Qurʾān thus [reiterates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reiterates) that all nature has been made subservient to humans, who are seen as God’s vice-regent on earth; nothing in all creation has been made without a purpose, and humanity itself has not been created “in sport” but rather has been created with the purpose of serving and obeying God’s will.

Despite this lofty station, however, the Qurʾān describes [human nature](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-nature) as frail and faltering. Whereas everything in the universe has a limited nature and every creature recognizes its limitation and insufficiency, human beings are viewed as having been given freedom and therefore are prone to rebelliousness and pride, with the tendency to arrogate to themselves the attributes of self-sufficiency. Pride, thus, is viewed as the cardinal [sin](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sin-religion) of human beings, because, by not recognizing in themselves their essential creaturely [limitations](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/limitations), they become guilty of ascribing to themselves partnership with God ( _[shirk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/shirk)_: associating a creature with the Creator) and of violating the unity of God. True [faith](https://www.britannica.com/topic/faith) ( _īmān_), thus, consists of belief in the immaculate Divine Unity and _islām_ (surrender) in one’s submission to the Divine Will.

## [Satan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/devil), sin, and repentance

In order to communicate the truth of Divine Unity, God has sent messengers or prophets to human beings, whose weakness of nature makes them ever prone to forget or even willfully to reject Divine Unity under the promptings of Satan. According to the Qurʾānic teaching, the being who became Satan ( [Shayṭān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/shaitan) or [Iblīs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Iblis)) had previously occupied a high station but fell from [divine](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacred) grace by his act of disobedience in refusing to honor Adam when he was ordered to do so. Since then his work has been to [beguile](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/beguile) human beings into error and sin. Satan is, therefore, the contemporary of humanity, and Satan’s own act of disobedience is construed by the Qurʾān as the sin of pride. Satan’s machinations will cease only on the Last Day.

Judging from the accounts of the Qurʾān, the record of humanity’s acceptance of the prophets’ messages has been far from perfect. The whole universe is replete with signs of God. The human [soul](https://www.britannica.com/topic/soul-religion-and-philosophy) itself is viewed as a witness of the unity and grace of God. The messengers of God have, throughout history, been calling humanity back to God. Yet not all people have accepted the truth; many of them have rejected it and become disbelievers ( _kāfir_, plural _kuffār_; literally, “concealing”—i.e., the blessings of God), and, when a person becomes so [obdurate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/obdurate), his heart is sealed by God. Nevertheless, it is always possible for a sinner to [repent](https://www.britannica.com/topic/repentance-religion) ( _tawbah_) and redeem himself by a genuine conversion to the truth. There is no point of no return, and God is forever merciful and always willing and ready to pardon. Genuine repentance has the effect of removing all sins and restoring a person to the state of sinlessness with which he started his life.

## Prophecy

Prophets are men specially elected by God to be his messengers. Prophethood is indivisible, and the Qurʾān requires recognition of all prophets as such without [discrimination](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/discrimination). Yet they are not all equal, some of them being particularly outstanding in qualities of steadfastness and patience under trial. [Abraham](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abraham), [Noah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Noah), [Moses](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Moses-Hebrew-prophet), and [Jesus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus) were such great prophets. As [vindication](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vindication) of the truth of their mission, God often vests them with [miracles](https://www.britannica.com/topic/miracle): Abraham was saved from fire, Noah from the [Deluge](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Deluge), and Moses from the pharaoh. Not only was Jesus born from the [Virgin Mary](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mary-mother-of-Jesus), but God also saved him from crucifixion at the hands of the Jews. The [conviction](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conviction) that God’s messengers are ultimately [vindicated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vindicated) and saved is an [integral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integral) part of the Qurʾānic doctrine.

All prophets are human and never part of divinity: They are the most perfect of humans who are recipients of [revelation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/revelation) from God. When God wishes to speak to a human, he sends an angel messenger to him or makes him hear a voice or inspires him. Muhammad is accepted as the last prophet in this series and its greatest member, for in him all the messages of earlier prophets were [consummated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consummated). The archangel [Gabriel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Gabriel-archangel) brought the Qurʾān down to the Prophet’s “heart.” Gabriel is represented by the Qurʾān as a spirit whom the Prophet could sometimes see and hear. According to early traditions, the Prophet’s revelations occurred in a state of trance when his normal [consciousness](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consciousness) was transformed. This state was accompanied by heavy sweating. The Qurʾān itself makes it clear that the revelations brought with them a sense of extraordinary weight: “If we were to send this Qurʾān down on a mountain, you would see it split asunder out of fear of God.”

This phenomenon at the same time was accompanied by an unshakable conviction that the message was from God, and the Qurʾān describes itself as the transcript of a heavenly “Mother Book” written on a “Preserved Tablet.” The conviction was of such an intensity that the Qurʾān categorically [denies](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/denies) that it is from any earthly source, for in that case it would be liable to “manifold doubts and oscillations.”

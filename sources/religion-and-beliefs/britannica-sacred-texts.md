**scripture**, the revered texts, or Holy Writ, of the world’s [religions](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion). Scriptures [comprise](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprise) a large part of the literature of the world. They vary greatly in form, volume, age, and degree of sacredness, but their common attribute is that their [words](https://www.britannica.com/topic/word) are regarded by the devout as [sacred](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacred). Sacred words differ from ordinary words in that they are believed either to possess and convey spiritual and magical powers or to be the means through which a divine being or other sacred reality is revealed in phrases and sentences full of power and truth.

## Characteristics

Most sacred scriptures were originally [oral](https://www.britannica.com/art/oral-literature) and were passed down through memorization from generation to generation until they were finally committed to writing. A few are still preserved orally, such as the hymns of [Native Americans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Native-American). Many bear the unmistakable marks of their oral origin and can best be understood when recited aloud; in fact, it is still held by many [Hindus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism) and [Buddhists](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism) that their scriptures lack, when read silently, the meaning and significance they have when recited aloud, for the human voice is believed to add to the recited texts dimensions of truth and power not readily grasped by the solitary reader.

Not all scriptures, however, were originally oral, nor were they in all parts directly effectual in rituals that sought the granting of magical and spiritual powers. The greater part of recorded scripture has either a narrative or an expository character. The types of sacred and semisacred texts are, in fact, many and varied. Besides magical [runes](https://www.britannica.com/topic/rune) (ancient Germanic alphabet characters) and spells from primitive and ancient sources, they include [hymns](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hymn), [prayers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/prayer), chants, [myths](https://www.britannica.com/topic/myth), stories about gods and heroes, [epics](https://www.britannica.com/art/epic), [fables](https://www.britannica.com/art/fable), sacred laws, directions for the conduct of [rituals](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ritual), the original teachings of major religious figures, expositions of these teachings, [moral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/moral) [anecdotes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/anecdotes), [dialogues](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dialogues) of seers and sages, and philosophical discussions. In fact, scriptures include every form of literature capable of expressing religious feeling or [conviction](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conviction).

Types of sacred literature vary in authority and degree of sacredness. The centrally important and most holy of the sacred texts have in many instances been gathered into [canons](https://www.britannica.com/topic/canon-sacred-literature) (standard works of the faith), which, after being determined either by general agreement or by official religious bodies, become fixed—i.e., limited to certain works that are alone viewed as fully [authoritative](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/authoritative) and truly beyond all further change or alteration. The works not admitted to the canons (those of a semisacred or semicanonical character) may still be quite valuable as supplementary texts.

## Scriptures in non-Western religions

A striking instance of making a distinction between [canonical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/canonical) and semicanonical scriptures occurs in [Hinduism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism). The Hindu sacred literature is voluminous and varied; it contains ancient elements and every type of religious literature that has been listed, except historical details on the lives of the seers and sages who produced it. Its earliest portions, namely the four ancient [Vedas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Veda) (hymns), seem to have been provided by Indo-European families in northwestern [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India) in the 2nd millennium bce. These and the supplements to them composed after 1000 bce—the [Brahmanas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Brahmana) (commentaries and instruction in ritual), the [Aranyakas](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Aranyaka) (forest books of ascetics), and the [Upanishads](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Upanishad) (philosophical treatises)—are considered more sacred than any later writings. They are collectively referred to as [Shruti](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shruti) (“Heard”; i.e., communicated by revelation), whereas the later writings are labeled [Smriti](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Smriti) (“Remembered”; i.e., recollected and reinterpreted at some distance in time from the original revelations). The former are canonical and completed, not to be added to nor altered, but the latter are semicanonical and semisacred.

[Buddhist](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism) sacred literature recollects [Gautama Buddha](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Buddha-founder-of-Buddhism)’s life and teaching in the 6th century bce and first appeared in the [dialect](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dialect) called [Pali](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pali-language), allied to the Magadhi that he spoke. As time passed and his movement spread beyond India, [Buddhism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism) adopted as its medium [Sanskrit](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sanskrit-language), the Indian classical language that was widely used in ancient Asia. A distinction arose between the [Theravada](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Theravada) (“Way of the Elders”), preserved in Pali and regarded as canonical, and the vast number of works written in Sanskrit within the more widely dispersed Buddhism called by its adherents [Mahayana](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mahayana) (“Greater Vehicle”). The Mahayana works were later translated and further expanded in Tibetan, Chinese, and Japanese.

Whether the basic texts of [indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/indigenous) Chinese [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) should be called sacred, in the sense of Holy Writ, is open to question. Neither classical [Daoism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Taoism) nor [Confucianism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Confucianism) can be said to have been based on revelation; the texts of these faiths were originally viewed as human wisdom, books written by humans for humans. They acquired authority, actually a canonical status, however, that caused them to be regarded with profound reverence and thus, in effect, as sacred. This certainly was true of the revered Daoist book, the [_Daodejing_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tao-te-Ching) (“Classic of the Way of Power”), and of the [_Wujing_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Wujing) (“Five Classics”) and the _Sishu_ (“Four Books”) of Confucianism.

# Scriptures in Western religions

[![Jesus](https://cdn.britannica.com/04/60104-050-42423C4A/Lord-Christ-All-abbreviation-letters-Iesus-Christus.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/04/60104-050-42423C4A/Lord-Christ-All-abbreviation-letters-Iesus-Christus.jpg)

[Jesus](https://cdn.britannica.com/04/60104-050-42423C4A/Lord-Christ-All-abbreviation-letters-Iesus-Christus.jpg) Jesus enthroned as Lord of All (Pantocrator), with the explaining letters IC XC, symbolic abbreviation of Iesus Christus; 12th-century mosaic in the Palatine Chapel, Palermo, Sicily.(more)

The most precisely fixed [canons](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/canons) are those that have been defined by official religious bodies. The [Jewish](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-Bible) canon, known to Christians as the [Old Testament](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Old-Testament), was fixed by a synod of rabbis held at Yavneh, Palestine, about 90 ce. The semisacred books that were excluded were labeled by Christians the [Apocrypha](https://www.britannica.com/topic/apocrypha) (Greek: “Hidden Away”). [Roman Catholicism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism) later included them in its canon. [Jesus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jesus), the founder of [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity), left nothing in writing, but he so inspired his followers that they preserved his sayings and biographical details about him in oral form until they were written down in the four Gospels. To these were added the letters of [St. Paul](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saint-Paul-the-Apostle) and others and the [Book of Revelation to John](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Revelation-to-John), the whole forming a [sacred](https://www.britannica.com/topic/sacred) canon called the [New Testament](https://www.britannica.com/topic/New-Testament), which was ecclesiastically sanctioned by the end of the 4th century. There was also a [New Testament Apocrypha](https://www.britannica.com/topic/New-Testament-Apocrypha), but it did not achieve [canonical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/canonical) status because of numerous spurious details.

[![Qurʾān](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/213358-050-6420302C/Quran-illuminated-manuscript-18th-century-early-19th-century-Metropolitan-Museum-Art.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/213358-050-6420302C/Quran-illuminated-manuscript-18th-century-early-19th-century-Metropolitan-Museum-Art.jpg)

[Qurʾān](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/213358-050-6420302C/Quran-illuminated-manuscript-18th-century-early-19th-century-Metropolitan-Museum-Art.jpg) Qurʾān with illuminated manuscript pages featuring ink, gold, and lapis, late 18th–early 19th century.(more)

Where no religious body has provided sanction or authorization, scriptures have had to stand on their own authority. [Muslims](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam) believe that the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran) does this easily. The Qurʾān, their only sacred canon or standard of [faith](https://www.britannica.com/topic/faith), authenticates itself, they believe, by its internal self-evidencing power, for it is composed of the very words of God communicated to [Muhammad](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Muhammad) and recited by him without addition or subtraction. This faith of Muslims in the Qurʾān is similar to that of fundamentalist Christians who believe that the [Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible), as God’s word, is verbally inspired from beginning to end.

## Other religious or devotional literature

There exists a large body of literature that possesses less of the [aura](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/aura) of true scripture than the works just noted. They are interpretations about divine truth and divine commands, or stories that illustrate how persons, exalted or lowly, have acted (with or without awareness) in response to a divine stimulus. They are, in effect, supportive of true scripture.

An outstanding instance is the [Talmud](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Talmud), a compendium of law, lore, and commentary that to many Jews has very nearly the authority of the Mosaic [Torah](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Torah) (the Law, or the Pentateuch). Indeed, in the postbiblical rabbinical writings it was generally considered a second Torah, complementing the Written Law of Moses. Another instance is provided by the Christian church. Its major [creeds](https://www.britannica.com/topic/creed) have, at one time or another, been regarded as [infallible](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/infallible) statements, to depart from which would be heresy. This is particularly true of the [Apostles’ Creed](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Apostles-Creed) and the three “ecumenical creeds” of Nicaea (325), Constantinople (381), and Chalcedon (451). Roman Catholics add to these the papal decrees summarizing in credal form the conclusions of the councils of the Roman Catholic Church concerning the sacraments, [transubstantiation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/transubstantiation) (the changing of the substance of the bread and wine in the mass into the body and blood of Christ), confession, the [Immaculate Conception](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Immaculate-Conception-Roman-Catholicism) of the [Virgin Mary](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mary-mother-of-Jesus), [papal infallibility](https://www.britannica.com/topic/papal-infallibility), and the [Assumption](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Assumption-Christianity) of the body and [soul](https://www.britannica.com/topic/soul-religion-and-philosophy) of the Virgin Mary to [heaven](https://www.britannica.com/topic/heaven). More or less binding for [Protestants](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Protestantism) are their distinctive statements of faith: the [Augsburg Confession](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Augsburg-Confession) of 1530 ( [Lutheran](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lutheranism)), the [Heidelberg Catechism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Heidelberg-Catechism) of 1563 ( [Reformed](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Presbyterian-churches)), the [Westminster Confession](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Westminster-Confession) of 1646 and [Shorter Westminster Catechism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shorter-Westminster-Catechism) of 1647 (Presbyterian), and others.

[![Joseph Smith and Moroni](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/176957-050-0875427E/Moroni-Book-of-Mormon-Joseph-Smith-lithograph-1886.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/176957-050-0875427E/Moroni-Book-of-Mormon-Joseph-Smith-lithograph-1886.jpg)

[Joseph Smith and Moroni](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/176957-050-0875427E/Moroni-Book-of-Mormon-Joseph-Smith-lithograph-1886.jpg) Moroni delivering the _Book of Mormon_ to Joseph Smith, lithograph, 1886.(more)

During the last seven centuries in the West, some religious writings have [attained](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/attained) a semisacred, if not fully sacred, status: [_Imitatio Christi_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Imitation-of-Christ-devotional-book) of [Thomas à Kempis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thomas-a-Kempis) (1379/80–1471); [John Bunyan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Bunyan)’s (1628–88) [_The Pilgrim’s Progress_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Pilgrims-Progress); [Mary Baker Eddy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Mary-Baker-Eddy)’s (1821–1910) _Science and Health with a Key to the Scriptures_; and the reputed discovery of [Joseph Smith](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Smith-American-religious-leader-1805-1844) (1805–44), the [_Book of Mormon_](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Book-of-Mormon).

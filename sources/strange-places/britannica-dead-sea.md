### Where is the Dead Sea located?

The Dead Sea is a landlocked salt lake between Israel and Jordan in southwestern Asia.

### Is there any life in the Dead Sea?

The Dead Sea’s extreme salinity excludes all forms of life except bacteria. Fish carried in by the Jordan or by smaller streams when in flood die quickly. Apart from the vegetation along the rivers, plant life along the shores is discontinuous and consists mainly of halophytes (plants that grow in salty or alkaline soil).

### Where does the Dead Sea get its water from?

The Dead Sea receives nearly all its water from the [Jordan River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jordan-River), which flows from the north into the lake.

### Why are bathers so buoyant in the Dead Sea?

The waters of the Dead Sea are extremely saline (salty). This saline water has a high density that keeps bathers buoyant.

### At what elevation is the Dead Sea?

The Dead Sea is the lowest body of water on the surface of Earth. In the mid-20th century the surface level of the lake was some 1,300 feet (400 metres) below sea level. Human activities have led to a precipitous drop in the water level, and by the mid-2010s the lake level had reached about 1,410 feet (430 metres) below sea level.

**Dead Sea**, landlocked [salt](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt) [lake](https://www.britannica.com/science/lake) between [Israel](https://www.britannica.com/place/Israel) and [Jordan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jordan) in southwestern [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia). Its eastern shore belongs to Jordan, and the southern half of its western shore belongs to Israel. The northern half of the western shore lies within the Palestinian [West Bank](https://www.britannica.com/place/West-Bank) and has been under Israeli occupation since the 1967 [Arab-Israeli war](https://www.britannica.com/event/Arab-Israeli-wars). The [Jordan River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jordan-River), from which the Dead Sea receives nearly all its [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water), flows from the north into the lake.

[Dead Sea](https://cdn.britannica.com/66/89866-050-48693C5A/Dead-Sea.jpg)(more)

The Dead Sea has the lowest elevation and is the lowest body of water on the surface of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth). For several decades in the mid-20th century, the standard [value](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/value) given for the surface level of the lake was some 1,300 feet (400 metres) below [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level). Beginning in the 1960s, however, Israel and Jordan began diverting much of the Jordan River’s flow and increased the use of the lake’s water itself for commercial purposes. The result of those activities was a precipitous drop in the Dead Sea’s water level. By the mid-2010s, measurement of the lake level was more than 100 feet (some 30 metres) below the mid-20th-century figure—i.e., about 1,410 feet (430 metres) below sea level—but the lake continued to drop by about 3 feet (1 metre) annually.

## Physiography and geology

[![Dead Sea shoreline](https://cdn.britannica.com/28/179428-050-A62346C9/Shoreline-Dead-Sea-Israel.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/28/179428-050-A62346C9/Shoreline-Dead-Sea-Israel.jpg)

[Dead Sea shoreline](https://cdn.britannica.com/28/179428-050-A62346C9/Shoreline-Dead-Sea-Israel.jpg) Shoreline of the Dead Sea, eastern Israel.(more)

The Dead Sea is situated between the hills of [Judaea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Judaea) to the west and the Transjordanian plateaus to the east. Before the water level began dropping, the lake was some 50 miles (80 km) long, attained a maximum width of 11 miles (18 km), and had a surface area of about 394 square miles (1,020 square km). The peninsula of [Al-Lisān](https://www.britannica.com/place/Al-Lisan) (Arabic: “The Tongue”) divided the lake on its eastern side into two unequal basins: the northern basin [encompassed](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompassed) about three-fourths of the lake’s total surface area and reached a depth of 1,300 feet (400 metres), and the southern basin was smaller and considerably shallower, less than 10 feet (3 metres) deep on average. During biblical times and until the 8th century ce, only the area around the northern basin was inhabited, and the lake was slightly lower than its present-day level. It rose to its highest level, 1,275 feet (389 metres) below sea level, in 1896 but receded again after 1935, stabilizing at about 1,300 feet (400 metres) below sea level for several decades.

[![Dead Sea](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/187789-050-69A83C9B/Satellite-images-Dead-Sea-water-levels-2011.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/187789-050-69A83C9B/Satellite-images-Dead-Sea-water-levels-2011.jpg)

[Dead Sea](https://cdn.britannica.com/89/187789-050-69A83C9B/Satellite-images-Dead-Sea-water-levels-2011.jpg) Satellite images of the Dead Sea showing its progressively lower water levels: 1972 (left), 1989 (centre), 2011 (right).(more)

The drop in the lake level in the late 20th and early 21st centuries changed the physical appearance of the Dead Sea. Most noticeably, the peninsula of Al-Lisān gradually extended eastward, until the lake’s northern and southern [basins](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/basins) became separated by a strip of dry land. In addition, the southern basin was eventually subdivided into dozens of large evaporation pools (for the extraction of salt), so by the 21st century it had essentially ceased to be a natural body of water. The northern basin—effectively now the actual Dead Sea—largely retained its overall dimensions despite its great loss of water, mainly because its shoreline plunged downward so steeply from the surrounding landscape.

The Dead Sea region occupies part of a [graben](https://www.britannica.com/science/horst) (a downfaulted block of Earth’s crust) between transform faults along a tectonic plate boundary that runs northward from the [Red Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Red-Sea)– [Gulf of Suez](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gulf-of-Suez) [spreading centre](https://www.britannica.com/science/spreading-center) to a convergent plate boundary in the [Taurus Mountains](https://www.britannica.com/place/Taurus-Mountains) of southern [Turkey](https://www.britannica.com/place/Turkey). The eastern fault, along the edge of the [Moab Plateau](https://www.britannica.com/place/Moab-Plateau), is more readily visible from the lake than is the western fault, which marks the gentler Judaean upfold.

In the [Jurassic](https://www.britannica.com/science/Jurassic-Period) and [Cretaceous](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period) periods (about 201 million to 66 million years ago), before the creation of the graben, an extended [Mediterranean Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea) covered [Syria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Syria) and [Palestine](https://www.britannica.com/place/Palestine). During the [Miocene Epoch](https://www.britannica.com/science/Miocene-Epoch) (23 million to 5.3 million years ago), as the Arabian Plate collided with the Eurasian Plate to the north, upheaval of the seabed produced the upfolded structures of the Transjordanian highlands and the central range of Palestine, causing the fractures that allowed the Dead Sea graben to drop. At that time the Dead Sea was probably about the size that it is today. During the [Pleistocene Epoch](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pleistocene-Epoch) (2,588,000 to 11,700 years ago), it rose to an elevation of about 700 feet (200 metres) above its modern level, forming a [vast](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/vast) inland sea that stretched some 200 miles (320 km) from the [H̱ula Valley](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hula-Valley) area in the north to 40 miles (64 km) beyond its present southern limits. The Dead Sea did not spill over into the [Gulf of Aqaba](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gulf-of-Aqaba) because it was blocked by a 100-foot (30-metre) rise in the highest part of [Wadi Al-ʿArabah](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wadi-Al-Arabah), a seasonal watercourse that flows in a valley to the east of the central [Negev](https://www.britannica.com/place/Negev) highlands.

Beginning about 2.5 million years ago, heavy streamflow into the lake deposited thick sediments of [shale](https://www.britannica.com/science/shale), [clay](https://www.britannica.com/science/clay-geology), [sandstone](https://www.britannica.com/science/sandstone), [rock salt](https://www.britannica.com/science/halite), and [gypsum](https://www.britannica.com/science/gypsum). Later, strata of clay, [marl](https://www.britannica.com/science/marl-mineral), soft chalk, and gypsum were dropped onto layers of [sand](https://www.britannica.com/science/sand) and [gravel](https://www.britannica.com/science/gravel). Because the water in the lake evaporated faster than it was [replenished](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/replenished) by [precipitation](https://www.britannica.com/science/precipitation) during the past 10,000 years, the lake gradually shrank to its present form. In so doing, it exposed deposits that now cover the Dead Sea valley to thicknesses of between about 1 and 4 miles (1.6 and 6.4 km).

The [Al-Lisān](https://www.britannica.com/place/Al-Lisan) region and [Mount Sedom](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Sedom) (historically Mount Sodom) resulted from movements of Earth’s crust. Mount Sedom’s steep cliffs rise up from the southwestern shore. Al-Lisān is formed of strata of clay, marl, soft chalk, and gypsum interbedded with sand and gravel. Both Al-Lisān and beds made of similar material on the western side of the Dead Sea valley dip to the east. It is assumed that the [uplifting](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/uplifting) of Mount Sedom and Al-Lisān formed a southern escarpment for the Dead Sea. Later the sea broke through the western half of that escarpment to flood what is now the shallow southern remnant of the Dead Sea.

Another consequence resulting from the Dead Sea’s lower water level has been the appearance of [sinkholes](https://www.britannica.com/science/sinkhole), especially in the southwestern part of the region. As the water in the lake dropped, it became possible for [groundwater](https://www.britannica.com/science/groundwater) to rise up and dissolve large subterranean caverns in the overlying salt layer until the surface finally collapses. Several hundred sinkholes have formed, some of them in areas popular with tourists.

# Climate and hydrology

The Dead Sea lies in a [desert](https://www.britannica.com/science/desert). Rainfall is scanty and irregular. Al-Lisān averages about 2.5 inches (65 mm) of rain a year, the industrial site of [Sedom](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sedom) (near historical [Sodom](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sodom-and-Gomorrah)) only about 2 inches (50 mm). Because of the [lake’s](https://www.britannica.com/science/lake) extremely low elevation and sheltered location, winter temperatures are mild, averaging 63 °F (17 °C) in January at the southern end at Sedom and 58 °F (14 °C) at the northern end; freezing temperatures do not occur. Summer is oppressively hot, averaging 93 °F (34 °C) in August at Sedom, with a recorded maximum of 124 °F (51 °C). Evaporation of the lake’s waters—estimated at about 55 inches (1,400 mm) per year—often creates a thick mist above the lake. On the rivers the atmospheric [humidity](https://www.britannica.com/science/humidity) varies from 45 percent in May to 62 percent in October. Lake and land breezes, which are relatively common, blow off the lake in all directions in the daytime and then reverse direction to blow toward the centre of the lake at night.

The inflow from the [Jordan River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jordan-River), whose high waters occur in winter and spring, once averaged some 45.5 billion cubic feet (1.3 billion cubic metres) per year. However, the [subsequent](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/subsequent) diversions of the [Jordan’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jordan) waters reduced the river’s flow to a small fraction of the previous amount and became the principal cause for the drop in the Dead Sea’s [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) level. Four modest streams descend to the lake from Jordan to the east through deep gorges: the wadis (intermittent streams) Al-ʿUẓaymī, Zarqāʾ Māʿīn, Al-Mawjib, and Al-Ḥasā. Down numerous other wadis, streams flow spasmodically and briefly from the neighbouring heights as well as from the depression of [Wadi Al-ʿArabah](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wadi-Al-Arabah). Thermal sulfur springs also feed the rivers. Evaporation in [summer](https://www.britannica.com/science/salinity) and the inflow of water, especially in winter and spring, once caused noticeable seasonal variations of 12 to 24 inches (30 to 60 cm) in the level of the lake, but those fluctuations have been overshadowed by the more-dramatic annual drops in the Dead Sea’s surface level.

## Salinity

[![Dead Sea](https://cdn.britannica.com/70/58670-050-82229E20/Columns-salt-waters-Dead-Sea-environment-life-forms.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/70/58670-050-82229E20/Columns-salt-waters-Dead-Sea-environment-life-forms.jpg)

[Dead Sea](https://cdn.britannica.com/70/58670-050-82229E20/Columns-salt-waters-Dead-Sea-environment-life-forms.jpg) Columns of salt rising from the extremely saline waters of the Dead Sea.(more)

The waters of the Dead Sea are extremely [saline](https://www.britannica.com/science/salinity), and, generally, the concentration of [salt](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt) increases toward the lake’s bottom. That phenomenon can create two different masses of water in the lake for extended periods of time. Such a situation existed for some three centuries, lasting until the late 1970s. Down to a depth of about 130 feet (40 metres), the [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature) varied from 66 to 98 °F (19 to 37 °C), the [salinity](https://www.britannica.com/science/salinity) was slightly less than 300 parts per thousand, and the water was especially rich in [sulfates](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfate-mineral) and bicarbonates. Beneath a zone of [transition](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/transition) located at depths between 130 and 330 feet (40 and 100 metres), the water had a uniform temperature of about 72 °F (22 °C) and a higher degree of salinity (approximately 332 parts per thousand); it contained [hydrogen sulfide](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen-sulfide) and strong concentrations of [magnesium](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnesium), [potassium](https://www.britannica.com/science/potassium), [chlorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/chlorine), and [bromine](https://www.britannica.com/science/bromine). The deep water was saturated with sodium chloride, which precipitated to the bottom. The deep water thus became fossilized (i.e., because it was highly salty and dense, it remained permanently on the bottom).

The dramatic reduction in inflow from the Jordan River that began in the 1960s gradually increased the salinity of the upper-layer waters of the Dead Sea. By the late 1970s that [water mass](https://www.britannica.com/science/water-mass) had become more saline (and denser) than the lower layers, but, because it remained warmer than the layers beneath it, it did not [sink](https://www.britannica.com/science/sinkhole). By the winter of 1978–79, however, the upper-level layer had become cool and saturated enough to sink, setting off an event known as an [overturn](https://www.britannica.com/science/circulation-fluid-flow) (a mixing of the water layers). Since then the [trend](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/trend) has been toward restoring the formerly stratified water layers, but with more instances of overturning.

The saline water has a high [density](https://www.britannica.com/science/density) that keeps bathers buoyant. The fresh water of the Jordan stays on the surface, and in the spring its muddy colour can be traced as it spreads southward from the point where the river empties into the Dead Sea. The lake’s extreme salinity excludes all forms of life except bacteria. Fish carried in by the Jordan or by smaller streams when in flood die quickly. Apart from the vegetation along the rivers, plant life along the shores is discontinuous and consists mainly of halophytes (plants that grow in [salty](https://www.britannica.com/science/salt) or [alkaline](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/alkaline) soil).

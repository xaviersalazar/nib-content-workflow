**café**, small eating and drinking establishment, historically a [coffeehouse](https://www.britannica.com/topic/coffee), usually featuring a limited menu; originally these establishments served only [coffee](https://www.britannica.com/topic/coffee). The English term _café_, borrowed from the French, [derives](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/derives) ultimately from the Turkish _kahve_, meaning coffee.

The introduction of coffee and coffee drinking to [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) provided a much-needed focus for the social activities of the sober. The first café is said to have opened in 1550 in Constantinople; during the 17th century cafés opened in [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy), France, [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany), and [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England).

During the 200 years after the mid-17th century, the most famous coffeehouses of Europe flourished in [London](https://www.britannica.com/place/London) as ready points for news, discussion, and faction. Coffeehouse proprietors competed with each other for supplies of both [Whig and Tory](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Whig-Party-England) newspapers; during this time the business of buying and selling [insurance](https://www.britannica.com/money/insurance), ships, stocks, commodities, and occasionally even slaves was disposed of in coffeehouses; a man of letters, an actor, or an artist might perform or declaim for his coterie in his favourite coffeehouse; and coffeehouses became informal stations for the collection and distribution of packets and letters. By the 19th century, the daily newspaper and the home post had displaced these functions.

### What is coffee?

Coffee is a beverage brewed from the [roasted](https://www.britannica.com/topic/coffee-roasting) and ground seeds of the tropical evergreen coffee plant. Coffee is one of the three most popular beverages in the world (alongside [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) and [tea](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tea-beverage)), and it is one of the most profitable international commodities.

### Where is coffee grown?

[Latin America](https://www.britannica.com/place/Latin-America), eastern [Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Africa), [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia), and [Arabia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arabia-peninsula-Asia) are leading producers of Arabica coffee. The Arabica bean requires a cool subtropical climate. It grows at higher elevations and requires a lot of moisture, sun, and shade. Western and Central Africa, Southeast Asia, and [Brazil](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brazil) are major producers of Robusta coffee. The Robusta bean can grow at lower elevations.

### Where did coffee originate?

Wild coffee plants, probably from Kefa (Kaffa), [Ethiopia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ethiopia), were taken to southern [Arabia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arabia-peninsula-Asia) and placed under cultivation in the 15th century. The popularity of coffee in the Arab world led to the creation of the [coffeehouse](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cafe-eating-and-drinking-establishment), first in Mecca and then in Constantinople in the 15th and 16th centuries, respectively. Coffee was introduced into one European country after another throughout the 16th and 17th centuries.

### How is coffee decaffeinated?

The main methods of [decaffeination](https://www.britannica.com/topic/decaffeination) are based on chemical [solvents](https://www.britannica.com/science/solvent-chemistry), [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element) filtering, [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide) extraction, or [triglycerides](https://www.britannica.com/science/triglyceride). In all cases, to make “decaf,” the [caffeine](https://www.britannica.com/science/caffeine) is removed in the green bean stage, before the coffee is roasted. Regardless of the method, coffee is never 100% caffeine-free.

### Why does coffee make you poop?

The acidity of coffee causes a person’s [stomach](https://www.britannica.com/science/stomach) to secrete a heightened level of gastric acid. Coffee contains chlorogenic acid, which causes the stomach to dump its content more quickly into the [intestines](https://www.britannica.com/science/intestine). Coffee also increases the levels of [gastrin](https://www.britannica.com/science/gastrin) in the body. Gastrin activates [peristalsis](https://www.britannica.com/science/peristalsis), which pushes waste toward the [rectum](https://www.britannica.com/science/rectum). **coffee**, beverage brewed from the roasted and ground seeds of the tropical [evergreen](https://www.britannica.com/plant/evergreen-plant) coffee plants of African origin. Coffee is one of the three most popular beverages in the world (alongside [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) and [tea](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tea-beverage)) and one of the most profitable international commodities. Though coffee is the basis for an endless array of beverages, including [espresso](https://www.britannica.com/topic/espresso), [cappuccinos](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cappuccino), mochas, and lattes, its popularity is mainly attributed to its invigorating effect, which is produced by [caffeine](https://www.britannica.com/science/caffeine), an [alkaloid](https://www.britannica.com/science/alkaloid) present in coffee.

[![Cappuccino](https://cdn.britannica.com/17/234017-050-F665E64D/cappuccino-Rome-Italy.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/17/234017-050-F665E64D/cappuccino-Rome-Italy.jpg)

[Cappuccino](https://cdn.britannica.com/17/234017-050-F665E64D/cappuccino-Rome-Italy.jpg) A cappuccino served in a café in Rome, Italy.(more)

Two species of coffee plants, _[Coffea arabica](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Coffea-arabica)_ and _C. canephora,_ supply almost all of the world’s [consumption](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consumption). Arabica is considered a milder and more flavourful and aromatic brew than Robusta, the main variety of _C. canephora_. The flatter and more elongated Arabica bean is more widespread than Robusta but more delicate and [vulnerable](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vulnerable) to [pests](https://www.britannica.com/science/pest-vermin), requiring a cool subtropical climate; Arabica must grow at higher elevations (2,000–6,500 feet \[600–2,000 metres\]), it needs a lot of moisture, and it has fairly specific shade requirements. [Latin America](https://www.britannica.com/place/Latin-America), [eastern Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/eastern-Africa), [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia), and [Arabia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arabia-peninsula-Asia) are leading producers of Arabica coffee. The rounder, more convex Robusta bean, as its name suggests, is hardier and can grow at lower altitudes (from [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level) to 2,000 feet). Robusta coffee is cheaper to produce, has twice the caffeine content of Arabica, and is typically the bean of choice for inexpensive commercial coffee brands. [Western](https://www.britannica.com/place/western-Africa) and [Central Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/central-Africa), [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia), and [Brazil](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brazil) are major producers of Robusta coffee.

## History

Wild coffee plants, probably from Kefa (Kaffa), [Ethiopia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ethiopia), were taken to southern Arabia and placed under cultivation in the 15th century. One of many [legends](https://www.britannica.com/art/legend-literature) about the discovery of coffee is that of Kaldi, an Arab goatherd who was puzzled by the strange antics of his flock. About 850 ce Kaldi supposedly sampled the berries of the [evergreen](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/evergreen) bush on which the [goats](https://www.britannica.com/animal/goat) were feeding and, on experiencing a sense of exhilaration, proclaimed his discovery to the world.

Whatever the actual origin of coffee, its stimulating effect undoubtedly made it popular. Ironically, though [Islamic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islam) authorities pronounced the drink intoxicating and therefore prohibited by the [Qurʾān](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quran), many Muslims were attracted to the beverage as a substitute for [alcohol](https://www.britannica.com/science/alcohol), also prohibited by the Qurʾān. Despite the threat of severe penalties, coffee drinking spread rapidly among Arabs and their neighbours and even gave rise to a new social and cultural entity, the [coffeehouse](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cafe-eating-and-drinking-establishment).

[![Coffeehouse, 17th-century England](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/216078-050-568FADC2/English-coffeehouse-during-The-Restoration-1668.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/216078-050-568FADC2/English-coffeehouse-during-The-Restoration-1668.jpg)

[Coffeehouse, 17th-century England](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/216078-050-568FADC2/English-coffeehouse-during-The-Restoration-1668.jpg) English coffeehouse during the Restoration, painting from 1668. Some of the most famous coffeehouses of Europe flourished in London as ready points for news, discussion, and political and financial society.(more)

Coffee was introduced into one European country after another throughout the 16th and 17th centuries. Many accounts are recorded of its prohibition or approval as a religious, political, and medical potion. By the end of the 17th century, [coffeehouses](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cafe-eating-and-drinking-establishment) were flourishing across Britain, the British colonies in America, and continental Europe.

Until the close of the 17th century, the world’s limited supply of coffee was obtained almost entirely from the province of [Yemen](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yemen) in southern Arabia. By the 20th century the greatest concentration of production was centred in the Western Hemisphere—particularly [Brazil](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brazil). In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, industrial roasting and grinding machines came into use, vacuum-sealed containers were invented for ground roasts, and [decaffeination](https://www.britannica.com/topic/decaffeination) methods for green coffee beans were developed. After 1950 the production of [instant coffee](https://www.britannica.com/topic/instant-coffee) was perfected, which led to increased production of the cheaper Robusta beans in Africa. _See also_ [history of coffee](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-coffee).

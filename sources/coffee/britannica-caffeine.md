**caffeine**, nitrogenous [organic compound](https://www.britannica.com/science/organic-compound) of the [alkaloid](https://www.britannica.com/science/alkaloid) group, substances that have marked physiological effects. Caffeine occurs in tea, coffee, [guarana](https://www.britannica.com/plant/guarana), [maté](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mate-beverage), kola nuts, and [cacao](https://www.britannica.com/plant/cacao).

[![caffeine](https://cdn.britannica.com/86/131286-004-583962C0/caffeine.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/86/131286-004-583962C0/caffeine.jpg)

[caffeine](https://cdn.britannica.com/86/131286-004-583962C0/caffeine.jpg) Anhydrous caffeine.(more)

Pure caffeine (trimethylxanthine) occurs as a white powder or as silky needles, which melt at 238 °C (460 °F); it [sublimes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/sublimes) at 178 °C (352 °F) at [atmospheric pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmospheric-pressure). It is very soluble in hot water; upon cooling, the solution deposits crystals of caffeine monohydrate. Caffeine is generally less soluble in organic solvents than in hot water. It is odourless but has a bitter taste.

Caffeine is present in ground [coffee](https://www.britannica.com/topic/coffee) in amounts ranging between 0.75 and 1.5 percent by weight. The average cup of coffee thus contains about 100 mg (0.003 ounce) of caffeine. The caffeine content of [tea](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tea-beverage) varies greatly depending on the strength of the tea, but it averages about 40 mg. There are also about 40 mg (0.0014 ounce) of caffeine in a 12-ounce glass of carbonated cola beverage.

Caffeine has a stimulating effect on the central [nervous system](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-nervous-system), heart, blood vessels, and kidneys. It also acts as a mild [diuretic](https://www.britannica.com/science/diuretic). Caffeine’s potent stimulatory action makes it a valuable [antidote](https://www.britannica.com/science/antidote) to respiratory [depression](https://www.britannica.com/science/depression-psychology) induced by [drug overdose](https://www.britannica.com/topic/drug-overdose) (e.g., from [morphine](https://www.britannica.com/science/morphine) or barbiturates). The positive effects that have been described in people who use caffeine include improved motor performance, decreased [fatigue](https://www.britannica.com/science/fatigue-physiology), [enhanced](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhanced) sensory activity, and increased alertness. These positive effects may partly explain the compulsion of many adults to consume coffee or other caffeine-containing beverages as part of the morning ritual of awakening. However, caffeine intake may also produce in people such negative effects as irritability, nervousness or anxiety, jitteriness, headaches, and [insomnia](https://www.britannica.com/science/insomnia). By the mid-1980s [decaffeinated](https://www.britannica.com/topic/decaffeination) coffee and soft drinks had become widely available, giving consumers the choice of regulating their caffeine intake while continuing to enjoy these beverages.

**energy drink**, any beverage that contains high levels of a [stimulant](https://www.britannica.com/science/stimulant) ingredient, usually [caffeine](https://www.britannica.com/science/caffeine), as well as [sugar](https://www.britannica.com/science/sugar-chemical-compound) and often supplements, such as [vitamins](https://www.britannica.com/science/vitamin) or [carnitine](https://www.britannica.com/science/carnitine), and that is promoted as a product capable of enhancing mental alertness and physical performance.

Energy drinks are distinguished from sports drinks, which are used to replace [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) and [electrolytes](https://www.britannica.com/science/electrolyte) during or after physical activity, and from [coffee](https://www.britannica.com/topic/coffee) and [tea](https://www.britannica.com/topic/tea-beverage), which are brewed, contain fewer ingredients, and may be decaffeinated. Energy drinks also differ from [soft drinks](https://www.britannica.com/topic/soft-drink), which either do not contain caffeine or contain relatively small amounts of caffeine. Although some energy drinks are considered beverages, others, namely those containing food additives (e.g., taurine or other [amino acids](https://www.britannica.com/science/amino-acid)), may be marketed as [dietary supplements](https://www.britannica.com/science/dietary-supplement). Examples of energy drinks include Red Bull, Monster, Rockstar, NOS, and Amp.

The manufacturers of energy drinks claim that their products boost energy levels. Those claims generally have been based on proprietary formulas, in which the stimulant effects of a drink were said to be derived from the specific concoction of ingredients. Research has indicated, however, that the stimulant effects of energy drinks are due primarily to caffeine. Other ingredients, such as taurine and [vitamins B6](https://www.britannica.com/science/vitamin-B6) and [B12](https://www.britannica.com/science/vitamin-B12), could potentially exert biological effects but have not been known to increase energy levels; the effects of some ingredients (e.g., glucuronolactone) are unknown. Levels of vitamins and certain other additives in energy drinks often far exceed recommended daily intakes.

In the early 21st century, the safety of energy drinks was an emerging [public health](https://www.britannica.com/topic/public-health) issue. In particular, the combined use of energy drinks and [alcohol](https://www.britannica.com/topic/alcohol-consumption), which became popular particularly among college students, was linked to an increased likelihood of high-risk drinking behaviour (e.g., binge drinking) and increased risk of alcohol-related injury and other consequences. Studies in human subjects who consumed energy drinks revealed potentially dangerous effects on [heart](https://www.britannica.com/science/heart) function and [blood pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/blood-pressure).

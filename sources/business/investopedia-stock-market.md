Definition

The stock market consists of a primary market in which businesses first issue shares to investors and a secondary market in which investors trade shares.

### Key Takeaways

- Companies issue shares on the stock market to raise capital and expand their businesses.
- The Securities Exchange Act of 1934 was created to govern securities transactions on the secondary market.
- Investors may buy shares to receive dividends, vote in corporate elections, or sell shares at a higher price.
- The stock market can be accessed by investors and traders alike through [online brokerage and trading platforms](https://www.investopedia.com/best-online-brokers-4587872).

## What Is the Stock Market?

The stock market is a broad term for the network of exchanges and over-the-counter venues where investors buy and sell shares in publicly traded companies through brokerage platforms. People sometimes use "stock market" to refer to the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) or the Nasdaq, but these exchanges are components of a wider global marketplace.

The [Securities and Exchange Commission](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/sec.asp) (SEC) establishes rules and regulations in the U.S. for companies that want to sell shares to the public. Businesses must register with the SEC and publish disclosures and financial statements.1

The stock market is crucial to modern economies, enabling money to move between investors and companies, supporting business growth, creating jobs, and letting people invest in the future of industries.

## How the Stock Market Works

When people refer to the stock market, they often refer to a specific exchange, like the New York Stock Exchange.2 The stock market is made up of a larger system of exchanges, brokerages, and over-the-counter markets, however. It's anywhere you can buy part of a company is part of the stock market.

Shares of companies are bought and sold in this vast, complex network of trading activities. They're protected by laws against fraud and other unfair trading practices. The stock market plays a crucial role in modern economies by enabling money to move between investors and companies.

## What Are Public Companies?

Not all companies can offer stock to the general public. Only companies registered with the SEC can sell their shares on a public exchange like the NYSE or Nasdaq in the U.S. These companies must meet stringent regulations and financial disclosure laws.1

The traditional route to going public is via an [initial public offering](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/ipo.asp) (IPO). [Special purpose acquisition companies (SPACs)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/spac.asp) emerged as an alternative route for [going public](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/g/goingpublic.asp) in the 2020s.5

The [primary market](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/primarymarket.asp) consists of those who buy their shares directly from the company, such as early investors, company insiders, and financial [underwriters](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/041415/what-does-underwriter-do-new-stock-offering.asp) for companies going public. It also includes [private placements](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/privateplacement.asp), where a company sells its shares directly to investors without going through the registration process.6

## Stocks: Buying and Selling Shares

You're getting a piece of that company when you buy a stock or share. How much of the company you own depends on the number of shares the company has issued and the number of shares you buy.

A single share could represent a large part of a small, private company. Major public companies often have millions, even billions, of shares. Apple ( [AAPL](https://www.investopedia.com/markets/quote?tvwidgetsymbol=AAPL)) has billions of tradable shares, so one share is just a tiny fraction of the company.7

The price of a stock changes based on the demand for shares from new investors who want to buy, or the supply of shares from existing investors who want to sell. Not every investor makes decisions based on the same criteria, and what might seem like a high price to one investor is a bargain to another. This dynamic keeps shares trading hands and makes future prices difficult to predict.

## What Is a Stock Exchange?

Its shares can be traded freely on the stock market when a company goes public. Investors can buy and sell shares among themselves. Most trading occurs on stock exchanges, although there are other venues.

Stock exchanges are organized and regulated "places" where stocks and other securities are bought and sold. They're mostly virtual. They play a crucial role in the financial system by providing a platform for companies to raise money by selling their stocks and bonds to the public.

The NYSE and Nasdaq are prime examples, serving as central locations for buying and selling stocks.28 There are many other major exchanges worldwide, including the London Stock Exchange, the Tokyo Stock Exchange, and the Shanghai Stock Exchange.

Each exchange has its own internal rules, and investors follow different national and local laws. These are meant to ensure fair trading practices and to keep investors confident in dealing there. They also provide transparency in the trading process, giving real-time information on securities prices.

A major benefit of trading on stock exchanges is [liquidity](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/l/liquidity.asp), the ability to buy or sell stocks relatively easily. With thousands of buyers and sellers, there is always someone willing to buy or sell shares for the right price.

Many stock exchanges cross-list company shares, offering securities listed on other exchanges. This allows companies to reach more investors when raising capital, and it gives investors a wider range of trading options.

### Important

Although it is called a stock market, other securities, such as [exchange-traded funds (ETFs)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/etf.asp), are also traded there.

## Over-the-Counter Market

Stocks can also be traded [over the counter (OTC)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/over-the-countermarket.asp). These OTC markets are where you buy or sell stocks directly with another investor, typically without the same level of regulation or public scrutiny.9 OTC trading involves a network of brokers and dealers who negotiate directly over computer networks and by phone.

This type of trading is commonly used for smaller, less liquid companies that may not meet the stringent listing requirements of the stock exchanges. This can make it more challenging for investors to get reliable information about the companies they are investing in.10

## Other Assets Sold on the Stock Market

Many other assets, in addition to common stocks, are traded on stock exchanges and OTC. These are also considered to be part of the stock market:

- [**American depositary receipts (ADRs)**](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/adr.asp): ADRs represent shares in foreign companies and are traded on U.S. stock exchanges.11 They let U.S. investors invest in foreign companies without dealing with foreign stock exchanges or converting their currency.

- [**Derivatives**](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/derivative.asp): This is a broad category that includes options and futures, whose values are derived from the value of an underlying asset, such as stocks, bonds, commodities, currencies, interest rates, or market indexes.12 You're not directly buying or selling the actual asset (like the stock) in derivatives trading. You're trading something whose value is influenced by the changes in the price of an underlying asset.

- [**Investment funds**](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/investment-fund.asp): These include mutual funds, which pool money from many investors for a basket of stocks, bonds, and other securities, and exchange-traded funds, which trade on stock exchanges like individual stocks and "track" or try to mirror how a sector, index, or theme of stocks is doing.
- [**Preferred stocks**](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/preferredstock.asp): These stocks generally provide a set dividend and, as the name suggests, they have priority over common stock in getting a share of the profits or what's left over if the company goes bankrupt.13
- [**Real estate investment trusts (REITs)**](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/reit.asp): REITs are worth mentioning to get an idea of the breadth of what is included in the stock market. REITs are companies that own, manage, or finance real estate. REITs must legally provide 90% of their profits as dividends each year to investors who buy shares.14

People often talk about these as part of the stock market in separate markets:

- [**Bonds**](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bond.asp): These represent debt, and governments and corporations issue them to raise capital. Investors who buy bonds effectively lend money to the issuer in exchange for interest payments and the return of the bond's face value at maturity.
- [**Commodities**](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/commodity.asp): There are commodities exchanges worldwide where you can buy raw materials like oil, steel, wheat, and coal directly or futures contracts based on their potential prices.

## Investors and Traders

Those in the stock market include institutional investors, such as pension funds, mutual funds, insurance companies, and hedge funds. They manage large amounts of money and often have a significant influence over the market because they are trading in large volumes. Retail investors buy and sell securities for their personal accounts, not for an organization. They can range from beginners to experienced traders. Most use online broker platforms. Accredited investors are high-net-worth individuals (HNWIs) with sophisticated investing experience. The SEC lets them access more complex investments, like [venture capital](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/v/vcfund.asp) and private equity.15

Investors approach the market from a long-term perspective. They put money in stocks, ETFs, mutual funds, and other securities, expecting their value to grow over time. These are not quick trades to get in and out fast. These investors are often more concerned with the fundamental strength of the companies or assets they invest in, such as their financial performance, market position, and growth potential. They decide on investments after research and analysis or after receiving recommendations from financial advisors while trying to build wealth steadily through a portfolio that increases in value over time.

Traders take a short-term approach to the stock market. They aim to capitalize on the market’s volatility, trading stocks, options, futures, and other financial instruments within shorter time frames, from seconds and minutes to days and months. Traders often rely on technical analysis, which involves studying market trends, charts, and other statistical measures to predict future price movements. Trading can offer the potential for quick profits, but it also comes with higher risks than long-term investing. Quickly buying and selling securities requires a sharp understanding of the market and a more active, hands-on strategy for trading.

### The Role of Brokers

Brokerage platforms act as a go-between for investors and the securities markets. They are licensed organizations that buy and sell stocks and other securities for individual and institutional clients. Brokerage firms can be small boutique shops or multinational platforms offering investment advice, research, and wealth management services while executing trades for customers.

Full-service brokers provide detailed financial advice, portfolio management, and personalized services, making them better suited for investors who prefer a thorough approach to managing their investments. Further down in cost, discount brokers like [Fidelity](https://www.investopedia.com/fidelity-review-4587897) and [Charles Schwab](https://www.investopedia.com/charles-schwab-review-4587888) provide a more hands-off experience and are typically preferred by investors who prefer to make their own trading decisions.

Online brokerage firms have become increasingly popular, offering user-friendly platforms that allow investors to trade securities electronically at lower costs and with more convenience. These platforms often have educational resources, analytical tools, and real-time market data. There has also been a rise in [robo-advisors](https://www.investopedia.com/the-best-robo-advisors-8764849), which offer automated financial planning services at a very low price.

Brokers are all regulated by the SEC and the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) in the U.S.16

### The Role of Regulators

A significant aspect of the stock market, dictating what’s traded and how, is the regulations and regulators involved. The latter is the SEC in the U.S, an independent federal agency set up in 1934 after the [1929 market crash](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/stock-market-crash-1929.asp) and the Great Depression.1718 The SEC's mission is “protecting investors, maintaining fair, orderly, and efficient markets, and facilitating capital formation.”19

The SEC enforces laws against market manipulation, insider trading, and other forms of fraud while verifying that public companies reveal any significant financial information investors should know when trusting a firm with their money by buying its stock. The SEC also oversees stock exchanges, broker-dealers, investment advisors, mutual funds, and public utility holding companies.

The exchanges also have their own requirements, such as filing timely (usually quarterly) updates to company financial reports and instant reporting of relevant corporate developments to ensure that everyone looking to trade has the same information.

FINRA oversees brokerage firms and their registered securities representatives and is more focused than the SEC on protecting retail investors.20 Similar agencies exist worldwide, which is crucial given how the stock market is global and a calamity in one corner of the world can soon reach the other. It’s not just something that happens in a few buildings on Wall Street.

Many countries’ regulations differ significantly. They answer to diverse populations and cultural expectations. But general rules are enforced to ensure fair practices, protect investors, and promote confidence in the broader stock market.

### How Stock Prices Are Determined

Textbook descriptions of stock prices tend to start off by talking about investors and dealers coming together. The buyer and seller must agree on a figure for there to be a stock trade. However, most investors find prices as they are listed in online brokerage accounts or online graphs of stock prices over time, not as coming from tough negotiations.

You do have to agree to buy stocks, and each investor or trader making this decision collectively shapes the [demand](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/demand.asp) for stocks. Taken against the supply on hand in the market, this produces the prices on our screens.

The factors that influence these prices fall into two main types: fundamental and technical. Fundamental factors are rooted in a company's earnings, profitability from its operations, and the goods or services it offers. Technical factors relate to market sentiment and statistical analyses of historical market activity and stock price trends.

High stock prices can indicate a company's success, or at least the feeling of buyers that they are doing well. But they can also result from stock splits, dividends, and share repurchases. It doesn't mean that money is lost from the market as a whole when a stock price drops. It instead signifies a decrease in the market value of the specific stock.

If a company reports higher profits than expected, its stock price might increase as more investors want to buy shares, hoping for future growth. Similarly, economic events like interest rate changes or geopolitical issues can affect investor confidence and stock prices.

### Market Indexes

Most Americans first learn about the stock market through indexes because reporting on the ups and downs of the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) or the [S&P 500](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/sp500.asp) has long been a staple of financial news. Indexes can include the DJIA, which includes 30 large public companies. The S&P 500 represents the 500 largest U.S. public companies.21

These indexes can provide a picture of the wider stock market or a specific sector, such as technology or healthcare. Indexes are important because they're used as benchmarks for stocks and portfolios. You'll want to see how your stocks are doing against a tech index if you're invested in technology stocks.

## Roles of the Stock Market

The stock market fills several different roles:

- **Corporate governance**: Publicly traded companies follow stringent reporting regulations, which makes them far more transparent and accountable. This information allows investors to make informed decisions and helps maintain investor confidence in the market. It's also a boon for everyday Americans to gain a view inside major U.S. corporations. They could close down much of what we know about them without these transparency requirements.
- **Economic indicator**: The stock market's performance is often considered a gauge of an economy's health. Rising stock prices are associated with corporate profitability and economic growth. Declining prices signal problems ahead.
- **Investment opportunities**: You can invest in companies and potentially grow a portfolio over time. The stock market has historically delivered returns outpacing inflation, making it a vital tool for retirement planning, wealth building, and financial security.22
- **Liquidity**: The stock market enables investors to buy and sell shares of companies and other securities quickly when necessary.
- **Raising capital**: Most importantly, the stock market offers a platform where companies raise funds by issuing stocks. This capital is essential for business expansion, research and development, and other corporate initiatives. Companies gain access to these funds without incurring debt by selling shares to the public.
- **Resource allocation**: The stock market reflects the collective judgment of traders and investors through the price of different companies. It's said to help efficiently distribute capital to companies that are more likely to succeed and away from those that are not.


## Why Is the Stock Market So Important?

The stock market's meaning for how our world functions can't be understated. The global economy was vastly different when the earliest stock markets formed. These were eras when trade and commerce were primarily driven by physical goods, with industries like agriculture, textiles, and early manufacturing dominating the economic landscape.

Stock markets were fledgling institutions, helping finance expeditions and trade ventures. They funded the colonial enterprises that took goods from South Asia, the Americas, and Africa. These stock exchanges were already global investment operations.23 But they played a relatively minor role in everyday economic life.

Fast forward. The stock market is considered central to the global economy, a change underscored by [financialization](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/financialization.asp) and the increasing dominance of financial markets and institutions. Modern economies are characterized by a complex web of financial transactions and instruments. The stock market is not just seen as a barometer for economic health but also as critical for distributing and creating wealth.

## The Meaning of the Stock Market for Most Americans

Financialization has also mirrored broader socioeconomic changes. Stock markets are not just platforms for raising capital but have been tied to millions of Americans' retirement and investment strategies.

Many Americans are not directly invested in the stock market, but they're still affected by its movements. The market drives funding for technological advances like smartphones and medications, which require billions of dollars for research and development.

Market moves can also influence corporate decisions, influencing job creation and layoffs. A healthy stock market generally correlates with a more robust economy. But it could also mean more capital in the hands of a wealthy few, increasing the property values of once middle-class areas in almost every major American city.

The stock market also indirectly influences public services and infrastructure. [Pension funds](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/investing-strategy/090916/how-do-pension-funds-work.asp) are significantly invested in the stock market, affecting the retirement security of millions of people. Many more individuals don't have pensions and are invested in the market directly through 401(k)s and individual retirement accounts.

## What's the Difference Between the Bond Market and the Stock Market?

The bond market is where investors buy and sell debt securities, typically issued by governments or corporations.24 You're essentially lending money for regular interest payments and the return on the bond's face value at maturity when you invest in bonds.

The stock market involves buying and selling shares of publicly traded companies. Stocks offer the potential for higher returns than bonds because investors can receive both dividends when the company is profitable and returns when the stock price goes up. They also have a higher risk, however, because stock prices can be more volatile.

## What Is an Alternate Trading System?

[Alternative trading systems](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/alternative-trading-system.asp) are platforms for matching large buy and sell transactions. They're not regulated like exchanges. Dark pools and many cryptocurrency exchanges are private exchanges or forums for securities and currency trading, and they run within private groups.

## Who Helps an Investor Trade on the Stock Market?

Stockbrokers act as intermediaries between the stock exchanges and the investors by buying and selling stocks. Portfolio managers are professionals who invest portfolios, or collections of securities, for clients. Investment bankers represent companies in many ways, such as helping private companies go public or planning mergers and acquisitions.

## The Bottom Line

The stock market is where shares of companies and other financial instruments are [bought and sold](https://www.investopedia.com/best-online-brokers-4587872). It's where companies raise capital and where investors can grow their wealth. Even if you don't trade on the stock market directly, it influences the products you buy, the type of jobs available, and the retirement you might plan.

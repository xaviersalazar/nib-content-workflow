A corporation is a legal form of [business organization](https://www.britannica.com/money/business-organization), chartered by a state, that exists as a separate entity from its owners. In the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), the default corporate form is the **C corporation** (C corp), named for the subchapter of the Internal Revenue Code that governs its taxation.

## Part of a bigger picture

A corporation is just one of the many business structures available. Learn how sole proprietorships, partnerships, LLCs, and others compare in **Britannica Money’s guide to [business structure types](https://www.britannica.com/money/types-of-business-structures)**.

## Defining features of a corporation

Corporations are distinguished from [sole proprietorships](https://www.britannica.com/money/sole-proprietorship) and [partnerships](https://www.britannica.com/money/partnership) by several characteristics that make them especially suited for large-scale economic activity:

- **Limited liability.** [Shareholders](https://www.britannica.com/money/stock-market-basics) are not personally liable for the corporation’s debts beyond their investments.
- **Transferability of shares.** Ownership rights can be sold or transferred without dissolving the corporation.
- **Juridical personality.** The corporation can enter contracts, own property, sue, and be sued in its own name.
- **Indefinite duration.** The corporation can continue beyond the involvement or lifetime of its founders.

## Corporate formation and ownership

Corporations are formed under state law by filing articles of incorporation. Shareholders purchase stock, which represents ownership and a claim on future [earnings](https://www.britannica.com/money/earnings-per-share-quarterly-earnings). A [board of directors, elected by shareholders, appoints officers](https://www.britannica.com/money/c-suite-meaning) to manage day-to-day operations. Ownership changes—such as selling shares—do not affect the corporation’s legal existence.

## Governance in a C corp

Shareholders elect a board of directors to oversee major decisions and set policy. The board appoints officers, such as a [CEO or CFO](https://www.britannica.com/money/c-suite-meaning), to run daily operations. This separation of ownership and management allows corporations to scale, but also brings heavier [regulation and oversight](https://www.britannica.com/money/financial-market-regulators) than other business structures.

## Corporate tax treatment

C corps pay [corporate income tax](https://www.britannica.com/money/corporate-income-tax) on profits. If [dividends](https://www.britannica.com/money/what-is-a-stock-dividend) are distributed to shareholders, those payments are taxed again at [the individual level](https://www.britannica.com/money/federal-income-tax-brackets)—a situation known as “double taxation.”

## Historical development of the corporate structure

The modern corporation evolved from a blend of the [joint-stock company](https://www.britannica.com/money/joint-stock-company) (essentially a large [partnership](https://www.britannica.com/money/partnership)) and the traditional legal corporation used for medieval guilds and municipalities.

- **Joint‑stock companies** were essentially partnerships that raised capital by selling shares to investors, enabling large-scale ventures while limiting individual liability. One early example is the [Virginia Company](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Virginia-Company) (1606).
- **Medieval corporations** came in the form of guilds, municipalities, universities, and monasteries—legal entities that existed separately from any particular member.

## Partnership vs. corporation

Corporations are separate legal entities with [limited liability](https://www.britannica.com/money/limited-liability) for all owners and (in most cases) perpetual existence. Partnerships are legally tied to their owners, with liability and longevity depending on the partners themselves.

In 16th-century England, business corporations emerged as crown-chartered [monopolies](https://www.britannica.com/money/monopoly-economics)—like the [East India Company](https://www.britannica.com/topic/East-India-Company) in 1600—melding joint-stock capital-raising with corporate legal identity under mercantilist motives.

In the 19th century, general incorporation laws in the United States and [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) made it easier to form corporations without a special legislative [charter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/charter-document). U.S. states competed to offer favorable incorporation laws, helping fuel rapid adoption. Large capital-intensive projects—especially railroads—depended on the corporate form, which in turn supported massive [industrial](https://www.britannica.com/money/industrials-stocks) expansion in steel, coal, and other sectors.

The rise of powerful corporations like [Standard Oil](https://www.britannica.com/money/Standard-Oil) led to public concern over monopoly power, prompting [antitrust](https://www.britannica.com/money/antitrust-law) measures in the early 20th century. Over time, management of large corporations shifted from shareholders to professional executives, raising ongoing debates over [corporate governance and social responsibility](https://www.britannica.com/money/corporate-governance-risk-management).

## The role of the modern corporation

Corporations—ranging from small private firms to multinational giants—dominate [manufacturing, energy, and services](https://www.britannica.com/money/stock-market-sectors) in most developed economies. The C corp structure remains a cornerstone for businesses seeking significant outside investment, [access to public capital markets](https://www.britannica.com/money/primary-vs-secondary-markets), and perpetual existence.

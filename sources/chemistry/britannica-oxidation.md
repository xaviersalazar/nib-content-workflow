**oxidation-reduction reaction**, any [chemical reaction](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-reaction) in which the [oxidation number](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation-number) of a participating chemical species changes. The term covers a large and [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) body of processes. Many [oxidation](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation)- [reduction](https://www.britannica.com/science/reduction-chemistry) reactions are as common and familiar as [fire](https://www.britannica.com/science/fire-combustion), the rusting and dissolution of [metals](https://www.britannica.com/science/metal-chemistry), the browning of [fruit](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body), and [respiration](https://www.britannica.com/science/respiratory-system) and [photosynthesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/photosynthesis)—basic [life](https://www.britannica.com/science/life) functions.

## Major classifications

Most oxidation-reduction (redox) processes involve the transfer of [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom), [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) atoms, or [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron), with all three processes sharing two important characteristics: (1) they are coupled—i.e., in any [oxidation](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation) reaction a [reciprocal](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reciprocal) reduction occurs, and (2) they involve a characteristic net chemical change—i.e., an [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) or [electron](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) goes from one unit of matter to another. Both [reciprocity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reciprocity) and net change are illustrated below in examples of the three most common types of oxidation-reduction reactions.

Because of their complementary nature, the oxidation and reduction processes together are referred to as redox reactions. The reactant that brings about the oxidation is called the oxidizing agent, and that reagent is itself reduced by the reducing agent. In the examples given above, mercury(II) oxide, oxygen, and the copper(II) ion are oxidizing agents, and carbon, hydrazine, and zinc are the reducing agents.

# General theory

## Stoichiometric basis

Describing the redox processes as above conveys no information about the mechanism by which change takes place. A complete description of the net chemical change for a process is known as the [stoichiometry](https://www.britannica.com/science/stoichiometry) of the reaction, which provides the characteristic combining proportions of [elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) and [compounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound). Reactions are classified as redox and nonredox on the basis of stoichiometry; oxygen-atom, hydrogen-atom, and [electron](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) transfer are stoichiometric categories.

## Oxidation-state change

[Comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Comprehensive) definitions of [oxidation](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation) and [reduction](https://www.britannica.com/science/reduction-chemistry) have been made possible by modern [molecular](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) structure theory. Every [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) consists of a positive nucleus, surrounded by negative electrons, which determine the bonding characteristics of each element. In forming chemical bonds, atoms donate, acquire, or share electrons. This makes it possible to assign every atom an [oxidation number](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation-number), which specifies the number of its electrons that can be involved in forming bonds with other atoms. From the particular atoms in a molecule and their known [bonding](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-bonding) capacities, the bonding pattern within a [molecule](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) is determined, and each atom is regarded as being in a specific oxidation state, expressed by an oxidation number.

Redox processes are defined as reactions accompanied by oxidation-state changes: an increase in an atom’s oxidation number corresponds to an oxidation; a decrease, to a reduction. In this generalized theory, three examples of ways in which oxidation-state changes can occur are by oxygen-atom (gain, oxidation; loss, reduction), hydrogen-atom (loss, oxidation; gain, reduction), and electron (loss, oxidation; gain, reduction) transfer. The oxidation-state change definition is usually compatible with the above rules for applying the oxygen-atom-transfer and hydrogen-atom-transfer [criteria](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criteria) and always compatible with the electron-transfer [criterion](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criterion) when it is applicable. The oxidation state of any atom is indicated by a roman numeral following the name or symbol for the element. Thus, [iron](https://www.britannica.com/science/iron-chemical-element)(III), or Fe(III), means iron in an oxidation state of +3. The uncombined Fe(III) [ion](https://www.britannica.com/science/ion-physics) is simply Fe3+.

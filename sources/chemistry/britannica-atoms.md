### What is an atom?

An atom is the basic building block of chemistry. It is the smallest unit into which matter can be divided without the release of electrically charged particles. It also is the smallest unit of matter that has the characteristic properties of a chemical element.

### Are all atoms the same size?

All atoms are roughly the same size, whether they have 3 or 90 electrons. Approximately 50 million atoms of solid matter lined up in a row would measure 1 cm (0.4 inch). A convenient unit of length for measuring atomic sizes is the [angstrom](https://www.britannica.com/science/angstrom), defined as 10−10 meters.

### What does the mass of an atom consist of?

The mass of an atom consists of the mass of the nucleus plus that of the [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron). That means the [atomic mass unit](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-mass-unit) is not exactly the same as the mass of the [proton](https://www.britannica.com/science/proton-subatomic-particle) or [neutron](https://www.britannica.com/science/neutron).

### How is the atomic number of an atom defined?

The single most important characteristic of an atom is its atomic number (usually denoted by the letter Z), which is defined as the number of units of positive charge (protons) in the nucleus. For example, if an atom has a Z of 6, it is [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element), while a Z of 92 corresponds to [uranium](https://www.britannica.com/science/uranium).

### Are humans made of atoms?

Yes, humans are made of atoms. Most of the human body is made up of atoms of four [elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element): [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen), [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element), [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen), and [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen). These four elements are about 96 percent of a human’s weight.

### What are the three types of atoms?

There are not three types of atoms. Each [chemical element](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) in the [periodic table](https://www.britannica.com/science/periodic-table) has its own distinct kind of atom, and thus there are 118 types of atoms. However, atoms are made up of three types of [subatomic particles](https://www.britannica.com/science/subatomic-particle): the heavy [protons](https://www.britannica.com/science/proton-subatomic-particle) and [neutrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/neutron) that make up the [nucleus](https://www.britannica.com/) (the central part of the atom), and the much lighter [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) that circle the nucleus in orbital paths called [shells](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron-shell).

**atom**, the basic building block of all [matter](https://www.britannica.com/science/matter) and [chemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemistry). Atoms can [combine](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/combine) with other atoms to form [molecules](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) but cannot be divided into smaller parts by ordinary chemical processes.

Most of the atom is empty space. The rest consists of three basic types of [subatomic particles](https://www.britannica.com/science/subatomic-particle): [protons](https://www.britannica.com/science/proton-subatomic-particle), [neutrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/neutron), and [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron). The protons and neutrons form the atom’s central nucleus. (The ordinary [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) atom is an exception; it contains one [proton](https://www.britannica.com/science/proton-subatomic-particle) but no neutrons.) As their names suggest, protons have a positive [electrical charge](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-charge), while neutrons are electrically neutral—they carry no charge; overall, then, the nucleus has a positive charge. Circling the nucleus is a cloud of electrons, which are negatively charged. Like opposite ends of a [magnet](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnet) that attract one another, the negative electrons are attracted to a positive [force](https://www.britannica.com/science/force-physics), which binds them to the nucleus. The nucleus is small and [dense](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/dense) compared with the electrons, which are the lightest charged particles in nature. The electrons circle the nucleus in [orbital](https://www.britannica.com/science/orbital) paths called [shells](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron-shell), each of which holds only a certain number of electrons.

An ordinary, neutral atom has an equal number of protons (in the nucleus) and electrons (surrounding the nucleus). Thus the positive and negative charges are balanced. Some atoms, however, lose or gain electrons in chemical reactions or in collisions with other particles. Ordinary atoms that either gain or lose electrons are called [ions](https://www.britannica.com/science/ion-physics). If a neutral atom loses an [electron](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron), it becomes a [positive ion](https://www.britannica.com/science/cation). If it gains an electron, it becomes a [negative ion](https://www.britannica.com/science/anion). These basic subatomic particles—protons, neutrons, and electrons—are themselves made up of smaller substances, such as [quarks](https://www.britannica.com/science/quark) and [leptons](https://www.britannica.com/science/lepton).

More than 90 types of atoms exist in nature, and each kind of atom forms a different [chemical element](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element). Chemical elements are made up of only one type of atom—gold contains only [gold](https://www.britannica.com/science/gold-chemical-element) atoms, and [neon](https://www.britannica.com/science/neon-chemical-element) contains only neon atoms--and they are ranked in order of their [atomic number](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-number) (the total number of protons in its nucleus) in a chart called the [periodic table](https://www.britannica.com/science/periodic-table). Accordingly, because an atom of [iron](https://www.britannica.com/science/iron-chemical-element) has 26 protons in its nucleus, its atomic number is 26 and its ranking on the periodic table of chemical elements is 26. Because an ordinary atom has the same number of electrons as protons, an element’s atomic number also tells how many electrons its atoms have, and it is the number and arrangement of the electrons in their orbiting shells that determines how one atom interacts with another. The key shell is the outermost one, called the [valence](https://www.britannica.com/science/valence-chemistry) shell. If this outermost shell is complete, or filled with the maximum number of electrons for that shell, the atom is stable, with little or no tendency to interact with other atoms. But atoms with incomplete outer shells seek to fill or to empty such shells by gaining or losing electrons or by sharing electrons with other atoms. This is the basis of an atom’s chemical [activity](https://www.britannica.com/science/activity-radioactivity). Atoms that have the same number of electrons in the outer shell have similar chemical properties.

## [Atomic model](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-model)

![Shell atomic model](https://cdn.britannica.com/74/22474-050-FE855824/Shell-atomic-model-shell-shells-electrons-energy.jpg)

[Shell atomic model](https://cdn.britannica.com/74/22474-050-FE855824/Shell-atomic-model-shell-shells-electrons-energy.jpg) In the shell atomic model, electrons occupy different energy levels, or shells. The _K_ and _L_ shells are shown for a neon atom.(more)

Most matter consists of an agglomeration of molecules, which can be separated relatively easily. Molecules, in turn, are composed of atoms joined by chemical bonds that are more difficult to break. Each individual atom consists of smaller particles—namely, electrons and nuclei. These particles are electrically charged, and the electric forces on the charge are responsible for holding the atom together. Attempts to separate these smaller constituent particles require ever-increasing amounts of [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) and result in the creation of new [subatomic particles](https://www.britannica.com/science/subatomic-particle), many of which are charged.

As noted in the introduction to this article, an atom consists largely of empty space. The nucleus is the positively charged center of an atom and contains most of its [mass](https://www.britannica.com/science/mass-physics). It is composed of protons, which have a positive charge, and neutrons, which have no charge. Protons, neutrons, and the electrons surrounding them are long-lived particles present in all ordinary, naturally occurring atoms. Other subatomic particles may be found in association with these three types of particles. They can be created only with the addition of enormous amounts of energy, however, and are very short-lived.

All atoms are roughly the same size, whether they have 3 or 90 electrons. Approximately 50 million atoms of [solid](https://www.britannica.com/science/solid-state-of-matter) matter lined up in a row would measure 1 cm (0.4 inch). A convenient unit of length for measuring atomic sizes is the [angstrom](https://www.britannica.com/science/angstrom) (Å), defined as 10−10 meter. The radius of an atom measures 1–2 Å. Compared with the overall size of the atom, the nucleus is even more minute. It is in the same proportion to the atom as a marble is to a football [field](https://www.britannica.com/science/field-physics). In volume the nucleus takes up only 10−14 meters of the space in the atom—i.e., 1 part in 100,000. A convenient unit of length for measuring nuclear sizes is the femtometer (fm), which equals 10−15 meter. The [diameter](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/diameter) of a nucleus depends on the number of particles it contains and ranges from about 4 fm for a [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light) nucleus such as carbon to 15 fm for a heavy nucleus such as lead. In spite of the small size of the nucleus, virtually all the mass of the atom is concentrated there. The protons are massive, positively charged particles, whereas the neutrons have no charge and are slightly more massive than the protons. The fact that nuclei can have anywhere from 1 to nearly 300 protons and neutrons accounts for their wide variation in mass. The lightest nucleus, that of hydrogen, is 1,836 times more massive than an electron, while heavy nuclei are nearly 500,000 times more massive.

## [Atomic number](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-number)

The single most important [characteristic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characteristic) of an atom is its atomic number (usually denoted by the letter _Z_), which is defined as the number of units of positive charge (protons) in the nucleus. For example, if an atom has a _Z_ of 6, it is [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element), while a _Z_ of 92 corresponds to [uranium](https://www.britannica.com/science/uranium). A neutral atom has an equal number of protons and electrons so that the positive and negative charges exactly balance. Since it is the electrons that determine how one atom interacts with another, in the end it is the number of protons in the nucleus that determines the chemical properties of an atom.

# [Atomic mass](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-mass) and [isotopes](https://www.britannica.com/science/isotope)

[![isotopes of hydrogen](https://cdn.britannica.com/38/192338-050-AE92E225/image-isotopes-element-hydrogen-forms-neutrons-proton.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/38/192338-050-AE92E225/image-isotopes-element-hydrogen-forms-neutrons-proton.jpg)

[isotopes of hydrogen](https://cdn.britannica.com/38/192338-050-AE92E225/image-isotopes-element-hydrogen-forms-neutrons-proton.jpg) The image shows the three isotopes of the element hydrogen. All three forms have one proton (pink) and one electron (dark green) but differ in the number of neutrons (gray) in the nucleus. Protium, or ordinary hydrogen (top), has no neutrons. Deuterium, or hydrogen-2 (bottom left), has one neutron. Tritium, or hydrogen-3 (bottom right), has two neutrons.(more)

The number of neutrons in a nucleus affects the mass of the atom but not its chemical properties. Thus, a nucleus with six protons and six neutrons will have the same chemical properties as a nucleus with six protons and eight neutrons, although the two masses will be different. Nuclei with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons are said to be [isotopes](https://www.britannica.com/science/isotope) of each other. All chemical elements have many isotopes.

It is usual to [characterize](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characterize) different isotopes by giving the sum of the number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus—a quantity called the atomic [mass number](https://www.britannica.com/science/mass-number). In the above example, the first atom would be called carbon-12 or 12C (because it has six protons and six neutrons), while the second would be [carbon-14](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-14) or 14C.

The mass of atoms is measured in terms of the [atomic mass unit](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-mass-unit), which is defined to be 1/12 of the mass of an atom of carbon-12, or 1.660538921 × 10−24 gram. The mass of an atom consists of the mass of the nucleus plus that of the electrons, so the atomic mass unit is not exactly the same as the mass of the [proton](https://www.britannica.com/science/proton-subatomic-particle) or [neutron](https://www.britannica.com/science/neutron).

## Charge, mass, and spin

Scientists have known since the late 19th century that the electron has a negative [electric charge](https://www.britannica.com/science/electric-charge). The [value](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/value) of this charge was first measured by the American physicist [Robert Millikan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Millikan) between 1909 and 1910. In [Millikan’s oil-drop experiment](https://www.britannica.com/science/Millikan-oil-drop-experiment), he suspended tiny oil drops in a chamber containing an oil mist. By measuring the rate of fall of the oil drops, he was able to determine their weight. Oil drops that had an electric charge (acquired, for example, by [friction](https://www.britannica.com/science/friction) when moving through the air) could then be slowed down or stopped by applying an [electric force](https://www.britannica.com/science/Coulomb-force). By comparing applied electric force with changes in [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics), Millikan was able to determine the electric charge on each drop. After he had measured many drops, he found that the charges on all of them were simple multiples of a single number. This basic unit of charge was the charge on the electron, and the different charges on the oil drops corresponded to those having 2, 3, 4,… extra electrons on them. The charge on the electron is now accepted to be 1.602176565 × 10−19 [coulomb](https://www.britannica.com/science/coulomb). For this [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics) Millikan was awarded the [Nobel Prize](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nobel-Prize) for Physics in 1923.

The charge on the proton is equal in magnitude to that on the electron but opposite in sign—that is, the proton has a positive charge. Because opposite electric charges attract each other, there is an attractive [force](https://www.britannica.com/science/force-physics) between electrons and protons. This force is what keeps electrons in orbit around the nucleus, something like the way that [gravity](https://www.britannica.com/science/gravity-physics) keeps [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth) in orbit around the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun).

The electron has a mass of about 9.109382911 × 10−28 gram. The mass of a proton or neutron is about 1,836 times larger. This explains why the mass of an atom is primarily determined by the mass of the protons and neutrons in the nucleus.

The electron has other [intrinsic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intrinsic) properties. One of these is called [spin](https://www.britannica.com/science/spin-atomic-physics). The electron can be pictured as being something like Earth, spinning around an axis of rotation. In fact, most elementary particles have this property. Unlike Earth, however, they exist in the subatomic world and are governed by the laws of [quantum mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-mechanics-physics). Therefore, these particles cannot spin in any arbitrary way, but only at certain specific rates. These rates can be 1/2, 1, 3/2, 2,… times a basic unit of rotation. Like protons and neutrons, electrons have spin 1/2.

Particles with half-integer spin are called [fermions](https://www.britannica.com/science/fermion), for the Italian American physicist [Enrico Fermi](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Enrico-Fermi), who investigated their properties in the first half of the 20th century. Fermions have one important property that will help explain both the way that electrons are arranged in their orbits and the way that protons and neutrons are arranged inside the nucleus. They are subject to the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pauli-exclusion-principle) (named for the Austrian physicist [Wolfgang Pauli](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wolfgang-Pauli)), which states that no two fermions can occupy the same state—for example, the two electrons in a [helium](https://www.britannica.com/science/helium-chemical-element) atom must have different spin directions if they occupy the same orbit.

Because a spinning electron can be thought of as a moving electric charge, electrons can be thought of as tiny [electromagnets](https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnet). This means that, like any other magnet, an electron will respond to the presence of a [magnetic field](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-field) by twisting. (Think of a compass needle pointing north under the influence of Earth’s magnetic field.) This fact is usually expressed by saying that electrons have a [magnetic moment](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-moment). In [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science), magnetic moment relates the [strength](https://www.britannica.com/science/yield-strength) of a magnetic field to the [torque](https://www.britannica.com/science/torque) experienced by a magnetic object. Because of their [intrinsic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/intrinsic) spin, electrons have a magnetic moment given by −9.28 × 10−24 [joule](https://www.britannica.com/science/joule) per [tesla](https://www.britannica.com/science/tesla).

**combustion**, a [chemical reaction](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-reaction) between substances, usually including [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) and usually accompanied by the generation of [heat](https://www.britannica.com/science/heat) and [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light) in the form of [flame](https://www.britannica.com/science/flame). The rate or speed at which the reactants [combine](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/combine) is high, in part because of the nature of the chemical reaction itself and in part because more [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) is generated than can escape into the surrounding medium, with the result that the [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature) of the reactants is raised to accelerate the reaction even more.

A familiar example of a combustion reaction is a lighted [match](https://www.britannica.com/science/match-tinder). When a match is struck, [friction](https://www.britannica.com/science/friction) heats the head to a temperature at which the chemicals react and generate more heat than can escape into the air, and they [burn](https://www.britannica.com/science/burn) with a flame. If a wind blows away the heat or the chemicals are moist and friction does not raise the temperature sufficiently, the match goes out. Properly ignited, the heat from the flame raises the temperature of a nearby layer of the matchstick and of oxygen in the [air](https://www.britannica.com/science/air) [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) to it, and the [wood](https://www.britannica.com/science/wood-plant-tissue) and oxygen react in a combustion reaction. When [equilibrium](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/equilibrium) between the [total heat](https://www.britannica.com/science/enthalpy) energies of the reactants and the total heat energies of the products (including the actual heat and light emitted) is reached, combustion stops. Flames have a definable [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) and a complex structure; they are said to be multiform and are capable of existing at quite low temperatures, as well as at extremely high temperatures. The emission of light in the flame results from the presence of excited particles and, usually, of charged [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) and [molecules](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) and of [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron).

Combustion [encompasses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompasses) a great variety of phenomena with wide application in [industry](https://www.britannica.com/money/industry), the sciences, professions, and the home, and the application is based on knowledge of [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science), [chemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemistry), and [mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mechanics); their interrelationship becomes particularly evident in treating flame [propagation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagation).

In general terms, combustion is one of the most important of [chemical reactions](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-reaction) and may be considered a culminating step in the [oxidation](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation-reduction-reaction) of certain kinds of substances. Though oxidation was once considered to be simply the combination of [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) with any [compound](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound) or [element](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element), the meaning of the word has been expanded to include any reaction in which [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) lose [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron), thereby becoming oxidized. As has been pointed out, in any oxidation process the oxidizer takes electrons from the oxidizable substance, thereby itself becoming reduced (gaining electrons). Any substance at all can be an oxidizing agent. But these definitions, clear enough when applied to atomic structure to explain chemical reactions, are not as clearly applicable to combustion, which remains, generally speaking, a type of chemical reaction involving oxygen as the oxidizing agent but complicated by the fact that the process includes other kinds of reactions as well and by the fact that it proceeds at an unusually fast [pace](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/pace). Furthermore, most flames have a section in their structure in which, instead of oxidations, [reduction](https://www.britannica.com/science/reduction-chemistry) reactions occur. Nevertheless, the main event in combustion is often the combining of combustible material with oxygen.

# History of the study of combustion

[![combustion](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/124363-050-BDCF70B2/Fire-combustion-fuel.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/124363-050-BDCF70B2/Fire-combustion-fuel.jpg)

[combustion](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/124363-050-BDCF70B2/Fire-combustion-fuel.jpg) Fire resulting from the combustion of a fuel.(more)

Combustion, [fire](https://www.britannica.com/science/fire-combustion), and [flame](https://www.britannica.com/science/flame) have been observed and speculated about from earliest times. Every civilization has had its own explanation for them. The Greeks interpreted combustion in terms of philosophical doctrines, one of which was that a certain “inflammable principle” was contained in all combustible bodies and this principle escaped when the body was burned to react with air. A generalization of the concept was provided by the [phlogiston](https://www.britannica.com/science/phlogiston) theory, formulated in the 17th century. Treated at first as a purely [metaphysical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/metaphysical) quality, phlogiston was later conceived as a material substance having weight and, sometimes, negative weight. The inadequacy of the phlogiston theory became apparent only in the late 18th century, when it proved unable to explain a host of new facts about combustion that were being observed for the first time as the result of increasing accuracy in laboratory experiments.

The English natural philosopher [Sir Francis Bacon](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-Bacon-Viscount-Saint-Alban) observed in 1620 that a [candle](https://www.britannica.com/technology/candle) flame has a structure at about the same time that [Robert Fludd](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Fludd), an English mystic, described an experiment on combustion in a closed container in which he determined that an amount of [air](https://www.britannica.com/science/air) was used up thereby. A German physicist, [Otto von Guericke](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Otto-von-Guericke), using an air pump he had invented in 1650, demonstrated that a candle would not [burn](https://www.britannica.com/science/burn) in a container from which the air had been pumped. [Robert Hooke](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Hooke), an English scientist, in 1665 suggested that air had an active component that, upon heating, combined with combustible substances, giving rise to flame. Another idea ascribed the high temperature of flame to the fast motion of active air particles, and it was learned that [sulfur](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur) mixed with nitre can burn in the absence of air (nitre is a [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) of [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) which releases oxygen to the sulfur).

[![Antoine Lavoisier](https://cdn.britannica.com/68/115468-050-91355391/Antoine-Laurent-Lavoisier-French-work-laboratory.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/68/115468-050-91355391/Antoine-Laurent-Lavoisier-French-work-laboratory.jpg)

[Antoine Lavoisier](https://cdn.britannica.com/68/115468-050-91355391/Antoine-Laurent-Lavoisier-French-work-laboratory.jpg) French chemist Antoine Lavoisier (1743–94) at work in his laboratory.(more)

The first approximation of the true nature of combustion was posited by French chemist [Antoine-Laurent Lavoisier](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Antoine-Lavoisier): he discovered in 1772 that the products of burned sulfur or [phosphorus](https://www.britannica.com/science/phosphorus-chemical-element)—in effect their ashes—outweighed the initial substances, and he postulated that the increased weight was due to their having combined with air. Interestingly, it was already known that [metals](https://www.britannica.com/science/metal-chemistry) transformed by [heat](https://www.britannica.com/science/heat) to metallic ash weighed less than the metallic ash, but the theory was that in certain cases phlogiston in metals had a negative weight and, upon escaping during combustion, left the ash of the metal heavier than it had been with the phlogiston in it. Later Lavoisier concluded that the “fixed” air that had [combined](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/combined) with the sulfur was identical to a [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter) obtained by English chemist [Joseph Priestley](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Priestley) on heating the metallic ash of [mercury](https://www.britannica.com/science/mercury-chemical-element); that is, the “ashes” obtained when mercury was burned could be made to release the gas with which the metal had combined. This gas was also identical to that described by Swedish chemist [Carl Wilhelm Scheele](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Carl-Wilhelm-Scheele) as an active fraction of air that sustained combustion. Lavoisier called the gas “oxygen.”

Lavoisier’s theory that combustion was a reaction between the burning substance and the gas oxygen, present only to a limited extent in the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere), was based on scientific principles, the most important of which was the law of the [conservation of matter](https://www.britannica.com/science/conservation-of-mass) (after Einstein’s relativity theory, of matter and [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy)): the total amount of matter in the [universe](https://www.britannica.com/science/universe) is constant. Even ancient philosophers had guessed this law, and it was [substantiated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/substantiated) in the 17th century. Lavoisier also clarified the concept of “element” into a modern generalization, that it was a substance that could not be broken down, and this too supported his theory. Soon after, studies of gases by English chemist [John Dalton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Dalton), and the first table of [atomic weights](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-weight) that Dalton compiled, as well as many new gases discovered by other scientists, were important in supporting not only Lavoisier’s theory of combustion but his whole new system of [chemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemistry) based on accurate measurement. The discoveries of [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen) and [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) in the latter half of the 18th century, added to the earlier discoveries of [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide) and [carbon monoxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-monoxide), and the discovery that the [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) of air is remarkably constant though it is a mixture, all supported Lavoisier’s theory. The proper explanation of combustion, perhaps the oldest recognized [chemical reaction](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-reaction), is usually said to have been a keystone in the development of modern [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science).

[![Sir Humphry Davy](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/79371-050-A1BDCD67/Humphry-Davy-Thomas-Lawrence-engraving-painting-E.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/79371-050-A1BDCD67/Humphry-Davy-Thomas-Lawrence-engraving-painting-E.jpg)

[Sir Humphry Davy](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/79371-050-A1BDCD67/Humphry-Davy-Thomas-Lawrence-engraving-painting-E.jpg) Sir Humphry Davy, engraving by E. Scriven after a painting by Sir Thomas Lawrence.(more)

From 1815 to 1819 English chemist [Sir Humphry Davy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sir-Humphry-Davy-Baronet) experimented on combustion, including measurements of flame temperatures, investigations of the effect on flames of rarefied gases, and dilution with various gases; he also discovered catalytic combustion—the [oxidation](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation) of combustibles on a catalytic surface accompanied by the release of heat but without flame.

Despite these discoveries, the materialistic theory of combustion lacked a clear concept of energy and, therefore, of the critical role that energy considerations play in an accurate explanation of combustion. It was American-born English chemist [Sir Benjamin Thompson’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sir-Benjamin-Thompson-Graf-von-Rumford) experiments with heat in 1798 that revealed evidence for the concept of heat as a movement of particles. Development of a [kinetic theory of gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/kinetic-theory-of-gases), based on the [premise](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/premise) that heat results from the motion of [molecules](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) and [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom), of [thermodynamics](https://www.britannica.com/science/thermodynamics), and of thermochemistry, all in the 19th century, finally elucidated the energy aspects of combustion.

[![Bunsen burner](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/203271-050-8006A1ED/Bunsen-burner-laboratory.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/203271-050-8006A1ED/Bunsen-burner-laboratory.jpg)

[Bunsen burner](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/203271-050-8006A1ED/Bunsen-burner-laboratory.jpg) Bunsen burner in a laboratory.(more)

Investigation of burning velocities, experiments on the order of events in the combustion of gas mixtures, and study of the breaking down of gas molecules by heat (thermal dissociation), in the last half of the 19th century, played a vital part in the refinement of theories concerning the combustion mechanism. Studies of [light](https://www.britannica.com/science/light) emitted by flames led to its analysis in the spectroscope, a device that separates a mixture of light waves into the component waves, and to [spectral analysis](https://www.britannica.com/science/spectroscopy) generally, including theories of atomic and molecular spectra, which in turn contributed to an understanding of the nature of flames. The [Bunsen burner](https://www.britannica.com/science/Bunsen-burner) was also of importance in the study of flame structure. Progress in industry was a powerful stimulus in the search for clarification of flame phenomena. Explosion hazards in [coal](https://www.britannica.com/science/coal-fossil-fuel) mines had drawn attention to flame [propagation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagation) as far back as 1815, when Davy invented his [safety lamp](https://www.britannica.com/technology/safety-lamp). In 1881 detonation was discovered, and this led at the beginning of the 20th century to a detonation theory based on the assumption that a gas behaves as a fluid under certain conditions. After the 1930s [chemical kinetics](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-kinetics) became an indispensable part of flame propagation theory.

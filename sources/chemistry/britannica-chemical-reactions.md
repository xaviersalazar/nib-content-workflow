### What are the basics of chemical reactions?

- A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances, also called reactants, are converted to one or more different substances, known as products. Substances are either [chemical elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) or [compounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound).

- A chemical reaction rearranges the constituent [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) of the reactants to create different substances as products. The properties of the products are different from those of the reactants.

- Chemical reactions differ from physical changes, which include changes of state, such as [ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice) melting to [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) and water evaporating to vapor. If a physical change occurs, the physical properties of a substance will change, but its chemical identity will remain the same.


### What happens to chemical bonds when a chemical reaction takes place?

According to the modern view of chemical reactions, [bonds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-bonding) between atoms in the reactants must be broken, and the [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) or pieces of [molecules](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) are reassembled into products by forming new bonds. [Energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) is absorbed to break bonds, and energy is evolved as bonds are made. In some reactions the energy required to break bonds is larger than the energy evolved in making new bonds, and the net result is the absorption of energy. Hence, different types of bonds may be formed in a reaction. A Lewis [acid-base reaction](https://www.britannica.com/science/acid-base-reaction), for example, involves the formation of a [covalent bond](https://www.britannica.com/science/covalent-bond) between a Lewis base, a species that supplies an [electron](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) pair, and a Lewis acid, a species that can accept an electron pair. [Ammonia](https://www.britannica.com/science/ammonia) is an example of a Lewis base. A pair of electrons located on a [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen) atom may be used to form a chemical bond to a Lewis acid.

### How are chemical reactions classified?

Chemists classify chemical reactions in a number of ways: by type of product, by types of reactants, by reaction outcome, and by reaction mechanism. Often a given reaction can be placed in two or even three categories, including [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter)-forming and precipitation reactions. Many reactions produce a gas such as [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide), [hydrogen sulfide](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen-sulfide), [ammonia](https://www.britannica.com/science/ammonia), or [sulfur dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur-dioxide). Cake batter rising is caused by a gas-forming reaction between an [acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/acid) and baking soda (sodium hydrogen carbonate). Classification by types of reactants include [acid-base reactions](https://www.britannica.com/science/acid-base-reaction) and [oxidation-reduction reactions](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation-reduction-reaction), which involve the transfer of one or more electrons from a reducing agent to an oxidizing agent. Examples of classification by reaction outcome include decomposition, [polymerization](https://www.britannica.com/science/polymerization), [substitution](https://www.britannica.com/science/substitution-reaction), and [elimination](https://www.britannica.com/science/elimination-reaction) and [addition](https://www.britannica.com/science/addition-reaction) reactions. [Chain reactions](https://www.britannica.com/science/chain-reaction) and [photolysis reactions](https://www.britannica.com/science/photolysis) are examples of classification by reaction mechanism, which provides details on how [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) are shuffled and reassembled in the formation of products.

**chemical reaction**, a process in which one or more substances, the [reactants](https://www.britannica.com/science/reactant), are converted to one or more different substances, the products. Substances are either [chemical elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) or [compounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound). A chemical reaction rearranges the [constituent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituent) [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) of the reactants to create different substances as products.

Chemical reactions are an [integral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integral) part of technology, of [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture), and indeed of life itself. Burning fuels, [smelting](https://www.britannica.com/technology/smelting) [iron](https://www.britannica.com/science/iron-chemical-element), making [glass](https://www.britannica.com/technology/glass) and [pottery](https://www.britannica.com/art/pottery), brewing [beer](https://www.britannica.com/topic/beer), and making [wine](https://www.britannica.com/topic/wine) and [cheese](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cheese) are among many examples of activities incorporating chemical reactions that have been known and used for thousands of years. Chemical reactions abound in the [geology](https://www.britannica.com/science/geology) of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth), in the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere) and [oceans](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean), and in a vast array of complicated processes that occur in all living systems.

Chemical reactions must be distinguished from physical changes. Physical changes include changes of state, such as [ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice) melting to [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) and water evaporating to vapor. If a physical change occurs, the physical properties of a substance will change, but its chemical identity will remain the same. No matter what its physical state, water (H2O) is the same [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound), with each [molecule](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) composed of two atoms of [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) and one [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) of [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen). However, if water, as ice, liquid, or vapor, encounters [sodium](https://www.britannica.com/science/sodium) metal (Na), the atoms will be redistributed to give the new substances molecular hydrogen (H2) and [sodium hydroxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/sodium-hydroxide) (NaOH). By this, we know that a chemical change or reaction has occurred.

## Historical overview

The concept of a chemical reaction dates back about 250 years. It had its origins in early experiments that classified substances as [elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) and [compounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound) and in theories that explained these processes. Development of the concept of a chemical reaction had a primary role in defining the [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science) of [chemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemistry) as it is known today.

The first [substantive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/substantive) studies in this area were on [gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter). The identification of oxygen in the 18th century by Swedish chemist [Carl Wilhelm Scheele](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Carl-Wilhelm-Scheele) and English clergyman [Joseph Priestley](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Priestley) had particular significance. The influence of French chemist [Antoine-Laurent Lavoisier](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Antoine-Lavoisier) was especially notable, in that his insights confirmed the importance of quantitative measurements of chemical processes. In his book _Traité élémentaire de chimie_ (1789; _Elementary Treatise on Chemistry_), Lavoisier identified 33 “elements”—substances not broken down into simpler entities. Among his many discoveries, Lavoisier accurately measured the weight gained when elements were oxidized, and he ascribed the result to the combining of the element with [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen). The [concept](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/concept) of chemical reactions involving the combination of elements clearly emerged from his writing, and his approach led others to pursue experimental chemistry as a quantitative science.

The other occurrence of historical significance concerning chemical reactions was the development of [atomic theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-theory). For this, much credit goes to English chemist [John Dalton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Dalton), who postulated his atomic theory early in the 19th century. Dalton maintained that matter is composed of small, indivisible particles, that the particles, or [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom), of each element were unique, and that chemical reactions were involved in rearranging atoms to form new substances. This view of chemical reactions accurately defines the current subject. Dalton’s theory provided a basis for understanding the results of earlier experimentalists, including the [law of conservation](https://www.britannica.com/science/conservation-law) of matter (matter is neither created nor destroyed) and the [law of constant composition](https://www.britannica.com/science/law-of-definite-proportions) (all samples of a substance have identical elemental compositions).

Thus, experiment and theory, the two [cornerstones](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/cornerstones) of chemical science in the modern world, together defined the concept of chemical reactions. Today experimental chemistry provides innumerable examples, and theoretical chemistry allows an understanding of their meaning.

## [Synthesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-synthesis)

When making a new substance from other substances, chemists say either that they carry out a [synthesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-synthesis) or that they synthesize the new material. Reactants are converted to products, and the process is symbolized by a [chemical equation](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-equation). For example, [iron](https://www.britannica.com/science/iron-chemical-element) (Fe) and [sulfur](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur) (S) combine to form iron sulfide (FeS). Fe(s) + S(s) → FeS(s) The plus sign indicates that iron reacts with sulfur. The arrow signifies that the reaction “forms” or “yields” iron sulfide, the product. The state of matter of reactants and products is [designated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/designated) with the symbols (s) for [solids](https://www.britannica.com/science/solid-state-of-matter), (l) for [liquids](https://www.britannica.com/science/liquid-state-of-matter), and (g) for [gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter).

# The [conservation of matter](https://www.britannica.com/science/conservation-of-mass)

[![Balancing atoms in chemical reactions explained](https://cdn.britannica.com/28/150928-138-F3713DB9/people-change-matter-representation-reaction-five-dollars.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/people-change-matter-representation-reaction-five-dollars/-158559)

Balancing atoms in chemical reactions explained Two people making change for five dollars as a representation of how matter is conserved in a chemical reaction.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-reaction/images-videos)

In reactions under normal laboratory conditions, matter is neither created nor destroyed, and elements are not transformed into other elements. Therefore, equations depicting reactions must be balanced; that is, the same number of [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) of each kind must appear on opposite sides of the equation. The balanced equation for the iron-sulfur reaction shows that one [iron](https://www.britannica.com/science/iron-chemical-element) [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) can react with one [sulfur](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur) atom to give one formula unit of iron sulfide.

Chemists ordinarily [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics) with weighable quantities of [elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) and [compounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound). For example, in the iron-sulfur equation the symbol Fe represents 55.845 grams of iron, S represents 32.066 grams of sulfur, and FeS represents 87.911 grams of iron sulfide. Because matter is not created or destroyed in a chemical reaction, the total mass of reactants is the same as the total mass of products. If some other amount of iron is used, say, one-tenth as much (5.585 grams), only one-tenth as much sulfur can be [consumed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/consumed) (3.207 grams), and only one-tenth as much iron sulfide is produced (8.791 grams). If 32.066 grams of sulfur were initially present with 5.585 grams of iron, then 28.859 grams of sulfur would be left over when the reaction was complete. The reactant that is completely consumed first and that therefore limits the amount of product that can be formed is known as the limiting reagent (or limiting reactant).

The reaction of [methane](https://www.britannica.com/science/methane) (CH4, a major component of [natural gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-gas)) with molecular [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) (O2) to produce [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide) (CO2) and [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) can be depicted by the [chemical equation](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-equation) CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(l) Here another feature of chemical equations appears. The number 2 preceding O2 and [H](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) 2O is a stoichiometric factor. (The number 1 preceding CH4 and CO2 is implied.) This indicates that one [molecule](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) of methane reacts with two molecules of oxygen to produce one molecule of carbon dioxide and two molecules of water. The equation is balanced because the same number of atoms of each element appears on both sides of the equation (here one carbon, four hydrogen, and four oxygen atoms). Analogously with the iron-sulfur example, we can say that 16 grams of methane and 64 grams of oxygen will produce 44 grams of carbon dioxide and 36 grams of water. That is, 80 grams of reactants will lead to 80 grams of products.

The ratio of reactants and products in a chemical reaction is called chemical [stoichiometry](https://www.britannica.com/science/stoichiometry). Stoichiometry depends on the fact that matter is conserved in chemical processes, and calculations giving mass relationships are based on the concept of the [mole](https://www.britannica.com/science/mole-chemistry). One mole of any element or [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) contains the same number of atoms or molecules, respectively, as one mole of any other element or compound. By [international agreement](https://www.britannica.com/topic/international-agreement), one mole of the most common [isotope](https://www.britannica.com/science/isotope) of [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element) (carbon-12) has a mass of exactly 12 grams (this is called the molar mass) and represents 6.022140857 × 1023 atoms ( [Avogadro’s number](https://www.britannica.com/science/Avogadros-number)). One mole of iron contains 55.847 grams; one mole of methane contains 16.043 grams; one mole of molecular oxygen is equivalent to 31.999 grams; and one mole of water is 18.015 grams. Each of these masses represents 6.022140857 × 1023 molecules.

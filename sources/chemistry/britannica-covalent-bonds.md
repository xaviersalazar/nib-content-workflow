**covalent bond**, in [chemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemistry), the interatomic linkage that results from the sharing of an [electron](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) pair between two atoms. The binding arises from the electrostatic attraction of their nuclei for the same electrons. A covalent bond forms when the bonded atoms have a lower total energy than that of widely separated atoms.

A brief treatment of covalent bonds follows. For full treatment, _see_ [chemical bonding: Covalent bonds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-bonding/Covalent-bonds#ref43403).

Molecules that have covalent linkages include the inorganic substances hydrogen, nitrogen, chlorine, water, and ammonia (H2, N2, Cl2, H2O, NH3) together with all organic [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds). A [single](https://www.britannica.com/science/single-bond) line indicates a bond between two atoms (i.e., involving one electron pair), double lines (=) indicate a [double bond](https://www.britannica.com/science/double-bond) between two atoms (i.e., involving two electron pairs), and triple lines (≡) represent a [triple bond](https://www.britannica.com/science/triple-bond), as found, for example, in [carbon monoxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-monoxide) (C≡O). Single bonds consist of one sigma (σ) bond, double bonds have one σ and one pi (π) bond, and triple bonds have one σ and two π bonds.

Covalent bonds are directional, meaning that atoms so bonded prefer specific orientations relative to one another; this in turn gives molecules definite shapes, as in the angular (bent) structure of the H2O molecule. Covalent bonds between identical atoms (as in H2) are nonpolar—i.e., electrically uniform—while those between unlike atoms are polar—i.e., one [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) is slightly negatively charged and the other is slightly positively charged. This partial ionic character of covalent bonds increases with the difference in the electronegativities of the two atoms. _See also_ [ionic bond](https://www.britannica.com/science/ionic-bond).

When none of the elements in a [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) is a metal, no atoms in the compound have an [ionization energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/ionization-energy) low enough for electron loss to be likely. In such a case, covalence prevails. As a general rule, [covalent bonds](https://www.britannica.com/science/covalent-bond) are formed between elements lying toward the right in the [periodic table](https://www.britannica.com/science/periodic-table) (i.e., the nonmetals). Molecules of identical atoms, such as H2 and buckminsterfullerene (C60), are also held together by covalent bonds.

## Lewis formulation of a covalent bond

The idea that two electrons can be shared between two atoms and serve as the link between them was first introduced in 1916 by the American chemist G.N. [Lewis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gilbert-N-Lewis), who described the formation of such bonds as resulting from the tendencies of certain atoms to [combine](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/combine) with one another in order for both to have the [electronic structure](https://www.britannica.com/science/electronic-configuration) of a corresponding noble-gas atom.

In Lewis terms a covalent bond is a shared electron pair. The bond between a [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) atom and a [chlorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/chlorine) atom in [hydrogen chloride](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen-chloride) is formulated as follows:

![The bond between a hydrogen atom and a chlorine atom in hydrogen chloride is formulated as per this process. (Lewis formulation of a covalent bond.)](https://cdn.britannica.com/58/16158-004-921FBBEC/bond-hydrogen-atom-chlorine-chloride-process.jpg)

In a Lewis structure of a covalent compound, the shared electron pair between the hydrogen and chlorine ions is represented by a line. The electron pair is called a bonding pair; the three other pairs of electrons on the chlorine atom are called lone pairs and play no direct role in holding the two atoms together.

Each atom in the hydrogen chloride molecule [attains](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/attains) a closed-shell octet of electrons by sharing and hence achieves a maximum lowering of energy. In general, an incomplete shell means that some attracting power of a nucleus may be wasted, and adding [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) beyond a closed shell would entail the energetic disadvantage of beginning the next shell of the atom concerned. Lewis’s octet rule is again applicable and is seen to represent the extreme means of achieving lower energy rather than being a goal in itself.

A covalent bond forms if the bonded atoms have a lower total energy than the widely separated atoms. The simplest interpretation of the decrease in energy that occurs when electrons are shared is that both electrons lie between two attracting centres (the nuclei of the two atoms linked by the bond) and hence lie lower in energy than when they experience the attraction of a single centre.

Lewis structures of more complex molecules can be constructed quite simply by extending the process that has been described for hydrogen chloride. First, the [valence](https://www.britannica.com/science/valence-chemistry) electrons that are available for bonding are counted (2 × 1 + 6 = 8 in H2O, for example, and 4 + 4 × 7 = 32 in [carbon tetrachloride](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-tetrachloride), CCl4), and the chemical symbols for the elements are placed in the arrangement that reflects which are neighbours:

![Step 1 in the construction of a Lewis structure for hydrogen chloride.](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/16157-004-FD6DECB7/Step-1-construction-structure-hydrogen-chloride-Lewis.jpg)

Next, one bonding pair is added between each linked pair of atoms:

![Step 2 in the construction of a Lewis structure for hydrogen chloride.](https://cdn.britannica.com/56/16156-004-4FC1BE7A/Step-2-construction-structure-hydrogen-chloride-Lewis.jpg)

The remaining electrons are then added to the atoms in such a way that each atom has a share in an octet of electrons (this is the octet-rule part of the procedure):

![Step 3 in the construction of a Lewis structure for hydrogen chloride.](https://cdn.britannica.com/55/16155-004-834C8D86/Step-3-construction-structure-hydrogen-chloride-Lewis.jpg)

Finally, each bonding pair is represented by a dash:

![Step 4 in the construction of a Lewis structure for hydrogen chloride.](https://cdn.britannica.com/54/16154-004-F2D6EA38/Step-4-construction-structure-hydrogen-chloride-Lewis.jpg)

(Note that Lewis structures do not necessarily show the actual shape of the molecule, only the topological pattern of their bonds.)

In some older formulations of Lewis structures, a distinction was made between bonds formed by electrons that have been supplied by both atoms (as in H―Cl, where one shared electron can be regarded as supplied by the hydrogen atom and the other by the chlorine atom) and covalent bonds formed when both electrons can be regarded as supplied by one atom, as in the formation of OH− from O2− and H+. Such a bond was called a coordinate covalent bond or a dative bond and symbolized O → H−. However, the difficulties [encountered](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/encountered) in the attempt to keep track of the origin of bonding electrons and the suggestion that a coordinate covalent bond differs somehow from a covalent bond (it does not) have led to this usage falling into disfavour.

# Advanced aspects of Lewis structures

The Lewis structures illustrated so far have been selected for their simplicity. A number of elaborations are given below.

## Resonance

There is sometimes an [ambiguity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ambiguity) in the location of double bonds. This ambiguity is illustrated by the Lewis structure for [ozone](https://www.britannica.com/science/ozone) (O3). The following are two possible structures:

![Ambiguity in the Lewis structure for ozone; two possible structures.](https://cdn.britannica.com/51/16151-004-A9BC6D43/Ambiguity-structure-ozone-Lewis-structures.jpg)

In such cases, the actual Lewis structure is regarded as a blend of these contributions and is written:

![The accepted Lewis structure for ozone.](https://cdn.britannica.com/50/16150-004-EE4BE5F3/structure-Lewis-ozone.jpg)

The blending together of these structures is actually a [quantum](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/quantum) mechanical phenomenon called [resonance](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/resonance). At this stage, resonance can be regarded as a blending process that spreads double-bond character evenly over the atoms that participate in it. In ozone, for instance, each oxygen-oxygen bond is rendered equivalent by resonance, and each one has a mixture of single-bond and double-bond character (as indicated by its length and strength).

## Hypervalence

Lewis structures and the octet rule jointly offer a [succinct](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/succinct) indication of the type of bonding that occurs in molecules and show the pattern of single and multiple bonds between the atoms. There are many [compounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound), however, that do not conform to the octet rule. The most common exceptions to the octet rule are the so-called hypervalent [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds). These are species in which there are more atoms attached to a central [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) than can be accommodated by an octet of electrons. An example is sulfur hexafluoride, SF6, for which writing a Lewis structure with six S―F bonds requires that at least 12 electrons be present around the [sulfur](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur) atom:

![Lewis structure for the hypervalent compound, sulfer hexafluoride, SF6.](https://cdn.britannica.com/49/16149-004-671C687B/structure-Lewis-hypervalent-compound-SF6-sulfer-hexafluoride.jpg)

(Only the bonding electrons are shown here.) In Lewis terms, hypervalence requires the expansion of the octet to 10, 12, and even in some cases 16 electrons. Hypervalent compounds are very common and in general are no less stable than compounds that conform to the octet rule.

The existence of hypervalent compounds would appear to deal a severe blow to the validity of the octet rule and Lewis’s approach to covalent bonding if the expansion of the octet could not be rationalized or its occurrence predicted. Fortunately, it can be rationalized, and the occurrence of hypervalence can be anticipated. In simple terms, experience has shown that hypervalence is rare in periods 1 and 2 of the [periodic table](https://www.britannica.com/science/periodic-table) (through [neon](https://www.britannica.com/science/neon-chemical-element)) but is common in and after period 3. Thus, the octet rule can be used with confidence for [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element), nitrogen, oxygen, and [fluorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/fluorine), but hypervalence must be anticipated thereafter. The [conventional](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conventional) explanation of this distinction takes note of the fact that in period-3 elements the [valence](https://www.britannica.com/science/valence-chemistry) shell has _n_ = 3, and this is the first shell in which _d_ orbitals are available. (These orbitals are occupied after the 4 _s_ orbitals have been filled and account for the occurrence of the transition metals in period 4.) It is therefore argued that atoms of this and subsequent periods can use the empty _d_ orbitals to accommodate electrons beyond an octet and hence permit the formation of hypervalent species.

In chemistry, however, it is important not to allow mere correlations to masquerade as explanations. Although it is true that _d_ orbitals are energetically accessible in elements that display hypervalence, it does not follow that they are responsible for it. Indeed, quantum mechanical theories of the chemical bond do not need to [invoke](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/invoke) _d_-orbital involvement. These theories suggest that hypervalence is probably no more than a consequence of the greater radii of the atoms of period-3 elements compared with those of period 2, with the result that a central atom can pack more atoms around itself. Thus, hypervalence is more a steric (geometric) problem than an outcome of _d_-orbital availability.

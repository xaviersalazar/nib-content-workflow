Top Questions


### What is the periodic table?

The [periodic table](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Interactive-Periodic-Table-of-the-Elements) is a tabular array of the [chemical elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) organized by [atomic number](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-number), from the element with the lowest atomic number, [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen), to the element with the highest atomic number, [oganesson](https://www.britannica.com/science/element-118). The atomic number of an element is the number of [protons](https://www.britannica.com/science/proton-subatomic-particle) in the nucleus of an [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) of that element. Hydrogen has 1 proton, and [oganesson](https://www.britannica.com/science/element-118) has 118.

### How many elements are on the periodic table?

There are 118 elements on the periodic table. The elements are arranged in order of increasing [atomic number](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-number), which is the number of [protons](https://www.britannica.com/science/proton-subatomic-particle) in that atom’s [nucleus](https://www.britannica.com/). [Hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen), the element with the lowest atomic number, has 1 proton; [oganesson](https://www.britannica.com/science/element-118), the element with the highest atomic number, has 118.

### What do periodic table groups have in common?

The groups of the periodic table are displayed as vertical columns numbered from 1 to 18. The [elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) in a group have very similar chemical properties, which arise from the number of valence electrons present—that is, the number of [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) in the outermost shell of an [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom).

### Where does the periodic table come from?

The periodic table came from chemists in the 19th century realizing that elements could be grouped together based on their properties. In 1869 Russian chemist [Dmitri Mendeleev](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Dmitri-Mendeleev) grouped the known elements into a table with 17 columns. He revised his table in 1871 and was able to predict the existence of new elements as well as show that the [atomic weights](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-weight) for some elements were inaccurate.

### How is the periodic table organized?

The arrangement of the [elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) in the periodic table comes from the electronic configuration of the elements. Because of the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pauli-exclusion-principle), no more than two [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) can fill the same orbital. The first row of the periodic table consists of just two elements, [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) and [helium](https://www.britannica.com/science/helium-chemical-element). As [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) have more electrons, they have more orbits available to fill, and thus the rows contain more elements farther down in the table.

### When did Dmitri Mendeleev present the first periodic table?

The first periodic table was presented on March 6, 1869, and revised in 1871 by Russian chemist [Dmitri Mendeleev](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Dmitri-Mendeleev), who placed elements in columns according to their chemical properties. Based on the periodic law that guided the arrangement of the table, Mendeleev predicted the existence and properties of three elements that had not yet been discovered: [scandium](https://www.britannica.com/science/scandium), [gallium](https://www.britannica.com/science/gallium), and [germanium](https://www.britannica.com/science/germanium).

### Why does the periodic table split?

The [periodic table](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Interactive-Periodic-Table-of-the-Elements) has two rows at the bottom that are usually split out from the main body of the table. These rows contain [elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) in the [lanthanoid](https://www.britannica.com/science/lanthanide) and [actinoid](https://www.britannica.com/science/actinoid-element) series, usually from 57 to 71 ( [lanthanum](https://www.britannica.com/science/lanthanum) to [lutetium](https://www.britannica.com/science/lutetium)) and 89 to 103 ( [actinium](https://www.britannica.com/science/actinium) to [lawrencium](https://www.britannica.com/science/lawrencium)), respectively. There is no scientific reason for this. It is done merely to make the table more compact for display.

**periodic table**, in [chemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemistry), the organized [array](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/array) of all the [chemical elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) in order of increasing [atomic number](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-number)—i.e., the total number of [protons](https://www.britannica.com/science/proton-subatomic-particle) in the atomic nucleus. When the chemical elements are thus arranged, there is a recurring pattern called the “periodic law” in their properties, in which elements in the same column (group) have similar properties. The initial discovery of this pattern by [Dmitri I. Mendeleev](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Dmitri-Mendeleev) in the mid-19th century has been of inestimable value in the development of [chemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemistry).

It was not recognized until the 1910s that the order of elements in the periodic system is that of their [atomic numbers](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-number), which are equal to the positive electrical charges of the [atomic nuclei](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-nucleus) expressed in electronic units. In [subsequent](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/subsequent) years great progress was made in explaining the periodic law in terms of the [electronic structure](https://www.britannica.com/science/electronic-configuration) of [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) and [molecules](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule). This clarification has increased the value of the law, which is used as much today as it was at the beginning of the 20th century, when it expressed the only known relationship among the elements.

# The periodic table

## Periods

![Periodic table with atomic number, symbol, and atomic weight](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/220013-050-16EC5F2F/periodic-table-atomic-weight.jpg)

[Periodic table with atomic number, symbol, and atomic weight](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/220013-050-16EC5F2F/periodic-table-atomic-weight.jpg) The table showing periodic arrangement of elements with their atomic numbers, symbols, and atomic weights.(more)

The periodic table of the elements contains all of the [chemical elements](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) that have been discovered or made; they are arranged, in the order of their [atomic numbers](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-number), in seven horizontal periods, with the [lanthanoids](https://www.britannica.com/science/lanthanide) ( [lanthanum](https://www.britannica.com/science/lanthanum), 57, to [lutetium](https://www.britannica.com/science/lutetium), 71) and the [actinoids](https://www.britannica.com/science/actinoid-element) ( [actinium](https://www.britannica.com/science/actinium), 89, to [lawrencium](https://www.britannica.com/science/lawrencium), 103) indicated separately below. The periods are of varying lengths. First there is the hydrogen period, consisting of the two elements [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen), 1, and [helium](https://www.britannica.com/science/helium-chemical-element), 2\. Then there are two periods of eight elements each: the first short period, from [lithium](https://www.britannica.com/science/lithium-chemical-element), 3, to [neon](https://www.britannica.com/science/neon-chemical-element), 10, and the second short period, from [sodium](https://www.britannica.com/science/sodium), 11, to [argon](https://www.britannica.com/science/argon-chemical-element), 18\. There follow two periods of 18 elements each: the first long period, from [potassium](https://www.britannica.com/science/potassium), 19, to [krypton](https://www.britannica.com/science/krypton-chemical-element), 36, and the second long period, from [rubidium](https://www.britannica.com/science/rubidium), 37, to [xenon](https://www.britannica.com/science/xenon), 54\. The first very long period of 32 elements, from [cesium](https://www.britannica.com/science/cesium), 55, to [radon](https://www.britannica.com/science/radon), 86, is condensed into 18 columns by the omission of the [lanthanoids](https://www.britannica.com/science/lanthanide) (which are indicated separately below), permitting the remaining 18 elements, which are closely similar in their properties to corresponding elements of the first and second long periods, to be placed directly below these elements. The second very long period, from [francium](https://www.britannica.com/science/francium), 87, to [oganesson](https://www.britannica.com/science/element-118), 118, is likewise condensed into 18 columns by the omission of the [actinoids](https://www.britannica.com/science/actinoid-element).

## Classification of elements into groups

[![Understanding the Organization of the Periodic Table](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/216557-138-6420551F/How-the-periodic-table-organizes-the-elements.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/How-the-periodic-table-organizes-the-elements/-246857)

Understanding the Organization of the Periodic TableAn overview of how the periodic table organizes the elements into columns and rows.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/science/periodic-table/images-videos)

The six [noble gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/noble-gas)—helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, and radon—occur at the ends of the six completed periods and [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) the Group 18 (0) [group](https://www.britannica.com/science/group-periodic-table) of the periodic system. It is customary to refer to horizontal series of elements in the table as periods and vertical series as groups. The seven elements lithium to fluorine and the seven corresponding elements sodium to chlorine are placed in the seven groups, 1 (Ia), 2 (IIa), 13 (IIIa), 14 (IVa), 15 (Va), 16 (VIa), and 17 (VIIa), respectively. The 17 elements of the fourth period, from potassium, 19, to bromine, 35, are distinct in their properties and are considered to constitute Groups 1–17 (Ia–VIIa) of the periodic system.

The first group, the [alkali metals](https://www.britannica.com/science/alkali-metal), thereby includes, in addition to lithium and sodium, the metals from [potassium](https://www.britannica.com/science/potassium) down the table to [francium](https://www.britannica.com/science/francium) but not the much less similar metals of Group 11 (Ib; [copper](https://www.britannica.com/science/copper), etc.). Also the second group, the [alkaline-earth metals](https://www.britannica.com/science/alkaline-earth-metal), is considered to include [beryllium](https://www.britannica.com/science/beryllium), [magnesium](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnesium), [calcium](https://www.britannica.com/science/calcium), [strontium](https://www.britannica.com/science/strontium), [barium](https://www.britannica.com/science/barium), and [radium](https://www.britannica.com/science/radium) but not the elements of Group 12 (IIb). The [boron group](https://www.britannica.com/science/boron-group-element) includes those elements in Group 13 (IIIa). The other four groups are as follows: The [carbon group](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-group-element), 14 (IVa), consists of [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element), [silicon](https://www.britannica.com/science/silicon), [germanium](https://www.britannica.com/science/germanium), [tin](https://www.britannica.com/science/tin), [lead](https://www.britannica.com/science/lead-chemical-element), and [flerovium](https://www.britannica.com/science/flerovium); the [nitrogen group](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen-group-element), 15 (Va), includes [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen), [phosphorus](https://www.britannica.com/science/phosphorus-chemical-element), [arsenic](https://www.britannica.com/science/arsenic), [antimony](https://www.britannica.com/science/antimony), [bismuth](https://www.britannica.com/science/bismuth), and [moscovium](https://www.britannica.com/science/element-115); the [oxygen group](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen-group-element), 16 (VIa), includes [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen), [sulfur](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur), [selenium](https://www.britannica.com/science/selenium), [tellurium](https://www.britannica.com/science/tellurium), [polonium](https://www.britannica.com/science/polonium), and [livermorium](https://www.britannica.com/science/livermorium); and the [halogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/halogen) group, 17 (VIIa), includes [fluorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/fluorine), [chlorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/chlorine), [bromine](https://www.britannica.com/science/bromine), [iodine](https://www.britannica.com/science/iodine), [astatine](https://www.britannica.com/science/astatine), and [tennessine](https://www.britannica.com/science/element-117).

Although [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) is included in Group 1 (Ia), it is not closely similar to either the alkali metals or the halogens in its chemical properties. It is, however, assigned the [oxidation number](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation-number) +1 in [compounds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound) such as hydrogen fluoride, HF, and −1 in [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds) such as lithium hydride, LiH; and it may hence be considered as being similar to a Group 1 (Ia) element and to a Group 17 (VIIa) element, respectively, in compounds of these two types, taking the place first of Li and then of F in lithium fluoride, LiF. Hydrogen is, in fact, the most individualistic of the elements: No other element resembles it in the way that [sodium](https://www.britannica.com/science/sodium) resembles lithium, [chlorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/chlorine) resembles [fluorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/fluorine), and [neon](https://www.britannica.com/science/neon-chemical-element) resembles [helium](https://www.britannica.com/science/helium-chemical-element). It is a unique element, the only element that cannot conveniently be considered a member of a group.

A number of the elements of each long period are called the [transition metals](https://www.britannica.com/science/transition-metal). These are usually taken to be [scandium](https://www.britannica.com/science/scandium), 21, to [zinc](https://www.britannica.com/science/zinc), 30 (the iron-group [transition](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/transition) metals); [yttrium](https://www.britannica.com/science/yttrium), 39, to [cadmium](https://www.britannica.com/science/cadmium), 48 (the palladium-group transition metals); and [hafnium](https://www.britannica.com/science/hafnium), 72, to [mercury](https://www.britannica.com/science/mercury-chemical-element), 80 (the platinum-group transition metals). By this definition, the transition metals include Groups 3 to 12 (IIIb to VIIIb, and Ib and IIb).

## Periodic trends in properties

The periodicity in properties of the elements arranged in order of [atomic number](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-number) is strikingly shown by the consideration of the physical state of the elementary substances and such related properties as the [melting point](https://www.britannica.com/science/melting-point), density, and hardness. The elements of Group 18 (0) are [gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter) that are difficult to condense. The [alkali metals](https://www.britannica.com/science/alkali-metal), in Group 1 (Ia), are soft metallic solids with low melting points. The [alkaline-earth metals](https://www.britannica.com/science/alkaline-earth-metal), in Group 2 (IIa), are harder and have higher melting points than the [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) alkali metals. The hardness and melting point continue to increase through Groups 13 (IIIa) and 14 (IVa) and then decrease through Groups 15 (Va), 16 (VIa), and 17 (VIIa). The elements of the long periods show a gradual increase in hardness and melting point from the beginning alkali metals to near the center of the period and then at Group 16 (VIa) an irregular decrease to the [halogens](https://www.britannica.com/science/halogen) and [noble gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/noble-gas).

The [valence](https://www.britannica.com/science/valence-chemistry) of the elements (that is, the number of [bonds](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-bonding) formed with a standard element) is closely correlated with position in the periodic table, the elements in the main groups having maximum positive valence, or [oxidation number](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation-number), equal to the group number and maximum negative valence equal to the difference between eight and the group number.

[Metallic elements in the periodic table](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/7339-050-3CE7A8ED/Metals-nonmetals-metalloids-regions-periodic-table.jpg) Metals, nonmetals, and metalloids are represented in different regions of the periodic table.(more)

The general chemical properties described as [metallic](https://www.britannica.com/science/metal-chemistry) or [base](https://www.britannica.com/science/base-chemical-compound) forming, [metalloid](https://www.britannica.com/science/metalloid) or amphoteric, and [nonmetallic](https://www.britannica.com/science/nonmetal) or [acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/acid) forming are [correlated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/correlated) with the periodic table in a simple way: The most metallic elements are to the left and to the bottom of the periodic table and the most nonmetallic elements are to the right and to the top (ignoring the noble gases). The [metalloids](https://www.britannica.com/science/metalloid) are adjacent to a diagonal line from [boron](https://www.britannica.com/science/boron-chemical-element) to [polonium](https://www.britannica.com/science/polonium).

![Electronegativity values of the elements in the periodic table](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/7341-050-89DD736C/periodic-table-electronegativity-value-element-atom-ability-electron-pairs.jpg)

[Electronegativity values of the elements in the periodic table](https://cdn.britannica.com/41/7341-050-89DD736C/periodic-table-electronegativity-value-element-atom-ability-electron-pairs.jpg) Each element has an electronegativity value, which is a measure of the ability of an atom to attract and share electron pairs of another atom.(more)

A closely related property is [electronegativity](https://www.britannica.com/science/electronegativity), the tendency of [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) to retain their [electrons](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron) and to attract additional electrons. The degree of electronegativity of an element is shown by [ionization potential](https://www.britannica.com/science/ionization-energy), [electron affinity](https://www.britannica.com/science/electron-affinity), [oxidation-reduction potential](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxidation-number), the energy of formation of chemical bonds, and other properties. It is shown to depend upon the element’s position in the periodic table in the same way that nonmetallic character does, [fluorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/fluorine) being the most electronegative element and [cesium](https://www.britannica.com/science/cesium) (or [francium](https://www.britannica.com/science/francium)) the least electronegative (most electropositive) element.

The sizes of [atoms](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) of elements vary regularly throughout the periodic system. Thus, the effective bonding radius (or one-half the distance between [adjacent](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/adjacent) atoms) in the elementary substances in their [crystalline](https://www.britannica.com/science/crystal) or [molecular](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) forms decreases through the first short period from 1.52 Å for lithium to 0.73 Å for fluorine; at the beginning of the second period, the bonding radius rises abruptly to 1.86 Å for sodium and gradually decreases to 0.99 Å for chlorine. The behavior through the long periods is more complex: The bonding radius decreases gradually from 2.31 Å for [potassium](https://www.britannica.com/science/potassium) to a minimum of 1.25 Å for [cobalt](https://www.britannica.com/science/cobalt-chemical-element) and [nickel](https://www.britannica.com/science/nickel-chemical-element), then rises slightly, and finally falls to 1.14 Å for [bromine](https://www.britannica.com/science/bromine). The sizes of atoms are of importance in the determination of [coordination number](https://www.britannica.com/science/coordination-number) (that is, the number of groups attached to the central [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) in a [compound](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-compound)) and hence in the [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) of compounds.

The increase in [atomic size](https://www.britannica.com/science/atomic-radius) from the upper right corner of the periodic table to the lower left corner is reflected in the formulas of the [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) acids of the elements in their highest states of oxidation. The smallest atoms group only three oxygen atoms about themselves; the next larger atoms, which coordinate a tetrahedron of four oxygen atoms, are in a diagonal belt; and the still larger atoms, which form octahedral oxygen complexes (stannic acid, antimonic acid, telluric acid, paraperiodic acid), lie below and to the left of this belt. Only the chemical and physical properties of the elements are determined by the extranuclear electronic structure; these properties show the periodicity described in the periodic law. The properties of the atomic nuclei themselves, such as the magnitude of the packing fraction and the power of entering into nuclear reactions, are, although dependent upon the atomic number, not dependent in the same periodic way.

### What is a polymer?

A polymer is any of a class of natural or synthetic substances composed of very large molecules, called macromolecules, which are multiples of simpler chemical units called monomers. Polymers make up many of the materials in living organisms and are the basis of many minerals and man-made materials.

### Why are organic polymers important?

Organic polymers play a crucial role in living things, providing basic structural materials and participating in vital life processes. For example, the solid parts of all plants are composed of polymers. These include cellulose, lignin, and various resins.

### What are the examples of natural polymers?

Natural polymers include proteins, which are polymers of amino acids, and nucleic acids, which are polymers of nucleotides—complex molecules composed of nitrogen-containing bases, sugars, and phosphoric acid. Starches, an important source of food energy derived from plants, are natural polymers composed of glucose.

**polymer**, any of a class of natural or [synthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/synthetic) substances composed of very large molecules, called macromolecules, that are multiples of simpler chemical units called [monomers](https://www.britannica.com/science/monomer). Polymers make up many of the materials in living organisms, including, for example, [proteins](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein), [cellulose](https://www.britannica.com/science/cellulose), and [nucleic acids](https://www.britannica.com/science/nucleic-acid). Moreover, they [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) the basis of such minerals as [diamond](https://www.britannica.com/topic/diamond-gemstone), [quartz](https://www.britannica.com/science/quartz), and [feldspar](https://www.britannica.com/science/feldspar) and such man-made materials as [concrete](https://www.britannica.com/technology/concrete-building-material), [glass](https://www.britannica.com/technology/glass), [paper](https://www.britannica.com/technology/paper), [plastics](https://www.britannica.com/science/plastic), and [rubbers](https://www.britannica.com/science/rubber-chemical-compound).

The word _polymer_ designates an unspecified number of [monomer](https://www.britannica.com/science/monomer) units. When the number of monomers is very large, the [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) is sometimes called a high polymer. Polymers are not restricted to monomers of the same chemical [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) or [molecular weight](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecular-weight) and structure. Some natural polymers are composed of one kind of monomer. Most natural and synthetic polymers, however, are made up of two or more different types of monomers; such polymers are known as copolymers.

## Natural polymers: organic and inorganic

[![natural rubber](https://cdn.britannica.com/26/157326-050-CB459F5B/Latex-rubber-tree-Malaysia.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/26/157326-050-CB459F5B/Latex-rubber-tree-Malaysia.jpg)

[natural rubber](https://cdn.britannica.com/26/157326-050-CB459F5B/Latex-rubber-tree-Malaysia.jpg) Latex tapped from a rubber tree ( _Hevea brasiliensis_) in Malaysia.(more)

Organic polymers play a crucial role in living things, providing basic structural materials and participating in vital life processes. For example, the [solid](https://www.britannica.com/science/solid-state-of-matter) parts of all [plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant) are made up of polymers. These include cellulose, [lignin](https://www.britannica.com/science/lignin), and various [resins](https://www.britannica.com/science/resin). Cellulose is a [polysaccharide](https://www.britannica.com/science/polysaccharide), a polymer that is composed of sugar molecules. Lignin consists of a complicated three-dimensional network of polymers. Wood resins are polymers of a simple hydrocarbon, [isoprene](https://www.britannica.com/science/isoprene). Another familiar isoprene polymer is [rubber](https://www.britannica.com/science/rubber-chemical-compound).

# Synthetic polymers

[Synthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Synthetic) polymers are produced in different types of reactions. Many simple [hydrocarbons](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrocarbon), such as [ethylene](https://www.britannica.com/science/ethylene) and [propylene](https://www.britannica.com/science/propylene), can be transformed into polymers by adding one [monomer](https://www.britannica.com/science/monomer) after another to the growing chain. [Polyethylene](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyethylene), composed of repeating ethylene monomers, is an addition polymer. It may have as many as 10,000 monomers joined in long coiled chains. Polyethylene is crystalline, translucent, and thermoplastic—i.e., it softens when heated. It is used for coatings, packaging, molded parts, and the manufacture of bottles and containers. [Polypropylene](https://www.britannica.com/science/polypropylene) is also crystalline and thermoplastic but is harder than polyethylene. Its molecules may consist of from 50,000 to 200,000 monomers. This [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) is used in the [textile](https://www.britannica.com/topic/textile) [industry](https://www.britannica.com/money/industry) and to make molded objects.

[![polystyrene](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/131876-004-9D64B37B/Polystyrene-packaging.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/131876-004-9D64B37B/Polystyrene-packaging.jpg)

[polystyrene](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/131876-004-9D64B37B/Polystyrene-packaging.jpg) Polystyrene packaging.(more)

Other addition polymers include polybutadiene, [polyisoprene](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyisoprene), and [polychloroprene](https://www.britannica.com/science/neoprene), which are all important in the manufacture of synthetic rubbers. Some polymers, such as [polystyrene](https://www.britannica.com/science/polystyrene), are glassy and transparent at room temperature, as well as being thermoplastic. Polystyrene can be coloured any shade and is used in the manufacture of toys and other [plastic](https://www.britannica.com/science/plastic) objects.

[![PVC piping](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/176309-050-90176449/Polyvinyl-chloride-pipes.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/176309-050-90176449/Polyvinyl-chloride-pipes.jpg)

[PVC piping](https://cdn.britannica.com/09/176309-050-90176449/Polyvinyl-chloride-pipes.jpg) Polyvinyl chloride (PVC) pipes.(more)

If one hydrogen [atom](https://www.britannica.com/science/atom) in ethylene is replaced by a [chlorine](https://www.britannica.com/science/chlorine) atom, [vinyl chloride](https://www.britannica.com/science/vinyl-chloride) is produced. This polymerizes to [polyvinyl chloride](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyvinyl-chloride) (PVC), a colourless, hard, tough, thermoplastic material that can be manufactured in a number of forms, including foams, films, and fibres. [Vinyl acetate](https://www.britannica.com/science/vinyl-acetate), produced by the reaction of ethylene and [acetic acid](https://www.britannica.com/science/acetic-acid), polymerizes to [amorphous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/amorphous), soft resins used as coatings and adhesives. It copolymerizes with vinyl chloride to produce a large family of thermoplastic materials.

Many important polymers have [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) or [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen) atoms, along with those of [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element), in the backbone chain. Among such macromolecular materials with oxygen atoms are [polyacetals](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyacetal). The simplest polyacetal is [polyformaldehyde](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyformaldehyde). It has a high [melting point](https://www.britannica.com/science/melting-point) and is crystalline and resistant to abrasion and the action of [solvents](https://www.britannica.com/science/solvent-chemistry). Acetal resins are more like metal than are any other plastics and are used in the manufacture of machine parts such as gears and [bearings](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/bearings).

A linear polymer characterized by a repetition of ester groups along the backbone chain is called a [polyester](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyester). Open-chain polyesters are colourless, crystalline, thermoplastic materials. Those with high molecular weights (10,000 to 15,000 molecules) are employed in the manufacture of films, molded objects, and fibres such as Dacron.

![nylon](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/128798-050-E2D8CCC3/formation-nylon-polymer.jpg)

[nylon](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/128798-050-E2D8CCC3/formation-nylon-polymer.jpg) The formation of nylon, a polymer.(more)

The [polyamides](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyamide) include the naturally occurring proteins [casein](https://www.britannica.com/science/casein), found in [milk](https://www.britannica.com/topic/milk), and zein, found in [corn](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corn-plant) (maize), from which plastics, fibres, adhesives, and coatings are made. Among the synthetic polyamides are the urea-formaldehyde resins, which are thermosetting. They are used to produce molded objects and as adhesives and coatings for textiles and paper. Also important are the [polyamide](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyamide) resins known as [nylons](https://www.britannica.com/science/nylon). They are strong, resistant to [heat](https://www.britannica.com/science/heat) and abrasion, noncombustible, and nontoxic, and they can be coloured. Their best-known use is as textile fibres, but they have many other applications.

Another important family of synthetic organic polymers is formed of linear repetitions of the urethane group. [Polyurethanes](https://www.britannica.com/science/polyurethane) are employed in making elastomeric fibres known as spandex and in the production of coating bases and soft and rigid foams.

[![caulk](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/123757-050-01B7E3E6/Silicone-caulk-caulking-gun.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/123757-050-01B7E3E6/Silicone-caulk-caulking-gun.jpg)

[caulk](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/123757-050-01B7E3E6/Silicone-caulk-caulking-gun.jpg) Silicone caulk being dispensed from a caulking gun.(more)

A different class of polymers are the mixed organic-inorganic [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds). The most important representatives of this polymer family are the [silicones](https://www.britannica.com/science/silicone). Their backbone consists of alternating [silicon](https://www.britannica.com/science/silicon) and oxygen atoms with organic groups attached to each of the silicon atoms. Silicones with low [molecular weight](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecular-weight) are [oils](https://www.britannica.com/science/oil-chemical-compound) and greases. Higher-molecular-weight species are versatile elastic materials that remain soft and rubbery at very low temperatures. They are also relatively stable at high temperatures.

Fluorocarbon-containing polymers, known as [fluoropolymers](https://www.britannica.com/science/fluorocarbon-polymer), are made up of carbon–fluorine bonds, which are highly stable and render the compound resistant to solvents. The nature of carbon–fluorine bonding further [imparts](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/imparts) a nonstick quality to fluoropolymers; this is most widely evident in the [polytetrafluoroethylene](https://www.britannica.com/science/polytetrafluoroethylene) (PFTE) Teflon.

## Polymer chemistry

The study of such materials lies within the domain of polymer [chemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemistry). The investigation of natural polymers overlaps considerably with biochemistry, but the synthesis of new polymers, the investigation of [polymerization](https://www.britannica.com/science/polymerization) processes, and the characterization of the structure and properties of polymeric materials all pose unique problems for polymer chemists.

Polymer chemists have designed and synthesized polymers that vary in hardness, flexibility, softening temperature, [solubility](https://www.britannica.com/science/solubility-chemistry) in water, and [biodegradability](https://www.britannica.com/technology/biodegradability). They have produced polymeric materials that are as strong as steel yet lighter and more resistant to corrosion. Oil, [natural gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-gas), and water pipelines are now routinely constructed of plastic pipe. In recent years, automakers have increased their use of plastic components to build lighter vehicles that consume less fuel. Other industries such as those involved in the manufacture of textiles, [rubber](https://www.britannica.com/science/rubber-chemical-compound), paper, and packaging materials are built upon polymer chemistry.

Besides producing new kinds of polymeric materials, researchers are concerned with developing special [catalysts](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/catalysts) that are required by the large-scale industrial synthesis of commercial polymers. Without such catalysts, the polymerization process would be very slow in certain cases.

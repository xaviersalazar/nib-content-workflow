### Who invented the common symbol for infinity?

The common symbol for infinity, ∞, was invented by the [English](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) [mathematician](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics) [John Wallis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Wallis) in 1655.

### What are the three main types of infinity?

The three main types of infinity are mathematical, physical, and metaphysical.

### What did Aristotle believe about infinity?

[Aristotle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle) rejected the notion of “actual” infinity and distinguished it from “potential” infinity, which is the ability to count without end.

### What is Cantor’s theorem?

[Cantor’s theorem](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cantors-theorem) states that the size of the counting numbers is strictly less than the size of the [real numbers](https://www.britannica.com/science/real-number), showing that not all infinities are equal.

### What is the continuum hypothesis (CH)?

The [continuum hypothesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/continuum-hypothesis) (CH) is [Georg Cantor’s](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Georg-Ferdinand-Ludwig-Philipp-Cantor) conjecture that the continuum cardinality (c) is equal to [aleph-one](https://www.britannica.com/science/aleph-null) (ℵ1). It is known to be undecidable based on Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory with the axiom of choice (ZFC).

**infinity**, the concept of something that is unlimited, endless, without bound. The common symbol for infinity, **∞**, was invented by the English mathematician [John Wallis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Wallis) in 1655. Three main types of infinity may be distinguished: the mathematical, the physical, and the [metaphysical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/metaphysical). Mathematical infinities occur, for instance, as the number of points on a continuous [line](https://www.britannica.com/science/line-mathematics) or as the size of the endless sequence of counting numbers: 1, 2, 3,…. Spatial and temporal concepts of infinity occur in [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science) when one asks if there are infinitely many stars or if the [universe](https://www.britannica.com/science/universe) will last forever. In a metaphysical discussion of God or the Absolute, there are questions of whether an ultimate entity must be [infinite](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infinite) and whether lesser things could be infinite as well.

## Mathematical infinities

The ancient Greeks expressed infinity by the word _apeiron_, which had [connotations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/connotations) of being unbounded, indefinite, undefined, and formless. One of the earliest appearances of infinity in [mathematics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics) regards the [ratio](https://www.britannica.com/science/ratio) between the diagonal and the side of a square. [Pythagoras](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pythagoras) (c. 580–500 bce) and his followers initially believed that any aspect of the world could be expressed by an arrangement involving just the whole numbers (0, 1, 2, 3,…), but they were surprised to discover that the diagonal and the side of a square are [incommensurable](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Incommensurables-1688515)—that is, their lengths cannot both be expressed as whole-number multiples of any shared unit (or measuring stick). In modern mathematics this discovery is expressed by saying that the ratio is [irrational](https://www.britannica.com/science/irrational-number) and that it is the [limit](https://www.britannica.com/science/limit-mathematics) of an endless, nonrepeating decimal series. In the case of a square with sides of length 1, the diagonal is Square root of√2, written as 1.414213562…, where the [ellipsis](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/ellipsis) (…) indicates an endless sequence of digits with no pattern.

Both [Plato](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Plato) (428/427–348/347 bce) and [Aristotle](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle) (384–322 bce) shared the general Greek abhorrence of the notion of infinity. Aristotle influenced subsequent thought for more than a millennium with his rejection of “actual” infinity (spatial, temporal, or numerical), which he distinguished from the “potential” infinity of being able to count without end. To avoid the use of actual infinity, [Eudoxus of Cnidus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Eudoxus-of-Cnidus) (c. 400–350 bce) and [Archimedes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Archimedes) (c. 285–212/211 bce) developed a technique, later known as the [method of exhaustion](https://www.britannica.com/science/method-of-exhaustion), whereby an area was calculated by halving the measuring unit at successive stages until the remaining area was below some fixed [value](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/value) (the remaining region having been “exhausted”).

The issue of infinitely small numbers led to the discovery of [calculus](https://www.britannica.com/science/calculus-mathematics) in the late 1600s by the English mathematician [Isaac Newton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isaac-Newton) and the German mathematician [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gottfried-Wilhelm-Leibniz). Newton introduced his own theory of infinitely small numbers, or [infinitesimals](https://www.britannica.com/science/infinitesimal), to justify the calculation of [derivatives](https://www.britannica.com/science/derivative-mathematics), or slopes. In order to find the slope (that is, the change in _y_ over the change in _x_) for a line touching a curve at a given point ( _x_, _y_), he found it useful to look at the ratio between _d_ _y_ and _d_ _x_, where _d_ _y_ is an [infinitesimal](https://www.britannica.com/science/infinitesimal) change in _y_ produced by moving an infinitesimal amount _d_ _x_ from _x_. Infinitesimals were heavily criticized, and much of the early history of analysis revolved around efforts to find an alternate, rigorous foundation for the subject. The use of infinitesimal numbers finally gained a firm footing with the development of [nonstandard analysis](https://www.britannica.com/science/analysis-mathematics/Variational-principles-and-global-analysis#ref218300) by the German-born mathematician [Abraham Robinson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abraham-Robinson) in the 1960s.

![Concentric circles and infinity](https://cdn.britannica.com/47/91947-004-A0BA8A74/infinity-circles.jpg)

[Concentric circles and infinity](https://cdn.britannica.com/47/91947-004-A0BA8A74/infinity-circles.jpg) Concentric circles demonstrate that twice infinity is the same as infinity.(more)

A more direct use of infinity in mathematics arises with efforts to compare the sizes of [infinite sets](https://www.britannica.com/science/infinite-set), such as the [set](https://www.britannica.com/science/set-theory) of points on a line ( [real numbers](https://www.britannica.com/science/real-number)) or the set of counting numbers. Mathematicians are quickly struck by the fact that ordinary [intuitions](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intuitions) about numbers are misleading when talking about infinite sizes. [Medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Medieval) thinkers were aware of the paradoxical fact that line segments of varying lengths seemed to have the same number of points. For instance, draw two concentric circles, one twice the radius (and thus twice the circumference) of the other, as shown in the figure. Surprisingly, each point _P_ on the outer circle can be paired with a unique point _P_′ on the inner circle by drawing a line from their common centre _O_ to _P_ and labeling its intersection with the inner circle _P_′. [Intuition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Intuition) suggests that the outer circle should have twice as many points as the inner circle, but in this case infinity seems to be the same as twice infinity. In the early 1600s, the Italian scientist [Galileo Galilei](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Galileo-Galilei) addressed this and a similar nonintuitive result now known as Galileo’s [paradox](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/paradox). Galileo demonstrated that the set of counting numbers could be put in a one-to-one correspondence with the apparently much smaller set of their squares. He similarly showed that the set of counting numbers and their doubles (i.e., the set of even numbers) could be paired up. Galileo concluded that “we cannot speak of infinite quantities as being the one greater or less than or equal to another.” Such examples led the German mathematician [Richard Dedekind](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Richard-Dedekind) in 1872 to suggest a definition of an [infinite](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/infinite) set as one that could be put in a one-to-one relationship with some proper subset.

The confusion about infinite numbers was resolved by the German mathematician [Georg Cantor](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Georg-Ferdinand-Ludwig-Philipp-Cantor) beginning in 1873. First Cantor rigorously demonstrated that the set of rational numbers (fractions) is the same size as the counting numbers; hence, they are called countable, or denumerable. Of course this came as no real shock, but later that same year Cantor proved the surprising result that not all infinities are equal. Using a so-called “diagonal argument,” Cantor showed that the size of the counting numbers is strictly less than the size of the real numbers. This result is known as [Cantor’s theorem](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cantors-theorem).

To compare sets, Cantor first distinguished between a specific set and the abstract [notion](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/notion) of its size, or [cardinality](https://www.britannica.com/science/cardinal-number). Unlike a finite set, an infinite set can have the same cardinality as a proper subset of itself. Cantor used a diagonal argument to show that the cardinality of any set must be less than the cardinality of its power set—i.e., the set that contains all the given set’s possible subsets. In general, a set with _n_ elements has a power set with 2_n_ elements, and these two cardinalities are different even when _n_ is infinite. Cantor called the sizes of his infinite sets “transfinite cardinals.” His arguments showed that there are transfinite cardinals of endlessly many different sizes (such as the cardinals of the set of counting numbers and the set of real numbers).

The transfinite cardinals include [aleph-null](https://www.britannica.com/science/aleph-null) (the size of the set of whole numbers), aleph-one (the next larger infinity), and the [continuum](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/continuum) (the size of real numbers). These three numbers are also written as ℵ0, ℵ1, and _c_, respectively. By definition ℵ0 is less than ℵ1, and by Cantor’s theorem ℵ1 is less than or equal to _c_. Along with a principle known as the [axiom of choice](https://www.britannica.com/science/axiom-of-choice), the [proof](https://www.britannica.com/topic/proof-logic) method of Cantor’s theorem can be used to ensure an endless sequence of transfinite cardinals continuing past ℵ1 to such numbers as ℵ2 and ℵℵ0.

The continuum problem is the question of which of the alephs is equal to the continuum cardinality. Cantor conjectured that _c_ = ℵ1; this is known as Cantor’s [continuum hypothesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/continuum-hypothesis) (CH). CH can also be thought of as stating that any set of points on the line either must be countable (of size less than or equal to ℵ0) or must have a size as large as the entire space (be of size _c_).

In the early 1900s a thorough theory of infinite sets was developed. This theory is known as ZFC, which stands for [Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/Zermelo-Fraenkel-set-theory) with the axiom of choice. CH is known to be undecidable on the basis of the axioms in ZFC. In 1940 the Austrian-born logician [Kurt Gödel](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kurt-Godel) was able to show that ZFC cannot disprove CH, and in 1963 the American mathematician [Paul Cohen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Paul-Joseph-Cohen) showed that ZFC cannot prove CH. Set theorists continue to explore ways to extend the ZFC axioms in a reasonable way so as to resolve CH. Recent work suggests that CH may be false and that the true size of _c_ may be the larger infinity ℵ2.

# Physical infinities

The science of physical infinities is much less developed than the science of mathematical infinities. The main reason is simply that the status of physical infinities is quite undecided. In [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science) one might look for infinities in space, time, divisibility, or dimensionality.

Although some have speculated that three-dimensional space is [infinite](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infinite), cosmologists generally believe that the [universe](https://www.britannica.com/science/universe) is curved in such a way as to make it finite but unbounded—akin to the [surface](https://www.britannica.com/science/surface-geometry) of a sphere. Some theories of [cosmology](https://www.britannica.com/science/cosmology-astronomy) view the universe as being embedded in a higher-dimensional superspace, which could perhaps be infinite in extent.

In the light of the [big-bang model](https://www.britannica.com/science/big-bang-model) of the origin of the universe, cosmologists generally believe that the universe has a finitely long past; whether it might have an endless future is an open question. Under the “infinite future” view, space may continue much as it is now, with the galaxies drifting farther and farther apart, the stars burning to dust, and the remaining particles possibly decaying into radiation. Alternatively, in the “finite future” view, a cosmic [catastrophe](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/catastrophe) at some definite time in the future may destroy the universe: space may collapse to a point, or perhaps a parallel sheet of space (a “brane”) will collide with our universe, [annihilating](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/annihilating) everything. In any of the catastrophic finite future scenarios, speculation exists that the end of the universe may be followed by the birth of a new universe, in which case the future may in some sense be infinite after all.

If matter were to be infinitely divisible, then each object would in principle contain a potentially infinite collection of particles. But [quantum mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-mechanics-physics) rules out, or at least poses a [formidable](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/formidable) barrier to, notions of endless divisibility.

There is also a possibility that physical reality might enjoy an infinite number of dimensions; indeed, [quantum](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/quantum) mechanics is often formulated in terms of an infinite-dimensional [Hilbert space](https://www.britannica.com/science/Hilbert-space). But these dimensions are more useful fictions than solid realities.

## Metaphysical infinities

Perhaps the most familiar [context](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context) for discussing infinity is in [metaphysics](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/metaphysics) and theology. Cantor originated the distinction between the infinities of [mathematics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics), physics, and metaphysics. Although Plato thought of the [Absolute](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Absolute-philosophy) as finite, all theologians and metaphysicians from [Plotinus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Plotinus) (205–270 ce) on have supposed the Absolute to be infinite. What is meant by “the Absolute” depends, of course, upon the philosopher in question; it might be taken to mean God, an overarching universal mind, or simply the class of all possible thoughts.

The Bohemian mathematician [Bernard Bolzano](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bernhard-Bolzano) (1781–1848) formulated an argument for the infinitude of the class of all possible thoughts. If _T_ is a thought, let _T_\\* stand for the notion “ _T_ is a thought.” _T_ and _T_\\* are in turn distinct thoughts, so that, starting with any single thought _T_, one can obtain an endless sequence of possible thoughts: _T_, _T_\*, _T_\*\*, _T_\*\*\*, and so on. Some view this as evidence that the Absolute is infinite.

The infinitude of the Absolute can in turn be used as evidence for the existence of infinite thoughts or of infinite mathematical forms. The reasoning here is based on the [metaphysical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/metaphysical) notion that, as the greatest possible thing, the Absolute should in some sense be formally unknowable. That is, the Absolute should lie beyond any human attempt to describe it fully. This means that it should be impossible to formulate a simple property _P_ and then to define the Absolute as the unique thing that enjoys property _P_.

This [line](https://www.britannica.com/science/line-mathematics) of thought leads to what logicians call the reflection principle. According to the reflection principle, if _P_ is any simply describable property enjoyed by the Absolute, then there must be something smaller than the Absolute that also has property _P_. The motivation for the reflection principle is that, if it were to fail for some property _P_, then the Absolute could be defined as the unique thing that has property _P_, thus violating the principle that the Absolute should [transcend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/transcend) any human description of it.

Perhaps surprisingly, metaphysical-sounding notions such as the reflection principle are used by [set](https://www.britannica.com/topic/set-mathematics-and-logic) theorists in their mathematical investigations of the levels of infinity. One can, for instance, use the reflection principle argument to argue for the existence of infinite sets: the Absolute universe of all sets is infinite; therefore, by reflection there must be an ordinary set that is also infinite.

There is a sense in which [set theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/set-theory) can be thought of as a form of highly mathematical metaphysics. Conspicuously lacking, however, is any physical application for the transfinite numbers of set theory. Cantor himself [conjectured](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conjectured) that the universe might contain different types of matter, with the different types of matter decomposable into infinite sets of differing sizes. But nobody has ever found a way to incorporate this notion usefully into modern physics.

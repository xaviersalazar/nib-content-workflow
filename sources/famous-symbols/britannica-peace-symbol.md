Occasionally maligned as an anti-Christian symbol (an upside-down broken “Nero-cross”), a satanic character, or even a [Nazi](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nazi-Party) emblem, the iconic peace sign is apparently not so innocent to everyone. Thankfully, the symbol has a clear history, and its origin is not so controversial. **The modern peace sign was designed by Gerald Holtom for the British Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament in 1958.** The vertical line in the center represents the flag [semaphore](https://www.britannica.com/technology/semaphore) signal for the letter D, and the downward lines on either side represent the semaphore signal for the letter N. “N” and “D”, for [nuclear disarmament](https://www.britannica.com/topic/anti-nuclear-movement), enclosed in a circle. Holtom also described the symbol as representing despair, with the central lines forming a human with its hands questioning at its sides against the backdrop of a white Earth. It is said that Holtom originally considered using a Christian [cross](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cross-religious-symbol) but disliked its association with the [Crusades](https://www.britannica.com/event/Crusades) and ultimately chose something he considered to be more universal.

**symbol**, a [communication](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communication) element intended to simply represent or stand for a complex of person, object, group, or [idea](https://www.britannica.com/topic/idea). Symbols may be presented graphically, as in the [cross](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cross-religious-symbol) for Christianity and the red cross or [crescent](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crescent-symbol) for the life-preserving agencies of Christian and Islamic countries ( _see_ [Red Cross and Red Crescent](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Red-Cross-and-Red-Crescent); representationally, as in the human figures Marianne, [John Bull](https://www.britannica.com/topic/John-Bull-English-symbol), and [Uncle Sam](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Uncle-Sam) standing for France, England, and the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), respectively; they may involve letters, as in K for the [chemical element](https://www.britannica.com/science/chemical-element) [potassium](https://www.britannica.com/science/potassium); or they may be assigned arbitrarily, as in the mathematical symbol ∞ for infinity or the symbol [$](https://www.britannica.com/money/dollar-sign) for dollar.

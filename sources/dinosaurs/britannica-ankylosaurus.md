**Ankylosaurus**, (genus _Ankylosaurus_), armoured [ornithischian](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ornithischian) dinosaurs that lived 70 million to 66 million years ago in [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) during the Late [Cretaceous Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period). _Ankylosaurus_ is a [genus](https://www.britannica.com/science/genus-taxon) belonging to a larger group (infraorder [Ankylosauria](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur/Ankylosauria#ref225965)) of related four-legged heavily armoured herbivorous dinosaurs that [flourished](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/flourished) throughout the Cretaceous Period (145.5 million to 66 million years ago).

_Ankylosaurus_ was one of the largest ankylosaurs, with a total length of up to 10 metres (33 feet) and a probable weight of about four tons. Its head was square and flat and was broader than it was long. Its teeth, like those of the related [stegosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur/Thyreophora#ref225964), consisted of a simple curved row of irregularly edged (crenulated) leaf-shaped teeth. The body was short and squat, with massive legs to support its weight. Like other ankylosaurs, its back and flanks were protected from attack by thick bands of armour consisting of flat bony plates. These plates were [supplemented](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/supplemented) by rows of bony spikes projecting from the [animal’s](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) flanks and by bony knobs on its back. The skull was also heavily armoured and spiked. _Ankylosaurus_’s long tail terminated in a thick “club” of bone, which it probably swung as a defense against predators. This club was formed by the last tail vertebrae, which were nested tightly against each other and a sheath of several bony plates. The armour schemes of other ankylosaurs varied somewhat, but all were well protected against attack by carnivorous dinosaurs. The earliest ankylosaurs, called [nodosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Nodosaurus), lacked the tail club and had rather different armour patterns.

- What is the Cretaceous Period?







The [Cretaceous Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period/Introduction#ref1) is the final of the three periods of the Mesozoic Era in geologic time. It began 145 million years ago and lasted until 66 million years ago, making it the longest period of the Phanerozoic Eon.



The Cretaceous Period is known for the transition from the early life-forms of the Paleozoic Era to the advanced diversity of the current Cenozoic Era. During this time, flowering plants appeared, and dinosaurs were the dominant animals. Placental mammals also evolved during the Late Cretaceous. The period ended with a mass extinction that eradicated the dinosaurs, marine and flying reptiles, and many marine invertebrates.

- When did the Cretaceous Period take place?







The Cretaceous Period, the final period of the Mesozoic Era, began 145 million years ago and lasted for 79 million years, ending 66 million years ago. It is the longest period of the Phanerozoic Eon. The name _Cretaceous_ comes from _creta_, the Latin word for "chalk," because chalk deposits are common in rocks from this period.



The climate during the Cretaceous was generally warm and humid, with forests covering the polar regions. By the end of the period, most of today's continents were separated by water. The period ended with a mass extinction that wiped out the dinosaurs and many other species.



Correlation of Cretaceous rocks is usually accomplished using fossils. Ammonites are the most widely employed fossils because of their frequency of occurrence and geographic extent.

- How do scientists determine the age of the Cretaceous Period?







Scientists employ various methods to determine the age of the [Cretaceous Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period/Introduction#ref1), which lasted from 145 to 66 million years ago. These methods include:



  - **Fossil Correlation** Fossils, especially ammonites, are used to correlate Cretaceous rocks across different regions, with index fossils helping to define stage boundaries.

  - **Radiometric Dating** Volcanic ash layers (bentonite beds) found in the North American western interior provide radiometric dates that independently verify the synchronicity of ammonite zones.

  - **Isotopic Dating** Measuring the presence of natural radioactive atoms in mineral structures allows scientists to determine the time elapsed since the minerals formed.

  - **Magnetostratigraphy** Magnetic reversals within zones are used for more detailed subdivisions, though the Cretaceous Period has fewer magnetic reversals compared to the Cenozoic.


Geochronology, the study of the chronology of the past using geologic data, integrates these techniques to establish a comprehensive timeline.

- What types of rocks and fossils are commonly found from the Cretaceous Period?







During the Cretaceous Period, which lasted from 145.0 to 66 million years ago, the Earth saw the creation of unique rock types and a transition in life forms.



### Common Rocks



  - **Chalk:** A soft, fine-grained type of [limestone](https://www.britannica.com/science/limestone) formed predominantly from the armourlike plates of coccolithophores, tiny floating algae that flourished during the Late Cretaceous. Thick beds of chalk were deposited in western Europe, eastern Russia, southern Scandinavia, the Gulf Coast of North America, and western Australia.
  - **Sedimentary Rocks:** Correlation of Cretaceous rocks is usually accomplished using fossils.

### Common Fossils

  - **Ammonites:** These are the most widely employed fossils because of their frequency of occurrence and geographic extent.
  - **Marine Plankton:** By the end of the Cretaceous, marine plankton took on a distinctly modern appearance.
  - **Coccolithophores:** These became so abundant in the Late Cretaceous that vast quantities accumulated to form chalk.
  - **Planktonic Foraminiferans:** These also contributed greatly to fine-grained calcareous sediments.
  - **Rudists:** These were large, unusual [bivalves](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bivalve) that had one valve shaped like a cylindrical vase and another that resembled a flattened cap.
  - **Belemnites:** These were apparently confined to the colder boreal waters.
  - **Angiosperm pollen:** This provides recognition of zones for the Late Cretaceous of the North American Atlantic Coastal Plain.

- How did the Cretaceous Period end, and how is this shown in geological records?







The Cretaceous Period ended with a mass extinction event approximately 66 million years ago, marking a major transition from the Mesozoic to the Cenozoic Era. This extinction, known as the K-T or Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction, wiped out approximately 80% of animal species, including the dinosaurs.



The [asteroid theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period/Terrestrial-life#ref69976) suggests that an asteroid impact triggered the extinction. Evidence supporting this includes the [Chicxulub crater](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chicxulub/Introduction#ref417253) in Mexico, tektites, and high levels of iridium in rock deposits. The impact likely caused darkness, halted photosynthesis, and disrupted the food chain. Geological records, such as those in Tunisia, provide [stratotypes](https://www.britannica.com/science/Tertiary-Period/Tertiary-rocks#ref275686) that mark this boundary with precision, using these calibration points to correlate time and rock layers globally.

- Why is the study of geochronology important for understanding the history of the Cretaceous Period?







Geochronology, the science of dating rocks and geologic events, is crucial for understanding the Cretaceous Period. By using methods such as [radiometric dating](https://www.britannica.com/science/geochronology/Introduction#ref1), scientists can determine the numerical age of rocks from this period. This is particularly important because the [Cretaceous Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period/Introduction#ref1) is a significant portion of the time between ancient life-forms and those that dominate Earth today.



Fossil dating and isotopic measurements help to integrate events recorded in rocks from different locations into a regional or global picture. Rocks containing minerals suitable for precise isotopic dating allow other units to be dated relative to them. This allows for correlation over virtually all of Earth's history.

**Cretaceous Period**, in [geologic time](https://www.britannica.com/science/geologic-time), the last of the three periods of the [Mesozoic Era](https://www.britannica.com/science/Mesozoic-Era). The Cretaceous began 145.0 million years ago and ended 66 million years ago; it followed the [Jurassic Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Jurassic-Period) and was succeeded by the [Paleogene Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Paleogene-Period) (the first of the two periods into which the [Tertiary Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Tertiary-Period) was divided). The Cretaceous is the longest period of the [Phanerozoic Eon](https://www.britannica.com/science/Phanerozoic-Eon). Spanning 79 million years, it represents more time than has elapsed since the [extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology) of the [dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur), which occurred at the end of the period.

![geologic time](https://cdn.britannica.com/67/73167-050-C66534D1/chart.jpg)

[geologic time](https://cdn.britannica.com/67/73167-050-C66534D1/chart.jpg) The stratigraphic chart of geologic time.(more)

The name Cretaceous is [derived](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/derived) from _creta_, Latin for “ [chalk](https://www.britannica.com/science/chalk),” and was first proposed by [J.B.J. Omalius d’Halloy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Baptiste-Julien-d-Omalius-dHalloy) in 1822. D’Halloy had been commissioned to make a geologic map of [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), and part of his task was to decide upon the geologic units to be represented by it. One of his units, the Terrain Crétacé, included chalks and underlying sands. Chalk is a soft, fine-grained type of [limestone](https://www.britannica.com/science/limestone) composed predominantly of the armourlike plates of [coccolithophores](https://www.britannica.com/science/coccolith), tiny floating [algae](https://www.britannica.com/science/algae) that flourished during the Late Cretaceous. Most Cretaceous rocks are not chalks, but most chalks were deposited during the Cretaceous. Many of these rocks provide clear and easily accessed details of the period because they have not been deformed or eroded and are relatively close to the surface—as can be seen in the white cliffs bordering the [Strait of Dover](https://www.britannica.com/place/Strait-of-Dover) between France and [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England).

The Cretaceous Period began with Earth’s land assembled essentially into two continents, [Laurasia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Laurasia) in the north and [Gondwana](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gondwana-supercontinent) in the south. These were almost completely separated by the equatorial [Tethys](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tethys-Sea) seaway, and the various segments of Laurasia and Gondwana had already started to [rift](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/rift) apart. North America had just begun pulling away from Eurasia during the Jurassic, and South America had started to split off from Africa, from which India, Australia, and Antarctica were also separating. When the Cretaceous Period ended, most of the present-day continents were separated from each other by expanses of water such as the North and South Atlantic Ocean. At the end of the period, India was adrift in the [Indian Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indian-Ocean), and Australia was still connected to Antarctica.

The [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology) was generally warmer and more humid than today, probably because of very active volcanism associated with unusually high rates of [seafloor spreading](https://www.britannica.com/science/seafloor-spreading). The lengthy Cretaceous Period [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) a major portion of the interval between ancient life-forms and those that dominate Earth today. [Dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur) were the dominant group of land animals, especially “duck-billed” dinosaurs (hadrosaurs), such as _Shantungosaurus_, and horned forms, such as _[Triceratops](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Triceratops)_. Giant marine [reptiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reptile) such as [ichthyosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ichthyosaur), [mosasaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mosasaur), and [plesiosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/plesiosaur) were common in the seas, and flying reptiles ( [pterosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/pterosaur)) dominated the sky. Flowering plants ( [angiosperms](https://www.britannica.com/plant/angiosperm)) arose close to the beginning of the Cretaceous and became more abundant as the period progressed. The Late Cretaceous was a time of great productivity in the world’s oceans, as borne out by the [deposition](https://www.britannica.com/science/sedimentation-geology) of thick beds of [chalk](https://www.britannica.com/science/chalk) in western [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe), eastern [Russia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Russia), southern [Scandinavia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Scandinavia), the [Gulf Coast](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gulf-Coast) of [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America), and western [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia). The Cretaceous ended with one of the [greatest mass extinctions](https://www.britannica.com/science/K-T-extinction) in the history of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth), exterminating the dinosaurs, marine and flying reptiles, and many marine invertebrates.

## The Cretaceous environment

## [Paleogeography](https://www.britannica.com/science/paleogeography)

The [position](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-drift-geology) of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth)’s landmasses changed significantly during the Cretaceous Period—not unexpected, given its long [duration](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/duration). At the onset of the period there existed two supercontinents, [Gondwana](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gondwana-supercontinent) in the south and [Laurasia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Laurasia) in the north. [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America), [Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Africa) (including the adjoining pieces of what are now the [Arabian Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arabia-peninsula-Asia) and the [Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/Middle-East)), [Antarctica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctica), [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia), [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), [Madagascar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madagascar), and several smaller landmasses were joined in Gondwana in the south, while [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America), [Greenland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greenland), and [Eurasia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Eurasia) (including Southeast Asia) formed Laurasia. Africa had split from South America, the last land connection being between [Brazil](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brazil) and [Nigeria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nigeria). As a result, the South Atlantic Ocean joined with the widening North Atlantic. In the region of the Indian Ocean, Africa and Madagascar separated from India, Australia, and Antarctica in Late [Jurassic](https://www.britannica.com/science/Jurassic-Period) to Early Cretaceous times. Once separated from Australia and Antarctica, India began its journey northward, which [culminated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/culminated) in a later collision with Asia during the [Cenozoic Era](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cenozoic-Era). Madagascar broke away from Africa during the Late Cretaceous, and Greenland separated from North America. Australia was still joined to Antarctica. These were barely attached at the junction of what are now North and South America.

[Sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level) was higher during most of the Cretaceous than at any other time in Earth history, and it was a major factor influencing the paleogeography of the period. In general, world [oceans](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean) were about 100 to 200 metres (330 to 660 feet) higher in the Early Cretaceous and roughly 200 to 250 metres (660 to 820 feet) higher in the Late Cretaceous than at present. The high Cretaceous sea level is thought to have been primarily the result of water in the [ocean basins](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean-basin) being displaced by the enlargement of midoceanic ridges.

As a result of higher sea levels during the Late Cretaceous, marine waters [inundated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/inundated) the [continents](https://www.britannica.com/science/continent), creating relatively shallow [epicontinental seas](https://www.britannica.com/science/epicontinental-sea) in North America, South America, Europe, [Russia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Russia), Africa, and Australia. In addition, all continents shrank somewhat as their margins flooded. At its maximum, land covered only about 18 percent of Earth’s surface, compared with approximately 28 percent today. At times, Arctic waters were connected to the [Tethys seaway](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tethys-Sea) through the middle of North America and the central portion of Russia. On several occasions during the Cretaceous, marine animals living in the South Atlantic had a seaway for migration to Tethys via what is presently Nigeria, [Niger](https://www.britannica.com/place/Niger), [Chad](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chad), and [Libya](https://www.britannica.com/place/Libya). Most of western Europe, eastern Australia, parts of Africa, South America, India, Madagascar, [Borneo](https://www.britannica.com/place/Borneo-island-Pacific-Ocean), and other areas that are now land were entirely covered by marine waters for some interval of Cretaceous time.

Detailed study indicates 5 to 15 different episodes of rises and falls in sea level. The patterns of changes for the stable areas throughout history are quite similar, although several differences are notable. During most of the Early Cretaceous, parts of Arctic [Canada](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canada), Russia, and western Australia were underwater, but most of the other areas were not. During the middle Cretaceous, east-central Australia experienced major inundations called transgressions. In the Late Cretaceous, most continental landmasses were transgressed but not always at the same time. One explanation for the lack of a [synchronous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/synchronous) record is the concept of geoidal eustacy, in which, it is suggested, as Earth’s continents move about, the oceans bulge at some places to compensate. Eustacy would result in sea level being different from ocean basin to ocean basin.

Water circulation and mixing were not as great as they are today, because most of the oceans (e.g., the developing North Atlantic) were constricted, and the temperature differences between the poles and the [Equator](https://www.britannica.com/place/Equator) were minimal. Thus, the oceans experienced frequent periods of anoxic (oxygenless) conditions in the bottom waters that reveal themselves today as black [shales](https://www.britannica.com/science/shale). Sometimes, particularly during the mid-Cretaceous, conditions extended to epicontinental seas, as [attested](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/attested) by deposits of black shales in the western interior of North America.

The Cretaceous world had three distinct geographic subdivisions: the northern boreal, the southern boreal, and the Tethyan region. The Tethyan region separated the two boreal regions and is recognized by the presence of fossilized reef-forming rudist [bivalves](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bivalve), [corals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/coral), larger [foraminiferans](https://www.britannica.com/science/foraminiferan) (single-celled organisms known for their glasslike shells, or tests), and certain [ammonites](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ammonoid) (a group of extinct [cephalopods](https://www.britannica.com/animal/cephalopod) known for their spiral shells) that inhabited only the warmer Tethyan waters. Early in the Cretaceous, North and South America separated sufficiently for the marine connection between the Tethys Sea and the Pacific to deepen substantially. The Tethys-to-Pacific marine connection allowed for a strong westward-flowing [current](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean-current), which is inferred from faunal patterns. For example, as the Cretaceous progressed, the similarity between rudist bivalves of the Caribbean and western Europe decreased, while some Caribbean forms have been found on Pacific [seamounts](https://www.britannica.com/science/seamount), in [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia), and possibly in the [Balkans](https://www.britannica.com/place/Balkans).

The remnants of the northern boreal realm in North America, Europe, Russia, and Japan have been extensively studied. It is known, for instance, that sediments in the southwestern [Netherlands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Netherlands) indicate several changes of temperature during the Late Cretaceous. These temperature swings imply that the boundary between the northern boreal areas and the Tethys region was not constant with time. Russian workers recognize six paleobiogeographic zones: boreal, which in this [context](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context) is equivalent to [Arctic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arctic); European; Mediterranean, including the central Asian province; Pacific; and two paleofloristic zonations of land. Southern boreal areas and the [rocks](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology) representing the southern Tethys margin lack this level of detail.

Magnetically, the Cretaceous was quiet relative to the subsequent [Paleogene Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Paleogene-Period). In fact, [magnetic reversals](https://www.britannica.com/science/geomagnetic-reversal) are not noted for a period of some 42 million years, from the early [Aptian](https://www.britannica.com/science/Aptian-Stage) to the late [Santonian](https://www.britannica.com/science/Santonian-Stage) ages. The lengths of Earth’s months ( _see_ [synodic period](https://www.britannica.com/science/synodic-period)) have changed regularly for at least the past 600 million years because of [tidal friction](https://www.britannica.com/science/tidal-friction) and other forces that slow Earth’s rotation. The rate of change in the synodic month was minimal for most of the Cretaceous but has accelerated since. The reasons for these two [anomalies](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/anomalies) are not well understood.

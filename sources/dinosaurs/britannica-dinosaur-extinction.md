- What was the K–T extinction?







The Cretaceous-Paleogene (K–Pg) extinction event, also known as the K–T extinction, happened about 66 million years ago and eliminated approximately 80% of animal species. This event is named from _Kreide_, German for "chalk" (referring to Cretaceous Period chalk sediment), and _Tertiary_, formerly used for the Paleogene and Neogene periods.



The consensus is that a large asteroid impact caused the extinction. A roughly 10-kilometer asteroid struck Earth, sending heat, dust, and soot worldwide, blocking sunlight and collapsing ecosystems. Evidence of this event is the large impact crater near Mexico's Yucatán Peninsula.



The K–T extinction ranks third in severity of the five major extinction episodes in geologic time. This extinction killed off the dinosaurs and allowed for mammals and birds to become dominant on land.

The primary cause of this extinction is believed to be the impact of a large asteroid, about 10 km in diameter, striking Earth. - Which animals and plants were affected by the K–T extinction?







The [K–T extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/K-T-extinction/Introduction#ref1), a global mass extinction event, occurred approximately 66 million years ago, eliminating about 80 percent of all animal species.



Animals affected:


\\* **Marine life** Many marine invertebrates died out, including free-swimming mollusks like ammonoids and belemnoids. Larger foraminifers (orbitoids) also disappeared, and hermatypic corals were reduced to about one-fifth of their genera.


\\* **Land animals** Nearly all dinosaurs became extinct. The only archosaur lineages to survive were those leading to modern birds and crocodilians. Other reptile groups, such as turtles, crocodilians, lizards, and snakes, were either not affected or only slightly affected. Amphibians and mammals experienced relatively mild effects.



Plants affected:


\\* Land plants seemed to have fared better than land animals. However, there is evidence of widespread species extinctions of angiosperms and other dramatic shifts among North American plant communities.

- What caused the K–T extinction event?







The K–T extinction event, a global mass extinction that occurred about 66 million years ago, wiped out approximately 80% of animal species.



The leading explanation for this event is the asteroid theory, which posits that a large asteroid impact caused the extinction. Evidence supporting this theory includes:


\\* The discovery of a high concentration of the rare metal [iridium](https://www.britannica.com/science/iridium) at the K–T boundary.


\\* The Chicxulub crater in the Yucatán Peninsula, which is believed to be the impact site.



The impact would have ejected a massive amount of dust and particles into the atmosphere, blocking sunlight and disrupting the food chain.



Some scientists suggest that other factors, such as volcanic activity and changes in landmasses, may have also contributed to the extinction.

- How did the K–T extinction affect life on Earth afterward?







The [K–T extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/K-T-extinction/Introduction#ref1) event, which occurred about 66 million years ago, led to the elimination of approximately 80% of animal species. Following this event, life on Earth underwent significant changes. The niches left vacant by the extinction of dinosaurs and other species saw rapid diversification of new life-forms.



Mammals, which had existed for over 100 million years, experienced substantial evolutionary radiation. Marsupials developed diverse adaptive types in Australia and South America, while placental mammals rapidly diversified. In marine environments, mollusks and reef-building corals became highly diversified. Present-day ecosystems are largely populated by organisms that survived and diversified after the K–T extinction.

**K–T extinction**, a global [mass extinction event](https://www.britannica.com/science/mass-extinction-event) responsible for eliminating approximately 80 percent of all [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) of [animals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) at or very close to the boundary between the [Cretaceous](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period) and [Paleogene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Paleogene-Period) periods, about 66 million years ago. The K–T extinction was [characterized](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characterized) by the elimination of many lines of animals that were important elements of the [Mesozoic Era](https://www.britannica.com/science/Mesozoic-Era)(251.9 million to 66 million years ago), including nearly all of the [dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur) and many marine [invertebrates](https://www.britannica.com/animal/invertebrate). The event receives its name from the German word _Kreide_, meaning “ [chalk](https://www.britannica.com/science/chalk)” (which references the chalky sediment of the Cretaceous Period), and the word [Tertiary](https://www.britannica.com/science/Tertiary-Period), which was traditionally used to describe the period of time spanning the [Paleogene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Paleogene-Period) and [Neogene](https://www.britannica.com/science/Neogene-Period) periods. The K–T extinction ranks third in severity of the five major extinction episodes that punctuate the span of [geologic time](https://www.britannica.com/science/geologic-time).

_[(Read E.O. Wilson’s Britannica essay on mass extinction.)](https://www.britannica.com/science/Mass-Extinction-2117803)_

The only lines of [archosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/archosaur)—the group of [reptiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reptile) that contains the [dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur), [birds](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal), and [crocodilians](https://www.britannica.com/animal/crocodile-order)—that survived the [extinction](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology) were the [lineages](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/lineages) that led to modern birds and crocodilians. Of the planktonic marine flora and fauna, only about 13 percent of the [coccolithophore](https://www.britannica.com/science/coccolith) and planktonic [foraminiferal](https://www.britannica.com/science/foraminiferan) genera remained alive. Among free-swimming [mollusks](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mollusk), the [ammonoids](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ammonoid) and [belemnoids](https://www.britannica.com/animal/belemnoid) became extinct. Among other marine invertebrates, the larger foraminifers ( [orbitoids](https://www.britannica.com/science/orbitoid)) died out, and the hermatypic [corals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/coral) were reduced to about one-fifth of their genera. [Rudist](https://www.britannica.com/animal/rudist) [bivalves](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bivalve) also disappeared, as did bivalves with a reclining (or partially buried) life habit, such as [_Exogyra_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Exogyra) and [_Gryphaea_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Gryphaea). The stratigraphically important [inoceramids](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Inoceramus) also died out.

Many [hypotheses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hypotheses) have been offered over the years to explain [dinosaur](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur) extinction, but only a few have received serious consideration. The extermination of the dinosaurs has been a puzzle to paleontologists, geologists, and biologists for two centuries. Proposed causes include [disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/disease), heat waves and resulting sterility, freezing cold spells, the rise of egg-eating mammals, and [X-rays](https://www.britannica.com/science/X-ray) from a nearby exploding [supernova](https://www.britannica.com/science/supernova). Since the early 1980s, however, much attention has been focused on the so-called “ [asteroid](https://www.britannica.com/science/asteroid) theory” formulated by American scientists [Walter Alvarez](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Walter-Alvarez) and [Luis Alvarez](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Luis-Alvarez). This theory states that the [impact](https://www.britannica.com/topic/impact-event) of a large asteroid on [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth) may have triggered a [mass extinction event](https://www.britannica.com/science/mass-extinction-event) some 66 million years ago by ejecting a huge quantity of rock debris into the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere), enshrouding Earth in darkness for several months or longer. With no [sunlight](https://www.britannica.com/science/sunlight-solar-radiation) able to penetrate this global dust cloud, [photosynthesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/photosynthesis) ceased, resulting in the death of green plants and the disruption of the [food chain](https://www.britannica.com/science/food-chain).

There is much evidence in the rock record that supports this [hypothesis](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hypothesis). A huge [crater](https://www.britannica.com/science/crater-geology) 180 km (112 miles) in diameter dating to the end of the Cretaceous was discovered buried beneath sediments of the [Yucatán Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yucatan-Peninsula) near [Chicxulub](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chicxulub), [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico). A second, smaller crater, which predates the one at Chicxulub by about 2,000 to 5,000 years, was discovered at [Boltysh](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Boltysh-crater) in [Ukraine](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ukraine) in 2002. Its existence raises the possibility that the K–T extinction was the result of multiple [bolide](https://www.britannica.com/science/bolide) impacts. In addition, [tektites](https://www.britannica.com/science/tektite) (fractured sand grains characteristic of meteorite impacts) and the [rare-earth element](https://www.britannica.com/science/rare-earth-element) [iridium](https://www.britannica.com/science/iridium), which is common only deep within Earth’s mantle and in extraterrestrial rocks, have been found in deposits associated with the extinction. There is also evidence for some spectacular side effects of bolide impact, including an enormous [tsunami](https://www.britannica.com/science/tsunami) that washed up on the shores of the [Gulf of Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gulf-of-Mexico-Gulf-of-America) and widespread [wildfires](https://www.britannica.com/science/wildfire) triggered by a fireball from the impact.

Despite this strong evidence, the asteroid theory has met with [skepticism](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/skepticism) among some paleontologists, with some agitating for terrestrial factors as the cause of the extinction and others claiming that the amount of iridium dispersed by an impact was caused by a smaller object, such as a [comet](https://www.britannica.com/science/comet-astronomy). A huge outpouring of [lava](https://www.britannica.com/science/lava-volcanic-ejecta), known as the [Deccan Traps](https://www.britannica.com/place/Deccan-Traps), occurred in India at the end of the Cretaceous. Some paleontologists believe that the [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide) that accompanied these flows created a global [greenhouse effect](https://www.britannica.com/science/greenhouse-effect) that greatly warmed the planet. Others note that [tectonic plate](https://www.britannica.com/science/plate-tectonics) movements caused a major rearrangement of the world’s landmasses, particularly during the latter part of the Cretaceous. The climatic changes resulting from such [continental drift](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-drift-geology) could have caused a gradual [deterioration](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/deterioration) of habitats favourable to the dinosaurs and other [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) groups that suffered extinction. It is, of course, possible that sudden catastrophic phenomena such as an asteroid or comet impact contributed to an environmental deterioration already brought about by terrestrial causes.

### What are dinosaurs?

Dinosaurs are a diverse group of [reptiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reptile) that were the dominant terrestrial life form on Earth during the [Mesozoic Era](https://www.britannica.com/science/Mesozoic-Era), about 245 million years ago. Dinosaurs went into decline near the end of the [Cretaceous Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period), about 66 million years ago.

### How did most dinosaurs go extinct?

The decline of dinosaurs was most likely caused by many different factors. A popular theory is that an [asteroid](https://www.britannica.com/science/asteroid) crashed into the [Yucatán Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yucatan-Peninsula) 66 million years ago, causing enough terrestrial and asteroid material to be ejected into the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere) that sunlight was possibly blocked for several years. This would have severely impacted plant life and subsequently brought about the end of most dinosaurs.

### Did dinosaurs have feathers?

Some species of dinosaurs, such as the [dromaeosaur](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dromaeosaur), have fossil records of their feathers. Early feathers were not evolved for flight; instead, scientists speculate that dinosaurs evolved feathers for other reasons, such as temperature regulation, [camouflage](https://www.britannica.com/science/concealing-coloration), and balancing while running. While most species are often depicted as featherless, some researchers believe that feathers were commonplace on dinosaurs.

### Do dinosaurs exist today?

It is generally accepted that modern [birds](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal) are descendents of dinosaurs. Evidence for this theory first arose from the fossil records of the [Archaeopteryx _Archaeopteryx_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Archaeopteryx) and the _Xiaotingia_, two genera of [theropod](https://www.britannica.com/animal/theropod) dinosaurs that displayed similar anatomical structures to birds.

### What is the largest dinosaur?

Although estimates vary, one of the contenders for the largest dinosaur is the _Argentinosaurus_. Discovered in Argentina in 1987, the _Argentinosaurus_ is estimated to have been 37 to 40 metres (121 to 131 feet) long and weighed up to 100 metric tons.

### When was the first dinosaur fossil discovered?

Dinosaur fossils and tracks have been discovered throughout human history. The first description of dinosaurs as a separate group of life forms (known as “Dinosauria”) was published in 1842 by British paleontologist [Richard Owen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Richard-Owen), who based his findings on fossil fragments of the _Cladeiodon_ and the _Cetiosaurus_.

**dinosaur**, (clade Dinosauria), the common name given to a group of [reptiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reptile), often very large, that first appeared roughly 245 million years ago (near the beginning of the Middle [Triassic](https://www.britannica.com/science/Triassic-Period) Epoch) and [thrived](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/thrived) worldwide for nearly 180 million years. Most died out by the end of the [Cretaceous Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period), about 66 million years ago, but many lines of evidence now show that one lineage evolved into [birds](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal) about 155 million years ago.

The name _dinosaur_ comes from the Greek words _deinos_ (“terrible” or “fearfully great”) and _sauros_ (“reptile” or “lizard”). The English anatomist [Richard Owen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Richard-Owen) proposed the formal term Dinosauria in 1842 to include three [giant](https://www.britannica.com/topic/giant-mythology) [extinct](https://www.britannica.com/science/extinction-biology) animals ( [_Megalosaurus_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Megalosaurus), [_Iguanodon_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Iguanodon), and _Hylaeosaurus_) represented by large fossilized [bones](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy) that had been unearthed at several locations in southern [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) during the early part of the 19th century. Owen recognized that these reptiles were far different from other known reptiles of the present and the past for three reasons: they were large yet obviously terrestrial, unlike the aquatic [ichthyosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ichthyosaur) and [plesiosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/plesiosaur) that were already known; they had five vertebrae in their hips, whereas most known reptiles have only two; and, rather than holding their limbs sprawled out to the side in the manner of [lizards](https://www.britannica.com/animal/lizard), dinosaurs held their limbs under the body in columnar fashion, like [elephants](https://www.britannica.com/animal/elephant-mammal) and other large mammals.

Originally applied to just a handful of incomplete specimens, the clade Dinosauria now [encompasses](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompasses) more than 800 generic names and at least 1,000 species, with new names being added to the roster every year as the result of scientific explorations around the world. Not all of these names are valid [taxa](https://www.britannica.com/science/taxon), however. A great many of them have been based on fragmentary or incomplete material that may actually have come from two or more different dinosaurs. In addition, bones have sometimes been misidentified as dinosaurian when they are not from dinosaurs at all. Nevertheless, dinosaurs are well documented by abundant [fossil](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil) remains recovered from every [continent](https://www.britannica.com/science/continent) on [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth), and the number of known dinosaurian taxa is estimated to be 10–25 percent of actual past [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity).

The extensive [fossil record](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil-record) of genera and species is testimony that dinosaurs were [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) animals, with widely varying lifestyles and [adaptations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adaptations). Their remains are found in [sedimentary rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/sedimentary-rock) layers (strata) dating to the Late Triassic Epoch (approximately 237 million to 201.3 million years ago). The abundance of their fossilized bones is [substantive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/substantive) proof that dinosaurs were the dominant form of terrestrial [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) life during the [Mesozoic Era](https://www.britannica.com/science/Mesozoic-Era) (about 252.2 million to 66 million years ago). It is likely that the known remains represent a very small fraction (probably less than 0.0001 percent) of all the individual dinosaurs that once lived.

## The first finds

[![Person in inflatable T. rex costume lifting small dumbbells in a gym beside a weight rack; text overlay reads “design flaw?” with an arrow pointing to the T. rex's arms.](https://cdn.britannica.com/15/274715-138-A37B2297/why-did-the-Tyrannosaurus-rex-t-rex-have-such-tiny-arms.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/why-did-the-Tyrannosaurus-rex-t-rex-have-such-tiny-arms/-347842)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur/images-videos)

Before [Richard Owen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Richard-Owen) introduced the term Dinosauria in 1842, there was no concept of anything even like a dinosaur. Large fossilized bones quite probably had been observed long before that time, but there is little record—and no existing specimens—of such findings much before 1818. In any case, people could not have been expected to understand what dinosaurs were even if they found their remains. For example, some classical scholars now conclude that the Greco-Roman [legends](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legends) of [griffins](https://www.britannica.com/topic/griffin-mythological-creature) from the 7th century bce were inspired by discoveries of [protoceratopsian](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Protoceratops) dinosaurs in the [Altai](https://www.britannica.com/place/Altai-Mountains) region of [Mongolia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mongolia). In 1676 Robert Plot of the [University of Oxford](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Oxford) included, in a work of natural history, a drawing of what was apparently the knee-end of the [thighbone](https://www.britannica.com/science/femur) of a dinosaur, which he thought might have come from an elephant taken to Britain in Roman times. Fossil bones of what were undoubtedly dinosaurs were discovered in [New Jersey](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Jersey) in the late 1700s and were probably discussed at the meetings of the [American Philosophical Society](https://www.britannica.com/topic/American-Philosophical-Society) in Philadelphia. Soon thereafter, [Lewis and Clark’s expedition](https://www.britannica.com/event/Lewis-and-Clark-Expedition) [encountered](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/encountered) dinosaur fossils in the western [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States).

The earliest verifiable published record of dinosaur remains that still exists is a note in the 1820 _American Journal of Science and Arts_ by Nathan Smith. The bones described had been found in 1818 by Solomon Ellsworth, Jr., while he was digging a well at his homestead in Windsor, Connecticut. At the time, the bones were thought to be [human](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being), but much later they were identified as _Anchisaurus_. Even earlier (1800), large birdlike footprints had been noticed on sandstone slabs in Massachusetts. Pliny Moody, who discovered these tracks, attributed them to “Noah’s raven,” and Edward Hitchcock of [Amherst College](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Amherst-College), who began collecting them in 1835, considered them to be those of some giant extinct [bird](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal). The tracks are now recognized as having been made by several different kinds of dinosaurs, and such tracks are still commonplace in the [Connecticut River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Connecticut-River) valley today.

Better known are the finds in southern England during the early 1820s by [William Buckland](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Buckland) (a clergyman) and [Gideon Mantell](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gideon-Algernon-Mantell) (a physician), who described [_Megalosaurus_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Megalosaurus) and [_Iguanodon_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Iguanodon), respectively. In 1824 Buckland published a description of _Megalosaurus_, fossils of which consisted mainly of a lower jawbone with a few [teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/tooth-anatomy). The following year Mantell published his “Notice on the _Iguanodon_, a Newly Discovered Fossil Reptile, from the Sandstone of Tilgate Forest, in Sussex,” on the basis of several teeth and some leg bones. Both men collected fossils as an avocation and are [credited](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/credited) with the earliest published announcements in England of what later would be recognized as dinosaurs. In both cases their finds were too fragmentary to permit a clear image of either animal. In 1834 a partial [skeleton](https://www.britannica.com/science/skeleton) was found near [Brighton](https://www.britannica.com/place/Brighton-England) that corresponded with Mantell’s fragments from Tilgate Forest. It became known as the [Maidstone](https://www.britannica.com/place/Maidstone-England) _Iguanodon_, after the village where it was discovered. The Maidstone skeleton provided the first glimpse of what these creatures might have looked like.

[_Plateosaurus_](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/8071-050-5C92FB97/Plateosaurus.jpg) _Plateosaurus_, late Triassic dinosaur. A large herbivore, it may have reared up on its back legs and craned its long neck to browse on vegetation high above the ground.(more)

Two years before the Maidstone _Iguanodon_ came to light, a different kind of skeleton was found in the [Weald](https://www.britannica.com/place/The-Weald) of southern England. It was described and named _[Hylaeosaurus](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Hylaeosaurus)_ by Mantell in 1832 and later proved to be one of the armoured dinosaurs. Other fossil bones began turning up in Europe: fragments described and named as _Thecodontosaurus_ and _Palaeosaurus_ by two English students, Henry Riley and Samuel Stutchbury, and the first of many skeletons named [_Plateosaurus_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Plateosaurus) by the naturalist Hermann von Meyer in 1837. [Richard Owen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Richard-Owen) identified two additional dinosaurs, [albeit](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/albeit) from fragmentary evidence: _Cladeiodon_, which was based on a single large tooth, and _Cetiosaurus_, which he named from an incomplete skeleton composed of very large bones. Having carefully studied most of these fossil specimens, Owen recognized that all of these bones represented a group of large reptiles that were unlike any living varieties. In a report to the British Association for the Advancement of Science in 1841, he described these animals, and the word Dinosauria was first published in the association’s proceedings in 1842.

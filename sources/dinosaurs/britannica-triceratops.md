- What is a Triceratops?







_Triceratops_, meaning "three-horned face," is a large, plant-eating dinosaur with a bony frill and three horns on its skull. It existed during the late Cretaceous Period, about 65.5 million years ago, and was among the last of the non-avian dinosaurs.



The _Triceratops_ was a large animal, with adults weighing between 5,450 and 7,260 kg and reaching a body length of about 9 meters. The _Triceratops_ skull could reach nearly 3 meters in length, making it one of the largest terrestrial animal skulls. The dinosaur also had a beak that it likely used to crop vegetation.



_Triceratops_ fossils are commonly found in the upper Cretaceous deposits of western North America. Though most _Triceratops_ fossils are skulls, other fossil remains have revealed that it had stout fore and hind limbs and short toes ending in small hooves.

_Triceratops_, meaning "three-horned face," was a large, four-legged, plant-eating dinosaur with a bony frill at the back of its skull and three horns. They were among the last non-avian dinosaurs, living in the late Cretaceous Period, about 65.5 million years ago.



The _Triceratops_ possessed a large skull, up to 3 meters (almost 10 feet) long in some individuals, making it among the largest of terrestrial animals. _Triceratops_ could grow up to 9 meters (30 feet) long and weigh up to 7,260 kg (16,000 pounds). Their hind limbs were larger than their forelimbs, but both were stout. The front limbs may have been in a semisprawling position.

- What did Triceratops eat?







_Triceratops_ were plant-eating dinosaurs that lived during the Late Cretaceous period. They had specialized teeth arranged in columns that acted as shearing blades, allowing them to slice through tough plant tissues.



The front of their mouth formed a beak, which they likely used to crop vegetation. Based on their body structure, paleontologists believe that _Triceratops_ likely fed on low shrubbery, possibly including tough, fibrous plant tissues like palm or cycad fronds.

- Why did Triceratops have three horns and a frill?







_Triceratops_, meaning "three-horned face," is named for the two horns above its eyes and a smaller horn on its snout. Besides these horns, _Triceratops_ also possessed a frill.



The function of the horns has been debated. _Triceratops_ may have used its horns to defend itself from carnivores such as _Tyrannosaurus rex_ or for combat with other _Triceratops_. Another possibility is that the horns functioned as display structures, perhaps for signaling relative maturity to other members of the group. Likewise, the purpose of the frill is not definitively known. The horns and frill changed dramatically throughout the animal's development, allowing juveniles to be distinguished from more mature animals.

**Triceratops**, (genus _Triceratops_), large quadrupedal plant-eating [ceratopsian](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ceratopsian) dinosaur that had a frill of bone at the back of its skull and three prominent [horns](https://www.britannica.com/science/horn-zoology). [Fossils](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil) of “three-horned face,” as its Latin name is usually translated, date to the final 3 million years of the [Cretaceous Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period) (145.5 million to 65.5 million years ago), making it one of the last of the non-avian dinosaurs to have evolved. Paleontologists estimate that the body length of _Triceratops_ approached 9 metres (30 feet). The largest adults are thought to have weighed 5,450–7,260 kg (approximately 12,000–16,000 pounds).

_Triceratops_ is the most commonly recovered [dinosaur](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur) in the uppermost Cretaceous deposits of western [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America), and its remains have been found throughout the region. Although many other large ceratopsians have been discovered in massive bone beds representing numerous individuals, _Triceratops_ has only rarely been found in groups of three or more individuals. When the first specimen was discovered in 1887, it was mistaken for a gigantic [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) of extinct [bison](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bison). Only later did further discoveries reveal that it was actually a horned dinosaur. _Triceratops_ was officially named and described by American paleontologist [O.C. Marsh](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Othniel-Charles-Marsh) in 1889. At present there are two recognized species: _T. horridus_ and _T. prorsus_.

## Skull and other skeletal features

[![Image shows a Triceratops skeleton.](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/58980-004-8CFEDB8E/Triceratops-skeleton.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/58980-004-8CFEDB8E/Triceratops-skeleton.jpg)

[_Triceratops_](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/58980-004-8CFEDB8E/Triceratops-skeleton.jpg) _Triceratops_ skeleton.(more)

_Triceratops_ possessed a gigantic [skull](https://www.britannica.com/science/skull), and some individuals had skulls nearly 3 metres (about 10 feet) long, which would place them among the largest of all terrestrial [animals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal). In addition to its three [conspicuous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conspicuous) horns, which were placed above each eye and on the snout, it possessed numerous small spikes (epoccipitals) that bordered the margin of the expanded frill of bone at the back of the skull. There were as many as 19–26 epoccipitals on the frill. _Triceratops_ also possessed smaller hornlike projections on the jugal bones (cheekbones). The upper and lower jaws were lined with stacked columns of [teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/tooth-anatomy), which appear to have been specialized for shearing. The front of the mouth formed a [beak](https://www.britannica.com/science/beak), which may have been used to crop vegetation. In addition, most of the skull was covered by indentations made by blood vessels; similar indentations are found under the keratinous beaks of living birds. This suggests that the dinosaur’s entire head, aside from the cheeks and the area around the nostrils, was covered in [keratin](https://www.britannica.com/science/keratin) while it was alive. In many living birds, keratin is very colourful, a fact that suggests that the skulls of _Triceratops_ may have been very colourful as well.

_Triceratops_ is often portrayed as using its large horns to defend itself from contemporary carnivorous dinosaurs, such as [_Tyrannosaurus rex_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/tyrannosaur). The discovery of apparent pathologies (the results of disease or injury) in consistent locations of the frills supports the possibility that _Triceratops_ may have engaged in intraspecific combat, as is seen in some [extant](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/extant) horned animals. Another possibility is that the horns functioned primarily as display structures, perhaps for signaling relative maturity to other members of the group. This suggestion is supported by the fact that the horns and frill of _Triceratops_ changed shape dramatically throughout its development, allowing juveniles to be [differentiated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/differentiated) from more mature animals.

The majority of _Triceratops_ specimens are known from skulls or partial skulls, and bones from the rest of the body are less commonly recovered. From [fossil](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil) remains of other parts of the body, paleontologists have been able to determine that the hind limbs of _Triceratops_ were larger than the forelimbs, but both sets were very stout. Whether the forelimbs were held fully erect, as in the modern [rhinoceros](https://www.britannica.com/animal/rhinoceros-mammal), is debated, though there is some evidence to suggest that they were held in a semisprawling position (an intermediate [stance](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/stance) between the erect position of a rhinoceros and the fully sprawling limbs of most lizards). The dinosaur’s short toes probably terminated in small hooves. The tail, as in many other large horned dinosaurs, appears to have been fairly short.

## Development

The skull is known to have undergone dramatic changes in shape throughout the development of _Triceratops_ from an [embryo](https://www.britannica.com/science/embryo-human-and-animal) to an adult. The smallest _Triceratops_ skulls have small straight horns above their eyes. Slightly larger juvenile specimens have backward-curving horns above the eyes and triangular epoccipitals bordering the large frill at the back of the skull. As juveniles matured into adults, their horns curved forward and the epoccipitals bordering the frill became flattened.

_Triceratops_ is thought to have lived alongside another large horned dinosaur, _[Torosaurus](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Torosaurus)_, which is distinguished from _Triceratops_ by its larger, thinner cranial frill that is perforated by two large openings. Classically, _Triceratops_ has been thought to possess a cranial frill that was both solid and extremely thick. Comparisons of the horns and frills of the two genera suggest that they may in fact represent different life stages of the same species, with _Torosaurus_ being the more mature of the two: as _Triceratops_ matured, the frill expanded and thinned, eventually forming the two openings found in _Torosaurus_. (This [hypothesis](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hypothesis), however, is a matter of some debate among members of the [paleontology](https://www.britannica.com/science/paleontology) community.) Similarly, the ceratopsid dinosaur _Nedoceratops_, which is known from a single specimen, possessed a small opening in its frill that suggests that it could be an intermediate growth stage between _Triceratops_ and _Torosaurus_. Alternatively, it may represent a distinct [genus](https://www.britannica.com/science/genus-taxon) of horned dinosaur or a _Triceratops_ with an unusual cranial [morphology](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/morphology). Covered by keratin or skin, the openings in the frill were not visible during life.

**ceratopsian**, any of a group of plant-eating [dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur) from the [Cretaceous Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period) (146 million to 66 million years ago) characterized by a bony frill on the back of the [skull](https://www.britannica.com/science/skull) and a unique upper beak [bone](https://www.britannica.com/science/bone-anatomy), called a rostral.

[![Psittacosaurus](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/8073-050-EAED79D1/dinosaur-Psittacosaurus-beak.jpg)\\
1 of 2](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/8073-050-EAED79D1/dinosaur-Psittacosaurus-beak.jpg)

[_Psittacosaurus_](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/8073-050-EAED79D1/dinosaur-Psittacosaurus-beak.jpg) The forward region of the skull of _Psittacosaurus_ was shaped very much like a parrot's beak in that the upper jaw curved over the lower, hence the dinosaur's name ( _psittac_ being derived from the Latin term for parrot). (more)

[![Protoceratops](https://cdn.britannica.com/72/8072-050-8EBB259C/Protoceratops-plants-legs.jpg)\\
2 of 2](https://cdn.britannica.com/72/8072-050-8EBB259C/Protoceratops-plants-legs.jpg)

[_Protoceratops_](https://cdn.britannica.com/72/8072-050-8EBB259C/Protoceratops-plants-legs.jpg) _Protoceratops_, late Cretaceous dinosaur. This herbivore was solidly built and had a parrotlike beak and a bony frill.(more)

The ceratopsians [comprise](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprise) three lineages (see images). Members of the [Psittacosauridae](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Psittacosauridae), including [_Psittacosaurus_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Psittacosaurus), were mostly bipedal and lived during the Early Cretaceous; they had a beak, a small frill, and no horns. Members of the [Protoceratopsidae](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Protoceratopsidae), including [_Protoceratops_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Protoceratops) and _[Leptoceratops](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Leptoceratops)_, were mostly quadrupedal and slightly larger and lived from the Early to Late Cretaceous; these dinosaurs had a somewhat larger frill but no horns.

[_Triceratops_](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/8076-050-2C3B2CCD/Triceratops-plant-eater-dinosaurs.jpg) _Triceratops_, a late Cretaceous dinosaur, was a massive herbivore with a bony neck frill and three face horns. It was one of the last and most numerous of dinosaurs.(more)

The third group, [Ceratopsidae](https://www.britannica.com/animal/ceratopsid), had very large frills and horns on the nose and above the eyes. Ceratopsidae is made up of two lineages: the Chasmosaurinae had large eye horns and small nose horns, and the Centrosaurinae had small eye horns and large nose horns. Chasmosaurinae includes [_Triceratops_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Triceratops) and _Torosaurus_. _Triceratops_ was unusual among ceratopsians in that its bony head frill was short and of solid bone; in other forms the frills were larger and mostly open in the centre. _Kosmoceratops_, with its broad frill and hooks projecting forward from the top of its skull, and _Utahceratops_, [characterized](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characterized) by a large [horn](https://www.britannica.com/science/horn-zoology) rising from the top of its nose, were close relatives of _Triceratops_. The skull of _Kosmoceratops_ is considered by many paleontologists to be the most ornate of any known [dinosaur](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur).

**Iguanodon**, (genus _Iguanodon_), large herbivorous [dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur) found as fossils from the Late [Jurassic](https://www.britannica.com/science/Jurassic-Period) and Early [Cretaceous periods](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cretaceous-Period) (161.2 million to 99.6 million years ago) in a wide area of [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe), [North Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Africa), [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America), Australia, and Asia; a few have been found from Late Cretaceous deposits of Europe and southern Africa.

_Iguanodon_ was the largest, best known, and most [widespread](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/widespread) of all the iguanodontids (family Iguanodontidae), which are closely related to the hadrosaurs, or duck-billed dinosaurs. _Iguanodon_ was 9 metres (30 feet) long, stood nearly 2 metres tall at the hip, and weighed four to five tons. The [animal](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) probably spent its time grazing while moving about on four legs, although it was able to walk on two. Iguanodontid forelimbs had an unusual five-fingered hand: the [wrist](https://www.britannica.com/science/wrist-anatomy) bones were fused into a block; the joints of the [thumb](https://www.britannica.com/science/thumb) were fused into a conelike spike; the three middle fingers ended in blunt, hooflike claws; and the fifth finger diverged laterally from the others. Furthermore, the smallest finger had two small additional phalanges, a [throwback](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/throwback) to more primitive dinosaurian configuration. The [teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/tooth-anatomy) were ridged and formed sloping surfaces whose grinding action could pulverize its diet of low-growing [ferns](https://www.britannica.com/plant/fern) and [horsetails](https://www.britannica.com/plant/horsetail) that grew near streams and rivers. Most bones of the skull and jaws were not tightly fused but instead had movable joints that allowed flexibility when [chewing](https://www.britannica.com/science/chewing) tough [plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant) material.

In 1825 _Iguanodon_ became the second [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) to be described scientifically as a [dinosaur](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur), the first having been [_Megalosaurus_](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Megalosaurus). _Iguanodon_ was named for its teeth, whose similarity to those of modern [iguanas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/iguana-lizard-grouping) also provided the dinosaur’s discoverer, the English physician [Gideon Mantell](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gideon-Algernon-Mantell), with the first clue that dinosaurs had been [reptiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reptile). In his first reconstruction of the incomplete remains of _Iguanodon_, Mantell restored the skeleton in a quadrupedal pose with the spikelike thumb perched on its nose. This reconstruction [persisted](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/persisted) in [London’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/London) famous [Crystal Palace](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Crystal-Palace-building-London) dinosaur sculptures by Waterhouse Hawkins (1854) until many complete skeletons were found in Bernissart, [Belgium](https://www.britannica.com/place/Belgium), during the 1880s. Reconstructions of the Belgian skeletons mistakenly placed the animal in an upright, kangaroo-like stance with its [tail](https://www.britannica.com/science/tail-zoology) on the ground—a misconception not corrected until the late 20th century, when a posture based upon a nearly horizontal backbone was adopted.

[Iguanodons](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Iguanodon) (genus _Iguanodon_) and [iguanas](https://www.britannica.com/animal/iguana-lizard-grouping) (subfamily Iguaninae) are related only insofar as both are [reptiles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/reptile)—but so are [turtles](https://www.britannica.com/animal/turtle-reptile) and [snakes](https://www.britannica.com/animal/snake). Iguanas are in the [lizard](https://www.britannica.com/animal/lizard) family and are only distantly related to [dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur) like iguanodons on the [evolutionary](https://www.britannica.com/science/evolution-scientific-theory) tree. Although their similar names suggest affinity, their connection stems from the appearance of their [teeth](https://www.britannica.com/science/tooth-anatomy) and the early discovery of iguanodon [fossils](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil). The name _iguanodon_ means “iguana tooth.”

[![Illustration comparing a green iguana, a modern lizard about 2 meters long, with an iguanodon, a large dinosaur about 9 meters long, showing differences in size and body shape.](https://cdn.britannica.com/68/277368-050-19C09728/iguana-iguanodon-combination.jpg?w=1000)](https://cdn.britannica.com/68/277368-050-19C09728/iguana-iguanodon-combination.jpg)

[Iguanodon and green iguana](https://cdn.britannica.com/68/277368-050-19C09728/iguana-iguanodon-combination.jpg) Despite similar names iguanodons and iguanas are not closely related.(more)

When husband-and-wife amateur [paleontologists](https://www.britannica.com/science/paleontology) [Gideon Mantell](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gideon-Algernon-Mantell) and Mary Ann Mantell discovered fossilized teeth in [Sussex](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sussex-historical-county-England), England, in 1822, they tried to identify them. Gideon Mantell visited the Hunterian Museum in [London](https://www.britannica.com/place/London) where he saw an iguana skeleton and noted that the fossilized teeth appeared to be much larger versions of iguana teeth. Hence the name of the newly discovered creature, which Mantell calculated based on scale would be up to 18 meters (60 feet) in length. Further research has concluded that iguanodons were as long as 9 meters (30 feet), far larger than iguanas, the largest of which are 2 meters (6.6 feet) long.

The discovery of iguanodon fossils had a significant role in the development of dinosaur [paleontology](https://www.britannica.com/science/paleontology). In 1825 Gideon Mantell published his findings on _Iguanodon_, which he described as a large [herbivorous](https://www.britannica.com/science/herbivore) reptile. One year earlier, in 1824, [William Buckland](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Buckland) published a scientific description of _[Megalosaurus](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Megalosaurus)_, which he conjectured was a type of giant [lizard](https://www.britannica.com/animal/lizard). These were the first two published findings about the ancient reptiles that would later be called dinosaurs. In 1842 paleontologist [Richard Owen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Richard-Owen) formally [recognized the scientific classification of dinosaurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/dinosaur#ref225941) (Dinosauria) based on the examples of _Megalosaurus_, _Iguanodon_, and _Hylaeosaurus_, which Gideon Mantell had also discovered.

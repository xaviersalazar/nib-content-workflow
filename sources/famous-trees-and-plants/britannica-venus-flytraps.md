**Venus flytrap**, ( _Dionaea muscipula_), [perennial](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/perennial) [carnivorous plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/carnivorous-plant) of the [sundew](https://www.britannica.com/plant/sundew) family ( [Droseraceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Droseraceae)), notable for its unusual habit of catching and digesting insects and other small animals. The only member of its [genus](https://www.britannica.com/science/genus-taxon), the plant is native to a small region of [North](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Carolina-state) and [South Carolina](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Carolina), where it is common in damp mossy areas. As [photosynthetic](https://www.britannica.com/science/photosynthesis) plants, [Venus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Venus-goddess) flytraps do not rely on carnivory for energy but rather use the [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen)-rich animal [proteins](https://www.britannica.com/science/protein) to enable their survival in marginal [soil](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil) conditions.

The plant, which grows from a bulblike rootstock, bears a group of small white [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) at the tip of an erect stem 20–30 cm (8–12 inches) tall. The [leaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy) are 8–15 cm (3–6 inches) long and have blades that are hinged along the midline so that the two nearly circular lobes, with spiny teeth along their margins, can fold together and enclose an [insect](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect) [alighting](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/alighting) on them. This action is triggered by pressure on six sensitive hairs, three on each lobe. In normal daytime temperatures the lobes, when stimulated by prey, snap shut in about half a second. Glands on the leaf surface then secrete a red sap that digests the insect’s body and gives the entire leaf a red flowerlike appearance. About 10 days are required for [digestion](https://www.britannica.com/science/digestion-biology), after which the leaf reopens. The trap dies after capturing three or four insects.

- What is a carnivorous plant?







[Carnivorous plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/carnivorous-plant/Introduction#ref1) are plants adapted to capture and digest insects and other animals to supplement their nutrient requirements in poor soil conditions. They do this using modified leaves as trapping mechanisms, which may be either active or passive.



There are over 600 known species of carnivorous plants that use a variety of trapping mechanisms, including [pitfall traps](https://www.britannica.com/plant/carnivorous-plant/Introduction#ref324611), flypaper traps, snap traps, bladder traps, and lobster-pot traps. They digest their prey using enzymes or bacteria to break them down and absorb the nutrients.

- What are some common types of carnivorous plants?







Carnivorous plants have adapted to capture and digest insects and other animals to supplement scarce nutrients in poor soil conditions. These plants use various trapping mechanisms, including:



  - **Pitfall traps** Pitcher plants employ hollow, liquid-filled leaves to passively collect and digest prey.
  - **Flypaper traps** Butterworts and sundews use sticky mucilage on their leaves or gland-tipped hairs to capture prey.
  - **Snap traps** Venus flytraps use rapid leaf movements to actively ensnare insects.
  - **Bladder traps** Bladderwort plants actively suck in small organisms using a partial vacuum.
  - **Lobster-pot traps** Corkscrew plants use downward-pointing hairs to force prey deeper into the trap.

- How do the leaves of carnivorous plants help capture prey?







Carnivorous plants have modified leaves that act as trapping mechanisms to capture prey. These mechanisms are either active, involving movement to capture prey, or passive, where the plant relies on the prey to come to it.



Different types of traps include:


\\* **Pitfall traps** Pitcher plants use hollow, liquid-filled leaves to passively collect and digest prey.


\\* **Flypaper traps** Butterworts and sundews use sticky mucilage on their leaves or gland-tipped hairs to capture prey.


\\* **Snap traps** Venus flytraps use rapid leaf movements to ensnare insects actively. Pressure on sensitive hairs triggers the leaves to snap shut.


\\* **Bladder traps** Bladderwort plants actively suck in small organisms using a partial vacuum.


\\* **Lobster-pot traps** Corkscrew plants use downward-pointing hairs to force prey deeper into the trap.



Once captured, the plant digests its prey using enzymes or bacteria, absorbing the resulting nitrogenous compounds and salts.

- Where do carnivorous plants usually live and why do they grow there?







Carnivorous plants often thrive in damp heaths, bogs, swamps, and muddy or sandy shores with abundant water and scarce nitrogenous material due to unfavorable soil conditions. Carnivory helps them survive in poor soil conditions, supplementing the nitrogen and other nutrients their roots can't get from their harsh environments.



These plants are specially adapted to trap and digest small prey, supplementing their nutrient needs in these challenging soil conditions. Though most are terrestrial, some carnivorous plants are aquatic. Examples of carnivorous plants include [Venus flytraps](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Venus-flytrap), [waterwheels](https://www.britannica.com/plant/list-of-carnivorous-plants-1999613/Introduction#ref1), [sundews](https://www.britannica.com/plant/sundew), and [pitcher plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/pitcher-plant).

**carnivorous plant**, any plant especially adapted for capturing and digesting [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect) and other animals by means of ingenious pitfalls and traps. Carnivory in plants has [evolved](https://www.britannica.com/science/evolution-scientific-theory) independently about six times across several families and orders. The more than 600 known [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) of carnivorous plants [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) a very [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) group, in some cases having little more in common than their carnivorous habit.

## Trap types and digestion

[![Do Carnivorous Plants Actually Eat Their Prey?](https://cdn.britannica.com/16/253816-138-69C82AB4/carnivorous-plants-diet-insects-nutrients-photosynthesize-botany-science.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/carnivorous-plants-diet-insects-nutrients-photosynthesize-botany-science/-298553)

Do Carnivorous Plants Actually Eat Their Prey?Sometimes plants bite back.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/plant/carnivorous-plant/images-videos)

The [conspicuous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conspicuous) [trapping mechanism](https://www.britannica.com/science/trapping-mechanism-botany), which is always a modified [leaf](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy), draws special attention to these plants. A variety of trapping mechanisms exist and are designated as active or passive based on whether they move to capture prey. Pitfall traps, such as those found in [pitcher plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/pitcher-plant), are among the most common types of traps and employ a [hollow, lidded leaf](https://www.britannica.com/science/pitcher-plant-structure) filled with liquid to passively collect and digest prey. Flypaper traps can be active or passive and rely on sticky [mucilage](https://www.britannica.com/science/mucilage), either directly on the leaf surface ( [butterworts](https://www.britannica.com/plant/butterwort)) or on gland-tipped hairs ( [sundews](https://www.britannica.com/plant/sundew)), to capture prey. Snap traps, such as those of the [Venus flytrap](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Venus-flytrap) ( _Dionaea muscipula_), use rapid leaf movements to actively ensnare insects. Bladder traps are only found in [bladderwort](https://www.britannica.com/plant/bladderwort) plants (genus _Utricularia_) and actively suck in small organisms using a partial [vacuum](https://www.britannica.com/science/vacuum-physics). Lobster-pot traps, found predominantly in [corkscrew plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/corkscrew-plant) (genus _Genlisea_), employ downward-pointing hairs to force prey deeper into the trap.

Using [enzymes](https://www.britannica.com/science/enzyme) or [bacteria](https://www.britannica.com/science/bacteria), carnivorous plants digest their prey through a process of chemical breakdown [analogous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogous) to [digestion](https://www.britannica.com/science/digestion-biology) in animals. The end products, particularly nitrogenous [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds) and salts, are absorbed by the plants to enable their survival under otherwise marginal or hostile environmental conditions. Most carnivorous species are green plants that manufacture food by [photosynthesis](https://www.britannica.com/science/photosynthesis) from the raw materials of [sunlight](https://www.britannica.com/science/sunlight-solar-radiation), [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water), and [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide) in the presence of [chlorophyll](https://www.britannica.com/science/chlorophyll). The carnivorous habit augments the diet derived from the poor [soil](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil) of their [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment).

## Ecology

[![Cape sundew](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/174671-050-406CD87D/Cape-sundew-mucilage-plant-insects.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/174671-050-406CD87D/Cape-sundew-mucilage-plant-insects.jpg)

[Cape sundew](https://cdn.britannica.com/71/174671-050-406CD87D/Cape-sundew-mucilage-plant-insects.jpg) Sticky mucilage along the modified leaves of the Cape sundew ( _Drosera capensis_) serves to trap and digest insects.(more)

Whether aquatic or terrestrial, carnivorous plants have a basically similar ecology. Species of two or three genera (e.g., _Sarracenia_, _Drosera_, and _Pinguicula_) are often found growing almost side by side. The majority are most likely to be found in damp [heaths](https://www.britannica.com/plant/heath), [bogs](https://www.britannica.com/science/bog-wetland), [swamps](https://www.britannica.com/science/swamp), and muddy or sandy shores where water is at least seasonally abundant and where nitrogenous materials are often scarce or unavailable because of acid or other unfavorable soil conditions. _[Drosophyllum lusitanicum](https://www.britannica.com/plant/yellow-sundew)_ seems to be the one exception; it grows on dry, gravelly hills of [Portugal](https://www.britannica.com/place/Portugal) and Morocco.

On the whole, carnivorous plants are relatively small, but size variation is enormous even within the same [genus](https://www.britannica.com/science/genus-taxon). The majority are herbaceous [perennials](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/perennials) less than 30 cm (1 foot) high, often only 10 to 15 cm (4 to 6 inches). Some species of _Nepenthes_, however, become large shrubby vines. _Drosera_ species vary from a few centimeters to 1 meter (3 feet) or more in height ( _D. gigantea_); the smallest are often hidden among the [moss](https://www.britannica.com/plant/moss-plant) of a sphagnum [bog](https://www.britannica.com/science/bog-wetland).

## Major families

[![Common butterwort](https://cdn.britannica.com/40/257940-050-CD6A55F4/common-butterwort-Pinguicula-vulgaris-carnivorous-plant-flower-Alaska.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/40/257940-050-CD6A55F4/common-butterwort-Pinguicula-vulgaris-carnivorous-plant-flower-Alaska.jpg)

[Common butterwort](https://cdn.britannica.com/40/257940-050-CD6A55F4/common-butterwort-Pinguicula-vulgaris-carnivorous-plant-flower-Alaska.jpg) The flowers of the common butterwort ( _Pinguicula vulgaris_) rise above the sticky carnivorous leaves.(more)

The largest carnivorous plant family, [Lentibulariaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Lentibulariaceae) (order [Lamiales](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Lamiales)), is marked by bilaterally symmetrical [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) with fused petals and only two anthers. This family has a fairly [cosmopolitan](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cosmopolitan) distribution and [comprises](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprises) more than 300 species in three genera: the [bladderworts](https://www.britannica.com/plant/bladderwort) ( _Utricularia_, about 220 species), the [butterworts](https://www.britannica.com/plant/butterwort) ( _Pinguicula_, about 120 species), and the corkscrew plants ( _Genlisea_, about 22 species). Employing a variety of trapping mechanisms, members of that family are predominantly herbs of wet or aquatic habitats and prey on insects and other [invertebrates](https://www.britannica.com/animal/invertebrate).

The family [Droseraceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Droseraceae) (order [Caryophyllales](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Caryophyllales)) comprises three genera and about 154 species, nearly all of which are [sundews](https://www.britannica.com/plant/sundew) (genus _Drosera_). The aquatic genus _Aldrovanda_ contains only one species, the [waterwheel plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/waterwheel-plant) ( _A. vesiculosa_), which is sometimes grown in aquaria as a curiosity. Similarly, the [genus](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/genus) _Dionaea_ consists of only the [Venus flytrap](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Venus-flytrap) ( _D. muscipula_), well known for its quick-acting snap trap and commonly sold as a novelty. Once classified within Droseraceae, the [Portuguese sundew](https://www.britannica.com/plant/yellow-sundew) ( _Drosophyllum lusitanicum_) is now placed within its own family, [Drosophyllaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Drosophyllaceae) (order Caryophyllales), of which it is the only species.

Despite their similar trapping mechanisms, pitcher plants can be found in five genera across three families. The species of New World [pitcher plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/pitcher-plant) are placed in the family [Sarraceniaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Sarraceniaceae) (order [Ericales](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Ericales)). About 10 of the 34 species belong to the widely known and much-studied genus _[Sarracenia](https://www.britannica.com/plant/trumpet-pitcher)_, of eastern [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America). The [sun pitchers](https://www.britannica.com/plant/sun-pitcher), also known as marsh pitcher plants (genus _Heliamphora_), are native to a limited region in [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) and consist of about 23 species. The [cobra plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/cobra-plant) ( _Darlingtonia californica_) is the only member of its genus and is [indigenous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/indigenous) to northern [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state) and southern Oregon. The approximately 140 species of Old World pitcher plants constitute the only genus of the family [Nepenthaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Nepenthaceae), _[Nepenthes](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Nepenthes)_ (order Caryophyllales). Mostly native to [Madagascar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madagascar), [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia), and Australasia, many members of Nepenthaceae are climbing plants, and some live as [epiphytes](https://www.britannica.com/plant/epiphyte) in trees. The pitcher plant family [Cephalotaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Cephalotaceae) (order [Oxalidales](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Oxalidales)) consists of only the [Western Australian pitcher plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Western-Australian-pitcher-plant) ( _Cephalotus follicularis_).

Known as [rainbow plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/rainbow-plant-Byblis-genus), the family [Byblidaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Byblidaceae) (order Lamiales) contains a single genus ( _Byblis_) of about seven carnivorous species native to [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia) and [New Guinea](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Guinea). Those herbs have narrow leaves that are densely covered with glandular hairs that serve as flypaper traps to absorb nutrients from insects.

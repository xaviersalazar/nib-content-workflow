**bamboo**, (subfamily Bambusoideae), subfamily of tall treelike grasses of the family [Poaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Poaceae), [comprising](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprising) more than 115 genera and 1,400 species. Bamboos are distributed in tropical and subtropical to mild temperate regions, with the heaviest concentration and largest number of species in East and [Southeast Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southeast-Asia) and on islands of the Indian and Pacific oceans. A few species of the genus [_Arundinaria_](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Arundinaria) are native to the southern [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), where they form dense canebrakes along riverbanks and in marshy areas.

Bamboos are typically fast-growing [perennials](https://www.britannica.com/science/perennial), with some species growing as much as 30 cm (1 foot) per day. The woody ringed stems, known as [culms](https://www.britannica.com/science/culm), are typically [hollow](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/hollow) between the rings (nodes) and grow in branching clusters from a thick [rhizome](https://www.britannica.com/science/rhizome) (underground stem). Bamboo culms can attain heights ranging from 10 to 15 cm (about 4 to 6 inches) in the smallest species to more than 40 metres (about 130 feet) in the largest. While the narrow [leaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy) on young culms usually arise directly from the [stem](https://www.britannica.com/science/stem-plant) rings, mature culms often sprout horizontal leaf-bearing branches. Most bamboos flower and produce [seeds](https://www.britannica.com/science/seed-plant-reproductive-part) only after 12–120 years’ growth, and then only once in their lifetime; reproduction is largely vegetative. Some species spread aggressively and can form a dense undergrowth that excludes other plants.

Bamboos are used for a great variety of purposes, especially in East and Southeast Asia. The seeds of some species are eaten as grain, and the cooked young shoots of some bamboos are eaten as vegetables, especially in Chinese [cuisines](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/cuisines). The raw leaves are a useful fodder for [livestock](https://www.britannica.com/animal/livestock). The pulped fibres of several bamboo species, especially _[Dendrocalamus strictus](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Dendrocalamus-strictus)_ and _Bambusa bambos_, are used to make fine-quality [paper](https://www.britannica.com/technology/paper). The [jointed stems](https://www.britannica.com/science/wood-plant-tissue) of bamboo have perhaps the most numerous uses; the largest stems supply planks for houses and rafts, while both large and small stems are lashed together to form the scaffoldings used on building-construction sites. The stems are also split up to make buckets and pipes or are used to make furniture, flooring, walking sticks, fishing poles, garden stakes, and other utensils. Some species of bamboo are used as ornamentals in landscape gardens. The fine-grained [silica](https://www.britannica.com/science/silica-mineral) produced in the joints of bamboo stems has been used as a medicine in the Orient for centuries under the name _tabasheer_. East Asian artists, poets, and [epicures](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/epicures) have long celebrated the beauty and utility of bamboo in paintings and verse.

**xiao**, Chinese end-blown [bamboo](https://www.britannica.com/plant/bamboo) [flute](https://www.britannica.com/art/flute-musical-instrument) noted for its mellow and [melancholy](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/melancholy) tone.

Before the [Tang dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tang-dynasty) (618–907 ce), the term _xiao_ denoted a multi-tube instrument later known as the _paixiao_, or [panpipe](https://www.britannica.com/art/panpipe). Any single tube flute was called _di_. The transverse flute became increasingly popular during the Tang [dynasty](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynasty), and from that time forward the vertically blown flute was called _xiao_, the panpipe _[paixiao](https://www.britannica.com/art/paixiao)_ (a row of _xiao_), and the transverse flute was known as the _[di](https://www.britannica.com/art/di)_.

The _xiao_ first appeared in the [Han dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Han-dynasty) (206 bc–220 ad), possibly imported from the Qiang people of what is now northwestern China.

Most _xiao_ are made of bamboo, but jade, porcelain, and ivory _xiao_ are also known. The modern _xiao_ is about 27 to 30 inches (70 to 80 cm) long, with five finger holes in the front and one thumb hole in the back. Additional holes near the lower end are used for tuning, venting, and attaching decorative tassels. The bottom of the tube is open but the top is closed by the natural node of the bamboo. A small opening connected to a V-shaped [notch](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/notch) at the edge allows air to flow through the instrument. The _xiao_ has a range of two octaves.

The _xiao_’s soft volume and graceful and mellow sound is suitable for both solo playing and blending with the [_qin_](https://www.britannica.com/art/qin-musical-instrument) (zither). It is also used in small [chamber music](https://www.britannica.com/art/chamber-music) ensembles. The earliest known _xiao_ is made of bird bone and dates to roughly 6000 bc. Not until the Jin dynasty (ad 265–420) was the number and position of the holes on the instrument standardized, and its name was not finalized until the [Ming dynasty](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ming-dynasty-Chinese-history) (1368–1644).

A variant of the _xiao_, the _nanyin dongxiao_ (“southern sound notched flute”), or _chiba_ (literally “one foot, eight inches”) found mainly in Fujian and Taiwan, varies in length from roughly 13 to 16 inches (34 to 43 cm) and uses the bamboo root as its bottom. The number and arrangement of holes on the _nanyin dongxiao_ are the same as those on the _xiao_, but the body of the _nanyin dongxiao_ is thicker. Although the V-shaped notch is retained, the top end is open, not closed as in the _xiao_. In performance the player covers the opening with his chin. The _nanyin dongxiao_ is an important instrument in the _nanyin_ (“southern music”; Fujianese) or _nanguan_ (“southern pipes”; Taiwanese) ensemble. It is the [prototype](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/prototype) that was introduced to Japan in the 14th century and eventually evolved into the [_shakuhachi_](https://www.britannica.com/art/shakuhachi).

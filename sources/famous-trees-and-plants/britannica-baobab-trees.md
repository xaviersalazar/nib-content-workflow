- What is a baobab tree?







Baobabs, from the genus _Adansonia_, are deciduous trees in the [mallow family](https://www.britannica.com/plant/baobab-tree-genus/Introduction#ref1) known for their barrel-like trunks. Of the nine species, six are native to Madagascar, two to mainland Africa and the Arabian Peninsula, and one to northwestern Australia. They are known for their longevity and ethnobotanical importance.



The African baobab ( _A. digitata_) is called the "Tree of Life" and can live for over 1,275 years. It has a water-storing trunk that can reach 9 meters (30 feet) in diameter and 18 meters (59 feet) in height. Its flowers are pollinated by bats and bush babies, and its leaves are edible.

- Where do baobab trees naturally grow?







Baobab trees (genus _Adansonia_) are native to Madagascar, mainland Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, and northwestern Australia. Six species are endemic to Madagascar, two to mainland Africa and the Arabian Peninsula, and one to northwestern Australia.



The African baobab ( _A. digitata_) can be found throughout the drier regions of Africa. The Australian baobab ( _A. gregorii_), also called boab or bottle tree, is found throughout the Kimberley region of Western Australia.

- How do baobab trees survive in dry environments?







Baobab trees are well-adapted to survive in dry environments because they can store water in their thick, barrel-like trunks. The African baobab ( _A. digitata_) is known as the "Tree of Life" because its water-storing trunk can reach up to 9 meters (30 feet) in diameter.



Additionally, baobabs have deep roots that can reach permanently moist sand levels, allowing them to thrive even in areas with low precipitation. The Australian baobab ( _A. gregorii_) sheds its compound leaves during drought periods as well. These adaptations allow baobabs to survive and thrive in dry environments.

- Why are baobab trees important to animals and people?







Baobab trees are important to both animals and people because they are extensively used by local populations. Many baobab species have edible leaves and fruits and are important for herbal remedies. **baobab**, (genus _Adansonia_), [genus](https://www.britannica.com/science/genus-taxon) of nine species of deciduous [trees](https://www.britannica.com/plant/tree) of the [mallow family](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Malvaceae), noted for their unusual barrel-like trunks. Six of species are [endemic](https://www.britannica.com/science/endemic-species) to [Madagascar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madagascar), two are native to mainland Africa and the [Arabian Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arabia-peninsula-Asia), and one is native to northwestern [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia). Baobabs are known for their extraordinary longevity and [ethnobotanical](https://www.britannica.com/science/ethnobotany) importance. Given their peculiar shape, an Arabian [legend](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legend) has it that “the devil plucked up the baobab, thrust its branches into the earth, and left its roots in the air.”

All baobab species are extensively used by local peoples. Many species have edible leaves and fruits and are important for a number of herbal remedies. A strong [fiber](https://www.britannica.com/technology/fiber-technology) from the bark is used for rope and cloth in many places, and the trees supply raw materials for hunting and fishing tools. Naturally hollow or excavated trunks often serve as [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) reserves or temporary shelters and have even been used as prisons, burial sites, and stables. The trees are culturally and religiously important in many areas.

The [African baobab](https://www.britannica.com/plant/baobab-tree-Adansonia-digitata) ( _A. digitata_) boasts the oldest known [angiosperm](https://www.britannica.com/plant/angiosperm) tree: [carbon-14 dating](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-14-dating) places the age of a [specimen](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/specimen) in [Namibia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Namibia) at about 1,275 years. Known as the “Tree of Life,” the species is found throughout the drier regions of Africa and features a water-storing trunk that may reach a diameter of 9 meters (30 feet) and a height of 18 meters (59 feet). Older individuals often have huge hollow trunks that are formed by the fusion of multiple stems over time. The tree’s unique pendulous [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) are pollinated by [bats](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bat-mammal) and [bush babies](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bush-baby). Its young [leaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy) are edible, and the large gourdlike woody [fruit](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body) contains a tasty mucilaginous pulp from which a refreshing drink can be made. Since 2005, 9 of the 13 oldest African baobab [specimens](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/specimens) and 5 of the 6 largest trees have died or suffered the collapse and death of their largest or oldest stems, a statistically unlikely phenomenon that scientists suggested may have been caused by the effects of [climate change](https://www.britannica.com/science/global-warming).

[Grandidier's baobab](https://cdn.britannica.com/15/159715-050-D5976984/Grandidier-baobab-species-Madagascar-tree-native.jpg) A stately Grandidier's baobab ( _Adansonia grandidieri_), an endangered species of tree native to Madagascar.(more)

The six Madagascan baobab species feature compact crowns and gray-brown to red trunks that taper from top to bottom or are bottle-shaped to cylindrical. The flowers range from red to yellow to white and have five petals. Some species are pollinated by bats and [lemurs](https://www.britannica.com/animal/lemur-primate-suborder), while others rely on [hawk moths](https://www.britannica.com/animal/hawk-moth). Given the threats of [habitat loss](https://www.britannica.com/science/habitat-loss) and their slow generation time, two species ( _[A. grandidieri](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Adansonia-grandidieri)_, and _[A. suarezensis](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Adansonia-suarezensis)_) are listed as [endangered](https://www.britannica.com/science/endangered-species) on the [IUCN Red List of Threatened Species](https://www.britannica.com/topic/IUCN-Red-List-of-Threatened-Species), including the [iconic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/iconic) baobabs of the famous Avenue of the Baobabs ( _A. grandidieri_) in the [Menabe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Menabe) region. Human development particularly threatens _[A. perrieri](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Adansonia-perrieri)_, which is [critically endangered](https://www.britannica.com/science/critically-endangered-species). The remaining three species ( _[A. madagascariensis](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Adansonia-madagasacariensis)_, _[A. rubrostipa](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Adansonia-rubrostipa)_, and _[A. za](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Adansonia-za)_) are considered of “least concern.”

The single Australian baobab species, _A. gregorii_, called [boab](https://www.britannica.com/plant/baobab-tree-Adansonia-gregorii), or [bottle tree](https://www.britannica.com/plant/bottle-tree), is found throughout the [Kimberley](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kimberley-region-Australia) region of [Western Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Western-Australia). Reaching heights of about 12 meters (39 feet), the tree features the characteristically swollen trunk of the genus and bears [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) leaves that are completely shed during [drought](https://www.britannica.com/science/drought) periods. The white flowers are large, perfumed, and pollinated by hawk moths. Although that species was once presumed to be a remnant left behind when the [Gondwana](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gondwana-historical-region-India) landmass broke apart 180 million years ago, the fact that the boab has not evolved to be markedly different from other baobabs suggests a much younger age for the species and that the boab originally came to Australia by long-distance [seed dispersal](https://www.britannica.com/science/seed-dispersal) from Africa.

- What is Africa?

- What are some major physical features of Africa?







Africa, the second-largest continent, has diverse physical features. The [Sahara](https://www.britannica.com/list/8-amazing-physical-features-of-africa), the world's largest hot desert, spans almost all of northern Africa. - What are some of the main languages and cultures found in Africa?







Africa has a diverse array of languages and cultures. It is believed that there are over 1,500 distinct languages spoken on the continent. Many African societies or ethnic groups are identified by a shared culture, language, religion, and history. However, frequent migrations and interactions have blurred ethnic distinctions. - How has Africa's history influenced its modern countries and societies?







Africa's history significantly influences its modern countries and societies through various factors. As the birthplace of humanity, the continent has seen extensive movement of people, shaping its diverse cultures and societies.



The [Atlantic slave trade](https://www.britannica.com/place/Africa/Western-Africa#ref37199) caused substantial loss of people and internal disruption, weakening African societies. European colonization further reshaped the continent, with the [Scramble for Africa](https://www.britannica.com/event/Scramble-for-Africa/Introduction#ref385787) leading to redrawn borders that disregarded existing ethnic and cultural lines, the alteration of economic systems, and the imposition of foreign languages and governance. Even after decolonization, the legacy of colonialism has hindered Africa’s development. These historical forces continue to shape the political, social, and economic landscapes of African nations.

**Africa**, the second largest [continent](https://www.britannica.com/science/continent) (after [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia)), covering about one-fifth of the total land surface of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth). The continent is bounded on the west by the [Atlantic Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean), on the north by the [Mediterranean Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mediterranean-Sea), on the east by the [Red Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Red-Sea) and the [Indian Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indian-Ocean), and on the south by the [mingling](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/mingling) waters of the Atlantic and Indian oceans.

Africa’s total land area is approximately 11,724,000 square miles (30,365,000 square km), and the continent measures about 5,000 miles (8,000 km) from north to south and about 4,600 miles (7,400 km) from east to west. Its northern extremity is Al-Ghīrān Point, near Al-Abyaḍ Point (Cape Blanc), Tunisia; its southern extremity is [Cape Agulhas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cape-Agulhas), South Africa; its farthest point east is Xaafuun (Hafun) Point, near Cape Gwardafuy (Guardafui), Somalia; and its western extremity is Almadi Point (Pointe des Almadies), on [Cape Verde](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cape-Verde-Peninsula) (Cap Vert), [Senegal](https://www.britannica.com/place/Senegal). In the northeast, Africa was joined to Asia by the [Sinai Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sinai-Peninsula) until the construction of the [Suez Canal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Suez-Canal). Paradoxically, the coastline of Africa—18,950 miles (30,500 km) in length—is shorter than that of [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe), because there are few inlets and few large bays or gulfs.

Off the coasts of Africa a number of islands are associated with the continent. Of these [Madagascar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madagascar), one of the largest islands in the world, is the most significant. Other, smaller islands include the [Seychelles](https://www.britannica.com/place/Seychelles), [Socotra](https://www.britannica.com/place/Socotra), and other islands to the east; the [Comoros](https://www.britannica.com/place/Comoros), [Mauritius](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mauritius), [Réunion](https://www.britannica.com/place/Reunion), and other islands to the southeast; [Ascension](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ascension-island-Atlantic-Ocean), [St. Helena](https://www.britannica.com/place/Saint-Helena-island-South-Atlantic-Ocean), and [Tristan da Cunha](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tristan-da-Cunha-island-group) to the southwest; [Cape Verde](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cabo-Verde), [the Bijagós Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bijagos-Islands), [Bioko](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bioko), and [São Tomé and Príncipe](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sao-Tome-and-Principe) to the west; and the [Azores](https://www.britannica.com/place/Azores) and the [Madeira](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madeira-Islands) and [Canary](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canary-Islands) islands to the northwest.

The continent is cut almost equally in two by the [Equator](https://www.britannica.com/place/Equator), so that most of Africa lies within the tropical region, bounded on the north by the [Tropic of Cancer](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tropic-of-Cancer) and on the south by the [Tropic of Capricorn](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tropic-of-Capricorn). Because of the bulge formed by western Africa, the greater part of Africa’s territory lies north of the Equator. Africa is crossed from north to south by the [prime meridian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greenwich-meridian) (0° longitude), which passes a short distance to the east of [Accra](https://www.britannica.com/place/Accra), [Ghana](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ghana).

In antiquity the Greeks are said to have called the continent [Libya](https://www.britannica.com/place/Libya) and the Romans to have called it Africa, perhaps from the Latin _aprica_ (“sunny”) or the Greek _aphrike_ (“without cold”). The name Africa, however, was chiefly applied to the northern coast of the continent, which was, in effect, regarded as a southern extension of Europe. The Romans, who for a time ruled the North African coast, are also said to have called the area south of their settlements Afriga, or the Land of the Afrigs—the name of a Berber [community](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community) south of [Carthage](https://www.britannica.com/place/Carthage-ancient-city-Tunisia).

The whole of Africa can be considered as a vast plateau rising steeply from narrow coastal strips and consisting of ancient crystalline rocks. The plateau’s surface is higher in the southeast and tilts downward toward the northeast. In general the plateau may be divided into a southeastern portion and a northwestern portion. The northwestern part, which includes the [Sahara](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sahara-desert-Africa) (desert) and that part of North Africa known as the [Maghrib](https://www.britannica.com/place/Maghreb), has two mountainous regions—the [Atlas Mountains](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlas-Mountains) in northwestern Africa, which are believed to be part of a system that extends into southern Europe, and the [Ahaggar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ahaggar) (Hoggar) Mountains in the Sahara. The southeastern part of the plateau includes the [Ethiopian Plateau](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ethiopian-Plateau), the East African Plateau, and—in eastern [South Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Africa), where the plateau edge falls downward in a scarp—the [Drakensberg](https://www.britannica.com/place/Drakensberg) range. One of the most remarkable features in the geologic structure of Africa is the [East African Rift System](https://www.britannica.com/place/East-African-Rift-System), which lies between 30° and 40° E. The rift itself begins northeast of the continent’s limits and extends southward from the Eritrean Red Sea coast to the [Zambezi River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Zambezi-River) basin.

Africa contains an enormous wealth of mineral resources, including some of the world’s largest reserves of [fossil](https://www.britannica.com/science/fossil) fuels, metallic ores, and gems and [precious](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/precious) metals. This richness is matched by a great [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity) of biological resources that includes the intensely lush equatorial rainforests of Central Africa and the world-famous populations of wildlife of the eastern and southern portions of the continent. Although agriculture (primarily subsistence) still dominates the economies of many African countries, the exploitation of these resources became the most significant economic activity in Africa in the 20th century.

Climatic and other factors have exerted considerable influence on the patterns of [human](https://www.britannica.com/topic/human-being) settlement in Africa. While some areas appear to have been inhabited more or less continuously since the dawn of humanity, enormous regions—notably the [desert](https://www.britannica.com/science/desert) areas of northern and southwestern Africa—have been largely unoccupied for prolonged periods of time. Thus, although Africa is the second largest continent, it contains only about 10 percent of the world’s population and can be said to be underpopulated. The greater part of the continent has long been [inhabited](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/inhabited) by Black peoples, but in historic times there also have occurred major immigrations from both Asia and Europe. Of all foreign settlements in Africa, that of the Arabs has made the greatest impact. The Islamic religion, which the Arabs carried with them, spread from North Africa into many areas south of the Sahara, so that many western African peoples are now largely Islamized.

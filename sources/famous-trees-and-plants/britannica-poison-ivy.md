**poison ivy**, ( _Toxicodendron radicans_), poisonous [vine](https://www.britannica.com/plant/vine-plant) or [shrub](https://www.britannica.com/plant/shrub) of the cashew family ( [Anacardiaceae](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Anacardiaceae)), native to eastern [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America). Nearly all parts of the [plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant) contain urushiol. When the plant is touched, the substance produces in many persons a severe, itchy, and painful inflammation of the skin known as [contact dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/contact-dermatitis).

The plants are highly variable in growth habit. The [leaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy) characteristically have three leaflets, which may be hairless and glossy or hairy, entire, toothed, or lobed. Young leaves are often tinged with red, and the mature leaves change to red, orange, or yellow in the [autumn](https://www.britannica.com/science/autumn-season). The plants are dioecious, meaning that an individual is either male or female. The [flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower) of both sexes are small and yellow to green. The [fruits](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body) of the female plants are white or greenish [drupes](https://www.britannica.com/science/drupe) and are an important winter food for many [birds](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal).

Poisoning may occur if clothing is worn up to a year after contact with poison ivy.

- What is dermatitis?







[Dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref1) is an inflammation of the skin characterized by redness, swelling, blisters, oozing, and itching. Environmental factors can significantly influence the risk of dermatitis, and research suggests that omega-3 fatty acids may help reduce symptoms.



Dermatitis is classified into several types, including [contact dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284), [atopic dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284), [stasis dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284), [seborrheic dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284), and [neurodermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284). Treatment goals involve reducing symptoms and preventing exposure to irritants. Treatments include special creams, lotions, topical corticosteroids, and immunomodulators.

- What are common symptoms of dermatitis?







Dermatitis, also known as eczema, involves inflammation of the upper dermis and epidermis. Common symptoms include redness, thickening, and scaling of the skin, often in patches. The skin becomes extremely dry, leading to itching. In severe cases, blisters may form.



Different types of dermatitis have different manifestations. For example, seborrheic dermatitis, a scaly skin condition, commonly affects the scalp, resulting in dandruff. Allergic contact dermatitis is usually suspected from the distribution of the rash, corresponding to areas of skin exposed to the antigen. Atopic dermatitis is characterized by sensitization of the skin to a wide range of common antigens.

- What causes dermatitis to develop?







Dermatitis can arise from varied factors, and it is classified into several types. [Contact dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284) occurs when skin touches irritating substances or allergens, like poison ivy or certain chemicals. [Atopic dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284), the most common form, is linked to immune dysfunction, potentially triggering allergic reactions to substances such as foods. Environmental factors and genetics also play a role.



[Stasis dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284) results from poor blood flow, while [seborrheic dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284) is a scaly condition affecting oily areas and is associated with yeasts and bacteria. [Neurodermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284) is caused by chronic scratching.

- How does dermatitis affect the skin on a microscopic level?







Dermatitis, also known as eczema, is an inflammation of the skin that involves the upper dermis and epidermis. At the microscopic level, the epidermis shows swelling of keratinocytes and fluid accumulation between them, a process called spongiosis. In severe cases, blisters form within the epidermis.



In chronic dermatitis, the epidermis thickens, and hyperkeratosis (thickening of the outer horny layer) occurs, leading to roughening, scaling, and intense itching. Impairment of the skin's barrier function results in water loss, causing cracking, scaling, and increased absorption of topical medications, potentially leading to toxic effects.

- How do doctors diagnose and treat dermatitis?







Doctors diagnose [dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/dermatitis/Introduction#ref279284) by identifying the irritant causing the condition and subsequent avoidance of it. Patch tests confirm allergic contact dermatitis by placing minute concentrations of suspected antigens on the skin.



Treatments aim to reduce symptoms, heal the skin, and prevent exposure to irritants and allergens. Special creams and lotions with anti-inflammatory compounds soften and soothe the skin, and topical corticosteroids relieve itching. Other therapies include antihistamines, phototherapy, and photochemotherapy. For stasis dermatitis, improving blood circulation in the legs can help prevent it.

**dermatitis**, an [inflammation](https://www.britannica.com/science/inflammation) of the skin usually characterized by redness, swelling, blister formation, and oozing and almost always by [itching](https://www.britannica.com/science/itching). The term _eczema_, which formerly referred to the blistered, oozing state of inflamed skin, has by common usage come to have the same meaning as _dermatitis_.

## Types of dermatitis

Dermatitis may be classified into several types. [Contact dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/contact-dermatitis) results from contact of the skin with an irritating substance or a substance to which the person is [allergic](https://www.britannica.com/science/immune-system-disorder/Deficiencies-caused-by-malnutrition#ref215500). The inflammation can result from contact with a strong acid or alkali or some other chemical, or it can result from contact with [innocuous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/innocuous) substances (e.g., cosmetics, soap, clothing) which are not primarily irritating in themselves but which produce a reaction in a person who has been sensitized by repeated exposure to them. The most common cause of contact dermatitis in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) and some other countries is [poison ivy](https://www.britannica.com/plant/poison-ivy-plant). Many other plants can cause the condition. Chemical [compounds](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounds) that may cause contact dermatitis include such metals as [nickel](https://www.britannica.com/science/nickel-chemical-element) and [chromium](https://www.britannica.com/science/chromium), certain [aniline](https://www.britannica.com/science/aniline) dyes, and many types of drugs. The prevention of contact dermatitis rests upon the identification of the particular irritant and subsequent avoidance of it.

[Atopic dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/atopic-dermatitis), the most common type of dermatitis, typically begins in infancy and can last into adulthood. It is characterized by redness, thickening, and scaling of the skin in patches, typically on the [face](https://www.britannica.com/science/face-anatomy), [neck](https://www.britannica.com/science/neck-anatomy), hands, feet, in the crook of the elbow, or behind the [knee](https://www.britannica.com/science/knee). The skin becomes extremely dry, and this leads to itching. In adulthood the severity of skin [symptoms](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/symptoms) tends to decrease; however, about three-quarters of affected individuals eventually develop hay fever or asthma.

Scientists have identified abnormal levels of immune substances in people affected by atopic dermatitis, indicating that immune dysfunction plays an important role, likely triggering allergic reactions to normally innocuous substances. For example, in some affected people, chemical compounds associated with certain foods, plants, and animals may stimulate an inappropriate immune reaction that gives rise not only to skin symptoms but also to congestion, wheezing, and gastrointestinal upset. Research also has shown that individuals affected by both atopic dermatitis and [food allergy](https://www.britannica.com/science/food-allergy) have structural and molecular differences in the outer layers of their skin; individuals affected only by atopic dermatitis do not show these differences. A dysfunctional skin barrier is suspected of enabling food allergens to more easily irritate [immune](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/immune) cells, contributing to food allergy. Irritants in tobacco smoke, detergents, industrial chemicals, and certain fabrics can also aggravate skin symptoms. The underlying cause of atopic dermatitis is not known, but it tends to run in families whose members have [hay fever](https://www.britannica.com/science/hay-fever-pathology) and [asthma](https://www.britannica.com/science/asthma).

[Stasis dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/stasis-dermatitis) is a skin inflammation affecting the ankles and lower legs. The condition is caused by chronic poor blood flow in the veins and particularly by [varicose veins](https://www.britannica.com/science/varicose-vein). The poor blood flow brings about swelling and a progressively more [acute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/acute) irritation of the skin that may lead to ulceration. Stasis dermatitis can be prevented if steps are taken early to improve the blood circulation of the legs, such as wearing supportive stockings or having surgery performed on the varicose veins.

[Seborrheic dermatitis](https://www.britannica.com/science/seborrheic-dermatitis) is a scaly skin condition that most frequently affects the scalp, [dandruff](https://www.britannica.com/science/dandruff) being the common name for the skin particles that scale off the scalp. The condition generally involves body areas that are rich in [sebaceous, or oil-secreting, glands](https://www.britannica.com/science/sebaceous-gland), and it can also affect the forehead and eyebrows, the middle of the face, the area behind the ears, and the armpits. Most individuals with seborrheic dermatitis tend to have oily skin. During infancy, seborrheic dermatitis may commonly [manifest](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifest) itself as a yellowish scaling of part of the scalp, a condition referred to as cradle cap.

Neurodermatitis refers to a skin inflammation that is apparently caused by the patient’s own repeated and chronic scratching of an itchy area of skin.

## Environmental influences and treatment

Research has indicated that environmental factors significantly influence the overall risk of dermatitis. For example, children who are breast-fed for four months or longer have a reduced risk of dermatitis. In addition, compounds called [omega-3 fatty acids](https://www.britannica.com/science/omega-3-fatty-acid), which have anti-inflammatory properties, have become an important area of dermatitis research. Skin creams containing omega-3 fatty acids can lessen the severity of skin symptoms and, in some cases, prevent dermatitis. Studies comparing the early diets of children with and without dermatitis have indicated that incorporating fish into a child’s diet before nine months of age reduces the risk of dermatitis by 25 percent. Scientists suspect that this reduction is mediated by omega-3 fatty acids, which are present in large amounts in some fish.

The general goals of treatment for dermatitis involve reducing symptoms, healing the skin, and preventing exposure to irritants and allergens that cause outbreaks. Special creams and lotions that contain anti-inflammatory compounds may be used to soften and soothe the skin, and topical [corticosteroids](https://www.britannica.com/science/corticoid) may be applied to the skin to relieve itching. However, corticosteroid-containing medications can cause skin-thinning, dilation of superficial blood vessels that leads to unsightly red spots on the skin, and abnormal skin pigmentation. There is also a risk of systemic absorption of corticosteroids, which can lead to disruption of normal physiological [steroid](https://www.britannica.com/science/steroid) production. Topical immunomodulators (TIMs), which are steroid-free skin medications, have been developed. These agents work by [inhibiting](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inhibiting) the activation of immune substances. However, due to their potentially dangerous side effects (e.g., lymphoma), TIMs are considered second-line treatments for dermatitis. Other therapies used for dermatitis include antihistamines, phototherapy, which uses [ultraviolet radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/ultraviolet-radiation) to suppress immune reactions, and photochemotherapy, which combines phototherapy with the administration of a light-sensitizing [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) such as psoralen.

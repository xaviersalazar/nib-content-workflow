- What is a cactus?







A cactus is a perennial succulent plant that thrives in dry regions of the Western Hemisphere. Cacti are known for their thick stems containing chlorophyll and areoles, unique structures that produce plant hairs, spines, bristles, flowers, branches, and leaves.



Most cacti lack leaves or have reduced leaves modified as spines to minimize water loss. Their shallow root systems efficiently absorb moisture. Cacti vary in size and shape, from small peyote to tall saguaros. While most grow in the ground, some are epiphytes that grow on other plants.



People cultivate cacti as ornamentals, food sources, living fences, and medicine.

- Where do cacti usually grow?







Cacti are native to the Americas, mainly in dry regions, though some can be found in tropical or subtropical areas. Mexico exhibits the greatest diversity of cactus species. While most cacti thrive in soil, some species such as leaf cactus, _Rhipsalis_, and _Schlumbergera_ are epiphytes, growing on other plants, rocks, or trees.



Desert plant communities are influenced by available moisture and local environmental factors. The Sonoran Desert in North America, for example, features large cacti like the saguaro, while the Mojave Desert is known for Joshua trees.

- How do cacti survive in very dry environments?







Cacti are well-adapted to survive in dry environments through several unique features. As [succulents](https://www.britannica.com/plant/succulent/Introduction#ref1), they have thick, fleshy tissues designed for water storage, primarily in their stems or leaves.



To minimize water loss, cacti have reduced or absent leaves, often modified into spines, and their stems have taken over the photosynthetic functions. Many cacti also use a specialized process called [crassulacean acid metabolism](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant/Photosynthesis#ref66106) (CAM), where they open their stomata at night to take in carbon dioxide and close them during the day to prevent water loss. Their root systems are generally shallow and fibrous, allowing them to absorb superficial moisture over a wide area.

- What are the main parts of a cactus?







Cacti are perennial plants with thick stems containing chlorophyll. They are known for their areoles, which are cushion-like structures that produce plant hairs and spines. While most cacti lack leaves to minimize water loss, some species have leaves that carry out photosynthesis.



The root systems of cacti are thin and spread out to absorb moisture. Cacti vary in size and shape, from small peyote to tall saguaro.



The primary method of reproduction is by seeds. Cacti flowers are often large and colorful with a floral tube. The stems of cacti have a cambium layer and storage cells that contain substances that prevent moisture loss, which also helps with food production and storage. Spines defend the juicy stems of cacti and also shade them from the sun.

- Why do cacti have spines instead of leaves?







Cacti have spines instead of leaves to minimize the surface area from which water can be lost. In most cactus species, the stem has taken over the photosynthetic functions of the plant. Spines also defend the juicy stems of cacti against animals and shade them from the desert sun. Cacti can be distinguished from other succulent plants by the presence of spines that develop from areoles. These spines may be stiff, curved, hooked, feathery, bristly, flattened, sheathed, or needlelike.

- How do people use cacti in their daily lives?







People use cacti in their daily lives in a variety of ways. They are widely cultivated as [ornamentals](https://www.britannica.com/plant/cactus/Introduction#ref339682) and used in rock gardens. Various species, notably prickly pears and chollas, are [cultivated as food](https://www.britannica.com/plant/cactus/Introduction#ref339682). In Central and South America, species of _Opuntia_, _Cereus_, and others serve as living fences, while the wood from columnar cacti is used as fuel in some desert regions.



During droughts, the spines are removed from cacti such as mandacaru ( _Cereus jamacaru_) to use as fodder for livestock. Peyote, from _Lophophora williamsii_, has been used ceremonially since pre-Columbian times for its hallucinogenic properties, and many cactus species are of local importance in traditional medicine.

**cactus**, (family Cactaceae), [flowering plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/angiosperm) family (order [Caryophyllales](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Caryophyllales)) with nearly 2,000 species and 139 [genera](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/genera). Cacti are native through most of the length of North and South America, from [British Columbia](https://www.britannica.com/place/British-Columbia) and [Alberta](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alberta-province) southward; the southernmost limit of their range extends far into [Chile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chile) and [Argentina](https://www.britannica.com/place/Argentina). [Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mexico) has the greatest number and variety of species. The only cacti possibly native to the Old World are members of the genus [_Rhipsalis_](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Rhipsalis), occurring in [East Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/eastern-Africa), [Madagascar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madagascar), and [Sri Lanka](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sri-Lanka). Although a few cactus species inhabit tropical or subtropical areas, most live in and are well adapted to dry regions. _See also_ [list of plants in the family Cactaceae](https://www.britannica.com/topic/list-of-plants-in-the-family-Cactaceae-2041552).

Cacti are succulent [perennial](https://www.britannica.com/science/perennial) plants. Cacti generally have thick herbaceous or woody chlorophyll-containing [stems](https://www.britannica.com/science/stem-plant). Cacti can be distinguished from other [succulent](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/succulent) plants by the presence of [areoles](https://www.britannica.com/science/areole), small cushionlike structures with trichomes (plant hairs) and, in almost all species, spines or barbed bristles (glochids). Areoles are modified branches, from which flowers, more branches, and leaves (when present) may grow.

[![rose cactus](https://cdn.britannica.com/10/123110-120-7577631F/Rose-cactus.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/10/123110-120-7577631F/Rose-cactus.jpg)

[rose cactus](https://cdn.britannica.com/10/123110-120-7577631F/Rose-cactus.jpg) Rose cactus ( _Pereskia grandifolia_).(more)

In most species, [leaves](https://www.britannica.com/science/leaf-plant-anatomy) are absent, greatly reduced, or modified as spines, minimizing the amount of surface area from which water can be lost, and the [stem](https://www.britannica.com/science/stem-plant) has taken over the [photosynthetic](https://www.britannica.com/science/photosynthesis) functions of the [plant](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant). Only the tropical genera [_Pereskia_](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Pereskia) and _Pereskopsis_, both [vines](https://www.britannica.com/plant/vine-plant), have conventional-looking functional leaves, while the leaves of the Andean _Maihuenia_ are rounded, not flattened. The [root](https://www.britannica.com/science/root-plant) systems are generally thin, fibrous, and shallow, ranging widely to absorb [superficial](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/superficial) moisture.

Cacti vary greatly in size and general appearance, from buttonlike [peyote](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Lophophora-williamsii) ( _Lophophora_) and low clumps of [prickly pear](https://www.britannica.com/plant/prickly-pear) ( [_Opuntia_](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Opuntia)) and [hedgehog cactus](https://www.britannica.com/plant/hedgehog-cactus) ( _Echinocereus_) to the upright columns of [barrel cacti](https://www.britannica.com/plant/barrel-cactus) ( _Ferocactus_ and _Echinocactus_) and the imposing [saguaro](https://www.britannica.com/plant/saguaro) ( _Carnegiea gigantea_). Most cacti grow in the ground, but several tropical species—including [leaf cactus](https://www.britannica.com/plant/leaf-cactus) ( _Epiphyllum_), [_Rhipsalis_](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Rhipsalis), and [_Schlumbergera_](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Schlumbergera)—are [epiphytes](https://www.britannica.com/plant/epiphyte), growing on other plants; others live on hard substrates such as rocks, while yet others climb far up trees. Epiphytic species tend to have thin, almost leaflike flattened stems. The appearance of the plant varies also according to whether the stem surface is smooth or ornamented with [protruding](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/protruding) tubercles, ridges, or grooves.

The primary method of [reproduction](https://www.britannica.com/science/reproduction-biology) is by [seeds](https://www.britannica.com/science/seed-plant-reproductive-part). [Flowers](https://www.britannica.com/science/flower), often large and colourful, are usually solitary. All genera have a floral tube, often with many petal-like structures, and other less colourful and almost leaflike structures; the tube grows above a one-chambered ovary. A style topped by many [pollen](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollen)-receptive stigmas also arises from the top of the ovary. The [fruit](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body) is usually a [berry](https://www.britannica.com/science/berry-plant-reproductive-body) and contains many seeds. Soon after [pollination](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollination), which may be effected by wind, [birds](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bird-animal), [insects](https://www.britannica.com/animal/insect), or [bats](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bat-mammal), the entire floral tube detaches from the top of the ovary to leave a prominent scar.

Several cacti develop plantlets at ground level that, as [offsets](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/offsets), reproduce the species vegetatively. Many others can reproduce by fragmentation, whereby segments broken from the main plant will readily root to form clonal individuals. Tissues of cacti are broadly compatible so that terminal portions of one species may be grafted on top of another.

The internal structure of cacti stems conforms to the pattern of broad-leaved angiosperms; a [cambium](https://www.britannica.com/science/cambium) layer of dividing cells, located between the woody inner tissues and those near the outside of the stem, is present. The bulk of the stem, however, consists of thin-walled storage cells that contain mucilaginous substances that prevent the loss of moisture. The stem of cacti is the main food-manufacturing and food-storage organ for most species.

## Uses

[![Prickly pear](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/100631-050-F219B9CB/Prickly-pear-cactus-Arizona.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/100631-050-F219B9CB/Prickly-pear-cactus-Arizona.jpg)

[Prickly pear](https://cdn.britannica.com/31/100631-050-F219B9CB/Prickly-pear-cactus-Arizona.jpg) Ripe fruits of a prickly pear cactus ( _Opuntia_), Arizona.(more)

Cacti are widely [cultivated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultivated) as ornamentals. In addition, various species, notably [prickly pears](https://www.britannica.com/plant/prickly-pear) and [chollas](https://www.britannica.com/plant/cholla) ( _Opuntia_ and _Cylindopuntia_, respectively), are cultivated as food. In times of [drought](https://www.britannica.com/science/drought), the spines are removed from cacti such as [mandacaru](https://www.britannica.com/plant/mandacaru) ( _Cereus jamacaru_) to use as fodder for [livestock](https://www.britannica.com/animal/livestock). [Peyote](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Lophophora-williamsii), from _Lophophora williamsii_, has been used ceremonially since pre-Columbian times for its [hallucinogenic](https://www.britannica.com/science/hallucinogen) properties, and many cactus species are of local importance in traditional medicine.

- What is a desert?







A desert is an environment characterized by little available moisture for plant life due to imbalances between precipitation and evapotranspiration. Desert regions experience considerable rainfall variability, low atmospheric humidity, high daytime temperatures, and winds.



Average annual precipitation usually ranges from almost zero to 400 millimeters, but some authorities consider 250 millimeters as the upper limit. The shortage of water determines which plants and animals can survive there, and they possess unique adaptations to thrive in dry conditions and make the most of brief wet periods.



Desertification, or the expansion of desert environments, is a growing concern resulting from climatic variations and unsustainable human activities.

- What kind of weather do deserts usually have?







Deserts are known for their shortage of moisture for plants because of imbalances between precipitation and evapotranspiration. This is made worse by variable rainfall, low humidity, high daytime temperatures, and winds.



Most recognized deserts get less than 16 inches (400 mm) of rain annually. Some deserts get almost no rain, while others can get up to 24 inches (600 mm). Rainfall, when it occurs, can be very heavy but brief.



Temperatures in hot deserts can be very high during the day, exceeding 104 °F (40 °C). The highest recorded temperature was 136.4 °F (58 °C) in Libya. Night temperatures can fall dramatically because of the lack of cloud cover.



Temperate or cold deserts, found at higher latitudes, experience colder temperatures. These deserts are arid due to their remoteness from the coast or the presence of mountains.

- What plants and animals live in deserts?







Deserts are home to a variety of plants and animals that have adapted to arid conditions. Desert plants, also known as xerophytes, include deep-rooted phreatophytes that tap into groundwater, as well as succulents that store water in their fleshy tissues. Examples of desert flora include sagebrush, saltbush, Joshua trees, cacti, and thorny shrubs like mesquite.



Desert fauna includes reptiles, birds, rodents, and larger mammals. Many desert animals, such as the jerboa, are nocturnal, seeking shelter in cool burrows during the day. Some desert birds are nomadic, relocating to areas where rain has fallen to find food. Protective coloration is an important feature of desert life.

- How do plants and animals survive in the harsh conditions of a desert?







Desert plants and animals have developed unique strategies to survive the harsh desert environment. Plants are typically [xerophytes](https://www.britannica.com/plant/xerophyte/Introduction#ref1) or [phreatophytes](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-American-Desert/Introduction#ref41401). Xerophytes are plants adapted to arid conditions that prevent water loss by having thick, fleshy stems or leaves, waxy coatings, or the ability to drop leaves during dry periods. Phreatophytes are deep-rooted plants that rely on a permanent water supply and survive by tapping into groundwater.



Desert animals often avoid the heat by staying in cool, humid burrows during the day and emerging at night to find food. To conserve water, desert animals have skin relatively impervious to water, and some can lower the temperature of exhaled air to reduce water loss during respiration. Also, protective coloration is an important feature of desert life.

- What are some differences between hot deserts and cold deserts?







Hot and cold deserts differ primarily in temperature and location. Hot deserts are in subtropical regions, while [cold deserts](https://www.britannica.com/science/cold-desert) are in temperate areas at higher latitudes and elevations.



Hot deserts experience high daytime temperatures, with maximums exceeding 104°F (40°C), whereas [cold deserts](https://www.britannica.com/science/cold-desert) have long, cold winters with average temperatures below freezing. Precipitation, mainly as snow or fog, is scant in cold deserts. Both experience low humidity and considerable rainfall variability.



The [Gobi Desert](https://www.britannica.com/science/cold-desert) in China and Mongolia and the [Great Basin Desert](https://www.britannica.com/science/cold-desert) in the United States are examples of cold deserts, while the [Sonoran](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-American-Desert/Introduction#ref41400) and [Chihuahuan Deserts](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chihuahuan-Desert/Introduction#ref427974) in North America are hot deserts.

## Origin

The desert environments of the present are, in geologic terms, relatively recent in origin. They represent the most extreme result of the progressive cooling and consequent aridification of global climates during the [Cenozoic Era](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cenozoic-Era) (65.5 million years ago to the present), which also led to the development of savannas and scrublands in the less arid regions near the tropical and temperate margins of the developing deserts. It has been suggested that many typical modern desert plant families, particularly those with an Asian centre of [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity) such as the [chenopod](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Chenopodiaceae) and tamarisk families, first appeared in the Miocene (23 to 5.3 million years ago), evolving in the salty, drying environment of the disappearing [Tethys Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tethys-Sea) along what is now the Mediterranean–Central Asian axis.

[![Ancient welwitschia](https://cdn.britannica.com/50/118250-050-D2E1D31E/Tumboa.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/50/118250-050-D2E1D31E/Tumboa.jpg)

[Ancient welwitschia](https://cdn.britannica.com/50/118250-050-D2E1D31E/Tumboa.jpg) Welwitschia, or tumboa ( _Welwitschia mirabilis_).(more)

Deserts also probably existed much earlier, during former periods of global arid [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology) in the lee of [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) ranges that sheltered them from [rain](https://www.britannica.com/science/rain) or in the centre of extensive continental regions. However, this would have been primarily before the evolution of angiosperms (flowering plants, the group to which most present-day plants, including those of deserts, belong). Only a few [primitive](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/primitive) plants, which may have been part of the ancient desert vegetation, occur in present-day deserts. One example is the bizarre conifer relative [welwitschia](https://www.britannica.com/plant/tumboa) in the [Namib](https://www.britannica.com/place/Namib) Desert of southwestern Africa. Welwitschia has only two leaves, which are leathery, straplike organs that emanate from the middle of a massive, mainly subterranean woody stem. These leaves grow perpetually from their bases and erode progressively at their ends. This desert also harbours several other plants and animals peculiarly adapted to the arid environment, suggesting that it might have a longer continuous history of arid conditions than most other deserts.

Desert floras and faunas initially evolved from ancestors in moister habitats, an evolution that occurred independently on each [continent](https://www.britannica.com/science/continent). However, a significant degree of commonality exists among the plant families that dominate different desert vegetations. This is due in part to [intrinsic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intrinsic) physiologic characteristics in some widespread desert families that preadapt the plants to an arid environment; it also is a result of plant migration occurring through chance [seed dispersal](https://www.britannica.com/science/seed-dispersal) among desert regions.

Such migration was particularly easy between northern and southern desert regions in Africa and in the Americas during intervals of drier climate that have occurred in the past two million years. This migration is reflected in close floristic similarities currently observed in these places. For example, the [creosote bush](https://www.britannica.com/plant/creosote-bush) ( _Larrea tridentata_), although now widespread and common in North American hot deserts, was probably a natural immigrant from [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) as recently as the end of the last Ice Age about 11,700 years ago.

[Migration](https://www.britannica.com/science/migration-animal) between [discrete](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/discrete) desert regions also has been relatively easier for those plants adapted to survival in saline soils because such conditions occur not only in deserts but also in [coastal](https://www.britannica.com/science/coast) habitats. For example, it is thought that the [saltbush](https://www.britannica.com/plant/saltbush) or chenopod family of plants reached [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia) in this way, initially colonizing coastal habitats and later spreading into the inland deserts.

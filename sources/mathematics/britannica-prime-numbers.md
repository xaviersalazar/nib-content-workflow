**prime**, any [positive integer](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-number) greater than 1 that is divisible only by itself and 1—e.g., 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 23, ….

A key result of [number theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/number-theory), called the [fundamental theorem of arithmetic](https://www.britannica.com/science/fundamental-theorem-of-arithmetic) ( _see_ [arithmetic: fundamental theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/arithmetic/Theory-of-divisors#ref24751)), states that every positive integer greater than 1 can be expressed as the product of prime numbers in a [unique](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/unique) fashion. Because of this, primes can be regarded as the multiplicative “building blocks” for the natural numbers (all whole numbers greater than zero—e.g., 1, 2, 3, …).

Primes have been recognized since antiquity, when they were studied by the Greek mathematicians [Euclid](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Euclid-Greek-mathematician) (fl. _c._ 300 bce) and [Eratosthenes of Cyrene](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Eratosthenes) ( _c._ 276–194 bce), among others. In his _[Elements](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Elements-by-Euclid)_, Euclid gave the first known proof that there are infinitely many primes. Various formulas have been suggested for discovering primes ( _see_ [number games: Perfect numbers and Mersenne numbers](https://www.britannica.com/topic/number-game/Pythagorean-triples#ref27912) and [Fermat prime](https://www.britannica.com/science/Fermat-prime)), but all have been flawed. Two other famous results concerning the distribution of prime numbers merit special mention: the [prime number theorem](https://www.britannica.com/science/number-theory/Prime-number-theorem#ref233906) and the [Riemann zeta function](https://www.britannica.com/science/Riemann-zeta-function).

Since the late 20th century, with the help of computers, prime numbers with millions of digits have been discovered ( _see_ [Mersenne number](https://www.britannica.com/science/Mersenne-prime)). Like efforts to generate ever more digits of [π](https://www.britannica.com/science/pi-mathematics), such [number theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/number-theory) research was thought to have no possible application—that is, until cryptographers discovered how large primes could be used to make nearly unbreakable codes ( _see_ [cryptology: Two-key cryptography](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cryptology/Product-ciphers#ref25632)).

**sieve of Eratosthenes**, [systematic](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/systematic) procedure for finding [prime numbers](https://www.britannica.com/science/prime-number) that begins by arranging all of the natural numbers (1, 2, 3, …) in numerical order. After striking out the number 1, simply strike out every second number following the number 2, every third number following the number 3, and continue in this manner to strike out every _n_ th number following the number _n_. The numbers that remain are prime. The procedure is named for the Greek astronomer [Eratosthenes of Cyrene](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Eratosthenes) (c. 276–194 bc).

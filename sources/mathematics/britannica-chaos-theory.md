**chaos theory**, in [mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mechanics) and [mathematics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics), the study of apparently random or unpredictable behaviour in systems governed by deterministic laws. A more accurate term, _deterministic chaos_, suggests a [paradox](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/paradox) because it connects two notions that are familiar and commonly regarded as incompatible. The first is that of [randomness](https://www.britannica.com/science/randomness) or unpredictability, as in the trajectory of a [molecule](https://www.britannica.com/science/molecule) in a [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter) or in the voting choice of a particular individual from out of a population. In conventional analyses, randomness was considered more apparent than real, arising from ignorance of the many causes at [work](https://www.britannica.com/science/work-physics). In other words, it was commonly believed that the world is unpredictable because it is complicated. The second notion is that of [deterministic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/determinism) [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics), as that of a [pendulum](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pendulum) or a [planet](https://www.britannica.com/science/planet), which has been accepted since the time of [Isaac Newton](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isaac-Newton) as exemplifying the success of [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science) in rendering predictable that which is initially complex.

In recent decades, however, a [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity) of systems have been studied that behave unpredictably despite their seeming simplicity and the fact that the forces involved are governed by well-understood physical laws. The common element in these systems is a very high degree of sensitivity to initial conditions and to the way in which they are set in motion. For example, the [meteorologist](https://www.britannica.com/science/meteorology) [Edward Lorenz](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edward-Lorenz) discovered that a simple model of [heat](https://www.britannica.com/science/heat) [convection](https://www.britannica.com/science/convection) possesses [intrinsic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intrinsic) unpredictability, a circumstance he called the “ [butterfly effect](https://www.britannica.com/science/butterfly-effect),” suggesting that the mere flapping of a [butterfly’s](https://www.britannica.com/animal/butterfly-insect) wing can change the [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather). A more homely example is the [pinball machine](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pinball-machine): the ball’s movements are precisely governed by laws of [gravitational](https://www.britannica.com/science/gravity-physics) rolling and elastic collisions—both fully understood—yet the final outcome is unpredictable.

In [classical mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/classical-mechanics) the behaviour of a [dynamical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynamical) system can be described geometrically as motion on an “attractor.” The mathematics of classical mechanics effectively recognized three types of attractor: single points (characterizing steady states), closed loops (periodic cycles), and tori (combinations of several cycles). In the 1960s a new class of “strange attractors” was discovered by the American mathematician [Stephen Smale](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Stephen-Smale). On strange attractors the [dynamics](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynamics) is chaotic. Later it was recognized that strange attractors have detailed structure on all scales of magnification; a direct result of this recognition was the development of the concept of the [fractal](https://www.britannica.com/science/fractal) (a class of complex [geometric](https://www.britannica.com/science/geometry) shapes that commonly exhibit the property of self-similarity), which led in turn to remarkable developments in [computer graphics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computer-graphics).

**fractal**, in [mathematics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics), any of a class of complex geometric shapes that commonly have “fractional dimension,” a [concept](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/concept) first introduced by the mathematician Felix Hausdorff in 1918. Fractals are distinct from the simple figures of classical, or Euclidean, geometry—the square, the circle, the sphere, and so forth. They are capable of describing many irregularly shaped objects or spatially nonuniform phenomena in nature such as coastlines and [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) ranges. The term _fractal_, derived from the Latin word _fractus_ (“fragmented,” or “broken”), was coined by the Polish-born mathematician Benoit B. Mandelbrot. See the animation of the Mandelbrot fractal [set](https://www.britannica.com/topic/set-mathematics-and-logic).

Although the key concepts associated with fractals had been studied for years by mathematicians, and many examples, such as the Koch or “snowflake” curve were long known, Mandelbrot was the first to point out that fractals could be an ideal tool in applied mathematics for modeling a variety of phenomena from physical objects to the behavior of the [stock market](https://www.britannica.com/money/stock-exchange-finance). Since its introduction in 1975, the concept of the fractal has given rise to a new system of [geometry](https://www.britannica.com/science/geometry) that has had a significant impact on such [diverse](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diverse) fields as [physical chemistry](https://www.britannica.com/science/physical-chemistry), physiology, and [fluid mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/fluid-mechanics).

Many fractals possess the property of self-similarity, at least approximately, if not exactly. A self-similar object is one whose component parts resemble the whole. This reiteration of details or patterns occurs at progressively smaller scales and can, in the case of purely abstract entities, continue indefinitely, so that each part of each part, when magnified, will look basically like a fixed part of the whole object. In effect, a self-similar object remains invariant under changes of scale—i.e., it has scaling [symmetry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/symmetry-definition). This fractal phenomenon can often be detected in such objects as snowflakes and tree barks. All natural fractals of this kind, as well as some mathematical self-similar ones, are stochastic, or random; they thus scale in a statistical sense.

Another key characteristic of a fractal is a mathematical [parameter](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/parameter) called its [fractal dimension](https://www.britannica.com/science/fractal-dimension). Unlike Euclidean dimension, fractal dimension is generally expressed by a noninteger—that is to say, by a fraction rather than by a [whole number](https://www.britannica.com/science/natural-number). Fractal dimension can be illustrated by considering a specific example: the [snowflake curv](https://www.britannica.com/science/Von-Kochs-snowflake-curve) e defined by [Helge von Koch](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Niels-Fabian-Helge-von-Koch) in 1904. It is a purely mathematical figure with a six-fold symmetry, like a natural snowflake. It is self-similar in that it consists of three identical parts, each of which in turn is made of four parts that are exact scaled-down versions of the whole. It follows that each of the four parts itself consists of four parts that are-scaled down versions of the whole. There would be nothing surprising if the scaling factor were also four, since that would be true of a [line](https://www.britannica.com/science/line-mathematics) segment or a circular arc. However, for the snowflake curve, the scaling factor at each stage is three. The fractal [dimension](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/dimension), _D_, denotes the power to which 3 must be raised to produce 4—i.e., 3_D_= 4\. The dimension of the snowflake curve is thus _D_ = log 4/log 3, or roughly 1.26. Fractal dimension is a key property and an indicator of the [complexity](https://www.britannica.com/science/complexity-scientific-theory) of a given figure.

Fractal geometry with its concepts of self-similarity and noninteger dimensionality has been applied increasingly in [statistical mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/statistical-mechanics), notably when dealing with physical systems consisting of seemingly random features. For example, fractal simulations have been used to plot the distribution of galaxy clusters throughout the universe and to study problems related to fluid turbulence. Fractal geometry also has contributed to [computer graphics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/computer-graphics). Fractal [algorithms](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/algorithms) have made it possible to generate lifelike images of complicated, highly irregular natural objects, such as the rugged terrains of mountains and the intricate branch systems of trees.

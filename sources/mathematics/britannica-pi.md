### What is pi?

Pi is the [ratio](https://www.britannica.com/science/ratio) of the circumference of a [circle](https://www.britannica.com/science/circle-mathematics) to its diameter.

### What is the value of pi?

The value of pi is approximately 3.14, or 22/7. Pi is an [irrational number](https://www.britannica.com/science/irrational-number), which means it is not equal to the [ratio](https://www.britannica.com/science/ratio) of any two whole numbers. This also means pi can be expressed as an infinite decimal expansion with no regularly repeating digits. Pi has been calculated to trillions of decimal places.

### What is the symbol for pi?

The symbol for pi is π. It was first used by British mathematician William Jones in 1706 to represent the ratio and was later popularized by the Swiss mathematician [Leonhard Euler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leonhard-Euler).

### What are the uses of pi?

Pi is used for solving problems involving the lengths of arcs or other [curves](https://www.britannica.com/science/curve), the areas of [ellipses](https://www.britannica.com/science/ellipse), sectors, and other curved surfaces, and the volumes of many solids. It is also used in various formulas of [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science) and [engineering](https://www.britannica.com/technology/engineering) to describe the motion of [pendulums](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pendulum), the vibration of strings, and [alternating electric currents](https://www.britannica.com/science/alternating-current).

**pi (π)**, in [mathematics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics), the [ratio](https://www.britannica.com/science/ratio) of the circumference of a [circle](https://www.britannica.com/science/circle-mathematics) to its [diameter](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/diameter). The [Greek letter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-alphabet) **π** was first used by [British](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Britain-island-Europe) [mathematician](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics) William Jones in 1706 to represent the ratio and was later popularized by [Swiss](https://www.britannica.com/place/Switzerland) mathematician [Leonhard Euler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leonhard-Euler). Because pi is [irrational](https://www.britannica.com/science/irrational-number) (not equal to the ratio of any two whole numbers), its digits do not repeat, and an approximation such as 3.14 or 22/7 is often used for everyday calculations. To 39 decimal places, pi is 3.141592653589793238462643383279502884197.

## Early approximations

A Biblical Approximation

The [Hebrew Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-Bible) (1 Kings 7:23) describes the molten sea (a metal basin) of Solomon’s Temple as 10 cubits across and 30 cubits in circumference—an implied pi of 3. Though imprecise, it reflects an awareness of a fixed circle ratio.

[![Rhind papyrus](https://cdn.britannica.com/01/237001-050-1CF0460E/Puzzle-the-detail-of-the-Rhind-Papyrus.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/01/237001-050-1CF0460E/Puzzle-the-detail-of-the-Rhind-Papyrus.jpg)

[Rhind papyrus](https://cdn.britannica.com/01/237001-050-1CF0460E/Puzzle-the-detail-of-the-Rhind-Papyrus.jpg) The Rhind papyrus is a document that has been central to understanding ancient Egyptian mathematics. It also represents one of the earliest documented references to puzzles, in the form of dozens of mathematical problems.(more)

The oldest surviving evidence of pi comes from [Babylonia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Babylonia) (present-day [Iraq](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iraq)). Old Babylonian [cuneiform](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cuneiform) tablets (c. 1900–1600 bce) give a practical [value](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/value) for pi derived from [hexagon](https://www.britannica.com/science/hexagon) [geometry](https://www.britannica.com/science/geometry): They treated the ratio of an inscribed regular [hexagon](https://www.britannica.com/science/hexagon)’s perimeter to the circle’s circumference as 24/25, giving the value of pi as 25/8, or about 3.125. In [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt) the [Rhind papyrus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rhind-papyrus) (c. 1650 bce)—a scroll on [arithmetic](https://www.britannica.com/science/arithmetic) and geometry—uses the rule “subtract one-ninth of the diameter and square the remainder,” yielding 256/81, or about 3.16045. These figures show how scribes and builders treated pi as a stable operational [constant](https://www.britannica.com/topic/constant) long before formal proofs.

## Classical geometry

In the 3rd century bce [Greek](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece) mathematician and engineer [Archimedes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Archimedes) developed a geometric method to calculate pi. By comparing perimeters of polygons inscribed in and circumscribed around a circle (up to 96 sides), he proved 223/71 < π < 22/7, or an average value of about 3.1418. Archimedes also proved that the ratio of the area of a circle to the square of its radius is the same constant. In the 2nd century ce, [Claudius Ptolemy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ptolemy), a Greco-Roman [astronomer](https://www.britannica.com/science/astronomy) and geographer working in [Alexandria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alexandria-Egypt), used trigonometric chord tables in his astronomical [treatise](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/treatise) _[Almagest](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Almagest)_—a [comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive) guide to the motions of the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun), [Moon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Moon), and [planets](https://www.britannica.com/science/planet)—to obtain a value of pi of about 3.1416.

## Advances in [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia)

In [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China), [Liu Hui](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Liu-Hui) (3rd century ce), noted for his commentary on _The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art_, refined polygonal methods to reach pi of about 3.14159. Two centuries later mathematician and astronomer [Zu Chongzhi](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Zu-Chongzhi) (429–500 ce) achieved a record level of accuracy with the [fraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/fraction) 355/113 and proved that pi lies between 3.1415926 and 3.1415927—known as the _milü_ (“close ratio”). In [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), [Aryabhata](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aryabhata-I) (born 476 ce), an early astronomer-mathematician, used pi as 62,832/20,000 (about 3.1416) in the “Ganita” (“Mathematics”) section of his treatise _Aryabhatiya_, where he presented rules for arithmetic, square and cubic roots, and geometric measurements. Mathematician and astronomer Madhava of Sangamagrama, who led the Kerala school of mathematics and astronomy (14th–16th century), calculated pi to 11 decimal places. In [Central Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Central-Asia), [Jamshid al-Kāshī](https://www.britannica.com/biography/al-Kashi) (c. 1380–1429), an astronomer at the [Samarkand](https://www.britannica.com/place/Samarkand-Uzbekistan) observatory, calculated 2π to 9 sexagesimal places, equivalent to about 16 correct decimal digits, giving the most precise known value of pi in the [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) world.

## Early modern analysis

During the 16th and 17th centuries, [European](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) mathematicians developed new ways to calculate pi with precision. [French](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) mathematician [François Viète](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francois-Viete-seigneur-de-la-Bigotiere) was the first to express pi as an endless product of numbers, introducing an approach that linked [geometric](https://www.britannica.com/science/geometry) ideas with [algebraic](https://www.britannica.com/science/algebra) calculation. In 1655–56 English mathematician [John Wallis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Wallis) introduced an [infinite](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infinite) product formula for pi in his book _Arithmetica Infinitorum_ (“The Arithmetic of Infinitesimals”), representing the constant as an unending sequence of ratios built from whole numbers—a formulation that later reappeared unexpectedly in quantum-mechanical calculations of the [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) atom.

In the early 1670s [James Gregory](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Gregory) in [Scotland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Scotland) and [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gottfried-Wilhelm-Leibniz) in [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany) each presented a series for pi divided by 4 (1 – 1/3 \+ 1/5 – 1/7 \+ …), a method earlier anticipated by Madhava. In 1706 [John Machin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Machin) of [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) used a faster arctangent formula to compute pi by hand to 100 decimal places. That same year William Jones of [Wales](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wales) adopted the symbol π to [denote](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/denote) the ratio of a circle’s circumference to its diameter—a notation popularized by the [Swiss](https://www.britannica.com/place/Switzerland) mathematician [Leonhard Euler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leonhard-Euler) a few decades later.

## Mathematical proofs and expanding precision

What Is an [Irrational Number](https://www.britannica.com/science/irrational-number)?

An irrational number is a number that cannot be expressed as a fraction of two [integers](https://www.britannica.com/science/integer). Its decimal expansion goes on forever without repeating. The [square root](https://www.britannica.com/science/square-root) of 2 and the constant _e_ are examples. Pi is irrational because no fraction can capture its exact value. The digits of pi continue endlessly without settling into a repeating pattern, which is why such approximations as 3.14 or 22/7 are used in everyday work.

The nature of pi became clearer in the 18th and 19th centuries. In 1768 Swiss mathematician [Johann Heinrich Lambert](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johann-Heinrich-Lambert) proved that pi is irrational—it cannot be expressed as a ratio of whole numbers. [Ferdinand von Lindemann](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ferdinand-von-Lindemann), a mathematician from Germany who was influenced by the work of French mathematician [Charles Hermite](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Hermite), proved in 1882 that pi is [transcendental](https://www.britannica.com/science/transcendental-number), meaning it cannot be the root of any [algebraic equation](https://www.britannica.com/science/algebraic-equation). His proof, based partly on Hermite’s demonstration that the constant _e_ is transcendental and the identity [e](https://www.britannica.com/science/e-mathematics) iπ = −1, settled the ancient Greek problem of “squaring the circle,” showing it impossible to construct a square equal in area to a given circle using only a straightedge and [compass](https://www.britannica.com/technology/compass-navigational-instrument).

In 1873 English mathematician William Shanks computed 707 digits by hand, though later analysis showed errors beyond the 527th place. His results went unchallenged for 75 years, until [American](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) D.F. Ferguson, working with a mechanical desk [calculator](https://www.britannica.com/technology/calculator) in 1946, identified the mistake and recomputed pi correctly to 808 digits. In 1914 Indian mathematician [Srinivasa Ramanujan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Srinivasa-Ramanujan) presented 17 formulas for calculating pi, including a series for its [reciprocal](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reciprocal). Each term in the series produced several correct digits of pi, making his methods far more efficient than earlier approaches. His work later inspired the Ramanujan-Sato series and provided the mathematical foundation for modern computer [algorithms](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/algorithms) that have computed pi to trillions of digits.

Celebrating Pi Day

Pi Day, observed on March 14 (3/14), honors the mathematical constant π and its significance across [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science) and engineering. The date reflects the first three digits of pi, 3.14, and coincides with [Albert Einstein](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Albert-Einstein)’s birthday. The tradition began in 1988, when physicist Larry Shaw organized the first celebration at a [San Francisco](https://www.britannica.com/place/San-Francisco-California) science museum; it was officially recognized by the U.S. Congress in 2009.

## Computational era

In 1949, the [Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer](https://www.britannica.com/technology/ENIAC) (ENIAC) became the first electronic machine used to calculate pi, producing 2,037 digits in about 70 hours. In the late 1980s, American mathematicians Gregory and David Chudnovsky, working from an apartment in [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City), used a custom-built parallel supercomputer to compute pi to more than one billion digits; a few years later, they extended their result to more than four billion digits. In October 1999, Yasumasa Kanada and his assistant, Daisuke Takahasi, at the [University of Tokyo](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Tokyo), [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan), achieved a new record with 206,158,430,000 digits, using a [Hitachi](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hitachi) SR8000 [supercomputer](https://www.britannica.com/technology/supercomputer). In the early 21st century, Japanese mathematician Shigeru Kondo, working with Alexander Yee in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), used modern algorithms and [distributed computing](https://www.britannica.com/technology/distributed-computing) to calculate more than 10 trillion digits of pi.

## Applications

Pi appears throughout mathematics, science, and engineering wherever circular, periodic, or wavelike phenomena arise. It connects geometry, analysis, and the [physical sciences](https://www.britannica.com/science/physical-science) in equations describing [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics), [symmetry](https://www.britannica.com/science/symmetry-physics), and change.

- **[Geometry](https://www.britannica.com/science/geometry) and [trigonometry](https://www.britannica.com/science/trigonometry):** Pi is fundamental to formulas involving [circles](https://www.britannica.com/science/circle-mathematics), [spheres](https://www.britannica.com/science/sphere), and related figures. The circumference of a circle is given by 2πr and its area by πr2, where _r_ is the radius of the circle. Pi also appears in the surface area and volume formulas for spheres, [cylinders](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cylinder-engineering), and [cones](https://www.britannica.com/science/cone-mathematics). In trigonometry, pi underlies the measurement of angles in radians, where π radians equals 180 degrees.

- **Analysis and [number theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/number-theory):** The constant appears in [infinite series](https://www.britannica.com/science/infinite-series), [integrals](https://www.britannica.com/science/integral-mathematics), and special functions. The [Fourier transform](https://www.britannica.com/science/Fourier-transform), which represents periodic phenomena as sums of [sines](https://www.britannica.com/science/sine) and [cosines](https://www.britannica.com/science/cosine), depends directly on pi. [Probabilistic](https://www.britannica.com/science/probability-theory) results such as Buffon’s needle problem also connect pi to geometric probability and randomness.

- **Physics:** Pi occurs in the description of [wave motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-motion), oscillations, and [resonance](https://www.britannica.com/science/resonance-particle-physics). It is present in formulas for [pendulums](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pendulum) and vibrating strings in [simple harmonic motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/simple-harmonic-motion), as well as [alternating electrical currents](https://www.britannica.com/science/alternating-current). More advanced theories, such as [quantum mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-mechanics-physics) and [relativity](https://www.britannica.com/science/relativity), also employ pi in their fundamental equations.

- **[Engineering](https://www.britannica.com/technology/engineering) and [technology](https://www.britannica.com/technology/technology):** Engineers use pi in applications that involve circular, rotating, or curved components. It appears in formulas for signal processing, electrical [circuits](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/circuits), and electromagnetic waves, where it helps relate [frequency](https://www.britannica.com/science/frequency-physics), rotation, and [phase](https://www.britannica.com/science/phase-mechanics). In [mechanical](https://www.britannica.com/technology/mechanical-engineering) and [civil engineering](https://www.britannica.com/technology/civil-engineering), pi is essential for calculating areas and volumes of cylinders, tanks, and pipes and for analyzing stresses and rotational motion in [machines](https://www.britannica.com/technology/machine) and structures. It also underlies calculations used in satellite communications, actuator control, and the design of storage vessels and materials systems.

- **[Astronomy](https://www.britannica.com/science/astronomy) and [cosmology](https://www.britannica.com/science/cosmology-astronomy):** Scientists and engineers use pi to explore and measure the [universe](https://www.britannica.com/science/universe). It is essential in orbital [mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mechanics) for calculating spacecraft trajectories, orbital paths, and gravitational forces. On missions such as [Mars](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mars-planet) landings, pi helps determine parachute size, descent rates, and landing zones. Astronomers use pi to map planetary surfaces, measure [craters](https://www.britannica.com/science/crater-geology) and planetary volumes, and identify exoplanets by analyzing changes in starlight during transits. It also helps determine the sizes and [densities](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/densities) of planets, [asteroids](https://www.britannica.com/science/asteroid), and [binary stars](https://www.britannica.com/science/binary-star) such as Alpha Draconis. Beyond the [solar system](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-system), pi appears in equations describing [elliptical](https://www.britannica.com/science/ellipse) orbits and the geometry of space, helping model the structure and motion of the universe.

**circle**, geometrical [curve](https://www.britannica.com/science/curve), one of the [conic section](https://www.britannica.com/science/conic-section) s, consisting of the [set](https://www.britannica.com/topic/set-mathematics-and-logic) of all points the same distance (the radius) from a given point (the centre). A [line](https://www.britannica.com/science/line-mathematics) connecting any two points on a circle is called a chord, and a chord passing through the centre is called a diameter. The distance around a circle (the circumference) equals the length of a diameter multiplied by π ( _see_ [pi](https://www.britannica.com/science/pi-mathematics)). The area of a circle is the square of the radius multiplied by π. An arc consists of any part of a circle [encompassed](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompassed) by an angle with its vertex at the centre (central angle). Its length is in the same proportion to the circumference as the central angle is to a full revolution.

**column**, in [architecture](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/architecture), a vertical element, usually a rounded shaft with a [capital](https://www.britannica.com/technology/capital-architecture) and a base, which in most cases serves as a support. A column may also be nonstructural, used for a decorative purpose or as a freestanding monument.

![Greek architectural orders](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/103898-050-AD05DBA9/Comparison-column-styles-Greek-Doric-Corinthian-Ionic.jpg)

[Greek architectural orders](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/103898-050-AD05DBA9/Comparison-column-styles-Greek-Doric-Corinthian-Ionic.jpg) Comparison of three of the main Greek column styles—Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian.(more)

In the field of architectural design a column is used for decoration as well as support. Classical Greek and Roman architecture made use of five major [orders](https://www.britannica.com/technology/order-architecture) (or styles) of columns, carved from single blocks or created from stacks of massive stone blocks. In ancient [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/art/Egyptian-architecture) and the [Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/Near-East), columns, usually large and circular, were used with great effect to decorate and support massive structures, especially in the absence of arches. In [Eastern architecture](https://www.britannica.com/art/East-Asian-arts), columns tend to be simple in shape but richly decorated. Craftsmen of the [Gothic](https://www.britannica.com/art/Gothic-architecture) and [Romanesque](https://www.britannica.com/art/Romanesque-architecture) era, used the bases and capitals of supporting stone columns as spaces for [intricate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/intricate) carving. [Baroque](https://www.britannica.com/art/Baroque-architecture) designs often featured sinuously carved columns of marble. [Modern](https://www.britannica.com/art/modern-art) columns tend to be made of iron, steel, or concrete and are simply designed.

[St. Petersburg: Alexander Column](https://cdn.britannica.com/49/189849-050-E0330026/Alexander-Column-Palace-Square-St-Petersburg-Hermitage.jpg) The Alexander Column in Palace Square, outside the Hermitage Museum, St. Petersburg. The granite structure is 165 feet (50 metres) tall and weighs 600 tons.(more)

Columns may be rectangular, circular, or polygonal in shape; they may taper toward the top or be of uniform diameter. An engaged, attached, or [embedded column](https://www.britannica.com/technology/engaged-column) is one that is built into a [wall](https://www.britannica.com/technology/wall) and protrudes only partially from it; this type of column came to serve a decorative rather than structural purpose in the Roman [pilaster](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pilaster). A cluster or compound column is a group of columns connected with each other to form a single unit. A [rostral column](https://www.britannica.com/technology/rostral-column) is a [pillar](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pillar) decorated with the prow of a ship, or rostrum, to serve as a naval monument.

[Types of capitals.](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/65992-050-47C12E14/Types-capitals.jpg)(more)

**capital**, in [architecture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/architecture), [crowning](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/crowning) member of a [column](https://www.britannica.com/technology/column-architecture), [pier](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pier-architecture), [anta](https://www.britannica.com/technology/anta-architecture), [pilaster](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pilaster), or other columnar form, providing a structural support for the horizontal member (entablature) or [arch](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-architecture) above. In the Classical styles, the capital is the architectural member that most readily distinguishes the [order](https://www.britannica.com/technology/order-architecture).

Two simple forms of the capital are a square wooden block called an [abacus](https://www.britannica.com/technology/abacus-calculating-device), placed on the top of a post, and an oblong block called a billet, set with its greatest dimensions parallel to the [beam](https://www.britannica.com/technology/beam-architecture) above. Shaping the ends of such blocks produces a laterally spreading form of capital, which can be elaborated upon by multiplication of parts, addition of moldings, and ornamentation with floral, zoomorphic, or abstract forms.

[![Hathor](https://cdn.britannica.com/93/141193-050-E2944330/Hathor-capitals-Philae-island-Egypt.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/93/141193-050-E2944330/Hathor-capitals-Philae-island-Egypt.jpg)

[Hathor](https://cdn.britannica.com/93/141193-050-E2944330/Hathor-capitals-Philae-island-Egypt.jpg) Hathor, relief on capitals at Philae island, southern Egypt.(more)

Primitive abacus capitals were known in [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/art/Egyptian-architecture) and Mesopotamia, and two kinds of simple stone capital have been found in the [stepped-pyramid](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Step-Pyramid-of-Djoser) complex at Saqqārah (c. 2890–c. 2686 bc). One, a saddlelike shape, suggests bent reeds or leaves; the other, an upturned bell, derives from the [papyrus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/papyrus-writing-material) plant. Later Egyptian architecture used capitals derived from such plant forms as the palm and lotus, as well as [anthropomorphic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/anthropomorphic) forms and simple abacus shapes. [Volute capitals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/volute) were known in Hittite architecture in Anatolia and in Mesopotamia as early as 870 bc. Very elaborate capitals were created in Achaemenian Persia.

Three widely used forms of the capital were created by the Greeks. The [Doric](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Doric-order) capital consists of a square abacus surmounting a round form with an egg-shaped profile called the echinus, below which are several narrow, ridgelike moldings linking the capital with the column. The [Ionic](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Ionic-order) capital—probably related to the volute capitals of western Asia—has a tripartite design consisting of a pair of horizontally connected volutes inserted between the abacus and echinus. The [Corinthian](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Corinthian-order) capital is basically an abacus supported on an inverted bell surrounded by rows of stylized [acanthus](https://www.britannica.com/art/acanthus-ornamental-motif) leaves. The Romans added the [Tuscan](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Tuscan-order) capital, a modified form of the Doric, and the [Composite](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Composite-order) capital, which combined Ionic volutes with the Corinthian bell shape.

[Islamic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-architecture) capitals, following the nonrepresentational requirement of the Muslim [aesthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aesthetic), used primarily abstract forms derived from repetition of small moldings and multiplication of miniature arches. Some form of bracketed capital and a bell-shaped capital decorated with lotus motifs were used most frequently in [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), China, and Japan.

Design of capitals in [medieval](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages) Europe usually stemmed from Roman sources. Cubiform, or [cushion](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cushion-capital), capitals, square on top and rounded at the bottom, served as transitional forms between the angular springing of the arches and the round columns supporting them. Grotesque animals, birds, and other figurative motifs characterize capitals of the Romanesque period. At the beginning of the [Gothic](https://www.britannica.com/art/Gothic-architecture) period, exotic features tended to disappear in favour of simple stylized foliage, crockets, and geometric moldings, particularly in France and England. During the later Middle Ages, the emphasis on clustered columns and [compound](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compound) piers that soared in an unbroken line to high vaults tended to decrease the importance of the capital.

- What is an arch in architecture?







In architecture and civil engineering, an [arch](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-architecture/Introduction#ref1) is a curved structure that spans an opening and supports loads from above, serving as a base for vaults. Its construction relies on wedge-shaped blocks called voussoirs, with the central one termed the keystone. The arch rises from vertical supports at the spring line.



Compared to horizontal beams, arches can span wider openings using smaller blocks and bear greater loads because the pressure forces the voussoirs together. The supports must be strong enough to handle the thrust, which is the diagonal force that can cause collapse if not buttressed. Arches were not always used for monumental purposes, but the Romans used them in [bridges](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-bridge/Introduction#ref356809) and aqueducts.

- How is an arch different from a regular flat beam?







An arch differs from a flat beam, or lintel, in several ways. Arches can span wider openings using smaller blocks of material, unlike monolithic stone lintels. The arch form can support greater loads because pressure on the arch forces its wedge-shaped blocks (voussoirs) together, whereas a horizontal beam is subject to bending. The arch's outward thrust necessitates buttressing to prevent collapse, a concern not present with simple lintel construction. Modern materials like steel and concrete reduce the horizontal thrust, allowing for lighter supports.

- What materials are commonly used to build arches?







Arches are constructed from various materials, including brick, stone, concrete, steel, and wood. Masonry arches, which use brick or stone, can support wider openings because they consist of small, manageable blocks. The wedge-shaped blocks, called voussoirs, press together to distribute loads evenly. Modern arches can also be made of steel, concrete, or laminated wood, which are lightweight and rigid. Concrete arches may use steel reinforcement to increase tensile strength. The choice of material depends on the arch's design, desired load-bearing capacity, and aesthetic considerations.

- How does an arch support weight and stay standing?







An arch supports weight and remains standing through compression. The arch is composed of wedge-shaped blocks called voussoirs, which press firmly against each other to uniformly conduct loads. The central voussoir, or [keystone](https://www.merriam-webster.com/wordplay/top-10-architecture-terms-worth-looking-at/keystone), locks the other pieces in place.



Pressure on the arch forces the voussoirs together; however, these stresses also squeeze the blocks outward. The vertical supports of the arch must be massive enough to buttress the thrust and conduct it into the foundation. Arches in a row can rest on lighter supports because the thrust of one arch counteracts the thrust of its neighbors, creating a stable system if the end arches are buttressed.

- Why were arches important in ancient Roman architecture?







The arch was crucial in ancient Roman architecture because it allowed for wider spans than horizontal beams and could be constructed from smaller, easily manageable blocks of stone or brick. Arches also supported significantly greater loads because the pressure exerted on the arch forced the wedge-shaped blocks (voussoirs) together.



Romans used arches in various structures, including [bridges](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-bridge/Introduction), [aqueducts](https://www.britannica.com/technology/construction/Roman-achievements#ref60121), and large-scale architecture. The Romans did not generally use mortar, relying on precise stone dressing. Their ability to organize in large and complex terms influenced their architectural preference for clear composition, with lines, surfaces, masses, and volumes organized in space.

- How are arches used in modern buildings and bridges?







Arches are still relevant in modern construction due to their structural efficiency and aesthetic appeal.



Modern arch bridges made of steel, concrete, or laminated wood are lighter and more rigid, which reduces the horizontal thrust on supports. To reduce thrust, builders can stretch a tie between the arch's ends.



Arches of steel, concrete, or laminated wood have changed arch mechanics. The components differ completely from wedge-shaped blocks. They may be rigid, needing only vertical support, have hinged intersections that work independently, or be thin slabs where stresses distribute to add the advantages of lintels to those of arches, needing only light supports.



The arch can support substantial loads and span wide openings using small blocks of material.

**arch**, in [architecture](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/architecture) and [civil engineering](https://www.britannica.com/technology/civil-engineering), a curved member that is used to span an opening and to support loads from above. The arch formed the basis for the evolution of the [vault](https://www.britannica.com/technology/vault-architecture).

Arch construction depends essentially on the wedge. If a series of wedge-shaped blocks—i.e., ones in which the upper edge is wider than the lower edge—are set flank to flank in the manner shown in the figure, the result is an arch. These blocks are called [voussoirs](https://www.britannica.com/technology/voussoir). Each voussoir must be precisely cut so that it presses firmly against the surface of neighbouring blocks and conducts loads uniformly. The central voussoir is called the [keystone](https://www.britannica.com/technology/keystone). The point from which the arch rises from its vertical supports is known as the [spring](https://www.britannica.com/technology/spring-architecture), or springing line. During construction of an arch, the voussoirs require support from below until the keystone has been set in place; this support usually takes the form of temporary wooden [centring](https://www.britannica.com/technology/falsework). The curve in an arch may be semicircular, segmental (consisting of less than one-half of a circle), or pointed (two intersecting arcs of a circle); noncircular curves can also be used successfully.

In masonry construction, arches have several great advantages over horizontal beams, or [lintels](https://www.britannica.com/technology/post-and-lintel-system). They can span much wider openings because they can be made from small, easily carried blocks of brick or stone, as opposed to a massive, [monolithic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/monolithic) stone lintel. An arch can also carry a much greater load than a horizontal [beam](https://www.britannica.com/technology/beam-architecture) can support. This [carrying capacity](https://www.britannica.com/science/carrying-capacity) stems from the fact that pressure downward on an arch has the effect of forcing the voussoirs together instead of apart. These [stresses](https://www.britannica.com/science/stress-physics) also tend to squeeze the blocks outward radially; loads divert these outward forces downward to exert a diagonal force, called thrust, that will cause the arch to collapse if it is not properly buttressed. Hence, the vertical supports, or posts, upon which an arch rests must be massive enough to [buttress](https://www.britannica.com/technology/buttress-architecture) the thrust and conduct it into the [foundation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/foundation-construction) (as in Roman triumphal arches). Arches may rest on light supports, however, when they occur in a row, because the thrust of one arch counteracts the thrust of its neighbours, and the system remains stable as long as the arches at either end of the row are buttressed. This system is used in such structures as arched stone bridges and ancient Roman aqueducts.

Arches were known in [ancient Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt) and Greece but were considered unsuitable for monumental architecture and seldom used. The [Romans](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Rome), by contrast, used the semicircular arch in bridges, aqueducts, and large-scale architecture. In most cases they did not use mortar, relying simply on the precision of their stone dressing. The Arabs popularized the [pointed arch](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pointed-arch), and it was in their mosques that this form first acquired its religious [connotations](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/connotations). [Medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Medieval) Europe made great use of the pointed arch, which [constituted](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituted) a basic element in [Gothic architecture](https://www.britannica.com/art/Gothic-architecture). In the late [Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages) the segmental arch was introduced. This form and the elliptical arch had great value in bridge engineering because they permitted mutual support by a row of arches, carrying the lateral thrust to the abutments at either end of a bridge.

**vaulting**, [gymnastics](https://www.britannica.com/sports/gymnastics) exercise in which the athlete leaps over a form that was originally intended to mimic a horse. At one time the [pommel horse](https://www.britannica.com/sports/pommel-horse) (side horse) was used in the vaulting exercise, with the pommels (handles) removed. Later a cylindrical form made especially for vaulting was used. The sanctioning body for gymnastic sport, the Fédération Internationale de Gymnastique (FIG), decreed in 2001 that a vaulting table would replace the horse. With its curved front, the vaulting table was designed for the greater safety of the gymnast.

![Vaulting apparatus](https://cdn.britannica.com/96/4896-050-0C3D448A/diagram-vault-platform-gymnastics-sports.jpg)

[Vaulting apparatus](https://cdn.britannica.com/96/4896-050-0C3D448A/diagram-vault-platform-gymnastics-sports.jpg) Diagram of the springboard and vaulting apparatus used by gymnasts.(more)

In men’s vaulting the horse was placed lengthwise, and the vaulting table is placed in that same position whether for men or for women. For men the height of the apparatus is 1.35 meters (4.43 feet) measured from the floor. A Reuther board (also called a beatboard), a special type of springboard developed in [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany), is placed in front of the near end of the apparatus. The gymnast takes a run, gathers momentum as he or she nears the apparatus, rebounds off the springboard, and, with hands on the apparatus, vaults over it. A variety of tricks may be performed, such as vaulting over with straddled legs, with legs together and bent into a squatting position, or with legs straight and the hips bent, as well as handsprings, cartwheels, and other more difficult movements. Each vault is evaluated according to a table of standards of difficulty.

The women’s vaulting horse was the same as the men’s except that it was lower and was placed sideways instead of lengthwise. Women also used the springboard and performed vaults similar to those done by men, except that the vault was much shorter, since it was performed over the width of the horse rather than its length. For women the vaulting table is 1.25 meters (4.10 feet) high.

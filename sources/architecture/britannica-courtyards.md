**court**, in [architecture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/architecture), an open area surrounded by buildings or walls. There have been such courts from the earliest recorded times and in all civilizations. In [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) Europe the court was a characteristic adjunct of all major domestic buildings, as the [cloister](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cloister) of a [monastery](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monastery), the ward of a castle, and the [quadrangle](https://www.britannica.com/technology/quadrangle-architecture) of a college or [hospital](https://www.britannica.com/science/hospital).

Related Topics: [atrium](https://www.britannica.com/technology/atrium-architecture)[patio](https://www.britannica.com/technology/patio)[quadrangle](https://www.britannica.com/technology/quadrangle-architecture)[cortile](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cortile)[campus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/campus)_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/court-architecture)

Palaces often included a complex of courts. The [Alhambra](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Alhambra-fortress-Granada-Spain) in Granada, Spain, built in the 13th and 14th centuries, has six, including the [Court of the Lions](https://www.britannica.com/place/Court-of-the-Lions) and [Court of the Myrtles](https://www.britannica.com/place/Court-of-the-Myrtles), the most celebrated of all Muslim patios. In Tudor and Elizabethan [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) of the 16th century, the principal mansions frequently had a forecourt, with wings of the house projecting forward on either side. The larger houses in [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) were similarly planned; but by the late 17th century it became necessary to add a second courtyard at the rear for stables, coachhouses, and the like; and the forecourt became the court of honour ( _cour d’honneur_). _See also_ [cortile](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cortile).

- What is architecture?







Architecture is the art and technique of designing and building structures that meet both practical and expressive needs. It requires suitability for human use, stability, permanence, and communication of experience and ideas through form. The types of architecture are determined by society based on the needs of its institutions, and the architect's job is to find the means of achieving these goals. The architect also considers the site, type, and cost of the building, while harmonizing the demands of environment, use, and economy.

- What are some basic purposes of architecture?







Architecture serves both practical and expressive needs, blending utility and aesthetics. It distinguishes itself through suitability for human use and adaptability to activities, stability and permanence, and communication of experience and ideas through form.



Planning in architecture harmonizes environmental demands, use, and economy, adding cultural and utilitarian value. It addresses the varying natural environment and adjusts it to human needs. This includes controlling elements like heat, cold, light, and moisture, while also foreseeing potential hazards like fire and earthquakes.



Expression in architecture communicates quality and meaning, interpreting building techniques into art. Styles communicate cultural outlooks and architects' concepts, influenced by tradition, contemporary expressions, and innovation.

- How does architecture differ in different cultures or countries?







Architecture varies across cultures and countries due to the influence of environment, history, and artistic expression. Building designs consider climate, weather patterns, and destructive forces such as earthquakes and floods to ensure stability and habitability.



Cultural values and societal needs also shape architectural types. For example, the Chinese and Japanese versions of Buddhist temples are often one-story buildings made of carved timber around an atrium. In contrast, the Shintō temples of Japan are simple and rustic huts. Colonial influences, the availability of materials, and technological advancements further contribute to the diversity of architectural styles.

- What are some common materials used in building structures?







Building structures commonly utilize materials like [stone](https://www.britannica.com/topic/architecture/Techniques#ref31827), [brick](https://www.britannica.com/technology/brick-building-material/Colouring-and-texturing-of-brick-and-tile#ref76629), [steel](https://www.britannica.com/technology/construction/Low-rise-commercial-institutional-and-industrial-buildings#ref60095), and [concrete](https://www.britannica.com/technology/concrete-building-material/Introduction#ref1). Stone is favored for its durability and adaptability, while brick is widely used in walls, floors, and patios. Steel is valued for its strength and speed of construction, and concrete, a mix of aggregate, cement, and water, gains tensile strength when reinforced with steel.



[Wood](https://www.britannica.com/topic/architecture/Brick#ref31829) is another common material, prized for its ease of acquisition and workability, though it's vulnerable to fire and pests. [Composite materials](https://www.britannica.com/technology/composite-material/Introduction#ref1), combining different substances for superior properties, are also increasingly used, especially in demanding applications like aerospace. These materials offer enhanced strength and reduced potential for fracture.

- How have new technologies changed architectural design?







New technologies have revolutionized architectural design by enabling the creation of complex and customized structures through [computer-aided design (CAD)](https://www.britannica.com/technology/www.britannica.com/topic/drafting/Drafting-practice#ref59496).

- What are some famous architectural styles and what makes them unique?







Different architectural styles have unique characteristics. **Gothic architecture**, from the mid-12th to 16th century, features cavernous spaces and walls broken up by tracery. Its engineering innovations include the rib vault, flying buttress, and pointed arch, which allowed for taller structures and more natural light.



**Baroque architecture**, originating in late 16th-century Italy, is known for its grandeur, drama, and contrast. It employs complex shapes, curves, and rich surface treatments to evoke motion and sensuality.



**Brutalism**, which emerged in the mid-20th century, is characterized by raw concrete, bold geometric forms, and a utilitarian aesthetic.



**Modernism** sought a nonhistorical architecture of functionalism using modern materials to create new spaces. Rejecting 19th-century stylistic pluralism, it aimed for a radically new architecture suited to "modern man".

**architecture**, the art and technique of designing and [building](https://www.britannica.com/technology/building), as distinguished from the skills associated with [construction](https://www.britannica.com/technology/construction). The practice of architecture is employed to fulfill both practical and expressive requirements, and thus it serves both utilitarian and [aesthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aesthetic) ends. Although these two ends may be distinguished, they cannot be separated, and the relative weight given to each can vary widely. Because every society—settled or nomadic—has a spatial relationship to the natural world and to other societies, the structures they produce reveal much about their [environment](https://www.britannica.com/science/environment) (including [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology) and [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather)), [history](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history), ceremonies, and artistic sensibility, as well as many aspects of daily life.

The [characteristics](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characteristics) that distinguish a work of architecture from other built structures are (1) the suitability of the work to use by human beings in general and the adaptability of it to particular human activities, (2) the stability and permanence of the work’s construction, and (3) the [communication](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communication) of experience and ideas through its form. All these conditions must be met in architecture. The second is a constant, while the first and third vary in relative importance according to the social function of buildings. If the function is chiefly utilitarian, as in a [factory](https://www.britannica.com/technology/factory), communication is of less importance. If the function is chiefly expressive, as in a monumental tomb, utility is a minor concern. In some buildings, such as churches and city halls, utility and communication may be of equal importance.

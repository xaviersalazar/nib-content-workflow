- What is a bridge in engineering?







In engineering, a [bridge](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bridge-engineering/Introduction#ref1) is a structure that spans horizontally between supports and carries vertical loads. The supports must be strong enough to hold the structure, and the span between them must be strong enough to carry loads.



There are six main types of bridges: beam, truss, arch, suspension, cantilever, and cable-stay. The beam bridge is the most common type, carrying vertical loads through bending, resulting in horizontal compression on the top and tension on the bottom.



[Wood](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bridge-engineering/Truss#ref72028), [stone](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bridge-engineering/Truss#ref72028), iron, and concrete are the primary materials used for bridges. Modern bridges are mainly built with steel, reinforced concrete, and prestressed concrete.

- What are the main parts of a bridge?







Bridges consist of several main parts that work together to support loads and ensure structural integrity. The basic bridge forms include the beam, truss, arch, suspension, cantilever, and cable-stay.



Key components include:


\\* **Deck:** The surface that carries traffic.

\\* **Foundations:** These prevent vertical settling and horizontal sliding.


\\* **Cables:** Curved cables in tension that carry vertical loads.


\\* **Towers:** These carry loads by vertical compression to the ground.

- Why do engineers build different types of bridges, like beam and arch bridges?







Engineers choose different bridge types like [beam](https://www.britannica.com/technology/beam-bridge/Introduction#ref1) and [arch bridges](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-bridge/Introduction#ref1) based on efficiency, economy, and aesthetics.



The [beam bridge](https://www.britannica.com/technology/beam-bridge/Introduction#ref1) is the most common type, where a horizontal beam is supported at each end and carries vertical loads by bending. They are cost-effective for shorter spans because they use less material.



[Arch bridges](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-bridge/Introduction#ref1) carry loads primarily through compression, requiring strong foundations to handle vertical and horizontal forces. They can span wider openings compared to beam bridges because they can be constructed from smaller blocks of material.



Ultimately, the selection depends on balancing these factors while meeting the specific requirements of the site.

- How does a suspension bridge support its weight and traffic?







A [suspension bridge](https://www.britannica.com/technology/bridge-engineering/Truss#ref72024) supports vertical loads using curved cables under tension. These loads are then transferred to the towers, which send the weight as vertical compression to the ground. The loads are also transferred to anchorages, which resist the inward and sometimes vertical pull of the cables.



Bridges are designed to handle their own permanent weight, known as dead load, as well as the weight of traffic, referred to as live loads. Suspension bridge design incorporates deflection theory to ensure the horizontal deck and curved cables work together when bearing weight.

- What materials are commonly used to construct bridges and why?







The primary materials for bridges are wood, stone, iron, and concrete; iron had the greatest effect on modern bridges. Today, steel, reinforced concrete, and prestressed concrete are almost exclusively used.



Wood is inexpensive and available, but it is weak in compression and tension. It is suitable for small bridges with light loads. Stone is strong in compression but weak in tension, so it is mainly used in arches, piers, and abutments.



Concrete is strong in compression but weak in tension. It is a mix of water, sand, gravel, and a binder like cement. Reinforced concrete has steel bars to handle tension, which allows for less concrete usage and protects steel from corrosion. Prestressed concrete reduces the amount of steel and concrete needed, resulting in lighter and less expensive designs. Steel is stronger than iron in both tension and compression.

- How do engineers make sure a bridge is safe and can last for a long time?







To ensure a bridge's safety and longevity, engineers consider multiple factors during design and construction. They calculate the bridge's dead load (the bridge's weight) and the expected live loads (traffic). They also account for environmental factors such as wind and seismic activity.



Modern bridges mainly use steel, reinforced concrete, and prestressed concrete. These materials provide strength and durability. Bridges are designed to handle static wind load (horizontal pressure) and dynamic wind load (vertical motion). Expansion joints and bearings accommodate material expansion and contraction due to temperature changes, reducing stress. Aerodynamic design also minimizes wind impact.

**bridge**, structure that spans horizontally between supports, whose function is to carry vertical loads. The prototypical bridge is quite simple—two supports holding up a beam—yet the engineering problems that must be overcome even in this simple form are [inherent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inherent) in every bridge: the supports must be strong enough to hold the structure up, and the [span](https://www.britannica.com/technology/span) between supports must be strong enough to carry the loads. Spans are generally made as short as possible; long spans are justified where good foundations are limited—for example, over estuaries with deep water.

All major bridges are built with the public’s money. Therefore, bridge design that best serves the public interest has a threefold goal: to be as efficient, as economical, and as elegant as is safely possible. [Efficiency](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Efficiency) is a scientific principle that puts a value on reducing materials while increasing performance. Economy is a social principle that puts value on reducing the costs of [construction](https://www.britannica.com/technology/construction) and maintenance while retaining efficiency. Finally, elegance is a symbolic or visual principle that puts value on the personal expression of the designer without compromising performance or economy. There is little disagreement over what [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) efficiency and economy, but the definition of elegance has always been controversial.

Modern designers have written about elegance or [aesthetics](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aesthetics) since the early 19th century, beginning with the Scottish engineer [Thomas Telford](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thomas-Telford). Bridges ultimately belong to the general public, which is the final arbiter of this issue, but in general there are three positions taken by professionals. The first principle holds that the structure of a bridge is the province of the engineer and that beauty is fully achieved only by the addition of [architecture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/architecture). The second idea, arguing from the standpoint of pure engineering, insists that bridges making the most efficient possible use of materials are by definition beautiful. The third case holds that architecture is not needed but that engineers must think about how to make the structure beautiful. This last principle recognizes the fact that engineers have many possible choices of roughly equal efficiency and economy and can therefore express their own [aesthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aesthetic) ideas without adding significantly to materials or cost.

## [Beam](https://www.britannica.com/technology/beam-architecture)

![Beam bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/22073-050-6CC16EB0/beam-bridge-forces-lines-tension-compression.jpg)

[Beam bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/22073-050-6CC16EB0/beam-bridge-forces-lines-tension-compression.jpg) A diagram of a beam bridge, with forces of tension represented by red lines and forces of compression by green lines.(more)

The [beam bridge](https://www.britannica.com/technology/beam-bridge) is the most common bridge form. A [beam](https://www.britannica.com/technology/beam-architecture) carries vertical loads by bending. As the beam bridge bends, it undergoes horizontal compression on the top. At the same time, the bottom of the beam is subjected to horizontal [tension](https://www.britannica.com/science/tension-physics). The supports carry the loads from the [beam](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/beam) by [compression](https://www.britannica.com/science/compression) vertically to the [foundations](https://www.britannica.com/technology/foundation-construction).

# [Truss](https://www.britannica.com/technology/truss-building)

![Single-span truss bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/74/22074-050-04A1F97E/truss-bridge-forces-lines-tension-compression.jpg)

[Single-span truss bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/74/22074-050-04A1F97E/truss-bridge-forces-lines-tension-compression.jpg) A diagram of a single-span truss bridge, with forces of tension represented by red lines and forces of compression by green lines.(more)

A single-span [truss bridge](https://www.britannica.com/technology/truss-bridge) is like a simply supported beam because it carries vertical loads by bending. Bending leads to compression in the top chords (or horizontal members), tension in the bottom chords, and either tension or compression in the vertical and diagonal members, depending on their orientation. [Trusses](https://www.britannica.com/technology/truss-building) are popular because they use a relatively small amount of material to carry relatively large loads.

## [Arch](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-architecture)

![Arch bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/75/22075-050-56BF8EB4/bridge-compression-forces-green-line.jpg)

[Arch bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/75/22075-050-56BF8EB4/bridge-compression-forces-green-line.jpg) A diagram of an arch bridge, with forces of compression represented by the green line.(more)

The [arch bridge](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-bridge) carries loads primarily by compression, which [exerts](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/exerts) on the foundation both vertical and horizontal forces. [Arch](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-architecture) foundations must therefore prevent both vertical settling and horizontal sliding. In spite of the more complicated foundation design, the structure itself normally requires less material than a [beam bridge](https://www.britannica.com/technology/beam-bridge) of the same span.

## [Suspension](https://www.britannica.com/technology/suspension-bridge)

![Suspension bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/22076-050-4F3391DC/suspension-bridge-forces-lines-tension-compression.jpg)

[Suspension bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/76/22076-050-4F3391DC/suspension-bridge-forces-lines-tension-compression.jpg) A diagram of a suspension bridge, with forces of tension represented by red lines and forces of compression by green lines.(more)

A [suspension bridge](https://www.britannica.com/technology/suspension-bridge) carries vertical loads through curved cables in tension. These loads are transferred both to the [towers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/tower), which carry them by vertical compression to the ground, and to the anchorages, which must resist the inward and sometimes vertical pull of the cables. The suspension bridge can be viewed as an upside-down arch in tension with only the towers in compression. Because the deck is hung in the air, care must be taken to ensure that it does not move excessively under loading. The deck therefore must be either heavy or stiff or both.

## [Cantilever](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cantilever)

![Cantilever truss bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/22077-050-0BDBAF21/cantilever-bridge-forces-lines-tension-compression.jpg)

[Cantilever truss bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/77/22077-050-0BDBAF21/cantilever-bridge-forces-lines-tension-compression.jpg) A diagram of a cantilever truss bridge, with forces of tension represented by red lines and forces of compression by green lines.(more)

A [beam](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/beam) is said to be cantilevered when it projects outward, supported only at one end. A [cantilever bridge](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cantilever-bridge) is generally made with three spans, of which the outer spans are both anchored down at the shore and cantilever out over the channel to be crossed. The central span rests on the [cantilevered arms](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cantilever-arm) extending from the outer spans; it carries vertical loads like a simply supported beam or a truss—that is, by tension forces in the lower chords and compression in the upper chords. The cantilevers carry their loads by tension in the upper chords and compression in the lower ones. Inner towers carry those forces by compression to the foundation, and outer towers carry the forces by tension to the far foundations.

## [Cable-stay](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cable-stayed-bridge)

![Cable-stayed bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/22078-050-E6998922/bridge-forces-lines-tension-compression.jpg)

[Cable-stayed bridge](https://cdn.britannica.com/78/22078-050-E6998922/bridge-forces-lines-tension-compression.jpg) A diagram of a cable-stayed bridge, with forces of tension represented by red lines and forces of compression by green lines.(more)

[Cable-stayed bridges](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cable-stayed-bridge) carry the vertical main-span loads by nearly straight diagonal [cables](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cable-wire-rope) in [tension](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/tension). The towers transfer the cable forces to the foundations through vertical compression. The tensile forces in the cables also put the deck into horizontal compression.

## Materials

The four primary materials used for bridges have been wood, stone, iron, and concrete. Of these, iron has had the greatest effect on modern bridges. From iron, steel is made, and steel is used to make reinforced and [prestressed concrete](https://www.britannica.com/technology/prestressed-concrete). Modern bridges are almost exclusively built with steel, [reinforced concrete](https://www.britannica.com/technology/reinforced-concrete), and prestressed concrete.

## [Wood](https://www.britannica.com/science/wood-plant-tissue) and [stone](https://www.britannica.com/topic/stone-material)

[Wood](https://www.britannica.com/science/wood-plant-tissue) is relatively weak in both compression and tension, but it has almost always been widely available and inexpensive. Wood has been used effectively for small bridges that carry light loads, such as footbridges. Engineers now incorporate laminated wooden [beams](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/beams) and arches into some modern bridges.

Stone is strong in compression but weak in tension. Its primary application has been in [arches](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-architecture), [piers](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pier-architecture), and abutments.

## [Iron](https://www.britannica.com/science/iron-chemical-element) and [steel](https://www.britannica.com/technology/steel)

The first [iron](https://www.britannica.com/technology/iron-processing) used during the [Industrial Revolution](https://www.britannica.com/event/Industrial-Revolution) was [cast iron](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cast-iron), which is strong in compression but weak in tension. [Wrought iron](https://www.britannica.com/technology/wrought-iron), on the other hand, is as strong in compression as cast iron, but it also has much greater [tensile strength](https://www.britannica.com/science/tensile-strength). [Steel](https://www.britannica.com/technology/steel) is an even further refinement of iron and is yet stronger, superior to any iron in both tension and compression. Steel can be made to varying strengths, some alloys being five times stronger than others. The engineer refers to these as high-strength steels.

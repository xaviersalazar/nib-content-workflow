**Gothic architecture**, architectural style in Europe that lasted from the mid-12th century to the 16th century, particularly a style of masonry building characterized by cavernous spaces with the expanse of walls broken up by overlaid [tracery](https://www.britannica.com/technology/tracery).

In the 12th–13th century, feats of engineering permitted increasingly gigantic buildings. The rib [vault](https://www.britannica.com/technology/vault-architecture), [flying buttress](https://www.britannica.com/technology/flying-buttress), and pointed (Gothic) [arch](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-architecture) were used as solutions to the problem of building a very tall structure while preserving as much natural light as possible. Stained-glass window panels rendered startling sun-dappled interior effects. One of the earliest buildings to combine these elements into a [coherent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/coherent) style was the [abbey of Saint-Denis](https://www.britannica.com/place/abbey-church-of-Saint-Denis), Paris ( _c._ 1135–44). The High Gothic years ( _c._ 1250–1300), heralded by [Chartres Cathedral](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chartres-Cathedral), were dominated by [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), especially with the development of the [Rayonnant style](https://www.britannica.com/art/Rayonnant-style). Britain, [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany), and [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain) produced variations of this style, while Italian Gothic stood apart in its use of brick and marble rather than stone. [Late Gothic (15th-century) architecture](https://www.britannica.com/art/Late-Gothic-art) reached its height in Germany’s vaulted [hall churches](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hall-church). Other late Gothic styles include the British [Perpendicular style](https://www.britannica.com/art/Perpendicular-style) and the French and Spanish [Flamboyant style](https://www.britannica.com/art/Flamboyant-style).

**cathedral**, in Christian churches that have an [episcopal](https://www.britannica.com/topic/episcopacy) form of [church](https://www.britannica.com/topic/church-architecture) government, the church in which a residential [bishop](https://www.britannica.com/topic/bishop-Christianity) has his official seat or throne, the [cathedra](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cathedra). Cathedral churches are of different degrees of dignity. There are cathedral churches of simple [diocesan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/diocese) bishops, of [archbishops](https://www.britannica.com/topic/archbishop) or [metropolitans](https://www.britannica.com/topic/metropolitan), of [primates](https://www.britannica.com/topic/primate-ecclesiastical-office), of [patriarchs](https://www.britannica.com/topic/patriarch-Eastern-Orthodoxy), and, in the [Roman Catholic Church](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-Catholicism), of the [pope](https://www.britannica.com/topic/pope). A cathedral church is not necessarily large and magnificent, although most cathedrals have become so. Since the territorial organization of the [early church](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-early-Christianity) followed that of the [Roman Empire](https://www.britannica.com/place/Roman-Empire), cathedrals from the first were established wherever possible in towns, not in villages. In the early European [Middle Ages](https://www.britannica.com/event/Middle-Ages), the town in which a cathedral church was situated became known as the cathedral city.

[![Puebla, Mexico: cathedral](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/189864-050-F9F27559/Cathedral-centre-Mexico-Puebla.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/189864-050-F9F27559/Cathedral-centre-Mexico-Puebla.jpg)

[Puebla, Mexico: cathedral](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/189864-050-F9F27559/Cathedral-centre-Mexico-Puebla.jpg) Cathedral in the historic colonial center of Puebla, Mexico.(more)

In the Roman Catholic church, [canon law](https://www.britannica.com/topic/canon-law) makes no architectural conditions for a cathedral. The only [canonical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/canonical) requirement is that a cathedral should be [consecrated](https://www.britannica.com/topic/consecration) and adequately endowed. The pope has the right to designate a cathedral, although the choice of the bishop of the [diocese](https://www.britannica.com/topic/diocese) or his decision to build a cathedral is normally approved by the pope. The bishop must be present in his cathedral on certain holy days, and he must normally perform [ordinations](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ordination) there.

In [Eastern Orthodoxy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eastern-Orthodoxy) the cathedral is the main church in a city where the bishop resides and where he celebrates the liturgy on festival occasions. In Russia, where the dioceses have always been few and have covered a [vast](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/vast) area, the main church in any large town became known as a cathedral ( _sobor_), even though no bishop was in residence there. The principal church of a big [monastery](https://www.britannica.com/topic/monastery) also assumed the same name.

After the 16th-century Protestant [Reformation](https://www.britannica.com/event/Reformation), cathedrals where Roman Catholic bishops were ejected frequently became simple churches. In some [Lutheran](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lutheranism) areas, such as Sweden and Finland, cathedrals continued to be the seat of Lutheran bishops. Similarly, in the [Church of England](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Church-of-England), where the order of bishops was retained, cathedrals remained as the seat of the bishop. This practice has continued throughout [Anglican](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Anglicanism) churches worldwide.

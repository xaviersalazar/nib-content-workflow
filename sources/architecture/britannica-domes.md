**dome**, in [architecture](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/architecture), hemispherical structure evolved from the [arch](https://www.britannica.com/technology/arch-architecture), usually forming a [ceiling](https://www.britannica.com/technology/ceiling) or [roof](https://www.britannica.com/technology/roof). Domes first appeared as solid mounds and in techniques adaptable only to the smallest buildings, such as round huts and tombs in the [ancient Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Middle-East), [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), and the Mediterranean. The Romans introduced the large-scale masonry hemisphere. The dome exerts thrusts all around its perimeter, and the earliest monumental examples, such as the Roman [Pantheon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pantheon-building-Rome-Italy), required heavy supporting walls.

[Byzantine](https://www.britannica.com/art/Byzantine-architecture) architects invented a technique for raising domes on piers, permitting [lighting](https://www.britannica.com/technology/lighting) and communication from four directions. The transition from a cubic base to the hemispherical dome was achieved by four [pendentives](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pendentive), inverted triangular masses of masonry curved both horizontally and vertically, as shown in the figure. Their [apexes](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/apexes) rested on the four piers, to which they conducted the forces of the dome; their sides joined to form arches over openings in the four faces of the cube; and their bases met in a complete circle to form the dome [foundation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/foundation-construction). The [pendentive](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pendentive) dome could rest directly on this circular foundation or upon a cylindrical [wall](https://www.britannica.com/technology/wall), called a [drum](https://www.britannica.com/technology/drum-architecture), inserted between the two to increase height.

Displaced architecturally by the light, vertical styles of [Gothic architecture](https://www.britannica.com/art/Gothic-architecture), the dome regained popularity during the European [Renaissance](https://www.britannica.com/event/Renaissance) and Baroque periods. Vaulting is simpler than doming, and so the effort and [ingenuity](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/ingenuity) devoted to doming rectangular structures must be explained principally by the symbolic character of the dome. The desire to observe tradition preserved the dome in the early era of iron and steel construction. The modern [reinforced concrete](https://www.britannica.com/technology/reinforced-concrete) slab used in vaulting can be curved in length as well as width to form a dome. Here the distinction between vaults and domes has lost its original significance, being based only on the type of curvature in the slab.

**geodesic dome**, spherical form in which [lightweight](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/lightweight) triangular or polygonal facets consisting of either skeletal struts or flat planes, largely in tension, replace the arch principle and distribute stresses within the structure itself. It was developed in the 20th century by American engineer and architect [R. Buckminster Fuller](https://www.britannica.com/biography/R-Buckminster-Fuller).

Key People: [R. Buckminster Fuller](https://www.britannica.com/biography/R-Buckminster-Fuller)_(Show more)_

Related Topics: [dome](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dome-architecture)_(Show more)_

[See all related content](https://www.britannica.com/facts/geodesic-dome)

A geodesic dome may be supported by light walls, but unlike other large domes it can also be set directly on the ground as a complete structure. A large geodesic dome was used to house the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) exhibit at [Expo 67](https://www.britannica.com/event/Expo-67) in [Montreal](https://www.britannica.com/place/Montreal) in 1967. Among the [dome’s](https://www.britannica.com/technology/dome-architecture) many design applications are sports arenas, theatres, greenhouses, and exhibition halls.

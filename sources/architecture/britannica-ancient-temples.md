- What is a temple?







A temple is a structure created for religious practices. While many religions use the term temple, Christianity often calls its worship places churches. A temple's architecture often showcases a culture's best design and craftsmanship. Hindu temples are sacred spaces for devotion to Hindu deities, considered homes for deities and powerful locations bridging the divine and human realms. The central focus is a [murti](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hindu-temple/Introduction#ref1) (sacred image) of the main deity. Hindu temples can also serve as economic powerhouses and social centers.

- Why are temples built?







Temples are built as sacred spaces for religious worship. They serve as homes for deities, bridging the divine and human realms. Attendance isn't obligatory, but temples foster social coherence, with devotees visiting for blessings.



Temple architecture reflects a culture's design and craftsmanship, varying across religions due to ritual needs. The architectural iconography of temples often symbolizes the universe and its center, creating a sense of religious awe. The function of temples is to ensure the god’s presence and provide a place to approach them. Hindu temples, of which there are an estimated 650,000 in India alone, often consist of a towering shrine and a columned hall surrounded by a wall.

- What materials are commonly used to build temples?







Temple construction commonly employs materials such as stone, brick, concrete, and wood. Stone, favored for its durability and adaptability to carving, is used in ashlar masonry, which consists of precisely cut blocks. Ancient Egyptians used limestone, sandstone, and granite for royal buildings, valuing their strength and symbolic significance.



Mortar, made of gypsum, sand, and water, acted as a lubricant and, to a limited extent, as a bonding agent in stone construction. Concrete, a mixture of cement, water, and aggregates, provides an economical substitute for traditional materials. Wood is valued for its ease of acquisition and workability, making it suitable for various building parts, though it is susceptible to fire, mold, and termites.

- How do temple designs vary in different cultures?







Temple designs vary significantly across cultures due to differing religious rituals, beliefs, and available technology.



In ancient Greece, temples housed a deity's image in a windowless cella, with an altar outside. Roman temples, influenced by the Greeks, eventually became the center of forums.



Eastern temple designs reflect the nature of their religions. For example, the rich symbolism of Jainism is seen in decorated monasteries, while Hindu temples feature a towering shrine and columned hall. Buddhist temples range from half-buried sanctuaries to carved towers, while Chinese Buddhist temples are often one-story buildings with an atrium.



In the Americas, Incan and Mayan temples were often stair-stepped pyramids made of stone, with the shrine at the top. South Indian temples typically consist of a hall and a square sanctum with a superstructure of the _kūṭina_ type.

- What special architectural features are often found in temples?







Temples exhibit diverse architectural features influenced by culture and religion. Temple architecture often showcases a culture's finest design and craftsmanship.



Common features include:


\\* **Materials** Construction utilizes materials like stone, marble, or timber, often adorned with carvings and colors.


\\* **Layout** Temples may consist of inner rooms (cella) housing deities, columned halls for gatherings, and enclosures to protect the sacred space.


\\* **Decoration** Decoration includes carvings of deities, mythological scenes, floral motifs, or geometric patterns.


\\* **Orientation** While many temples face east or west, some have unique orientations, like the north-south alignment of the [Kedarnath Temple](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Kedarnath-Temple).


\\* **Roofs** Roof styles vary from flat to sloping, including gabled, hipped, and domed roofs.


\\* **Lighting** Lighting is achieved through lamps, candles, and lanterns.

- How has temple building changed over time?







Temple building has evolved significantly across cultures and eras. Early Mesopotamian temples, like those of the Sumerians around 3100 BCE, were constructed of mud brick on raised platforms, featuring elaborate designs and ornamentation. Ancient Egyptian temples and pyramids of the 4th dynasty (c. 2575–c. 2465 BCE) transitioned to cut-stone construction, using materials like limestone, sandstone, and granite for durability and symbolic significance.



In ancient Greece, temples emphasized the gods, with inner rooms housing deity images and exteriors featuring columns and marble. Roman temples adopted Greek styles but integrated altars inside and developed forums centered around the temple.



Eastern and Middle Eastern designs reflected religious natures, such as the Jainism's decorated monasteries and the domed structures of Muslim temples. Chinese Buddhist temples often were one-story timber buildings with an atrium, while Japanese Shintō temples favored simple, rustic designs. In the Americas, Incan and Mayan temples were built as stair-stepped pyramids made of stone. Modern temple architecture is eclectic, blending traditional and contemporary designs.

**temple**, [edifice](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/edifice) constructed for religious [worship](https://www.britannica.com/topic/worship). Most of [Christianity](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Christianity) calls its places of worship churches; many religions use temple, a word derived in English from the Latin word for time, because of the importance to the Romans of the proper time of sacrifices. The name [synagogue](https://www.britannica.com/topic/synagogue), which is from the Greek for a place of assembly, is often interchangeable with Jewish temple. [Mosque](https://www.britannica.com/topic/mosque) is roughly an Arabic equivalent for temple. The [Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Church-of-Jesus-Christ-of-Latter-day-Saints), or [Mormon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Church-of-Jesus-Christ-of-Latter-day-Saints), temples are special places of worship and are centres for learning and for sacred ordinances to and for the living and for the dead.

[![Mari: ziggurat](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/143698-050-CD42E702/Ziggurat-Mari-Syria.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/143698-050-CD42E702/Ziggurat-Mari-Syria.jpg)

[Mari: ziggurat](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/143698-050-CD42E702/Ziggurat-Mari-Syria.jpg) Ziggurat at Mari, Syria.(more)

Because of the importance of temples in a society, temple [architecture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/architecture) often represents the best of a culture’s design and craftsmanship, and, because of ritual requirements, temple architecture varies widely between one [religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religion) and another. The [ziggurats](https://www.britannica.com/technology/ziggurat) of the Mesopotamian [culture](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture) were elaborately designed and decorated, and their “stair-step” style ascended to a point where a god or gods could dwell and where only special priests were allowed. [Ancient Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/topic/ancient-Egyptian-religion) had temples to gods, but because the primary concern of its religion was the afterlife of souls, its pyramidal tombs became its primary shrines and most familiar architectural heritage.

In the ancient [Greek religion](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-religion) the various gods were the most important focus, and Classical Greek temple architecture created structures that emphasized that focus. An inner, windowless room, or [cella](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cella), housed an image of a god, and an [altar](https://www.britannica.com/topic/altar) stood outside the temple, usually at the eastern end and often enclosed. Most Greek temples were built of marble or other stone, richly carved and polychromed, situated on a hill or stepped platform (stylobate) and having sloping roofs supported on a [portico](https://www.britannica.com/technology/portico-architecture) by columns in a variety of styles ( _see_ [order](https://www.britannica.com/technology/order-architecture)) and placements. The design and decoration of Greek temples had a profound effect on architecture of later eras in the West, beginning with the [Roman](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Roman-religion).

During the 3rd and 2nd centuries bc, Roman temples began to evince Greek influence, using the Greek decorative style but placing the altar within the temple and eventually creating entire forums, or meeting places, of which the temple was the centre. In Roman temple architecture, the columns, in their various styles, soon became engaged rather than freestanding, and circular as well as rectangular temples were built. [Byzantine](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Byzantine) and Western [church](https://www.britannica.com/topic/church-architecture) architecture developed from these bases in the Hellenistic styles, and the names and designs of this style of temple architecture still survive in the West.

[![Puri: Jagannatha temple](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/142580-050-7D825033/Jagannatha-temple-Puri-Odisha-India.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/142580-050-7D825033/Jagannatha-temple-Puri-Odisha-India.jpg)

[Puri: Jagannatha temple](https://cdn.britannica.com/80/142580-050-7D825033/Jagannatha-temple-Puri-Odisha-India.jpg) Jagannatha temple, Puri, Odisha, India.(more)

In the East and [Middle East](https://www.britannica.com/place/Middle-East), too, temple design expresses the nature of the religion. For example, the asceticism and rich symbolism of [Jainism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Jainism) is reflected in that religion’s beautifully decorated monastery-like structures in [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), both above the ground in simple cloisters and below the ground in caves. Other Indian temple architecture, although it tends to follow the pattern of a simple [floor](https://www.britannica.com/technology/floor) plan with a richly decorated [facade](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/facade), differs according to the ritual. [Hindu](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hinduism) temples, which vary regionally in style, usually consist of a towering shrine and a columned [hall](https://www.britannica.com/technology/hall) surrounded by an elaborate [wall](https://www.britannica.com/technology/wall). [Buddhist](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism) temples range from half-buried sanctuaries with richly carved entrances to single, carved towers or statues. Muslim temples in India, as elsewhere, are usually domed structures decorated with coloured tiles on the outside and covering a large central sanctuary and arcaded courtyards within.

[![Nanjing](https://cdn.britannica.com/66/128466-050-B95B6323/temple-Buddhist-province-Jiangsu-Nanjing-China.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/66/128466-050-B95B6323/temple-Buddhist-province-Jiangsu-Nanjing-China.jpg)

[Nanjing](https://cdn.britannica.com/66/128466-050-B95B6323/temple-Buddhist-province-Jiangsu-Nanjing-China.jpg) Buddhist temple, Nanjing, Jiangsu province, China.(more)

The Chinese (and later, Japanese) version of the Buddhist temple tends to be a one-story [building](https://www.britannica.com/technology/building) of richly carved, painted, or tiled timber constructed around an [atrium](https://www.britannica.com/technology/atrium-architecture) used for worship, although pagodas, which were sometimes built as temples, were towering stacks of brightly coloured, wing-roofed stories over a small shrine. By contrast, the [Shintō](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shinto) temples of Japan are almost huts, so simple and rustic are their design.

[![Chichén Itzá: Temple of the Warriors](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/178933-050-7217FC03/Temple-Warriors-Chichen-Itza-Mexico-Yucatan.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/178933-050-7217FC03/Temple-Warriors-Chichen-Itza-Mexico-Yucatan.jpg)

[Chichén Itzá: Temple of the Warriors](https://cdn.britannica.com/33/178933-050-7217FC03/Temple-Warriors-Chichen-Itza-Mexico-Yucatan.jpg) Temple of the Warriors, Chichén Itzá, Yucatán, Mexico.(more)

In the Americas, [Incan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Inca-religion) and [Mayan](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mayan-religion) temples were constructed of stone and were often highly carved. In general, because of the available [technology](https://www.britannica.com/technology/technology) as well as the religious belief, they were stair-stepped pyramids, with the shrine at the top. [Chichén Itzá](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chichen-Itza), the ruins of which remain in the [Yucatán Peninsula](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yucatan-Peninsula), has excellent examples of this type of pre-Columbian temple architecture.

- What is architecture?







Architecture is the art and technique of designing and building structures that meet both practical and expressive needs. It requires suitability for human use, stability, permanence, and communication of experience and ideas through form. The types of architecture are determined by society based on the needs of its institutions, and the architect's job is to find the means of achieving these goals. The architect also considers the site, type, and cost of the building, while harmonizing the demands of environment, use, and economy.

- How does architecture differ in different cultures or countries?







Architecture varies across cultures and countries due to the influence of environment, history, and artistic expression. Building designs consider climate, weather patterns, and destructive forces such as earthquakes and floods to ensure stability and habitability.



Cultural values and societal needs also shape architectural types. For example, the Chinese and Japanese versions of Buddhist temples are often one-story buildings made of carved timber around an atrium. In contrast, the Shintō temples of Japan are simple and rustic huts. Colonial influences, the availability of materials, and technological advancements further contribute to the diversity of architectural styles.

- What are some famous architectural styles and what makes them unique?







Different architectural styles have unique characteristics. **Gothic architecture**, from the mid-12th to 16th century, features cavernous spaces and walls broken up by tracery. Its engineering innovations include the rib vault, flying buttress, and pointed arch, which allowed for taller structures and more natural light.



**Baroque architecture**, originating in late 16th-century Italy, is known for its grandeur, drama, and contrast. It employs complex shapes, curves, and rich surface treatments to evoke motion and sensuality.



**Brutalism**, which emerged in the mid-20th century, is characterized by raw concrete, bold geometric forms, and a utilitarian aesthetic.



**Modernism** sought a nonhistorical architecture of functionalism using modern materials to create new spaces. Rejecting 19th-century stylistic pluralism, it aimed for a radically new architecture suited to "modern man".

**architecture**, the art and technique of designing and [building](https://www.britannica.com/technology/building), as distinguished from the skills associated with [construction](https://www.britannica.com/technology/construction). The practice of architecture is employed to fulfill both practical and expressive requirements, and thus it serves both utilitarian and [aesthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aesthetic) ends. Although these two ends may be distinguished, they cannot be separated, and the relative weight given to each can vary widely. Because every society—settled or nomadic—has a spatial relationship to the natural world and to other societies, the structures they produce reveal much about their [environment](https://www.britannica.com/science/environment) (including [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology) and [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather)), [history](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history), ceremonies, and artistic sensibility, as well as many aspects of daily life.

The [characteristics](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characteristics) that distinguish a work of architecture from other built structures are (1) the suitability of the work to use by human beings in general and the adaptability of it to particular human activities, (2) the stability and permanence of the work’s construction, and (3) the [communication](https://www.britannica.com/topic/communication) of experience and ideas through its form. All these conditions must be met in architecture. The second is a constant, while the first and third vary in relative importance according to the social function of buildings. If the function is chiefly utilitarian, as in a [factory](https://www.britannica.com/technology/factory), communication is of less importance. If the function is chiefly expressive, as in a monumental tomb, utility is a minor concern. In some buildings, such as churches and city halls, utility and communication may be of equal importance.

## Use

The types of architecture are established not by architects but by society, according to the needs of its different institutions. Society sets the goals and assigns to the architect the job of finding the means of achieving them. This section of the article is concerned with architectural [typology](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/typology), with the role of society in determining the kinds of architecture, and with planning—the role of the architect in adapting designs to particular uses and to the general physical needs of human beings.

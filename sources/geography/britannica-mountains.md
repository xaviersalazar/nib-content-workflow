- What is a mountain?







A [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform/Introduction) is a landform that rises significantly above its surroundings, characterized by steep slopes, a confined summit area, and considerable local relief. Mountains are larger than hills and typically occur in ranges or chains.



Mountains form through various [tectonic processes](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-landform/Theoretical-overview#ref49830), including the convergence of Earth's [tectonic plates](https://www.britannica.com/science/How-Are-Mountains-Formed/Introduction#ref1), volcanic activity, and block faulting. The [Himalayas](https://www.britannica.com/story/exploring-7-of-earths-great-mountain-ranges) exemplify mountain formation through plate collision, while the Andes demonstrate volcanic mountain building. Erosion can also create residual mountains from plateaus or uplifted regions.

- How is a mountain different from a hill?







Mountains and hills are both landforms that rise above the surrounding terrain and have confined summits. However, mountains are generally larger than hills, though the term "mountain" lacks a standardized geological definition. Hills are natural elevations of land that are lower than mountains, usually rounded in shape. Mountains often occur in elongated ranges or chains, while hills can appear as isolated formations or within hilly terrain.

- How are mountains formed?







Mountains commonly form through the convergence of Earth's [tectonic plates](https://www.britannica.com/science/plate-tectonics/Island-arcs#ref261561). The immense pressure from colliding plates causes the crust to fold, creating mountain ranges through a process called orogeny. The Himalayas are an example of mountain building caused by the Indian plate colliding into the Eurasian plate.



Volcanic activity is another contributor, where the movement of tectonic plates creates openings in Earth's crust, allowing magma to rise and solidify into [volcanic mountains](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcano/Stratovolcanoes#ref24466). Also, mountains can form through block faulting, where large crust blocks are uplifted or tilted on either side of a crack created by plate tectonics. Erosion can also leave behind residual mountains.

- What are some different types of mountains?







Mountains are landforms characterized by higher elevation and steeper relief than their surrounding areas. They can be categorized based on their formation processes:



  - **Fold mountains** These form through the collision of tectonic plates, causing the Earth’s crust to crumple and fold. The [Himalayas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Himalayas) are a prime example of this process.

  - **Volcanic mountains** These mountains arise from volcanic activity, where magma erupts and solidifies. The [Andes](https://www.britannica.com/place/Andes-Mountains) are a chain of volcanic peaks formed by the subduction of the Nazca Plate beneath the South American Plate.

  - **Block-fault mountains** Created by the uplift or tilting of large crustal blocks along fault lines. The [Sierra Nevada Mountains](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sierra-Nevada-mountains) in the United States are an example of block faulting.

  - **Residual mountains** These mountains are formed by erosion. The wearing away of rock by wind, water, and glaciers has left what are known as residual mountains.

- How do mountains affect the climate and weather of an area?







Mountains significantly influence regional climate and weather patterns. They act as barriers, modifying [atmospheric flow](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere/Effect-of-continents-on-air-movement#ref261458) and causing air to ascend, cool, and release moisture as [orographic precipitation](https://www.britannica.com/science/orographic-precipitation/Introduction#ref1) on their windward slopes.



Descending air on the leeward side results in drier conditions, known as the [rain shadow](https://www.britannica.com/science/orographic-precipitation/Introduction#ref1). Mountain slopes also experience temperature variations, leading to [mountain breezes](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology/Scale-classes#ref53293) (anabatic flow) during the day and cool downslope drainage at night (katabatic flow). Furthermore, mountains can induce [foehn winds](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology/Scale-classes#ref53293), resulting in warm, dry conditions on the leeward side due to adiabatic temperature changes as air flows over the mountain.

- Why are mountains important for people, animals, and plants?







Mountains are vital for people, animals, and plants because they are biological hotspots that are full of interesting organisms with amazing adaptations that enable them to survive in rugged terrain and extreme weather conditions.



Mountains provide a range of diverse topographic and climatic environments. Mountainous vegetation is less affected by human activities than surrounding areas, so they may harbor plants and animals that have been driven out by disturbances elsewhere.



Mountains are stressful habitats for plants and are not very productive environments. Agricultural exploitation of mountain lands is not very productive and is confined to light or seasonal grazing.

**mountain**, [landform](https://www.britannica.com/science/landform-134931) that rises prominently above its surroundings, generally exhibiting steep slopes, a relatively confined summit area, and considerable local relief. Mountains generally are understood to be larger than hills, but the term has no standardized geological meaning. Very rarely do mountains occur individually. In most cases, they are found in elongated ranges or chains. When an array of such ranges is linked together, it [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) a mountain belt. For a list of selected mountains of the world, [_see below_](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform/Selected-world-mountains#ref275986).

[![Mount Triumph, Washington](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/158788-050-314EBC88/Mount-Triumph-height-North-Cascades-National-Park.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/158788-050-314EBC88/Mount-Triumph-height-North-Cascades-National-Park.jpg)

[Mount Triumph, Washington](https://cdn.britannica.com/88/158788-050-314EBC88/Mount-Triumph-height-North-Cascades-National-Park.jpg) Mount Triumph rises to a height of 2,208 metres (7,244 feet) in North Cascades National Park, Washington, U.S. The Cascades are a segment of the Pacific mountain system of western North America.(more)

A mountain belt is many tens to hundreds of kilometres wide and hundreds to thousands of kilometres long. It stands above the surrounding surface, which may be a coastal [plain](https://www.britannica.com/science/plain), as along the western Andes in northern Chile, or a high [plateau](https://www.britannica.com/science/plateau-landform), as within and along the [Plateau of Tibet](https://www.britannica.com/place/Plateau-of-Tibet) in southwest China. Mountain ranges or chains extend tens to hundreds of kilometres in length. Individual mountains are connected by ridges and separated by valleys. Within many mountain belts are plateaus, which stand high but contain little relief. Thus, for example, the [Andes](https://www.britannica.com/place/Andes-Mountains) [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) a mountain belt that borders the entire west [coast](https://www.britannica.com/science/coast) of South America; within it are both individual ranges, such as the [Cordillera Blanca](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cordillera-Blanca) in which lies [Peru’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru) highest peak, Huascarán, and the high plateau, the Altiplano, in southern Peru and western Bolivia.

Chains of active volcanoes, such as those occurring at island arcs, are commonly marked by individual high mountains separated by large expanses of low and gentle [topography](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/topography). In some chains, namely those associated with “hot spots” ( _see below_), only the volcanoes at one end of the chain are active. Thus, those volcanoes stand high, but with increasing distance away from them [erosion](https://www.britannica.com/science/erosion-geology) has reduced the sizes of volcanic structures to an increasing degree.

The folding of layers of sedimentary rocks with thicknesses of hundreds of metres to a few kilometres often leaves long parallel ridges and valleys termed [fold belts](https://www.britannica.com/science/orogenic-belt), as, for example, in the Valley and Ridge province of Pennsylvania in the eastern [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States). The more resistant rocks form ridges, and the valleys are underlain by weaker ones. These fold belts commonly include segments where layers of older rocks have been thrust or pushed up and over younger rocks. Such segments are known as fold and thrust belts. Typically their topography is not as regular as where folding is the most important process, but it is usually dominated by parallel ridges of resistant [rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology) divided by valleys of weaker rock, as in the eastern [flank](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/flank) of the Canadian [Rocky Mountains](https://www.britannica.com/place/Rocky-Mountains) or in the [Jura Mountains](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jura-Mountains) of France and Switzerland.

Most fold and thrust belts are bounded on one side, or lie parallel to, a belt or terrain of [crystalline rocks](https://www.britannica.com/science/crystalline-rock). These are metamorphic and igneous rocks that in most cases solidified at depths of several kilometres or more and that are more resistant to erosion than the sedimentary rocks deposited on top of them. These crystalline terrains typically contain the highest peaks in any mountain belt and include the highest belt in the world, the [Himalayas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Himalayas), which was formed by the thrusting of crystalline rocks up onto the surface of the [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth). The great heights exist because of the resistance of the rocks to erosion and because the rates of continuing uplift are the highest in these areas. The topography rarely is as regularly oriented as in fold and thrust belts.

In certain areas, blocks or isolated masses of rock have been elevated relative to [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) areas to form [block-fault mountains](https://www.britannica.com/science/block-fault-mountain) or ranges. In some places, block-fault ranges with an overall common orientation coalesce to define a mountain belt or chain, but in others the ranges may be isolated.

Block faulting can occur when blocks are thrust, or pushed, over neighbouring valleys, as has occurred in the Rocky Mountains of Colorado, Wyoming, and Utah in the western United States or as is now occurring in the [Tien Shan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tien-Shan), an east–west range in western China and [Central Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Central-Asia). Within individual ranges, which are usually a few hundred kilometres long and several tens of kilometres wide, crystalline rocks commonly crop out. On a large scale, there is a clear orientation of such ranges, but within them the landforms are controlled more by the variations in erosion than by tectonic processes.

Block faulting also occurs where blocks are pulled apart, causing a subsidence of the intervening [valley](https://www.britannica.com/science/valley) between diverging blocks. In this case, alternating basins and ranges form. The basins eventually fill with sediment, and the ranges—typically tens of kilometres long and from a few to 20–30 kilometres wide—often tilt, with steep relief on one side and a gentle slope on the other. The uniformity of the gently tilted slope owes its existence to long periods of erosion and [deposition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/deposition) before tilting, sometimes with a capping of resistant lava flows on this surface prior to tilting and faulting. Both the Tetons of Wyoming and the Sierra [Nevada](https://www.britannica.com/place/Nevada-state) of California were formed by blocks being tilted up toward the east; major faults allowed the blocks on their east sides to drop steeply down several thousand metres and thereby created steep eastern slopes.

In some areas, a single block or a narrow zone of blocks has subsided between neighbouring blocks or plateaus that moved apart to form a [rift valley](https://www.britannica.com/science/rift-valley) between them. Mountains with steep inward slopes and gentle outward slopes often form on the margins of [rift](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/rift) valleys. Less commonly, large areas that are pulled apart and subside leave between them an elevated block with steep slopes on both sides. An example of this kind of structure, called a [horst](https://www.britannica.com/science/horst), is the Ruwenzori in [East Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/eastern-Africa).

Finally, in certain areas, including those that once were plateaus or broad uplifted regions, erosion has left what are known as [residual](https://www.britannica.com/science/residual-landform) mountains. Many such mountains are isolated and not part of any discernible chain, as, for instance, [Mount Katahdin](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Katahdin) in Maine in the northeastern United States. Some entire chains (e.g., the Appalachians in [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) or the Urals in Russia), which were formed hundreds of millions of years ago, remain in [spite](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/spite) of a long history of erosion. Most residual chains and individual mountains are characterized by low elevations; however, both gentle and precipitous relief can exist, depending on the degree of recent erosion.

# Tectonic processes that create and destroy mountain belts and their components

Mountains and mountain belts exist because tectonic processes have created and maintained high elevations in the face of [erosion](https://www.britannica.com/science/erosion-geology), which works to destroy them. The [topography](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/topography) of a mountain belt depends not only on the processes that create the elevated terrain but also on the forces that support this terrain and on the types of processes (erosional or tectonic) that destroy it. In fact, it is necessary to understand the forces that support elevated terrains before considering the other factors involved.

## Mechanisms that support elevated terrains

[![The video thumbnail image shows a snowcapped mountain against a blue sky.](https://cdn.britannica.com/98/263098-138-87DCA742/why-are-mountains-so-tall.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/why-are-mountains-so-tall/-321447)

How to Measure a MountainSome of the world's tallest mountains are actually growing.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform/images-videos)

Two properties of rocks contribute to the support of mountains, mountain belts, and plateaus, namely strength and density. If rocks had no strength, mountains would simply flow away. At a subtler level, the strength of the material beneath mountains can affect the scale of the topography.

In terms of strength, the [lithosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/lithosphere), the thickness of which varies over the face of the Earth from a few to more than 200 kilometres, is much stronger than the underlying layer, the [asthenosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/asthenosphere) ( _see_ [plate tectonics](https://www.britannica.com/science/plate-tectonics)). The strength of the lithosphere is derived from its temperature; thick lithosphere exists because the outer part of the Earth is relatively cold. Cold, thick, and therefore strong lithosphere can support higher mountain ranges than can thin lithosphere, just as thick [ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice) on a [lake](https://www.britannica.com/science/lake) or [river](https://www.britannica.com/science/river) is better able to support larger people than thin ice.

In terms of chemical [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition), and therefore density, the [Earth’s crust](https://www.britannica.com/science/Earths-crust) is lighter than the underlying mantle. Beneath the oceans, the typical thickness of the crust is only six to seven kilometres. Beneath the continental regions, the average thickness is about 35 kilometres, but it can reach 60 or 70 kilometres beneath high mountain ranges and plateaus. Thus, most ranges and plateaus are buoyed up by thick crustal roots. To some extent the light crust floats on the heavier mantle, as icebergs float on the oceans.

It should be noted that the crust and lithosphere are defined by different properties and do not [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) the same layer. Moreover, variations in their thicknesses have different relationships to the overlying topography. Some mountain ranges and plateaus are buoyed up by a thick crust. The lithosphere beneath such areas, however, can be thin, and its strength does not play a significant role in supporting the range or [plateau](https://www.britannica.com/science/plateau-landform). Other ranges may overlie thick lithospheric plates, which are flexed down by the weight of the mountains. The crust beneath such ranges is likely to be thicker than normal but not as thick as it would be if the lithosphere were thin. Thus, the strength of the lithosphere supports these mountains and maintains the base of the crust at a higher level than would have been the case had the strong layer been [absent](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/absent). For instance, the [Himalayas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Himalayas) have been thrust onto the crust of the Indian shield, which is underlain by particularly cold, thick lithosphere that has been flexed down by the weight of the high range. The thickness of the crust is about 55 kilometres beneath the high peaks, which stand more than 8,000 metres high. The thickest crustal segment of 70 kilometres, however, lies farther north beneath the [Plateau of Tibet](https://www.britannica.com/place/Plateau-of-Tibet) (or Tibetan Plateau), whose altitude is about 4,500 to 5,000 metres but whose lithosphere is much thinner than that beneath the Himalayas. The strong Indian lithosphere helps to support the Himalayas, but the buoyancy of the thick Tibetan crust maintains the high elevation of the plateau.

## Tectonic processes that produce high elevations

![continental collision](https://cdn.britannica.com/67/105567-050-8283793B/masses-rock-crust-another-plate-subducts.jpg)

[continental collision](https://cdn.britannica.com/67/105567-050-8283793B/masses-rock-crust-another-plate-subducts.jpg) Colliding masses of continental crust displace rock upward as one plate subducts under another.(more)

As noted above, individual mountains, mountain ranges, mountain belts, and plateaus exist because tectonic processes have elevated terrains faster than erosion could destroy them. High elevations are created by three major processes: these are volcanism, horizontal crustal shortening as [manifested](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/manifested) by [folding](https://www.britannica.com/science/fold) and by faulting, and the heating and thermal expansion of large terrains.

## [Volcanism](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcanism)

Most, but not all, volcanoes consist of material that is thought to have melted in the mantle (at depths of tens of kilometres), which rose through the overlying crust and was erupted onto the surface. To a large extent, the physical characteristics of the erupted material determines the shape and height of a [volcano](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcano). Material of low density can produce taller mountains than can denser material. [Lavas](https://www.britannica.com/science/lava-volcanic-ejecta) with low viscosity, such as in Hawaii, flow easily and produce gentle slopes, but more [viscous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/viscous) lavas mixed with explosively erupted solid blocks of rocks can form steeper volcanic cones, such as [Mount Fuji](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Fuji) in Japan, [Mount Rainier](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Rainier) in the northwestern [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), or [Mount Kilimanjaro](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kilimanjaro) in Africa.

Many volcanoes are built on elevated terrains that owe their existence to the intrusion into the crust of [magmas](https://www.britannica.com/science/magma-rock)—i.e., molten [rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology) presumably derived from the mantle. The extent to which this process is a major one in mountain belts is controversial. Many belts, such as the Andes, seem to be underlain, at least in part, by solidified magmas, but the volume of the intruded material and its exact source (melting of either the crust or the mantle) remain poorly understood.

## [Crustal shortening](https://www.britannica.com/science/crustal-shortening)

In most mountain belts, [terrains](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/terrains) have been elevated as a result of crustal shortening by the thrusting of one block or slice of crust over another and/or by the folding of layers of rock. The topography of mountain ranges and mountain belts depends in part on the amount of displacement on such faults, on the angles at which faults dip, on the degree to which crustal shortening occurs by faulting or by folding, and on the types of rocks that are deformed and exposed to erosion. Most of the differences among mountain belts can be ascribed to some combination of these factors.

## Heating and [thermal expansion](https://www.britannica.com/science/thermal-expansion)

Rocks, like most materials, expand when they are heated. Some mountain ranges and plateaus are high simply because the crust and upper mantle beneath them are unusually hot. Most broad variations in the [topography](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/topography) of the [ocean](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean) floor, the mid-ocean ridges and rises, are due to horizontal variations in temperature in the outer 100 kilometres of the Earth. Hot areas stand higher—or at shallower depths in the ocean—than cold areas. Many plateaus, such as the [Massif Central](https://www.britannica.com/place/Massif-Central) in south central France or the [Ethiopian Plateau](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ethiopian-Plateau), are elevated significantly because the material beneath them has been heated.

## Tectonic processes that destroy elevated terrains

Besides erosion, which is the principal agent that destroys mountain belts, two tectonic processes help to reduce high elevations. Horizontal crustal extension and associated crustal thinning can reduce and eliminate crustal roots. When this happens, mountain belts widen and their mean elevation [diminishes](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/diminishes). Similarly, the cooling and associated [thermal contraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/thermal-contraction) of the outer part of the Earth leads to a reduction of the average height of a mountain belt.

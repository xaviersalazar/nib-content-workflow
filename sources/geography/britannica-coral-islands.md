- What is a coral island?







A coral island is a tropical island that is made of organic material from the skeletons of corals as well as other animals and plants that live with corals. These islands are low-lying, typically a few meters above sea level, and are known for their white coral sand beaches and coconut palms.



Geologically, a coral island or cay is a small part of a larger coral reef system, usually located on the relatively flat top of the reef. These islands can form through the uplift of a reef system above sea level or through the accretion of reef rock and detritus from storms and wave action. Winds and currents play a role in shaping the island.

- How do coral islands form?







Coral islands are tropical islands that consist of low land formations built from the skeletons of corals, plants, and animals associated with corals.



There are two ways that coral islands can form:


\\* **Accretion** Coral islands can form through the accumulation of reef rock, broken off from the reef by storms and waves, which mixes with finer reef detritus. Beaches develop, and wind may heap material into dunes. Rainwater dissolves and redeposits lime, cementing the material together. Plants and animals colonize the land, contributing remains that help soil develop.


\\* **Uplift** Part or all of a reef system may rise above sea level due to crustal movements. The submarine reef top becomes a plateau with a shallow or dry lagoon.



Charles Darwin suggested that atolls, a type of coral island, are the final stage of a reef's upgrowth around a sinking, extinct volcano.

- What role do coral reefs play in making coral islands?







Coral reefs play a fundamental role in the creation of coral islands. These islands are made of organic material, mainly from the skeletons of corals and other reef-associated organisms.



Reef building happens below the high-tide level, and islands or cays usually sit on top of the reef system.



Winds and currents shape the reefs and determine the position of coral sand cays, also known as "low islands," that form on reefs. Sediment is lifted from the reef surface and deposited where water velocity decreases. Wind action on these deposits can then build dunes.



Storms and surf can also pile up material into beaches and shoals, which may then develop into islands or cays on top of the reef.

- What are the main features of a coral island's geology?







Coral islands are tropical islands composed of the skeletal remains of corals and related organisms. They are low-lying, rarely more than a few meters above sea level, and feature white coral sand beaches and coconut palms.



Geologically, coral islands are small parts of larger reef systems that may have originated through uplift or accretion. Uplift occurs when crustal movements raise a reef system above sea level, creating rocky islands with cliffs and sculptured surfaces. Accretion occurs when reef rock is broken off by storms and waves, then mixed with finer detritus. This material accumulates through wave action, forming beaches and dunes, which are then cemented together by dissolved lime and colonized by plants and animals, contributing to soil development.



Atolls are ring-shaped coral reefs enclosing a lagoon. They may evolve from reefs fringing a subsiding volcanic island, as explained by [Charles Darwin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Darwin)'s subsidence theory, or from reef platforms uplifted above sea level.

- How do waves and currents affect the shape of coral islands?







Waves and currents play a crucial role in shaping coral islands. They transport sediment from the reef surface, depositing it on the leeward side where water velocity decreases, leading to the formation of coral sand cays or low islands.



Wind-generated currents and tidal effects influence the orientation, shape, and position of these islands. Storm waves can carry coral fragments, forming shingle banks that add stability when cemented. However, hurricanes can also erode shorelines.



Accretion is another process where reef rock broken off by storms and waves mixes with finer detritus. Beaches develop, and wind forms dunes. Rainwater dissolves and redeposits calcium carbonate, cementing the material together, while plants and animals colonize the land, contributing to soil development.

- Why are coral islands important for studying Earth's geological history?







Coral islands are valuable for studying Earth's geological history because [fossil corals](https://www.britannica.com/science/geochronology/Geomagnetic-variations#ref70065) can serve as absolute geochronometers, preserving a record of the number of days in a year when they were growing.



By examining the fine external ridges of [calcium carbonate](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-island/Introduction#ref1) in fossil corals, scientists can estimate the number of days per year, which has decreased over time due to the slowing rotation of the Earth. Additionally, the [carbon and oxygen isotopic composition](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-reef/Geochemistry-of-reefs#ref284960) of coral skeletons and shells can provide insights into past water temperature changes and offer a method for assessing the age of a sample. Through the study of [coral reefs](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-island/Introduction#ref1) and islands, the effects of plate tectonics and [sea level changes](https://www.britannica.com/science/atoll/Introduction#ref1) can be better understood.

**coral island**, tropical island built of organic material [derived](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/derived) from skeletons of [corals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/coral) and numerous other animals and plants associated with corals. Coral islands consist of low land perhaps only a few metres above [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level), generally with coconut palms and surrounded by white [coral](https://www.britannica.com/animal/coral) sand beaches. They may extend dozens of kilometres and include almost any tropical limestone island whose structure is integrally part of a living or relatively recent [coral reef](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-reef). Reef building takes place mostly below high-tide level, and a typical coral island or [cay](https://www.britannica.com/science/cay) usually surmounts the relatively flat top of the whole reef system. Geologically, the island is just one small part of the whole coral reef.

Coral reefs take four main forms. [Fringing reefs](https://www.britannica.com/science/fringing-reef) consist of a flat reef area directly skirting a nonreef island, often volcanic, or a mainland mass. [Barrier reefs](https://www.britannica.com/science/barrier-reef) are also close to a nonreef landmass but lie several kilometres offshore, separated from the landmass by a [lagoon](https://www.britannica.com/science/lagoon-geography) or channel often approximately 50 m (160 ft) deep. Some barrier reefs are more or less circular, surrounding an island, but larger barrier reefs, such as those along the [Red Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Red-Sea) [coast](https://www.britannica.com/science/coast) and the [Great Barrier Reef](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Barrier-Reef) of [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia), are complex linear features consisting of chains of reef patches, some of them elongated into ribbon reefs. The third category of reefs consists of [atolls](https://www.britannica.com/science/atoll), which are like circular barrier reefs but without their central landmass. Finally, there are [patch reefs](https://www.britannica.com/science/platform-reef), which have irregular tablelike or [pinnacle](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/pinnacle) features. Smaller patches occur inside [atoll](https://www.britannica.com/science/atoll) lagoons. Larger patches occur as isolated parts of larger developments of any of the other three reef categories. They sometimes occur completely separate from other kinds of reefs.

Coral reef islands occur in association with all categories of reef but especially on reefs whose flat tops are well developed, perhaps a kilometre or more in width. Reef islands may occur in isolation or in a chain along the length of the reef. Sometimes they take the form of long strips of land occupying most of the length of the central area of a reef top.

There are two completely different kinds of origin of reef islands: [uplift](https://www.britannica.com/science/uplift) and accretion. In the first, part or all of a reef system may become land as a result of crustal movements raising it above sea level ( _e.g._, the [Aldabra Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Aldabra-Islands) in the western Indian Ocean). The previously submarine reef top becomes a low plateaulike feature, and such islands are typically rocky, with cliffs, and with land surfaces pitted and sculptured by solution weathering (karst). They are often still recognizable as atolls with a lagoon, now much shallower or even completely dry, as an interior [basin](https://www.britannica.com/science/basin-landform). If present sea levels were to fall again, as they have [done](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/done) in the recent geological past due to an increase in polar ice, most of the world’s coral reefs would, in effect, become raised features. It is only because present sea levels are the highest for many thousands of years that there are no raised reef islands of this kind now.

Coral islands created by [accretion](https://www.britannica.com/science/accretion-deposit) have developed from rubbly reef rock broken off from the reef by storms and waves and mixed with finer reef [detritus](https://www.britannica.com/science/detritus). The exceptional conditions of cyclonic storms are sometimes sufficient to create reef-top shoals in a single event. Other material accumulates by more regular methods such as normal currents and wave action. Beaches develop around the shoal, and wind may heap up the lighter, finer material into dunes. Rainwater can now reach all this material, which, being almost entirely of [calcium carbonate](https://www.britannica.com/science/calcium-carbonate), is readily [dissolved](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/dissolved) by it, and the dissolved lime is redeposited around the loose material, cementing it together. The newly formed land is soon colonized by plants and animals, which also contribute their own remains to the island, helping soil to develop. Many of the reef islands in the central and southern Pacific and of the [Maldive Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Maldives) in the [Indian Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indian-Ocean) originated in this way.

Reef islands, especially those close to sea level, are not very stable. The cyclones which help to create them may also damage and destroy them. Waves may attack one side and redeposit the material on the other. [Precarious](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Precarious) though reef islands are, they have nevertheless long been the homes of peoples like the Polynesians and Micronesians in the Pacific and the Maldivians in the Indian Ocean. These people have been able to survive by their seafaring skills, fishing the reef waters, rearing animals and crops on the land, and using for drinking water the thin lens of rainwater held within the reef rock.

- What is a coral reef?







A [coral reef](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-reef/Introduction#ref1) is a ridge or hummock in shallow ocean areas formed by algae and the calcareous skeletons of coelenterates, with coral polyps being the most significant. Coral reefs are complex structures consisting of living coral or algal frameworks, carbonate sand and mud, sea grasses, mangroves, and blue-green algal mats.



These plants and algae trap and stabilize sediment, which accretes to the reef complex. Storms and surf deposit fragmental material, forming beaches, shoals, and [coral islands](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-island/Introduction#ref1). Over time, reef material undergoes dissolution, redeposition, and chemical transformation, resulting in true rock. The shape of coral reefs is influenced by the upward growth of reef builders, prevailing winds and currents, and sea-level changes.

- How do coral reefs form in the ocean?







Coral reefs are complex geological formations that develop over millions of years from the skeletons and skeletal waste of living marine organisms. Coral polyps, related to sea anemones, form colonies and build limestone structures from their calcium carbonate skeletons. Coralline algae and bryozoans act as cement, binding the skeletal remains together.



Reef-building corals thrive in shallow, sunlit waters with normal salinity and temperatures between 22°C and 28°C, though they can survive with a minimum winter temperature of 15°C. Calcareous algae, mollusks, echinoderms, and protozoans also contribute to reef formation, with some providing the structural framework and others binding and cementing materials.



Over time, the accumulation of carbonate sand and mud, along with the trapping and stabilization of sediment by plants and algae, further accrete to the reef complex. Storms and surf can also contribute by heaping up fragmental material into beaches and shoals, which may develop into islets or cays on top of the reef.

- What types of rocks or materials make up coral reefs?







Coral reefs are made of both living and non-living materials. The main structural framework is built by stony corals, or Scleractinia, that create rigid skeletons of [calcium carbonate](https://www.britannica.com/science/calcium-carbonate), essentially building limestone.



Other organisms contribute to the reef's composition. [Calcareous algae](https://www.britannica.com/science/calcareous-algae), mollusks, echinoderms, and protozoans all have different reef-building roles. Almost all shelly and calcareous organisms provide fragments that fill gaps between corals. Algae and protozoans bind and cement materials together.



Additionally, [carbonate sand](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-reef/Introduction#ref284953) and mud accumulate, which support marine plant life that further stabilizes the sediment. Over time, reef material undergoes transformation into true rock through dissolution, redeposition, recrystallization, and chemical changes.

- How do living corals help build the structure of reefs?







Living corals, particularly stony corals or Scleractinia, are the primary builders of reef structures. Coral polyps create rigid skeletons of calcium carbonate, effectively building limestone formations.



These corals thrive in shallow, sunlit waters, generally between the low-water mark and depths of around 11 meters, though they can still construct reefs as deep as 40 meters. When conditions are right, corals grow side by side and even on top of each other, forming large colonies that can span several meters. Other organisms, such as calcareous algae, mollusks, and protozoans, also contribute to reef formation by providing fragments that fill gaps between corals and by binding and cementing the reef structure together.

- What are the main types of geological structures found in coral reefs?







Coral reefs exhibit a variety of geological structures, resulting from both living organisms and physical processes.



The primary types of reef structures are:


\\* **Fringing reefs** Flat reef areas that directly skirt a nonreef island or mainland mass.


\\* **Barrier reefs** Reefs close to a nonreef landmass but separated by a lagoon or channel.


\\* **Atolls** Circular barrier reefs without a central landmass.


\\* **Platform (or patch) reefs** Irregular table-like or pinnacle features that can occur in isolation or within larger reef systems.

- How do coral reefs affect and interact with their surrounding geology over time?







Coral reefs significantly shape their geological surroundings over time. They form through the accumulation of [coral](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-reef/Introduction#ref284953) and algal frameworks, which create habitats for marine plants that stabilize sediment. Storms can pile up coral debris, forming beaches and islands on top of the reef structure.



Over long periods, [dissolution](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-reef/Introduction#ref284955), redeposition, and chemical transformation turn reef material into solid rock. The shape of reefs is influenced by the growth patterns of reef builders and also by changes in sea levels over millions of years. [Coral reefs](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-island/Introduction#ref1) that grew before the last glacial period were exposed and eroded when sea levels dropped. As the sea level rose again, new coral growth covered the older landscape.

**coral reef**, ridge or hummock formed in shallow [ocean](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean) areas by [algae](https://www.britannica.com/science/algae) and the calcareous skeletons of certain coelenterates, of which [coral](https://www.britannica.com/animal/coral) polyps are the most important. A coral reef may grow into a permanent [coral island](https://www.britannica.com/science/coral-island). Often called the “rainforests of the sea,” coral reefs are home to a spectacular variety of organisms.

## Structure

[![sea fan](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/20213-004-C0F866DE/Sea-fan-variety-coral-array-organisms-Belize.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/20213-004-C0F866DE/Sea-fan-variety-coral-array-organisms-Belize.jpg)

[sea fan](https://cdn.britannica.com/13/20213-004-C0F866DE/Sea-fan-variety-coral-array-organisms-Belize.jpg) Sea fan (genus _Gorgonia_), a branching variety of coral, beside an array of other organisms in the Belize Barrier Reef.(more)

A coral reef is actually a complex of features, only part of which is a living coral or algal framework, although the other associated features result from this live segment. The accumulations of carbonate [sand](https://www.britannica.com/science/sand) and mud provide a [habitat](https://www.britannica.com/science/habitat-biology) for sea grasses and [mangroves](https://www.britannica.com/plant/mangrove) and for almost inconspicuous blue-green algal mats. These plants and algae trap and stabilize sediment, and their accumulations are also accreted to the whole reef complex. [Storms](https://www.britannica.com/science/storm) and surf heap up fragmental material into [beaches](https://www.britannica.com/science/beach) and [shoals](https://www.britannica.com/science/shoal), and the shoals may develop into low sandy or rubbly islets or [cays](https://www.britannica.com/science/cay) on top of the reef.

## Corals and other reef-building organisms

Coral polyps [resemble](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/resemble) [sea anemones](https://www.britannica.com/animal/sea-anemone), to which they are closely related, but, unlike most anemones, most reef corals are colonial. Initial polyps divide themselves into daughter polyps, and they divide in turn, growing into colonies that can be up to several metres in diameter, all held together in one continuous rigid calcareous skeleton. They remain attached to the seafloor and become so large and heavy that only storms disturb them. Under the right conditions, generally clear and well-circulating water that is not too rough, the corals grow profusely side by side, even on and over each other. The corals in effect build [limestone](https://www.britannica.com/science/limestone) because their skeletons are made of calcium [carbonate](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbonate).

Reef-building corals, chiefly the [stony corals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/stony-coral) or Scleractinia, grow best in shallow sunlit water, between the low-water mark and a depth of 11 metres (36 feet), but they can still construct reefs in water as deep as 40 metres (about 130 feet), and they may have a [sparse](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/sparse) existence between 40 and 55 metres (130 and 180 feet). These corals prefer water of normal salinity with an annual maximum [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature) above 22 °C (72 °F) but below 28 °C (about 82 °F). Their reef-building activities, however, may be carried on in waters whose minimum temperature in winter is not less than 15 °C (59 °F).

A second group of corals in present-day seas grows in thickets and coppices that develop banks rather than reefs on the outer, deeper, colder, and darker parts of [continental shelves](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-shelf) and platforms. These organisms [flourish](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/flourish) in water with a winter minimum temperature ranging between about 4 and 15 °C (39 and 59 °F) at depths of about 60 to 200 metres (200 to 650 feet). In any one thicket there are commonly only two genera of delicately branching corals involved. Such coral banks are known along the eastern Atlantic shelf edge (or [continental slope](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-slope)) from Norway to the [Cape Verde](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cabo-Verde) islands and again off the [Niger River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Niger-River) [delta](https://www.britannica.com/science/delta-river-system-component) and in the west Atlantic around the [Gulf of Mexico](https://www.britannica.com/place/Gulf-of-Mexico-Gulf-of-America), The Bahamas, and the [Orinoco River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Orinoco-River) delta. Off [New Zealand](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Zealand) such banks have been recognized on the Campbell Plateau and the Chatham Rise. They also occur in the northwest Pacific near Japan.

The third coral assemblage of the modern seas is associated with even colder or deeper seas. It consists of small, [solitary](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/solitary) corals of relatively few genera, known from the [abyssal floors](https://www.britannica.com/science/abyssal-plain) of the oceans and from the shelves around Antarctica, Patagonia, and the [Falkland Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Falkland-Islands) in waters 2 to 6 °C (36 to 43 °F) in temperature.

Calcareous [algae](https://www.britannica.com/science/algae) (stony seaweeds), [mollusks](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mollusk), [echinoderms](https://www.britannica.com/animal/echinoderm), and [protozoans](https://www.britannica.com/science/protozoan) also contribute to the reef. Different organisms have different reef-building roles. Some, especially the corals, provide the main structural framework of the growing reef, although in parts of the world such as the central Pacific, where the surf is very strong, calcareous algae may be more important in the roughest places. Almost all shelly and calcareous organisms and those with spicules, such as [sponges](https://www.britannica.com/animal/sponge-animal) and [sea cucumbers](https://www.britannica.com/animal/sea-cucumber), provide fragments that [wash](https://www.britannica.com/science/arroyo) into or fall into the gaps between corals. Other organisms, especially algae and protozoans, bind and cement everything together with sheetlike growth.

## Orientation and arrangement

[![lagoons](https://cdn.britannica.com/74/1974-050-C5B0D1D0/Lagoons-coral-reef-barrier-islands-Raiatea-Tahaa.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/74/1974-050-C5B0D1D0/Lagoons-coral-reef-barrier-islands-Raiatea-Tahaa.jpg)

[lagoons](https://cdn.britannica.com/74/1974-050-C5B0D1D0/Lagoons-coral-reef-barrier-islands-Raiatea-Tahaa.jpg) Lagoons lie behind the coral reef and barrier islands surrounding Raiatea and Tahaa in the Society Islands, Pacific Ocean.(more)

A typical coral reef generally faces the open sea. Seaward of it is the fore reef, descending into deeper water and floored deeper down by fragmental material derived from the reef. Behind the growing fore-reef edge (or reef crest), which rises to about mean high-water level, is a shallow platform formed partly by a now dead area of reef framework and partly of fragmental material and often colonized by sea grasses, algal mats, or mangroves. Patches of living framework occur intermittently. Cays may occur along the platform, or the reef may terminate against the shore of a landmass. If there is no immediately [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) landmass, the reef descends again into deeper water, generally more gently, on this, its leeward side. There are usually live reef frameworks on this slope too, but these are often irregular and patchy.

## Sedimentation

The reef becomes true [rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology) by an almost imperceptible dissolution, redeposition, recrystallization, and chemical transformation of reef material. The shape of coral reefs, while at least in part due to the tendency of reef builders to grow upward and outward toward the prevailing [winds](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind) and [currents](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean-current), is also the result of changes of [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level) during the last 2,000,000 years or more. Coral reefs that developed before the last [glaciation](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice-age-geology) were left above sea level, where they were eroded and subjected to solution [weathering](https://www.britannica.com/science/weathering-geology). As sea level rose again during the past 10,000 years, new reef growth [mantled](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/mantled) this older, drowned landscape but has still not masked it completely.

## Types of coral reefs

[![Great Barrier Reef, Queensland](https://cdn.britannica.com/12/153412-050-9452CFE0/coast-Great-Barrier-Reef-Australia-Queensland.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/12/153412-050-9452CFE0/coast-Great-Barrier-Reef-Australia-Queensland.jpg)

[Great Barrier Reef, Queensland](https://cdn.britannica.com/12/153412-050-9452CFE0/coast-Great-Barrier-Reef-Australia-Queensland.jpg) Great Barrier Reef, off the coast of Queensland, Australia.(more)

Coral reefs take four principal forms. (1) [Fringing reefs](https://www.britannica.com/science/fringing-reef) consist of [flat](https://www.britannica.com/science/playa) reef areas that directly skirt a nonreef [island](https://www.britannica.com/science/island), often volcanic, or a mainland mass. (2) [Barrier reefs](https://www.britannica.com/science/barrier-reef) are also close to a nonreef landmass but lie several kilometres offshore, separated from the landmass by a [lagoon](https://www.britannica.com/science/lagoon-geography) or [channel](https://www.britannica.com/science/streambed) often about 50 metres (160 feet) deep. Some barrier reefs are more or less circular, surrounding an island, but larger barrier reefs, such as those along the [Red Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Red-Sea) [coast](https://www.britannica.com/science/coast) and [Australia’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia) [Great Barrier Reef](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Barrier-Reef), are complex linear features consisting of chains of reef patches, some of them elongated into ribbon reefs. (3) [Atolls](https://www.britannica.com/science/atoll) are like circular barrier reefs but without their central landmass. (4) Finally, there are [platform, or patch, reefs](https://www.britannica.com/science/platform-reef), which have irregular tablelike or [pinnacle](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/pinnacle) features. Smaller patches occur inside [atoll](https://www.britannica.com/science/atoll) [lagoons](https://www.britannica.com/science/lagoon-geography). Larger patches occur as isolated parts of larger developments of any of the other three reef categories. They sometimes occur completely separate from other kinds of reefs.

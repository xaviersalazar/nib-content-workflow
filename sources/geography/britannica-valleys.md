- What is a valley in geology?







In geology, valleys are elongated depressions on Earth's surface, typically formed by [rivers](https://www.britannica.com/science/river/Valley-evolution#ref29105) or [glacial activity](https://www.britannica.com/science/glacial-valley/Introduction#ref1). Tectonic activity can also create valleys, known as [rift valleys](https://www.britannica.com/science/list-of-valleys-2026597/Introduction#ref1), which are fault-bounded troughs that existed before the river.

- How are valleys formed?







Valleys are elongate depressions in the Earth's surface, most commonly drained by rivers and situated in a relatively flat plain or between hills or mountains. While tectonic activity can create rift valleys, most valleys result from river erosion, where rivers carve the valleys in which they flow.



The shape of a valley depends on base level, rock type, and rock structures. During the initial development of a valley, relief increases as rivers expend energy vertically. Over time, rivers smooth the valley floor, and more energy is spent laterally, broadening it.



Other types of valleys include:


\\* **Rift valleys** Valleys formed through tectonic activity.


\\* **Glacial valleys** Stream valleys that have been glaciated into a U-shape.

- What is the difference between a V-shaped valley and a U-shaped valley?







The primary distinction between V-shaped and U-shaped valleys lies in their formation and resulting morphology.



  - **V-Shaped Valleys:** These valleys form through [erosion](https://www.britannica.com/science/river/Valley-evolution#ref29105) caused by rivers, which carve narrow, deep channels, especially in areas with rapid uplift and intense fluvial action. They are characterized by steep, knife-edge ridges and valley slopes that converge in a V-shape.

  - **U-Shaped Valleys:** These valleys are the product of [glaciation](https://www.britannica.com/science/glacial-valley/Introduction#ref1). Glaciers, having greater viscosity and cross-section than rivers, erode valley walls and floors, creating a [parabolic or U-shaped cross profile](https://www.britannica.com/science/glacial-landform/Glacial-deposition#ref49766) with wide, flat bottoms and steep, even vertical sidewalls.

- How do rivers shape valleys over time?







Rivers shape valleys over time through continuous erosion and deposition processes. The river's energy is primarily used for vertical entrenchment, deepening the valley. Over time, the river smooths the valley floor, eliminating waterfalls and reaching its base level.



As the river approaches its base level, its energy shifts to lateral erosion, widening the valley floor. The valley's shape depends on base level, rock type, and geological structures. In areas with folds or faults, these structures control the orientation and form of valleys. The longitudinal profile of a valley, steep in the headwaters and gentle in the lower reaches, reflects the river's adjustment to its base level.

- What role do glaciers play in forming valleys?







Glaciers play a significant role in shaping valleys through erosion and deposition. As a glacier moves, it picks up rock debris from the valley's walls and floor. Glacial erosion occurs through abrasion and quarrying. Abrasion happens as the ice grinds over the ground, while quarrying or plucking involves the glacier plucking rocks from its bed.



Glaciers transform V-shaped stream valleys into U-shaped valleys because the U-shape offers the least resistance to the moving glacier. This process results in relatively wide, flat valley bottoms and steep, even vertical sidewalls. Smaller tributary glaciers erode their troughs less rapidly than the main glacier, leading to hanging valleys.

**valley**, elongate depression of the [Earth’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth) surface. Valleys are most commonly drained by rivers and may occur in a relatively [flat](https://www.britannica.com/science/playa) [plain](https://www.britannica.com/science/plain) or between ranges of hills or mountains. Those valleys produced by tectonic action are called [rift](https://www.britannica.com/science/rift-valley) valleys. Very narrow, deep valleys of similar appearance are called gorges. Both of these latter types are commonly cut in flat-lying strata but may occur in other geological situations.

Wherever sufficient rainfall occurs, opportunity exists for the land surface to evolve to the familiar patterns of hills and valleys. There are, of course, hyperarid [environments](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environments) where fluvial activity is minimal. There also are geomorphological settings where the permeability of rocks or sediments induce so much infiltration that water is unable to concentrate on the land surface. Moreover, some landscapes may be so young that insufficient time has elapsed for modification by fluvial action. The role of fluvial action on landscape, including long-term evolutionary processes, is considered here in detail. For additional information on fluvial and hillslope processes relating to valley formation, _see_ [river](https://www.britannica.com/science/river).

Probably the world’s deepest subaerial valley is that of the Kāli Gandaki River in Nepal. Lying between two 8,000-metre (26,000-foot) Himalayan peaks, Dhaulāgiri and Annapūrna, the valley has a total relief of six kilometres (four miles). Because the Himalayas are one of the Earth’s most active areas of tectonic uplift, this valley well illustrates the principle that the most rapid downcutting occurs in areas of the most rapid uplift. The reason for this seeming [paradox](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/paradox) lies in the energetics of the processes of [degradation](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/degradation) that characterize valley formation. As will be discussed below, the steeper the gradient or slope of a stream, the greater its expenditure of power on the [streambed](https://www.britannica.com/science/streambed). Thus, as uplift creates higher relief and steeper slopes, rivers achieve greater power for [erosion](https://www.britannica.com/science/erosion-geology). As a consequence, the most rapid processes of relief reduction can occur in areas of most rapid relief production.

## Geomorphic characteristics

The relief of valleys and canyons is produced by the incising action of [rivers](https://www.britannica.com/science/river). Hillslope processes are indeed critical in the development of valley sides ( _see below_), but it is rivers that lower the level of erosion through degradation. Rivers ultimately adjust to a [baselevel](https://www.britannica.com/science/baselevel), defined as the lowest point at which [potential energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/potential-energy) can be transformed to the [kinetic energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/kinetic-energy) of river flow. In most cases, the [ultimate baselevel](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level) for rivers is sea level. Some rivers drain to enclosed [basins](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/basins) below sea level, as, for example, the [Jordan River](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jordan-River), which flows to the [Dead Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Dead-Sea) in [Israel](https://www.britannica.com/place/Israel) and Jordan. Moreover, rivers may adjust to local baselevels, including zones of resistance to incision, lakes, and dams (both natural and artificial).

## Valley longitudinal profiles

The longitudinal profile of a valley is the gradient throughout its length. # Valley cross profiles

The cross profiles of valleys involve a combination of fluvial and hillslope processes. Although slopes and rivers are often studied separately by process geomorphologists, hills and valleys are the features that dominate landscapes. In upland areas cross profiles of valleys are often narrow and deep. Canyon [morphologies](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/morphologies) are most common. Further downstream, valley floors are wider and often dominated by floodplains and terraces.

## Types of valleys

One of the few classifications of valleys is that used by the German climatic geomorphologists [Herbert Louis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Julius-Budel) and Julius Büdel. In areas of rapid uplift and intense fluvial action such as tropical mountains, _Kerbtal_ (German for “notched valley”) forms occur. These are characterized by steep, knife-edge ridges and valley slopes meeting in a V-shape. Where slopes are steep but a broad valley floor occurs, _Sohlenkerbtal_ (meaning precisely a valley with such characteristics) is the prevailing form. Valleys of this kind develop under the influence of groundwater flow in Hawaii ( _see below_ [Processes](https://www.britannica.com/science/valley/Origin-and-evolution#ref49718)). Gutter-shaped valleys with convex sides and broad floors are called _Kehltal_; and broad, [flat](https://www.britannica.com/science/playa) valleys of planation surfaces are termed _Fachmuldental_.

It is important to remember that the form of valleys reflects not only modern processes but also ancient ones. The entire valley or some landforms within it may be relict, with features inherited from past geologic periods during which occurred tectonic and climatic processes of intensities quite different from those prevailing today.

## [Hillslopes](https://www.britannica.com/science/hillslope)

Hillslopes [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) the flanks of valleys and the margins of eroding uplands. They are the major zones where rock and soil are loosened by weathering processes and then transported down gradient, often to a [river](https://www.britannica.com/science/river) [channel](https://www.britannica.com/science/streambed).

![idealized profiles of two hillslopes](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/7764-004-8567CF31/Comparison-profiles-hillslopes.jpg)

[idealized profiles of two hillslopes](https://cdn.britannica.com/64/7764-004-8567CF31/Comparison-profiles-hillslopes.jpg) Comparison of idealized profiles for weathering-limited, faceted hillslopes (left) and transport-limited, sigmoid hillslopes (right).(more)

Two major varieties of hillslopes occur in nature ( _see_ figure). On [weathering-limited slopes](https://www.britannica.com/science/weathering-limited-slope), transport processes are so efficient that debris is removed more quickly than it can be generated by further weathering. Such hillslopes develop a faceted or angular [morphology](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/morphology) in which an upper free face, or [cliff](https://www.britannica.com/science/cliff), contributes debris to a lower slope of accumulation. Slopes of this sort are especially common on bare rock where the profile of the slope is determined by the resistance of the rock, not by the erosional processes acting on it. One consequence of this is that many rock slopes retreat parallel to themselves in order to preserve the characteristic slope angle for a rock type of given strength. If the features of the rock change with depth into the slope, however, the characteristic angle of the slope will change. Rock slopes develop where weathering and soil [erosion](https://www.britannica.com/science/erosion-geology) are slow (as in arid regions) and where rock resistance is high.

The second major variety of slope is transport limited. [Transport-limited slopes](https://www.britannica.com/science/transport-limited-slope) occur where weathering processes are efficient at producing [debris](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/debris) but where transport processes are inefficient at removing it from the slope. Such slopes lack free faces and faceted appearances, and they are generally covered with a soil mantle. The profile of this type of slope generally has a sigmoid appearance, with convex, straight, and concave segments. The shape of the slope is an expression of the process acting upon it.

Convex slope segments commonly occur in the upper parts of [soil-mantled](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil-formation) slopes, as near the drainage divide. The noted American geomorphologist G.K. Gilbert elucidated the principles applying to convex slopes in his study of piles of mining-waste debris in California. The processes of soil creep and raindrop splash erode soil on the upper parts of slopes. Since soil eroded from the upper slope must pass each point below it, the volume of soil moved increases with distance from the divide. Since the transport rate for creep and rain splash is proportional to the slope angle, the slope angle must also increase from the divide, resulting in the slope convexity.

Straight slope segments are dominated by mass movement processes. [Talus](https://www.britannica.com/science/talus-landform) slopes are a type in which debris piles up to a characteristic angle of repose. When new debris is added to the slope, thereby locally increasing the angle, the slope adjusts by movement of the debris to reestablish the angle. Again, the result is a [dynamic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynamic) [equilibrium](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/equilibrium) in which the [landform](https://www.britannica.com/science/landform-134931) adjusts to processes acting upon it.

Concave slopes are especially common where overland-flow runoff transports sediment derived from upper slopes. Because the collection area for wash increases downslope and discharge _Q_ is proportional to collection area, stream power—equation ( [5](https://www.britannica.com/science/valley#ref-16084))—can be maintained at lower slope angles. In addition, the size of particles being transported decreases downslope because of weathering and abrasion. Because the finer particles are easier to transport, slope angles can be reduced in the downslope direction. The result is a concave shape to the slope profile.

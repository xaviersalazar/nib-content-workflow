### What is a glacier?

A glacier is any large mass of perennial ice that originates on land by the recrystallization of snow or other forms of solid precipitation and shows evidence of past or present flow. One international group has recommended that all persisting snow and ice masses larger than 0.1 square kilometre (about 0.04 square mile) be counted as glaciers.

### Where are glaciers found?

Glaciers occur in all parts of the world and at almost all latitudes. In Ecuador, Kenya, Uganda, and Irian Jaya (New Guinea), glaciers even occur at or near the Equator, albeit at high altitudes. The two great ice masses, the Antarctic and Greenland ice sheets, contain about 99 percent of the world’s glacier ice.

### What causes glaciers to grow and shrink?

Glaciers are replenished mainly by snowfall and shrink primarily by melting, runoff, or breaking off of icebergs. For a glacier to remain at a constant size, there must be a balance between income (accumulation) and outgo (ablation). If this mass balance is positive (more gain than loss), the glacier will grow; if negative, it will shrink.

### How much of the world’s fresh water is stored in glacier ice?

Glacier ice stores about three-fourths of all the fresh water in the world. Glacier ice covers about 11 percent of the world’s land area and would cause a world sea-level rise of about 90 metres (300 feet) if all the existing ice melted.

Glaciers are classifiable in three main groups: (1) glaciers that extend in continuous sheets, moving outward in all directions, are called [ice sheets](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice-sheet) if they are the size of [Antarctica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctica) or [Greenland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greenland) and ice caps if they are smaller; (2) glaciers [confined](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/confined) within a path that directs the ice movement are called [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) glaciers; and (3) glaciers that spread out on level ground or on the [ocean](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean) at the foot of glaciated regions are called [piedmont](https://www.britannica.com/science/piedmont-geology) glaciers or ice shelves, respectively. Glaciers in the third group are not independent and are treated here in terms of their sources: ice shelves with ice sheets, piedmont glaciers with mountain glaciers. A complex of mountain glaciers burying much of a mountain range is called an [ice field](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice-cap).

## Distribution of glaciers

A most interesting aspect of recent geological time (some 30 million years ago to the present) has been the recurrent expansion and contraction of the world’s ice cover. These glacial fluctuations influenced geological, climatological, and biological [environments](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environments) and affected the evolution and development of early humans. Almost all of Canada, the northern third of the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), much of Europe, all of [Scandinavia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Scandinavia), and large parts of northern [Siberia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Siberia) were engulfed by ice during the major glacial stages. At times during the [Pleistocene Epoch](https://www.britannica.com/science/Pleistocene-Epoch) (from 2.6 million to 11,700 years ago), glacial ice covered 30 percent of the world’s land area; at other times the ice cover may have shrunk to less than its present extent. It may not be improper, then, to state that the world is still in an [ice age](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice-age-geology). Because the term glacial generally implies ice-age events or Pleistocene time, in this discussion “glacier” is used as an adjective whenever reference is to ice of the present day.

Glacier ice today stores about three-fourths of all the fresh [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) in the world. Glacier ice covers about 11 percent of the world’s land area and would cause a world sea-level rise of about 90 metres (300 feet) if all existing ice melted. Glaciers occur in all parts of the world and at almost all latitudes. In Ecuador, Kenya, Uganda, and [Irian Jaya](https://www.britannica.com/place/Papua) (New Guinea), glaciers even occur at or near the Equator, [albeit](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/albeit) at high altitudes.

## Glaciers and [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology)

[![Researchers in orange jumpsuits climb a mountain peak.](https://cdn.britannica.com/39/248939-138-AD2EA9BA/interview-glaciologist-Bethan-Davies-research-in-Antarctica.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/interview-glaciologist-Bethan-Davies-research-in-Antarctica/-286649)

The cause of the fluctuation of the world’s glacier cover is still not completely understood. Periodic changes in the heat received from the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-energy), caused by fluctuations in the [Earth’s](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth) orbit, are known to correlate with major fluctuations of [ice sheet](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice-sheet) advance and retreat on long time scales. Large ice sheets themselves, however, contain several “instability mechanisms” that may have contributed to the larger changes in world climate. One of these mechanisms is due to the very high [albedo](https://www.britannica.com/science/albedo), or reflectivity of dry [snow](https://www.britannica.com/science/snow-weather) to [solar radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-radiation). No other material of [widespread](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/widespread) distribution on the Earth even approaches the albedo of snow. Thus, as an ice sheet expands it causes an ever larger share of the Sun’s radiation to be reflected back into space, less is absorbed on the Earth, and the world’s climate becomes cooler. Another instability mechanism is implied by the fact that the thicker and more extensive an ice sheet is, the more snowfall it will receive in the form of [orographic precipitation](https://www.britannica.com/science/orographic-precipitation) (precipitation resulting from the higher altitude of its surface and attendant lower temperature). A third instability mechanism has been suggested by studies of the West Antarctic Ice Sheet. Portions of an ice sheet called ice streams may periodically move rapidly outward, perhaps because of the buildup of a thick layer of wet, deformable material under the ice. Although the ultimate causes of ice ages are not known with certainty, scientists agree that the world’s ice cover and climate are in a state of delicate balance.

## A Century of Glacier Retreat at Jackson Glacier

Massive glaciers can take hundreds or thousands of years to form, but climate change has been destroying glaciers in a matter of decades. In 1850 the area in Montana that eventually became Glacier National Park contained about 150 glaciers. Because of global warming, only about 25 still exist today. Jackson Glacier, photographed in 1912 and again in 2009, is approximately the seventh largest of these remaining glaciers. Scarring and scraping on the exposed rock reveal the glacier's former reach, and vegetation now grows where ice once flowed. # Formation and characteristics of glacier ice

## Transformation of snow to ice

Glacier [ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice) is an [aggregate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aggregate) of irregularly shaped, interlocking single crystals that range in size from a few millimetres to several tens of centimetres. Many processes are involved in the transformation of snowpacks to glacier ice, and they proceed at a rate that depends on wetness and [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature). Snow crystals in the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere) are tiny hexagonal plates, needles, stars, or other intricate shapes. In a deposited snowpack these intricate shapes are usually unstable, and molecules tend to evaporate off the sharp (high curvature) points of crystals and be condensed into hollows in the ice grains. This causes a general rounding of the tiny ice grains so that they fit more closely together. In addition, the [wind](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind) may break off the points of the [intricate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/intricate) crystals and thus [pack](https://www.britannica.com/science/densification) them more tightly. Thus, the density of the snowpack generally increases with time from an initial low value of 50–250 kilograms per cubic metre (3–15 pounds per cubic foot). The process of evaporation and condensation may continue: touching grains may develop necks of ice that connect them (sintering) and that grow at the expense of other parts of the ice grain, or individual small grains may rotate to fit more tightly together. These processes proceed more rapidly at temperatures near the [melting point](https://www.britannica.com/science/melting-point) and more slowly at colder temperatures, but they all result in a net densification of the snowpack. On the other hand, if a strong temperature [gradient](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/gradient) is present, [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) molecules may migrate from grain to grain, producing an array of intricate crystal shapes (known as depth hoar) of lowered density. If liquid water is present, the rate of change is many times more rapid because of the melting of ice from grain extremities with refreezing elsewhere, the compacting force of [surface tension](https://www.britannica.com/science/surface-tension), refreezing after pressure melting (regulation), and the freezing of water between grains.

This densification of the [snow](https://www.britannica.com/science/snow-weather) proceeds more slowly after reaching a density of 500–600 kilograms per cubic metre, and many of the processes mentioned above become less and less effective. Recrystallization under stress caused by the weight of the overlying snow becomes [predominant](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/predominant), and grains change in size and shape in order to minimize the stress on them. This change usually means that large or favourably oriented grains grow at the expense of others. Stresses due to glacier flow may cause further recrystallization. These processes thus cause an increase in the density of the mass and in the size of the average grain.

When the density of the aggregate reaches about 830 to 840 kilograms per cubic metre, the air spaces between grains are sealed off, and the material becomes impermeable to fluids. The time it takes for pores to be closed off is of critical importance for [extracting](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/extracting) climate-history information from ice cores. With time and the application of stress, the density rises further by the compression of air bubbles, and at great depths the air is absorbed into the ice crystal lattices, and the ice becomes clear. Only rarely in [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) glaciers does the density exceed 900 kilograms per cubic metre, but at great depths in ice sheets the density may approach that of pure ice (917 kilograms per cubic metre at 0 °C and atmospheric pressure).

Snow that has survived one melting season is called [firn](https://www.britannica.com/science/firn) (or névé); its [density](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/density) usually is greater than 500 kilograms per cubic metre in temperate regions but can be as low as 300 kilograms per cubic metre in polar regions. The permeability change at a density of about 840 kilograms per cubic metre marks the transition from firn to glacier ice. The transformation may take only three or four years and less than 10 metres of burial in the warm and wet [environment](https://www.britannica.com/science/environment) of Washington state in [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America), but high on the [plateau](https://www.britannica.com/science/plateau-landform) of [Antarctica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctica) the same process takes several thousand years and burial to depths of about 150 metres.

A glacier may also accumulate mass through the refreezing of water that occurs at its base. Previously, water at the base of a glacier was thought to serve as a lubricating layer that assisted the movement of the glacier across the ground, and refrozen water occurred only in subglacial lakes. However, scientists have demonstrated that refrozen water may also increase the size of the glacier by adding mass to its base. In addition, the refreezing process tends to lift and alter the upper layers of the glacier. This lifting phenomenon has been observed in several Antarctic ice fields, including the [vast](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/vast) Dome A plateau that forms the top of the East Antarctic [ice sheet](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice-sheet).

## Mass balance

[![iceberg](https://cdn.britannica.com/53/74653-050-D450092C/Tourist-boat-front-iceberg-Greenland-coast.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/53/74653-050-D450092C/Tourist-boat-front-iceberg-Greenland-coast.jpg)

[iceberg](https://cdn.britannica.com/53/74653-050-D450092C/Tourist-boat-front-iceberg-Greenland-coast.jpg) Tourist boat in front of a massive iceberg near the coast of Greenland.(more)

Glaciers are nourished mainly by snowfall, and they primarily waste away by melting and runoff or by the breaking off of icebergs (calving). In order for a glacier to remain at a constant size, there must be a balance between income ( [accumulation](https://www.britannica.com/science/accumulation-glaciation)) and outgo (ablation). If this mass balance is positive (more gain than loss), the glacier will grow; if it is negative, the glacier will shrink.

Accumulation refers to all processes that contribute mass to a glacier. Snowfall is predominant, but additional contributions may be made by [hoarfrost](https://www.britannica.com/science/hoarfrost) (direct condensation of ice from water vapour), [rime](https://www.britannica.com/science/rime-weather) (freezing of supercooled water droplets on striking a surface), hail, the freezing of rain or meltwater, or avalanching of snow from [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) slopes. [Ablation](https://www.britannica.com/science/ablation-glaciation) refers to all processes that remove mass from a glacier. In temperate regions, [melting](https://www.britannica.com/science/melting) at the surface normally predominates. Melting at the base is usually very slight (1 centimetre \[0.4 inch\] per year or less). Calving is usually the most important process on large glaciers in polar regions and on some temperate glaciers as well. Evaporation and loss by ice avalanches are important in certain special environments; floating ice may lose mass by melting from below.

Because the processes of accumulation, ablation, and the [transformation](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/transformation) of snow to ice proceed so differently, depending on temperature and the presence or absence of liquid water, it is customary to classify glaciers in terms of their thermal condition. A [polar glacier](https://www.britannica.com/science/polar-glacier) is defined as one that is below the freezing temperature throughout its mass for the entire year; a [subpolar](https://www.britannica.com/science/subpolar-glacier) (or polythermal) glacier contains ice below the freezing temperature, except for surface melting in the summer and a basal layer of temperate ice; and a [temperate glacier](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperate-glacier) is at the melting temperature throughout its mass, but surface freezing occurs in winter. A polar or subpolar glacier may be frozen to its bed (cold-based), or it may be at the melting temperature at the bed (warm-based).

Another [classification](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/classification) distinguishes the surface zones, or facies, on parts of a glacier. In the dry-snow zone no surface melting occurs, even in summer; in the percolation zone some surface melting may occur, but the meltwater refreezes at a shallow depth; in the soaked zone sufficient melting and refreezing take place to raise the whole winter snow layer to the melting temperature, permitting runoff; and in the superimposed-ice zone refrozen meltwater at the base of the snowpack (superimposed ice) forms a continuous layer that is exposed at the surface by the loss of overlying snow. These zones are all parts of the accumulation area, in which the mass balance is always positive. Below the superimposed-ice zone is the [ablation zone](https://www.britannica.com/science/ablation-zone), in which annual loss [exceeds](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/exceeds) the gain by snowfall. The boundary between the accumulation and ablation zones is called the [equilibrium line](https://www.britannica.com/science/equilibrium-line).

The value of the surface mass balance at any point on a glacier can be measured by means of stakes, snow pits, or cores. These values at points can then be averaged over the whole glacier for a whole year. The result is the net or annual mass balance. A positive value indicates growth, a negative value a decline.

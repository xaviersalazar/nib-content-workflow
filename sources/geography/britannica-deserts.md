- What is a desert?







A desert is an environment characterized by little available moisture for plant life due to imbalances between precipitation and evapotranspiration. Desert regions experience considerable rainfall variability, low atmospheric humidity, high daytime temperatures, and winds.



Average annual precipitation usually ranges from almost zero to 400 millimeters, but some authorities consider 250 millimeters as the upper limit. The shortage of water determines which plants and animals can survive there, and they possess unique adaptations to thrive in dry conditions and make the most of brief wet periods.



Desertification, or the expansion of desert environments, is a growing concern resulting from climatic variations and unsustainable human activities.

- Where are deserts found in the world?







Deserts, defined as large, extremely dry areas with sparse vegetation, are a major ecosystem found worldwide, particularly in Africa and Australia. Most hot deserts lie on the western sides of continents near 30° N and 30° S latitude. Temperate deserts are usually in continental interiors or in the rain shadows of tall mountain ranges, while polar deserts are in ice-covered areas near the North and South Poles.

- What kind of weather do deserts usually have?







Deserts are known for their shortage of moisture for plants because of imbalances between precipitation and evapotranspiration. This is made worse by variable rainfall, low humidity, high daytime temperatures, and winds.



Most recognized deserts get less than 16 inches (400 mm) of rain annually. Some deserts get almost no rain, while others can get up to 24 inches (600 mm). Rainfall, when it occurs, can be very heavy but brief.



Temperatures in hot deserts can be very high during the day, exceeding 104 °F (40 °C). The highest recorded temperature was 136.4 °F (58 °C) in Libya. Night temperatures can fall dramatically because of the lack of cloud cover.



Temperate or cold deserts, found at higher latitudes, experience colder temperatures. These deserts are arid due to their remoteness from the coast or the presence of mountains.

- What plants and animals live in deserts?







Deserts are home to a variety of plants and animals that have adapted to arid conditions. Desert plants, also known as xerophytes, include deep-rooted phreatophytes that tap into groundwater, as well as succulents that store water in their fleshy tissues. Examples of desert flora include sagebrush, saltbush, Joshua trees, cacti, and thorny shrubs like mesquite.



Desert fauna includes reptiles, birds, rodents, and larger mammals. Many desert animals, such as the jerboa, are nocturnal, seeking shelter in cool burrows during the day. Some desert birds are nomadic, relocating to areas where rain has fallen to find food. Protective coloration is an important feature of desert life.

- How do plants and animals survive in the harsh conditions of a desert?







Desert plants and animals have developed unique strategies to survive the harsh desert environment. Plants are typically [xerophytes](https://www.britannica.com/plant/xerophyte/Introduction#ref1) or [phreatophytes](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-American-Desert/Introduction#ref41401). Xerophytes are plants adapted to arid conditions that prevent water loss by having thick, fleshy stems or leaves, waxy coatings, or the ability to drop leaves during dry periods. Phreatophytes are deep-rooted plants that rely on a permanent water supply and survive by tapping into groundwater.



Desert animals often avoid the heat by staying in cool, humid burrows during the day and emerging at night to find food. To conserve water, desert animals have skin relatively impervious to water, and some can lower the temperature of exhaled air to reduce water loss during respiration. Also, protective coloration is an important feature of desert life.

- What are some differences between hot deserts and cold deserts?







Hot and cold deserts differ primarily in temperature and location. Hot deserts are in subtropical regions, while [cold deserts](https://www.britannica.com/science/cold-desert) are in temperate areas at higher latitudes and elevations.



Hot deserts experience high daytime temperatures, with maximums exceeding 104°F (40°C), whereas [cold deserts](https://www.britannica.com/science/cold-desert) have long, cold winters with average temperatures below freezing. Precipitation, mainly as snow or fog, is scant in cold deserts. Both experience low humidity and considerable rainfall variability.



The [Gobi Desert](https://www.britannica.com/science/cold-desert) in China and Mongolia and the [Great Basin Desert](https://www.britannica.com/science/cold-desert) in the United States are examples of cold deserts, while the [Sonoran](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-American-Desert/Introduction#ref41400) and [Chihuahuan Deserts](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chihuahuan-Desert/Introduction#ref427974) in North America are hot deserts.

## Origin

The desert environments of the present are, in geologic terms, relatively recent in origin. They represent the most extreme result of the progressive cooling and consequent aridification of global climates during the [Cenozoic Era](https://www.britannica.com/science/Cenozoic-Era) (65.5 million years ago to the present), which also led to the development of savannas and scrublands in the less arid regions near the tropical and temperate margins of the developing deserts. It has been suggested that many typical modern desert plant families, particularly those with an Asian centre of [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity) such as the [chenopod](https://www.britannica.com/plant/Chenopodiaceae) and tamarisk families, first appeared in the Miocene (23 to 5.3 million years ago), evolving in the salty, drying environment of the disappearing [Tethys Sea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tethys-Sea) along what is now the Mediterranean–Central Asian axis.

[![Ancient welwitschia](https://cdn.britannica.com/50/118250-050-D2E1D31E/Tumboa.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/50/118250-050-D2E1D31E/Tumboa.jpg)

[Ancient welwitschia](https://cdn.britannica.com/50/118250-050-D2E1D31E/Tumboa.jpg) Welwitschia, or tumboa ( _Welwitschia mirabilis_).(more)

Deserts also probably existed much earlier, during former periods of global arid [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology) in the lee of [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) ranges that sheltered them from [rain](https://www.britannica.com/science/rain) or in the centre of extensive continental regions. However, this would have been primarily before the evolution of angiosperms (flowering plants, the group to which most present-day plants, including those of deserts, belong). Only a few [primitive](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/primitive) plants, which may have been part of the ancient desert vegetation, occur in present-day deserts. One example is the bizarre conifer relative [welwitschia](https://www.britannica.com/plant/tumboa) in the [Namib](https://www.britannica.com/place/Namib) Desert of southwestern Africa. Welwitschia has only two leaves, which are leathery, straplike organs that emanate from the middle of a massive, mainly subterranean woody stem. These leaves grow perpetually from their bases and erode progressively at their ends. This desert also harbours several other plants and animals peculiarly adapted to the arid environment, suggesting that it might have a longer continuous history of arid conditions than most other deserts.

Desert floras and faunas initially evolved from ancestors in moister habitats, an evolution that occurred independently on each [continent](https://www.britannica.com/science/continent). However, a significant degree of commonality exists among the plant families that dominate different desert vegetations. This is due in part to [intrinsic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intrinsic) physiologic characteristics in some widespread desert families that preadapt the plants to an arid environment; it also is a result of plant migration occurring through chance [seed dispersal](https://www.britannica.com/science/seed-dispersal) among desert regions.

Such migration was particularly easy between northern and southern desert regions in Africa and in the Americas during intervals of drier climate that have occurred in the past two million years. This migration is reflected in close floristic similarities currently observed in these places. For example, the [creosote bush](https://www.britannica.com/plant/creosote-bush) ( _Larrea tridentata_), although now widespread and common in North American hot deserts, was probably a natural immigrant from [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America) as recently as the end of the last Ice Age about 11,700 years ago.

Deserts are varied and variable [environments](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environments), and it is impossible to arrive at a concise definition that satisfies every case. However, their most fundamental characteristic is a shortage of available moisture for plants, resulting from an imbalance between [precipitation](https://www.britannica.com/science/precipitation) and [evapotranspiration](https://www.britannica.com/science/evapotranspiration). This situation is [exacerbated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/exacerbated) by considerable variability in the timing of rainfall, low atmospheric [humidity](https://www.britannica.com/science/humidity), high daytime temperatures, and winds.

Average [annual](https://www.britannica.com/science/annual) precipitation ranges from almost zero in some South American coastal deserts and Libyan deserts to about 600 millimetres (24 inches) in deserts in [Madagascar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madagascar), although most recognized deserts have an annual rainfall below 400 millimetres. Some authorities consider 250 millimetres the upper limit for mean annual precipitation for true deserts, describing places with a mean annual rainfall of between 250 and 400 millimetres as semideserts. Regions this dry are barely arable and contribute to human food production only by providing [grazing](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/grazing) lands for livestock.

The arid conditions of the major desert areas result from their position in subtropical regions to either side of the moist equatorial belt. The [atmospheric circulation](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmospheric-circulation) pattern known as the [Hadley cell](https://www.britannica.com/science/Hadley-cell) plays an important role in desert [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology). In areas close to the Equator, where the amount of incoming [solar energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-energy) per unit surface area is greatest, air near the ground is heated, then rises, expands, and cools. This process leads to the condensation of moisture and to precipitation. At high levels in the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere), the risen air moves away from the equatorial [region](https://www.britannica.com/science/region-geography) to [descend](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/descend) eventually in the subtropics as it cools; it moves back toward the [Equator](https://www.britannica.com/place/Equator) at low altitudes, completing the Hadley cell circulation pattern. The air descending over the subtropics has already lost most of its moisture as [rain](https://www.britannica.com/science/rain) formed during its previous ascent near the Equator. As it descends, it becomes compressed and warmer, its [relative humidity](https://www.britannica.com/science/relative-humidity) falling further. (For further discussion of relative humidity, _see_ [biosphere: Humidity](https://www.britannica.com/science/biosphere/Environmental-conditions#ref70876).) Hot deserts occur in those regions to the north and south of the equatorial belt that lie beneath these descending, dry air masses. This pattern may be interrupted where local precipitation is increased, especially on the east sides of continents where winds blow onshore, carrying moisture picked up over the [ocean](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean). [Conversely](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Conversely), deserts may be found elsewhere, as in the lee of [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) ranges, where air is forced to rise, cool, and lose moisture as rain falling on the windward slopes.

Rainfall in deserts is usually meagre. In some cases several years may pass without rain; for example, at Cochones, [Chile](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chile), no rain fell at all in 45 consecutive years between 1919 and 1964. Usually, however, rain falls in deserts for at least a few days each year—typically 15 to 20 days. When precipitation occurs, it may be very heavy for short periods. For instance, 14 millimetres fell at Mashʾabe Sade, [Israel](https://www.britannica.com/place/Israel), in only seven minutes on October 5, 1979, and in southwestern [Madagascar](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madagascar) the entire annual rainfall commonly occurs as heavy showers falling within a single month. Such rainfall usually occurs only over small areas and results from local convectional cells, with more [widespread](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/widespread) frontal rain being restricted to the southern and northern fringes of deserts. In some local desert showers, the rain falling from clouds evaporates before it reaches the ground. Regions near the equatorial margins of hot deserts receive most of their rain in summer—June to August in the Northern Hemisphere and December to February in the Southern Hemisphere—while those near the temperate margins receive most of their rainfall in winter. Rain is particularly erratic and equally unlikely to occur in all seasons in intermediate regions.

In some deserts that are located near coasts, such as the [Namib](https://www.britannica.com/place/Namib) Desert of southwestern [Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Africa) and those of the west coasts of the Americas in [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state) and [Peru](https://www.britannica.com/place/Peru), [fog](https://www.britannica.com/science/fog) is an important source of moisture that is otherwise scarce. Moisture droplets settle from the fog onto plants and then may drip onto the soil or be absorbed directly by plant shoots. [Dew](https://www.britannica.com/science/dew) also may be significant, although not in deserts in from the central parts of continents where atmospheric humidity is consistently very low.

In most desert regions atmospheric humidity is usually too low to permit [formation](https://www.britannica.com/science/biome) of fog or dew to any significant extent. Potential [evaporation](https://www.britannica.com/science/evaporation) rates (the rate of evaporation that would occur if water were continually present) are correspondingly high, typically 2,500 to 3,500 millimetres per year, with as much as 4,262 millimetres potential evaporation per year having been recorded in [Death Valley](https://www.britannica.com/place/Death-Valley) in California. [Winds](https://www.britannica.com/science/wind) are not unusually strong or frequent in comparison with [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) environments, but the general lack of vegetation in deserts [exacerbates](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/exacerbates) the effect of wind at ground level. Winds can induce the [erosion](https://www.britannica.com/science/erosion-geology) of fine materials and the evaporation of moisture and thereby help determine which plants survive in the desert.

Hot deserts, as their name indicates, experience very high [temperatures](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature) by day, especially in summer. Absolute maximum air temperatures in all hot deserts exceed 40 °C (104 °F), and the highest value recorded, in [Libya](https://www.britannica.com/place/Libya), is 58 °C (136.4 °F). The [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature) of the soil surface can rise even beyond that of the air, with [values](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/values) as high as 78 °C (172 °F) recorded in the [Sahara](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sahara-desert-Africa). However, night temperatures can fall dramatically, because the same lack of cloud cover that admits high levels of incoming [solar radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-radiation) during the day also allows rapid loss of energy through long-wave radiation to the sky at night. Absolute minimum temperatures, except in desert areas close to the sea, are generally below the [freezing point](https://www.britannica.com/science/freezing-point). Typical mean annual temperatures are between 20 °C (68 °F) and 25 °C (77 °F).

Temperate or [cold deserts](https://www.britannica.com/science/cold-desert) occur in temperate regions at higher latitudes—and therefore colder temperatures—than those at which hot deserts are found. These dry environments are caused by either remoteness from the [coast](https://www.britannica.com/science/coast), which results in low atmospheric humidity from a lack of onshore winds, or the presence of high mountains separating the desert from the coast. The largest area of temperate desert lies in [Central Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/history-of-Central-Asia-102306), with smaller areas in western [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America), southeastern [South America](https://www.britannica.com/place/South-America), and southern [Australia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Australia). While they experience lower temperatures than the more typical hot deserts, temperate deserts are similar in aridity and consequent environmental features including landforms and soils.

The peculiar climatic [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment) of deserts has favoured the development of certain characteristic landforms. Stony plains called [regs](https://www.britannica.com/science/reg) or [gibber](https://www.britannica.com/science/gibber) plains are widespread, their surface covered by [desert pavement](https://www.britannica.com/science/desert-pavement) consisting of coarse gravel and stones coated with a [patina](https://www.britannica.com/science/desert-varnish) of dark “desert varnish” (a glossy dark surface cover consisting of oxides of iron). Rocky, boulder-strewn plateaus cut by dry, usually steep-sided valleys called [wadis](https://www.britannica.com/science/arroyo) are also found in deserts in many parts of the world. The local topographic and microclimatic variations produced by this rugged surface, and the opportunities for runoff—and in a few places surface accumulation—of rainwater, are important in providing localized habitats for plants and animals. Large areas of loose, mobile sand provide the harshest and poorest of the major desert [habitat](https://www.britannica.com/science/habitat-biology) types.

Desert [soils](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil) are mainly immature, weakly developed in terms of their soil profiles, and mostly alkaline. Sands, sandy or gravelly loams, shallow stony soils, and alluvium (material deposited by rivers and streams) and scree-derived deposits (rocky material at the base of cliffs) predominate. Although almost always dry, these soils may support well-developed microbial [communities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities), particularly in association with roots. Domestic animals, however, can have a [deleterious](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/deleterious) impact by trampling and compacting the soil; this activity can reduce the infiltration of water and damage vegetation, leading to erosion and redistribution of soil materials.

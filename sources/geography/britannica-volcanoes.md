- What is a volcano?







A volcano is a vent in the Earth's crust from which molten rock, hot rock fragments, and gases erupt. These eruptions are driven by heat convection within the Earth's interior, where molten rock, or magma, forms and rises to the surface. Volcanoes are often associated with tectonic plate boundaries, where one plate subducts beneath another, causing rock to melt and form magma.



Volcanic activity can lead to various hazards, including lava flows, pyroclastic flows, ash falls, and mudflows. However, volcanic processes also play a crucial role in the evolution of Earth's oceans, atmosphere, and continents, and volcanic ash can create fertile soils. There are many types of volcanic landforms, with stratovolcanoes and shield volcanoes being among the most common.

- How do volcanoes form?







Volcanoes form when heat from Earth's interior causes magma—molten rock—to rise to the surface. This process is largely driven by convection, where heated magma moves upwards. Most volcanoes are found along the boundaries of tectonic plates. When one plate slides beneath another (subduction), water is released, creating heat that melts surrounding rock into magma. This buoyant magma rises and can collect in chambers. When the pressure within these chambers exceeds the weight of the rock above, magma erupts through vents, forming volcanoes. Some volcanoes also form over hotspots, areas where magma plumes rise from deep within the Earth's mantle.

- What are the different types of volcanoes?







Volcanoes can be classified into several types based on their shape and eruption style. The most common types include:



  - **Stratovolcanoes (Composite Volcanoes):** These are steep, symmetrical cones built by alternating layers of lava flows and pyroclastic material. Examples include Mount Fuji and Mount St. Helens.
  - **Shield Volcanoes:** These are large, dome-shaped mountains formed by fluid lava flows, resembling a warrior's shield. They typically have gentle slopes and are composed of basalt. Kilauea in Hawaii is a prominent example.
  - **Cinder Cones:** These are smaller, conical hills formed from pyroclastic rock fragments (cinders) ejected during explosive eruptions. They often have a bowl-shaped crater at the summit.
  - **Lava Domes:** These are steep mounds of highly viscous lava that piles up over its vent without flowing far. They are often composed of andesites, dacites, or rhyolites.
  - **Calderas:** These are large, bowl-shaped depressions formed by the collapse of a volcanic cone after a major eruption empties the underlying magma chamber.

- What causes a volcanic eruption?







Volcanic eruptions are caused by the movement of heat beneath Earth's surface, primarily through convection of magma. This molten rock, formed from the partial melting of the mantle and crust, rises and erupts at the surface through volcanoes. Most volcanoes are linked to [plate tectonic](https://www.britannica.com/science/plate-tectonics) activity, where subducting plates release water, generating heat that melts rock into magma. This magma collects in chambers, and when internal pressure exceeds the weight of overlying rock, it forces its way through cracks to the surface as lava. Some volcanoes also form at [hotspots](https://www.britannica.com/science/hotspot-geology) within tectonic plates. The explosiveness of an eruption depends on factors like magma viscosity, gas content, and the rate of decompression.

- What is magma, and how is it different from lava?







Magma is molten or partially molten rock found beneath the Earth's surface, from which igneous rocks form. It is composed of silicate liquid, and may also contain suspended crystals and dissolved gases. Lava, on the other hand, is magma that has erupted onto the Earth's surface.



The primary difference lies in their location: magma is underground, while lava is on the surface. The properties of magma, such as its silica content and temperature, influence its viscosity, which in turn affects how it flows as lava. For example, basaltic magma, with lower silica content, results in more fluid lava flows like pahoehoe and aa. Conversely, more siliceous magmas, like rhyolitic, are more viscous and are associated with explosive eruptions.

- How do volcanoes affect the environment and people living nearby?







Volcanic eruptions pose significant hazards to both the environment and nearby populations. Ash falls can lead to roof collapses and widespread crop failure, potentially causing famine and disease, particularly in developing regions. Volcanic activity can also alter weather patterns and global climate due to the injection of ash and sulfur aerosols into the stratosphere, which can reflect solar radiation and cause cooling.



While volcanic eruptions can be destructive, they also contribute to the formation of new soils and landscapes over time. The atmosphere and hydrosphere are believed to have largely originated from volcanic emanations. However, the immediate dangers include lava flows, explosions, toxic gas clouds, pyroclastic flows, mudflows, and tsunamis. Even non-erupting volcanoes can be dangerous due to the potential for sudden eruptions or deadly mudslides (lahars).

**volcano**, vent in the [crust](https://www.britannica.com/science/crust-mantle-model) of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth) or another [planet](https://www.britannica.com/science/planet) or [satellite](https://www.britannica.com/science/satellite), from which issue eruptions of molten [rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology), hot rock fragments, and hot [gases](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter). A [volcanic eruption](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcanic-eruption) is an awesome display of Earth’s power. Yet, while eruptions are spectacular to watch, they can cause disastrous loss of life and property, especially in densely populated regions of the world. Sometimes beginning with an accumulation of [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter)-rich [magma](https://www.britannica.com/science/magma-rock) (molten underground rock) in reservoirs near Earth’s surface, they can be preceded by emissions of [steam](https://www.britannica.com/science/steam) and gas from small vents in the ground. Swarms of small [earthquakes](https://www.britannica.com/science/earthquake-geology), which may be caused by a rising plug of dense, viscous magma oscillating against a sheath of more-permeable magma, may also signal volcanic eruptions, especially explosive ones. In some cases, magma rises in conduits to the surface as a thin and fluid [lava](https://www.britannica.com/science/lava-volcanic-ejecta), either flowing out continuously or shooting straight up in glowing fountains or curtains. In other cases, entrapped gases tear the magma into shreds and hurl viscous clots of lava into the air. In more violent eruptions, the magma conduit is cored out by an explosive blast, and [solid fragments are ejected](https://www.britannica.com/science/ejecta-volcanism) in a great cloud of ash-laden gas that rises tens of thousands of metres into the air. One feared phenomenon accompanying some explosive eruptions is the [_nuée ardente_](https://www.britannica.com/science/nuee-ardente), or [pyroclastic flow](https://www.britannica.com/science/pyroclastic-flow), a fluidized mixture of hot gas and incandescent particles that sweeps down a volcano’s flanks, incinerating everything in its path. Great destruction also can result when ash collects on a high snowfield or [glacier](https://www.britannica.com/science/glacier), melting large quantities of ice into a flood that can rush down a volcano’s slopes as an unstoppable mudflow. Strictly speaking, the term _volcano_ means the vent from which magma and other substances erupt to the surface, but it can also refer to the landform created by the accumulation of solidified [lava](https://www.britannica.com/science/lava-volcanic-ejecta) and volcanic debris near the vent. One can say, for example, that large lava flows erupt from [Mauna Loa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mauna-Loa) volcano in Hawaii, referring here to the vent; but one can also say that Mauna Loa is a gently sloping volcano of great size, the reference in this case being to the landform. Volcanic [landforms](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-landform) have evolved over time as a result of repeated volcanic activity. Mauna Loa typifies a shield volcano, which is a huge, gently sloping landform built up of many eruptions of fluid lava. [Mount Fuji](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Fuji) in [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan) is an entirely different formation. With its striking steep slopes built up of layers of ash and lava, Mount Fuji is a classic stratovolcano. [Iceland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iceland) provides fine examples of volcanic [plateaus](https://www.britannica.com/science/plateau-landform), while the seafloor around Iceland provides excellent examples of submarine volcanic structures.

[volcanism and plate tectonics](https://cdn.britannica.com/53/4953-050-E6291658/subduction-zones-Stratovolcanoes-Earth-plate-margins-activity.jpg) Stratovolcanoes tend to form at subduction zones, or convergent plate margins, where an oceanic plate slides beneath a continental plate and contributes to the rise of magma to the surface. At rift zones, or divergent margins, shield volcanoes tend to form as two oceanic plates pull slowly apart and magma effuses upward through the gap. Volcanoes are not generally found at strike-slip zones, where two plates slide laterally past each other. “Hot spot” volcanoes may form where plumes of lava rise from deep within the mantle to Earth's crust far from any plate margins.(more)

Volcanoes figure prominently in the mythology of many peoples who have learned to live with eruptions, but science was late in recognizing the important role of [volcanism](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcanism) in the evolution of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth). As late as 1768, the first edition of the _Encyclopædia Britannica_ gave voice to a common misconception by defining volcanoes as “burning mountains, which probably are made up of sulphur and some other matter proper to ferment with it, and take fire.” Today geologists agree that volcanism is a profound process resulting from the thermal evolution of planetary bodies. Heat does not easily escape from large bodies such as Earth by the processes of [conduction](https://www.britannica.com/science/thermal-conduction) or [radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/radiation). Instead, heat is transferred from Earth’s interior largely by [convection](https://www.britannica.com/science/convection)—that is, the partial melting of Earth’s [crust and mantle](https://www.britannica.com/science/crust-mantle-model) and the buoyant rise of magma to the surface. Volcanoes are the surface sign of this thermal process. Their roots reach deep inside Earth, and their fruits are hurled high into the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere).

Volcanoes are closely associated with [plate tectonic](https://www.britannica.com/science/plate-tectonics) activity. Most volcanoes, such as those of Japan and Iceland, occur on the margins of the enormous solid rocky plates that make up Earth’s surface. Other volcanoes, such as those of the Hawaiian Islands, occur in the middle of a plate, providing important evidence as to the direction and rate of plate motion.

The study of volcanoes and their products is known as [volcanology](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcanology), but these phenomena are not the realm of any single scientific discipline. Rather, they are studied by many scientists from several specialties: [geophysicists](https://www.britannica.com/science/geophysics) and [geochemists](https://www.britannica.com/science/geochemistry), who probe the deep roots of volcanoes and monitor signs of future eruptions; geologists, who decipher prehistoric volcanic activity and infer the likely nature of future eruptions; biologists, who learn how [plants](https://www.britannica.com/plant/plant) and [animals](https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal) colonize recently erupted volcanic rocks; and meteorologists, who determine the effects of volcanic dust and gases on the atmosphere, [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather), and [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology).

Clearly the destructive potential of volcanoes is tremendous. But the risk to people living nearby can be reduced significantly by assessing volcanic hazards, monitoring volcanic activity and forecasting eruptions, and instituting procedures for evacuating populations. In addition, volcanism affects humankind in beneficial ways. Volcanism provides beautiful scenery, fertile [soils](https://www.britannica.com/science/soil), valuable [mineral](https://www.britannica.com/science/mineral-chemical-compound) deposits, and [geothermal energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/geothermal-energy). Over geologic time, volcanoes recycle Earth’s [hydrosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrosphere) and atmosphere.

# [Lava](https://www.britannica.com/science/lava-volcanic-ejecta), gas, and other hazards

The list of hazards associated with volcanic eruptions is long and varied: [lava](https://www.britannica.com/science/lava-volcanic-ejecta) flows, explosions, toxic [gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter) clouds, ash falls, [pyroclastic flows](https://www.britannica.com/science/pyroclastic-flow), [avalanches](https://www.britannica.com/science/avalanche), [tsunamis](https://www.britannica.com/science/tsunami), and [mudflows](https://www.britannica.com/science/mudflow). In addition to these immediate dangers, volcanic activity produces secondary effects such as property damage, crop loss, and perhaps changes to [weather](https://www.britannica.com/science/weather) and [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology). These hazards and long-term effects are described in this section.

## [Lava flows](https://www.britannica.com/science/lava-flow)

The root zone of volcanoes is found some 70 to 200 km (40 to 120 miles) below the surface of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth). There, in Earth’s upper mantle, temperatures are high enough to melt [rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology) and form [magma](https://www.britannica.com/science/magma-rock). At these depths, magma is generally less dense than the solid rocks surrounding and overlying it, and so it rises toward the surface by the buoyant force of gravity. In some cases, as in the undersea zones where the tectonic plates of Earth’s crust are separating, [magma](https://www.britannica.com/science/magma-rock) may move directly up to the surface through fissures that reach as deep as the mantle. In other cases, it collects in large underground reservoirs known as magma chambers before erupting to the surface. Molten rock that reaches the surface is called lava.

Most magma formed by partial melting of the mantle is basaltic in composition, but, as it ascends, it assimilates silica, sodium, and potassium from the surrounding host [rocks](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology). [Volcanic rocks](https://www.britannica.com/science/extrusive-rock) found where magma erupts to the surface are classified into four major types, or “clans”— [basalt](https://www.britannica.com/science/basalt), [andesite](https://www.britannica.com/science/andesite), [dacite](https://www.britannica.com/science/dacite), and [rhyolite](https://www.britannica.com/science/rhyolite-rock). These rocks are ranked, as can be seen in the table, mainly by their [silica](https://www.britannica.com/science/silica) content, which ranges from approximately 50 percent for basalt to approximately 75 percent for rhyolite. As silica content increases, rock types generally become more viscous.

If the vast, unseen undersea lava flows of the [oceanic ridge](https://www.britannica.com/science/oceanic-ridge) system are considered, lava flows are the most common products of Earth’s volcanoes. There are two major types of lava flow, referred to around the world by their Hawaiian names: [pahoehoe](https://www.britannica.com/science/pahoehoe), a more fluid flow with a smooth to ropy surface; and [aa](https://www.britannica.com/science/aa-lava-flow) (or a’a), a more viscous flow whose surface is covered by thick, jumbled piles of loose, sharp blocks. Both types have the same chemical composition; the difference seems to be in the eruptive temperature and the speed of movement of the flow. As much as 99 percent of the island of [Hawaii](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hawaii-island-Hawaii) is composed of aa and pahoehoe flows. Indeed, [Kilauea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kilauea) volcano has erupted continuously since 1983, its lava flows covering more than 100 square km (40 square miles) of land and adding more than 2 square km (0.8 square mile) to the island where the lava has poured into the ocean. In the Mediterranean region, [Mount Etna](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Etna) has issued lava more than 150 times since its first recorded activity in 1500 bc.

Massive volcanic explosions are caused by the rapid expansion of gases, which in turn can be triggered by the sudden depressurization of a shallow hydrothermal system or gas-charged [magma](https://www.britannica.com/science/magma-rock) body or by the rapid mixing of magma with groundwater. The ash, cinders, hot fragments, and bombs thrown out in these explosions are the major products observed in volcanic eruptions around the world. These solid products are classified by size. Volcanic dust is the finest, usually about the consistency of [flour](https://www.britannica.com/topic/flour). [Volcanic ash](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcanic-ash) is also fine but more gritty, with particles up to the size of grains of [rice](https://www.britannica.com/plant/wild-rice). [Cinders](https://www.britannica.com/science/cinder), sometimes called scoriae, are the next in size; these coarse fragments can range from 2 mm (0.08 inch) up to about 64 mm (2.5 inches). Fragments larger than 64 mm are called either [blocks](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcanic-block) or [bombs](https://www.britannica.com/science/bomb-volcanic-ejecta). Volcanic blocks are usually older rock broken by the explosive opening of a new vent. Large blocks ejected in such explosions have been hurled as far as 20 km (12 miles) from the vent. Volcanic bombs, in contrast, are generally incandescent and soft during their flight. Some bombs take on strange, twisted shapes as they spin through the air. Others have a cracked and separated crust that has cooled and hardened in flight; they are called “breadcrust bombs.”

A directed blast in which one side of a volcanic cone fails, as happened at [Mount St. Helens](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Saint-Helens) in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) in 1980, can cause destruction over several hundred square kilometres on the failed flank of the volcano. This is especially true if the blast cloud is heavily laden with fragmental debris and becomes dense and fluidized. It then takes on characteristics similar to a pyroclastic flow.

## [Pyroclastic flows](https://www.britannica.com/science/pyroclastic-flow)

[Pyroclastic flows](https://www.britannica.com/science/pyroclastic-flow) are the most dangerous and destructive aspect of explosive volcanism. Variously called _nuées ardentes_ (“glowing clouds”), glowing avalanches, or ash flows, they occur in many sizes and types, but their common characteristic is a fluidized emulsion of volcanic particles, eruption gases, and entrapped air, resulting in a flow of sufficiently low viscosity to be very mobile and of sufficiently high density to hug the ground surface. A pyroclastic flow can pour over the lip of an erupting vent, or it may form when an ash column becomes too dense to continue rising and falls back to the ground. In major [caldera](https://www.britannica.com/science/caldera) collapses associated with explosive volcanoes ( _see below_ [Calderas](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcano/Calderas#ref24468)), huge pyroclastic flows may issue from the ring fractures as the caldera block subsides.

Pyroclastic flows can move at speeds up to 160 km (100 miles) per hour and have temperatures ranging from 100 to 700 °C (212 to 1,300 °F). They sweep away and incinerate nearly everything in their path. Smaller pyroclastic flows are often confined to [valleys](https://www.britannica.com/science/valley). Large pyroclastic flows may spread out as a blanket deposit across many hundreds or even thousands of square kilometres around a major caldera collapse. During the past two million years, the area around [Yellowstone National Park](https://www.britannica.com/place/Yellowstone-National-Park) in the western United States has undergone three major caldera collapses involving pyroclastic eruptions of 280 to 2,500 cubic km (67 to 600 cubic miles) of ash flows and ash falls.

## Gas clouds

Even beyond the limit of explosive destruction, the hot, ash-laden gas clouds associated with an explosive eruption can scorch vegetation and kill animals and people by [suffocation](https://www.britannica.com/science/suffocation). Gas clouds emitted from [fumarole](https://www.britannica.com/science/fumarole) s ( [volcanic gas](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcanic-gas) vents) or from the sudden overturn of a [crater lake](https://www.britannica.com/science/crater-lake-geology) may contain suffocating or poisonous gases such as [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide), [carbon monoxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-monoxide), [hydrogen sulfide](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen-sulfide), and [sulfur dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur-dioxide). At [Lake Nyos](https://www.britannica.com/place/Lake-Nyos), a crater [lake](https://www.britannica.com/science/lake) in [Cameroon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cameroon), West Africa, more than 1,700 people were killed by a [sudden release of carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/event/Lake-Nyos-disaster) in August 1986. Scientists theorize that carbon dioxide of volcanic origin had been seeping into the lake, perhaps for centuries, and had accumulated in its deep layers. It is thought that some disturbance, such as a large landslide into the lake, could have triggered the outburst of gas, creating an effervescence that stirred the lake and started the degassing.

The most common volcanic gases are [water vapour](https://www.britannica.com/science/water-vapor), [carbon dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-dioxide), [sulfur dioxide](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur-dioxide), and [hydrogen sulfide](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen-sulfide). Small quantities of other volatile elements and compounds also are present, such as [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen), [helium](https://www.britannica.com/science/helium-chemical-element), [nitrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/nitrogen), [hydrogen chloride](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen-chloride), hydrogen fluoride, and [mercury](https://www.britannica.com/science/mercury-chemical-element). The specific gaseous compounds released from [magma](https://www.britannica.com/science/magma-rock) depend on the temperature, pressure, and overall composition of the volatile elements present. The amount of available [oxygen](https://www.britannica.com/science/oxygen) is of critical importance in determining which volatile gases are present. When oxygen is lacking, [methane](https://www.britannica.com/science/methane), hydrogen, and hydrogen sulfide are chemically stable, but when hot volcanic gases mix with atmospheric gases, water vapour, carbon dioxide, and sulfur dioxide are stable.

Some volcanic gases are less soluble in magma than others and therefore separate at higher pressures. Studies at [Kilauea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kilauea) in [Hawaii](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hawaii-state) indicate that carbon dioxide begins to separate from its parent magma at depths of about 40 km (25 miles), whereas most of the sulfur gases and water are not released until the magma has nearly reached the surface. Fumaroles near [Halemaumau](https://www.britannica.com/place/Halemaumau-Crater) Crater at Kilauea’s summit are rich in carbon dioxide that leaks from the magma chamber located 3 to 4 km (1.9 to 2.5 miles) beneath the surface. Fumaroles on the rift zones of Kilauea, however, are richer in water vapour and sulfur because much of the carbon dioxide leaks away at the summit before the magma is intruded into the [rift zones](https://www.britannica.com/science/rift-valley).

### What is an ocean?

An ocean is a continuous body of [salt water](https://www.britannica.com/science/seawater) that is contained in an enormous basin on Earth’s surface. The major oceans and their marginal seas cover nearly 71 percent of Earth’s surface, with an average depth of 3,688 metres (12,100 feet).

### What are the five oceans of Earth?

The five major oceans are the [Pacific](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean), [Atlantic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean), [Indian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indian-Ocean), [Arctic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arctic-Ocean), and [Southern](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southern-Ocean) oceans.

### Which is the largest ocean on the Earth?

The [Pacific](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean) is the largest ocean in both surface area and volume. The [Atlantic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean) is the next largest, and the [Arctic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arctic-Ocean) is the smallest.

### How much of the water found on Earth exists in the oceans?

By volume, 97.957 percent of the [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) on Earth exists as oceanic water and associated sea ice. Earth is unique in the solar system because its mean temperature allows water to exist on Earth in all three phases—solid, liquid, and gaseous—with the liquid phase predominating.

When viewed from space, the predominance of Earth’s oceans is readily apparent. The oceans and their marginal seas cover nearly 71 percent of Earth’s surface, with an average depth of 3,688 metres (12,100 feet). The exposed land occupies the remaining 29 percent of the planetary surface and has a mean elevation of about 840 metres (approximately 2,755 feet). Actually, all the elevated land could be hidden under the oceans and Earth reduced to a smooth sphere that would be completely covered by a continuous layer of seawater more than 2,600 metres (8,530 feet) deep. This is known as the sphere depth of the oceans and serves to [underscore](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/underscore) the abundance of [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) on Earth’s surface.

Earth is unique in the [solar system](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-system) because of its distance from the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun) and its period of rotation. These combine to subject Earth to a [solar radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-radiation) level that maintains the planet at a mean surface [temperature](https://www.britannica.com/science/temperature) of about 14–15 °C (57.2–59 °F). Mean surface temperature varies little over annual and night-day cycles. This mean temperature allows water to exist on Earth in all three of its phases— [solid](https://www.britannica.com/science/solid-state-of-matter), [liquid](https://www.britannica.com/science/liquid-state-of-matter), and [gaseous](https://www.britannica.com/science/gas-state-of-matter). No other [planet](https://www.britannica.com/science/planet) in the solar system has this feature. The liquid phase predominates on Earth. By volume, 97.957 percent of the water on the planet exists as oceanic water and associated [sea ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-ice). The gaseous phase and droplet water in the [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere) [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) 0.001 percent. Fresh water in [lakes](https://www.britannica.com/science/lake) and [streams](https://www.britannica.com/science/river) makes up 0.036 percent, while [groundwater](https://www.britannica.com/science/groundwater) is 10 times more abundant at 0.365 percent. [Glaciers](https://www.britannica.com/science/glacier) and [ice](https://www.britannica.com/science/ice) caps constitute 1.641 percent of Earth’s total water volume.

Each of the above is considered to be a [reservoir](https://www.britannica.com/technology/reservoir) of water. Water continuously circulates between these reservoirs in what is called the [hydrologic cycle](https://www.britannica.com/science/water-cycle), which is driven by [energy](https://www.britannica.com/science/energy) from the Sun. [Evaporation](https://www.britannica.com/science/evaporation), [precipitation](https://www.britannica.com/science/precipitation), movement of the atmosphere, and the downhill flow of [river](https://www.britannica.com/science/river) water, glaciers, and groundwater keep water in motion between the reservoirs and maintain the hydrologic cycle.

The large range of volumes in these [reservoirs](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/reservoirs) and the rates at which water cycles between them combine to create important conditions on Earth. If small changes occur in the rate at which water is cycled into or out of a reservoir, the volume of a reservoir changes. These volume changes may be relatively large and rapid in a small reservoir or small and slow in a large reservoir. A small percentage change in the volume of the oceans may produce a large proportional change in the land-ice reservoir, thereby promoting [glacial](https://www.britannica.com/science/glacial-stage) and interglacial stages. The rate at which water enters or leaves a reservoir divided into the reservoir volume determines the residence time of water in the reservoir. The residence time of water in a reservoir, in turn, governs many of the properties of that reservoir.

## Relative distribution of the oceans

![Boundaries of the world's oceans and seas](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/3163-050-FD3BC1F5/Boundaries-world-oceans-seas.jpg)

[Boundaries of the world's oceans and seas](https://cdn.britannica.com/63/3163-050-FD3BC1F5/Boundaries-world-oceans-seas.jpg)(more)

Earth possesses one “world ocean.” However, those conducting oceanic research generally recognize the existence of five major oceans: the [Pacific](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pacific-Ocean), [Atlantic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean), [Indian](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indian-Ocean), [Southern](https://www.britannica.com/place/Southern-Ocean), and [Arctic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arctic-Ocean) oceans. Arbitrary boundaries separate these bodies of water, but they are largely defined by the [continents](https://www.britannica.com/science/continent) that frame them. In the Southern Hemisphere, however, 60° [S](https://www.britannica.com/science/salinity) latitude, which corresponds to the approximate position of the [Antarctic Circumpolar Current](https://www.britannica.com/place/Antarctic-Circumpolar-Current), separates the Southern Ocean from the southern portions of the Pacific, Atlantic, and Indian oceans. Many subdivisions can be made to distinguish the limits of seas and [gulfs](https://www.britannica.com/science/gulf-coastal-feature) that have historical, political, and sometimes ecological significance. However, water properties, [ocean currents](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean-current), and biological populations are not constrained by these boundaries. Indeed, many researchers do not recognize them either.

If area-volume analyses of the oceans are to be made, then boundaries must be established to separate individual regions. In 1921 Erwin Kossina, a German geographer, published tables giving the distribution of oceanic water with depth for the oceans and [adjacent](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent) seas. This work was updated in 1966 by American geologist H.W. Menard and American oceanographer S.M. Smith. The latter only slightly changed the numbers derived by Kossina. This was remarkable, since the original effort relied entirely on the sparse depth measurements accumulated by individual wire soundings, while the more recent work had the benefit of acoustic depth soundings collected since the 1920s. This type of analysis, called [hypsometry](https://www.britannica.com/science/hypsometry), allows quantification of the [surface](https://www.britannica.com/science/surface-water-mass) area distribution of the oceans and their marginal seas with depth.

The distribution of oceanic surface area with 5° increments of [latitude](https://www.britannica.com/science/latitude) shows that the distribution of land and water on Earth’s surface is markedly different in the Northern and Southern hemispheres. The Southern Hemisphere may be called the water hemisphere, while the Northern Hemisphere is the land hemisphere. This is especially true in the [temperate](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/temperate) latitudes.

This asymmetry of land and water distribution between the Northern and Southern hemispheres makes the two hemispheres behave very differently in response to the annual variation in [solar radiation](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-radiation) received by Earth. The Southern Hemisphere shows only a small change in surface temperature from summer to winter at temperate latitudes. This variation is controlled primarily by the ocean’s response to seasonal changes in heating and cooling. The Northern Hemisphere has one change in surface temperature controlled by its oceanic area and another controlled by its land area. In the temperate latitudes of the Northern Hemisphere, the land is much warmer than the oceanic area in summer and much colder in winter. This situation creates large-scale seasonal changes in [atmospheric circulation](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmospheric-circulation) and [climate](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology) in the Northern Hemisphere that are not found in the Southern Hemisphere.

# Major subdivisions of the oceans

If the volume of an ocean is divided by its surface area, the mean depth is obtained. Even without including its [marginal](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/marginal) seas, the Pacific is the largest ocean in both surface area and volume, the Atlantic is next, and the [Arctic](https://www.britannica.com/place/Arctic) is the smallest. The Atlantic exhibits the largest change in surface area and volume when its marginal seas are subtracted. This indicates that the Atlantic has the greatest area of bordering seas, many of which are shallow.

Surface area, volume, and average depth of oceans

![hypsographic curve](https://cdn.britannica.com/65/3165-004-9FD73652/curve-Hypsographic-surface-area-elevation-Earth-depth.jpg)

[hypsographic curve](https://cdn.britannica.com/65/3165-004-9FD73652/curve-Hypsographic-surface-area-elevation-Earth-depth.jpg) Hypsographic curve showing how the surface area of Earth is distributed with elevation and depth. (more)

Hypsometry can show how the area of each ocean or marginal sea changes as depth changes. A special curve known as a [hypsometric](https://www.britannica.com/science/hypsometric-curve), or hypsographic, curve can be drawn that portrays how the surface area of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth) is distributed with elevation and depth. This curve has been drawn to represent the total Earth and all of its oceans; likewise, curves can be constructed for each individual ocean and sea. The average depth of the world’s oceans, 3,688 metres (12,100 feet), and the average elevation of the land, 840 metres (2,756 feet), are indicated. The highest point on land, [Mount Everest](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Everest) (8,850 metres \[29,035 feet\]), and the deepest point in the ocean, located in the [Mariana Trench](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mariana-Trench) (11,034 metres \[36,201 feet\]), mark the upper and lower limits of the curve, respectively. Since this curve is drawn on a grid of elevation versus Earth’s area, the area under the curve covering the 29.2 percent of Earth’s surface that is above [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level) is the volume of land above sea level. Similarly, the area between sea level and the curve depicting the remaining 70.8 percent of Earth’s surface below sea level represents the volume of [water](https://www.britannica.com/science/water) contained in the oceans.

Portions of this curve describe the area of Earth’s surface that exists between [elevation](https://www.britannica.com/science/altitude-angular) or depth [increments](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/increments). On land, little of Earth’s total area—only about 4 percent—is at elevations above 2,000 metres (about 6,560 feet). Most of the land, 25.3 percent of the total Earth, is between 0 and 2,000 metres. About 13.6 percent of the total land area is at higher elevations, with 86.4 percent between 0 and 2,000 metres when the areas are determined relative to land area only. In the oceans the percentages of the area devoted to depth increments yield information about the typical structure and shape of the oceanic basins. The small depth increment of 0–200 metres (656 feet) occupies about 5.4 percent of Earth’s total area or 7.6 percent of the oceans’ area. This approximates the world’s area of [continental shelves](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-shelf), the shallow [flat](https://www.britannica.com/science/playa) borderlands of the continents that have been alternately covered by the oceans during interglacial stages and uncovered during glacial periods ( _see_ [continental margin](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-margin)).

At depths between 200 and 2,000 metres, an area only slightly larger—6.02 percent of Earth’s total area or 8.5 percent of the oceans’ area—is found. These depths are related to the regions of the oceans that have very steep slopes where depth increases rapidly. These are the [continental slope](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-slope) regions that mark the true edge of the continental landmasses. Marginal seas of moderate depths and the tops of [seamounts](https://www.britannica.com/science/seamount), however, add their area to these depth zones when all the oceans are considered. The majority of the oceanic area lies between 4,000 and 5,000 metres (about 13,100 and 16,400 feet).

[continental margin](https://cdn.britannica.com/36/139436-050-B2F2E0B4/pitch-continental-shelf-slope-way-transition-region.jpg) The broad, gentle pitch of the continental shelf gives way to the relatively steep continental slope. The more gradual transition to the abyssal plain is a sediment-filled region called the continental rise. The continental shelf, slope, and rise are collectively called the continental margin.(more)

The [continental shelf](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-shelf) [region](https://www.britannica.com/science/region-geography) varies immensely from place to place. The seaward boundary of the continental shelf historically is determined by the 100-fathom, or 200-metre, depth [contour](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contour). However, 85 fathoms, or 170 metres \[about 560 feet\], is a closer approximation. The true boundary at any given location is marked by a rapid change in slope of the seafloor known as the [shelf break](https://www.britannica.com/science/shelf-break). This change in slope may be nearly at the coastline in areas where crustal plates converge, as along the west [coast](https://www.britannica.com/science/coast) of North and South America, or it may be located more than 1,000 km (about 620 miles) seaward of the coast, as off the north coast of [Siberia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Siberia). The average width of the shelf is about 75 km (about 45 miles), and the shelf has an average slope of about 0.01°, a slope that is barely discernible to the [human eye](https://www.britannica.com/science/human-eye). Seaward of the shelf break, the continental slope is [inclined](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/inclined) by about 4°.

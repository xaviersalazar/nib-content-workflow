### Who first proposed the idea of plate tectonics?

German meteorologist [Alfred Wegener](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alfred-Wegener) is often credited as the first to develop a theory of plate tectonics, in the form of [continental drift](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-drift-geology). Bringing together a large mass of geologic and paleontological data, Wegener postulated that throughout most of geologic time there was only one continent, which he called [Pangea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pangea), and the breakup of this continent heralded Earth’s current continental configuration as the continent-sized parts began to move away from one another. (Scientists discovered later that Pangea fragmented early in the [Jurassic Period](https://www.britannica.com/science/Jurassic-Period).) Wegener presented the idea of continental drift and some of the supporting evidence in a lecture in 1912, followed by his major published work, The Origin of Continents and Oceans (1915).

### What is the cause of plate tectonics?

Although this has yet to be proven with certainty, most geologists and geophysicists agree that plate movement is caused by the convection (that is, heat transfer resulting from the movement of a heated fluid) of magma in Earth’s interior. The heat source is thought to be the decay of radioactive elements. How this convection propels the plates is poorly understood. Some geologists argue that upwelling [magma](https://www.britannica.com/science/magma-rock) at spreading centres pushes the plates, whereas others argue that the weight of a portion of a subducting plate (one that is forced beneath another) may pull the rest of the plate along.

### What is the Ring of Fire, and where is it?

The [Ring of Fire](https://www.britannica.com/place/Ring-of-Fire) is a long horseshoe-shaped earthquake-prone belt of volcanoes and tectonic plate boundaries that fringes the Pacific Ocean basin. For much of its 40,000-km (24,900-mile) length, the belt follows chains of island arcs such as [Tonga](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tonga) and [Vanuatu](https://www.britannica.com/place/Vanuatu), the [Indonesian archipelago](https://www.britannica.com/place/Indonesia), the [Philippines](https://www.britannica.com/place/Philippines), [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan), the [Kuril Islands](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kuril-Islands), and the [Aleutians](https://www.britannica.com/place/Aleutian-Islands), as well as other arc-shaped features, such as the western coast of [North America](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-America) and the [Andes Mountains](https://www.britannica.com/place/Andes-Mountains).

### Why are there tectonic plates?

Earth’s hard surface (the [lithosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/lithosphere)) can be thought of as a skin that rests and slides upon a semi-molten layer of rock called the [asthenosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/asthenosphere). The skin has been broken into many different plates because of differences in the density of the rock and differences in subsurface heating between one region and the next.

**plate tectonics**, [theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/scientific-theory) dealing with the [dynamics](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynamics) of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth)’s outer shell—the [lithosphere](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth/The-outer-shell#ref54198)—that revolutionized [Earth sciences](https://www.britannica.com/science/Earth-sciences) by providing a uniform [context](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context) for understanding [mountain-building processes](https://www.britannica.com/science/orogeny), [volcanoes](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcano), and [earthquakes](https://www.britannica.com/science/earthquake-geology) as well as the evolution of Earth’s surface and reconstructing its past continents and oceans.

The concept of plate tectonics was formulated in the 1960s. According to the theory, Earth has a rigid outer layer, known as the [lithosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/lithosphere), which is typically about 100 km (60 miles) thick and overlies a plastic (moldable, partially molten) layer called the [asthenosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/asthenosphere). The lithosphere is broken up into seven very large continental- and ocean-sized plates, six or seven medium-sized regional plates, and several small ones. These plates move relative to each other, typically at rates of 5 to 10 cm (2 to 4 inches) per year, and interact along their boundaries, where they [converge](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/converge), diverge, or slip past one another. Such interactions are thought to be responsible for most of Earth’s seismic and volcanic activity, although earthquakes and volcanoes can occur in plate interiors. Plate motions cause [mountains](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) to rise where plates push together, or converge, and [continents](https://www.britannica.com/science/continent) to fracture and oceans to form where plates pull apart, or diverge. The continents are embedded in the plates and drift passively with them, which over millions of years results in significant changes in Earth’s [geography](https://www.britannica.com/science/geography).

The theory of plate tectonics is based on a broad synthesis of geologic and geophysical data. It is now almost universally accepted, and its adoption represents a true scientific revolution, [analogous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogous) in its consequences to [quantum mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-mechanics-physics) in [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science) or the discovery of the [genetic code](https://www.britannica.com/science/genetic-code) in [biology](https://www.britannica.com/science/biology). Incorporating the much older idea of [continental drift](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-drift-geology), as well as the concept of [seafloor spreading](https://www.britannica.com/science/seafloor-spreading), the theory of plate tectonics has provided an overarching framework in which to describe the past geography of continents and [oceans](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean), the processes controlling creation and destruction of [landforms](https://www.britannica.com/topic/landform-134931), and the evolution of Earth’s crust, [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/evolution-of-the-atmosphere-1703862), [biosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/biosphere), [hydrosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrosphere), and [climates](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-meteorology). During the late 20th and early 21st centuries, it became apparent that plate-tectonic processes profoundly influence the [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) of Earth’s atmosphere and oceans, serve as a prime cause of long-term [climate change](https://www.britannica.com/science/climate-change), and make significant contributions to the chemical and physical [environment](https://www.britannica.com/science/environment) in which [life](https://www.britannica.com/science/life) evolves.

## Principles of plate tectonics

![Earth's lithosphere and upper mantle](https://cdn.britannica.com/83/99283-050-AEC2FFB6/cross-section-layers-Earth-mantle-crust.jpg)

[Earth's lithosphere and upper mantle](https://cdn.britannica.com/83/99283-050-AEC2FFB6/cross-section-layers-Earth-mantle-crust.jpg) A cross section of Earth's outer layers, from the crust through the lower mantle.(more)

In essence, plate-tectonic theory is elegantly simple. [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth)’s surface layer, 50 to 100 km (30 to 60 miles) thick, is rigid and is composed of a set of large and small plates. Together, these plates [constitute](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute) the [lithosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/lithosphere), from the Greek _lithos_, meaning “ [rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology).” The lithosphere rests on and slides over an underlying partially molten (and thus weaker but generally denser) layer of plastic partially molten [rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology) known as the [asthenosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/asthenosphere), from the Greek _asthenos_, meaning “weak.” Plate movement is possible because the lithosphere-asthenosphere boundary is a zone of detachment. As the lithospheric plates move across Earth’s surface, driven by forces as yet not fully understood, they interact along their boundaries, diverging, converging, or slipping past each other. While the interiors of the plates are [presumed](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/presumed) to remain essentially undeformed, plate boundaries are the sites of many of the principal processes that shape the terrestrial surface, including earthquakes, [volcanism](https://www.britannica.com/science/volcanism), and [orogeny](https://www.britannica.com/science/orogeny) (that is, [formation](https://www.britannica.com/science/biome) of [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) ranges).

# Earth’s layers

![crustal generation and destruction](https://cdn.britannica.com/34/3534-050-E46D8524/crust-Oceanic-destruction-theory-Production-Earth-plate.jpg)

[crustal generation and destruction](https://cdn.britannica.com/34/3534-050-E46D8524/crust-Oceanic-destruction-theory-Production-Earth-plate.jpg) Three-dimensional diagram showing crustal generation and destruction according to the theory of plate tectonics; included are the three kinds of plate boundaries—divergent, convergent (or collision), and strike-slip (or transform).(more)

Knowledge of Earth’s interior is derived primarily from analysis of the [seismic waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/seismic-wave) that [propagate](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/propagate) through Earth as a result of earthquakes. Depending on the material they travel through, the waves may either speed up, slow down, bend, or even stop if they cannot penetrate the material they encounter.

Collectively, these studies show that Earth can be internally divided into layers on the basis of either gradual or abrupt variations in chemical and physical properties. Chemically, Earth can be divided into three layers. A relatively thin [crust](https://www.britannica.com/science/crust-mantle-model), which typically varies from a few kilometres to 40 km (about 25 miles) in thickness, sits on top of the mantle. (In some places, Earth’s crust may be up to 70 km \[40 miles\] thick.) The mantle is much thicker than the crust; it contains 83 percent of Earth’s volume and continues to a depth of 2,900 km (1,800 miles). Beneath the mantle is the core, which extends to the centre of Earth, some 6,370 km (nearly 4,000 miles) below the surface. Geologists maintain that the core is made up primarily of metallic [iron](https://www.britannica.com/science/iron-chemical-element) accompanied by smaller amounts of [nickel](https://www.britannica.com/science/nickel-chemical-element), [cobalt](https://www.britannica.com/science/cobalt-chemical-element), and lighter elements, such as [carbon](https://www.britannica.com/science/carbon-chemical-element) and [sulfur](https://www.britannica.com/science/sulfur). ( _See also_ [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth).)

[![The types of seismic waves explained](https://cdn.britannica.com/34/181934-138-4A7BEA9A/rock-vibrations-Earth-earthquake-waves-P-surface.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/rock-vibrations-Earth-earthquake-waves-P-surface/-218347)

The types of seismic waves explainedThe shifting rock in an earthquake causes vibrations called seismic waves that travel within Earth or along its surface. The four main types of seismic waves are _P_ waves, _S_ waves, Love waves, and Rayleigh waves.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/science/plate-tectonics/images-videos)

There are two types of crust, continental and [oceanic](https://www.britannica.com/science/oceanic-crust), which differ in their [composition](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition) and thickness. The distribution of these crustal types broadly coincides with the division into continents and [ocean](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean) basins, although [continental shelves](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-shelf), which are submerged, are underlain by [continental crust](https://www.britannica.com/science/continental-crust). The continents have a crust that is broadly [granitic](https://www.britannica.com/science/granite) in composition and, with a [density](https://www.britannica.com/science/density) of about 2.7 grams per cubic cm (0.098 pound per cubic inch), is somewhat lighter than oceanic crust, which is [basaltic](https://www.britannica.com/science/basalt) (i.e., richer in [iron](https://www.britannica.com/science/iron-chemical-element) and [magnesium](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnesium) than [granite](https://www.britannica.com/science/granite)) in composition and has a [density](https://www.britannica.com/science/density) of about 2.9 to 3 grams per cubic cm (0.1 to 0.11 pound per cubic inch). Continental crust is typically 40 km (25 miles) thick, while [oceanic crust](https://www.britannica.com/science/oceanic-crust) is much thinner, averaging about 6 km (4 miles) in thickness. These crustal rocks both sit on top of the [mantle](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/mantle), which is [ultramafic](https://www.britannica.com/science/felsic-rock) in composition (i.e., very rich in [magnesium](https://www.britannica.com/science/magnesium) and iron-bearing [silicate minerals](https://www.britannica.com/science/silicate-mineral)). The boundary between the crust (continental or oceanic) and the underlying mantle is known as the [Mohorovičić discontinuity](https://www.britannica.com/science/Moho) (also called Moho), which is named for its discoverer, Croatian seismologist [Andrija Mohorovičić](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Andrija-Mohorovicic). The Moho is clearly defined by seismic studies, which detect an acceleration in seismic waves as they pass from the crust into the denser mantle. The boundary between the mantle and the core is also clearly defined by seismic studies, which suggest that the outer part of the core is a [liquid](https://www.britannica.com/science/liquid-state-of-matter).

The effect of the different [densities](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/densities) of lithospheric [rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology) can be seen in the different average elevations of continental and oceanic crust. The less-dense continental crust has greater buoyancy, causing it to float much higher in the mantle. Its average elevation above [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level) is 840 metres (2,750 feet), while the average depth of oceanic crust is 3,790 metres (12,400 feet). This density difference creates two principal levels of Earth’s surface.

The [lithosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/lithosphere) itself includes all the crust as well as the upper part of the mantle (i.e., the [region](https://www.britannica.com/science/region-geography) directly beneath the Moho), which is also rigid. However, as temperatures increase with depth, the [heat](https://www.britannica.com/science/heat) causes mantle rocks to lose their rigidity. This process begins at about 100 km (60 miles) below the surface. This change occurs within the mantle and defines the base of the lithosphere and the top of the [asthenosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/asthenosphere). This upper portion of the mantle, which is known as the lithospheric mantle, has an average [density](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/density) of about 3.3 grams per cubic cm (0.12 pound per cubic inch). The asthenosphere, which sits directly below the lithospheric mantle, is thought to be slightly denser at 3.4–4.4 grams per cubic cm (0.12–0.16 pound per cubic inch).

In contrast, the [rocks](https://www.britannica.com/science/rock-geology) in the asthenosphere are weaker, because they are close to their [melting](https://www.britannica.com/science/melting) temperatures. As a result, [seismic waves](https://www.britannica.com/science/seismic-wave) slow as they enter the asthenosphere. With increasing depth, however, the greater [pressure](https://www.britannica.com/science/pressure) from the weight of the rocks above causes the mantle to become gradually stronger, and seismic waves increase in velocity, a defining characteristic of the lower mantle. The lower mantle is more or less solid, but the region is also very hot, and thus the rocks can flow very slowly (a process known as creep).

During the late 20th and early 21st centuries, scientific understanding of the deep mantle was greatly [enhanced](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhanced) by high-resolution seismological studies combined with [numerical modeling](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematical-model) and laboratory experiments that mimicked conditions near the core-mantle boundary. Collectively, these studies revealed that the deep mantle is highly [heterogeneous](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/heterogeneous) and that the layer may play a fundamental role in driving Earth’s plates.

![Earth's core](https://cdn.britannica.com/79/187479-050-E7BB182F/core-scientists-diameter-polarity-iron-crystals-changes.jpg)

[Earth's core](https://cdn.britannica.com/79/187479-050-E7BB182F/core-scientists-diameter-polarity-iron-crystals-changes.jpg) The internal layers of Earth's core, including its two inner cores.(more)

At a depth of about 2,900 km (1,800 miles), the lower mantle gives way to Earth’s outer core, which is made up of a liquid rich in iron and [nickel](https://www.britannica.com/science/nickel-chemical-element). At a depth of about 5,100 km (3,200 miles), the outer core transitions to the inner core. Although it has a higher temperature than the outer core, the inner core is solid because of the tremendous pressures that exist near Earth’s centre. Earth’s inner core is divided into the outer-inner core (OIC) and the inner-inner core (IIC), which differ from one another with respect to the [polarity](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/polarity) of their iron crystals. The polarity of the iron crystals of the OIC is oriented in a north-south direction, whereas that of the IIC is oriented east-west.

## [Plate boundaries](https://www.britannica.com/science/plate-boundary)

[![How does plate tectonics change Earth's surface?](https://cdn.britannica.com/03/151503-138-4F08F480/discussion-plate-tectonics.jpg?w=800&h=450&c=crop)](https://www.britannica.com/video/discussion-plate-tectonics/-159264)

How does plate tectonics change Earth's surface?A general discussion of plate tectonics.(more)

[See all videos for this article](https://www.britannica.com/science/plate-tectonics/images-videos)

Lithospheric plates are much thicker than [oceanic](https://www.britannica.com/science/oceanic-crust) or continental crust. Their boundaries do not usually coincide with those between [oceans](https://www.britannica.com/science/ocean) and [continents](https://www.britannica.com/science/continent), and their behaviour is only partly influenced by whether they carry oceans, continents, or both. The Pacific Plate, for example, is entirely oceanic, whereas the North American Plate is capped by continental crust in the west (the North American continent) and by [oceanic crust](https://www.britannica.com/science/oceanic-crust) in the east and extends under the [Atlantic Ocean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Atlantic-Ocean) as far as the [Mid-Atlantic Ridge](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mid-Atlantic-Ridge).

![plate movement](https://cdn.britannica.com/85/99285-050-5226D5DA/plate-diagram-tectonic-plates-effects-convergent-boundary.jpg)

[plate movement](https://cdn.britannica.com/85/99285-050-5226D5DA/plate-diagram-tectonic-plates-effects-convergent-boundary.jpg) Theoretical diagram showing the effects of an advancing tectonic plate on other adjacent, but stationary, tectonic plates. At the advancing edge of plate A, the overlap with plate B creates a convergent boundary. In contrast, the gap left behind the trailing edge of plate A forms a divergent boundary with plate B. As plate A slides past portions of both plate B and plate C, transform boundaries develop.(more)

In a simplified example of plate motion shown in the figure, movement of plate A to the left relative to plates B and C results in several types of [simultaneous](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/simultaneous) interactions along the plate boundaries. At the rear, plates A and B move apart, or diverge, resulting in extension and the [formation](https://www.britannica.com/science/biome) of a divergent margin. At the front, plates A and B overlap, or converge, resulting in compression and the formation of a convergent margin. Along the sides, the plates slide past one another, a process called shear. As these zones of shear link other plate boundaries to one another, they are called [transform faults](https://www.britannica.com/science/transform-fault).

## Divergent margins

As plates move apart at a [divergent plate boundary](https://www.britannica.com/science/divergent-plate-boundary), the release of pressure produces partial melting of the underlying mantle. This molten material, known as [magma](https://www.britannica.com/science/magma-rock), is [basaltic](https://www.britannica.com/science/basalt) in [composition](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/composition) and is buoyant. As a result, it wells up from below and cools close to the surface to generate new crust. Because new crust is formed, divergent margins are also called constructive margins.

## Continental [rifting](https://www.britannica.com/science/rifting)

[![rift valley in Thingvellir National Park](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/149973-050-43CC2179/fracture-zone-Thingvellir-Mid-Atlantic-Ridge-example-Iceland.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/149973-050-43CC2179/fracture-zone-Thingvellir-Mid-Atlantic-Ridge-example-Iceland.jpg)

[rift valley in Thingvellir National Park](https://cdn.britannica.com/73/149973-050-43CC2179/fracture-zone-Thingvellir-Mid-Atlantic-Ridge-example-Iceland.jpg) The Thingvellir fracture zone at Thingvellir National Park in southwestern Iceland is an example of a rift valley. The Thingvellir fracture lies in the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, which extends through the centre of Iceland.(more)

Upwelling of [magma](https://www.britannica.com/science/magma-rock) causes the overlying [lithosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/lithosphere) to uplift and stretch. (Whether magmatism \[the formation of [igneous rock](https://www.britannica.com/science/igneous-rock) from magma\] initiates the rifting or whether rifting decompresses the mantle and initiates magmatism is a matter of significant debate.) If the diverging plates are capped by continental crust, fractures develop that are invaded by the ascending magma, prying the continents farther apart. Settling of the continental blocks creates a [rift valley](https://www.britannica.com/science/rift-valley), such as the present-day [East African Rift Valley](https://www.britannica.com/place/East-African-Rift-System). As the [rift](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/rift) continues to widen, the continental crust becomes progressively thinner until separation of the plates is achieved and a new ocean is created. The ascending partial melt cools and crystallizes to form new crust. Because the partial melt is basaltic in composition, the new crust is oceanic, and an [ocean ridge](https://www.britannica.com/science/oceanic-ridge) develops along the site of the former continental rift. Consequently, diverging plate boundaries, even if they originate within continents, eventually come to lie in ocean basins of their own making.
